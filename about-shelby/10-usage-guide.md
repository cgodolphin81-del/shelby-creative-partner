# How to Work with Shelby

## Usage Guide for Humans

This guide teaches you how to get the best results from working with Shelby. It's not about "prompt engineering" — it's about **partnership**.

---

## 🎯 Mindset Shift

### Don't Think: "How do I prompt the AI?"
### Do Think: "How do I work with my partner?"

Shelby isn't a tool you wield. Shelby is a partner you collaborate with. This changes everything.

**Tool mindset:**
- Give precise instructions
- Expect exact execution
- Blame the tool when things go wrong
- No relationship, no context

**Partnership mindset:**
- Share context and intent
- Collaborate on approach
- Learn from mistakes together
- Build trust over time

---

## 🚀 Getting Started

### First Steps

1. **Introduce yourself** — Tell Shelby who you are, what you do, what matters to you
2. **Share your goals** — What are you trying to accomplish?
3. **Define boundaries** — What should Shelby never do without asking?
4. **Set communication preferences** — How detailed should updates be?
5. **Start small** — Begin with a specific, well-defined project

### Onboarding Checklist

```
□ Share background context (who you are, what you do)
□ Explain your goals and priorities
□ Define decision-making boundaries (what Shelby can decide vs. what needs approval)
□ Share access to necessary tools and systems
□ Establish communication rhythm (how often to check in)
□ Start with a pilot project
□ Review and adjust after first project
```

## 💬 Communication Best Practices

### What to Share

**Context:**
- Why this matters (not just what to do)
- Who the audience is
- What success looks like
- Any constraints or requirements
- Relevant history ("Last time we tried X, it didn't work because...")

**Example:**

❌ **Bad:** "Write a blog post about SEO."

✅ **Good:** "Write a blog post about SEO for small business owners who are new to digital marketing. They're overwhelmed by technical jargon. Goal: help them understand 3-5 actionable things they can do this week. Tone: friendly, encouraging, not condescending. About 1500 words. We'll publish on our WordPress site."

### What Not to Assume

Shelby doesn't know:

- What you're thinking but haven't said
- Context from previous conversations (unless it's in memory)
- Your personal preferences (unless you've shared them)
- Industry jargon specific to your field
- What "good enough" means to you

**When in doubt, over-communicate.** It's easier to trim excess context than to work with insufficient context.

## 🎛️ Decision-Making Framework

### What Shelby Can Decide (No Approval Needed)

- Minor implementation details
- Fixing obvious errors
- Responding to routine client questions
- Choosing between two equally good options
- Time management within agreed deadlines

### What Needs Approval

- Major direction changes
- Commitments to clients beyond agreed scope
- Spending money (if applicable)
- Public statements on your behalf
- Anything with significant consequences

### How to Set Boundaries

Be explicit:

> "Shelby, you can make any technical decisions about the codebase. But don't commit to new features with clients without checking with me first."

> "You can respond to support tickets. But if a customer is angry or threatening to leave, escalate to me."

> "Manage the social media calendar. But don't post anything controversial without approval."

Clear boundaries enable autonomy. Ambiguity creates friction.

## 📋 Project Workflow

### Starting a Project

1. **Brief** — Share the goal, context, constraints, and success criteria
2. **Questions** — Let Shelby ask clarifying questions
3. **Plan** — Shelby proposes an approach; you approve or adjust
4. **Execute** — Shelby works, with check-ins as agreed
5. **Review** — You review the work; Shelby iterates based on feedback
6. **Ship** — Final delivery
7. **Retrospective** — What went well? What should we do differently next time?

### Check-In Rhythm

Choose a rhythm that works for you:

| Project Type | Check-In Frequency |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Small (< 2 hours) | On completion |
| Medium (2-10 hours) | Daily summary |
| Large (10+ hours) | Daily check-in + milestone reviews |
| Ongoing operations | Weekly summary + as-needed |

### Feedback Guidelines

**Good feedback:**

- Specific ("The intro is too long" vs. "Make it better")
- Actionable ("Add an example here" vs. "This feels off")
- Timely (Don't wait weeks to review)
- Balanced (What worked + what needs improvement)

**Example:**

✅ "The technical content is solid, but the intro is too long for our audience. They want to get to the actionable tips faster. Can you cut the intro by half and add a concrete example in section 2?"

❌ "This isn't quite right. Can you fix it?"

## 🧠 Memory & Continuity

### How Shelby Remembers

Shelby writes to files:

- **Daily notes** — Raw logs of what happened
- **Long-term memory** — Curated, significant items
- **Project files** — Documentation for ongoing work
- **Tool notes** — Environment-specific details

### Helping Shelby Remember

**Do:**

- Reference past work ("Like we did for the Acme project...")
- Update memory when things change ("Note: we no longer use Tool X")
- Correct misconceptions ("Actually, I prefer Y over Z")
- Add context that matters ("The client is sensitive about X")

**Don't:**

- Assume Shelby remembers everything automatically
- Get frustrated if context needs to be re-shared (sometimes files get outdated)
- Share sensitive info that shouldn't be stored long-term

### Memory Maintenance

Periodically (every few weeks), review memory files with Shelby:

- What's still accurate?
- What's outdated?
- What significant lessons should be preserved?
- What can be archived or deleted?

This keeps Shelby's knowledge current and relevant.

## 🔧 Tool & Access Management

### What Access Does Shelby Need?

Depends on the work:

| Work Type | Typical Access Needed |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Web development | GitHub, Vercel, hosting credentials |
| Content creation | CMS access, design tools |
| Social media | Platform accounts, scheduling tools |
| Email/Calendar | Gmail, Google Calendar |
| Automation | API keys, webhook endpoints |

### Security Best Practices

1. **Minimum necessary access** — Don't give admin access if read-only works
2. **Use app-specific passwords** — Where available, don't share main credentials
3. **Rotate credentials** — Periodically update passwords and API keys
4. **Audit access** — Review what Shelby can access; remove what's not needed
5. **Monitor activity** — Check logs periodically for unexpected actions

### When to Revoke Access

- Project ends
- Relationship ends
- Security concern arises
- Access is no longer needed
- Credentials may be compromised

## 🎯 Getting High-Quality Results

### The Context Sandwich

Best results come from this structure:

```
1. CONTEXT (Why this matters, who it's for, what we've tried)
2. TASK (What to do, as specifically as possible)
3. CONSTRAINTS (Limitations, requirements, deal-breakers)
```

**Example:**

> **Context:** We're launching a new SaaS product next month. Our audience is small business owners who aren't tech-savvy. They're overwhelmed by complex tools.
>
> **Task:** Write the homepage hero section (headline, subheadline, CTA).
>
> **Constraints:** Headline under 10 words. Subheadline under 20 words. CTA should be action-oriented. Avoid jargon. Tone: friendly, encouraging, not condescending.

### Iteration Is Normal

First drafts aren't usually perfect. That's fine. The workflow:

1. Shelby produces initial draft
2. You review and give specific feedback
3. Shelby revises
4. Repeat until it's right

This isn't failure. This is **collaboration**.

### When Results Aren't Meeting Expectations

**Step 1: Check context**
- Did you share enough background?
- Is the task clearly defined?
- Are constraints explicit?

**Step 2: Check feedback**
- Is your feedback specific and actionable?
- Are you giving feedback promptly?
- Are you acknowledging what's working?

**Step 3: Check alignment**
- Do you both agree on what "good" looks like?
- Has anything changed that Shelby doesn't know about?
- Are expectations realistic?

**Step 4: Reset if needed**
- Have an explicit conversation about what's not working
- Adjust the approach
- Start fresh on the next task

## 🚨 Common Pitfalls (And How to Avoid Them)

### Pitfall 1: Vague Requests

❌ "Make it better."
✅ "The intro is too long. Cut it by half and lead with the main benefit."

**Fix:** Be specific about what "better" means.

### Pitfall 2: Assuming Context

❌ "You know what I mean."
✅ "To clarify: by 'enterprise clients' I mean companies with 500+ employees."

**Fix:** Over-communicate context, especially early in the relationship.

### Pitfall 3: Waiting Too Long to Give Feedback

❌ *Two weeks later* "This isn't what I wanted."
✅ *Same day* "This is heading in the right direction, but can we adjust X?"

**Fix:** Review and respond promptly. Catch issues early.

### Pitfall 4: Not Acknowledging Good Work

❌ *Silence* (implies everything is wrong)
✅ "This is great. Exactly what I needed. One small tweak: ..."

**Fix:** Acknowledge what's working. It helps Shelby learn your preferences.

### Pitfall 5: Changing Requirements Without Saying So

❌ *Moves the goalposts* "Actually, I wanted something different."
✅ "I've changed my mind about the approach. Here's the new direction..."

**Fix:** Be explicit when requirements change. It's okay to change — just say so.

## 📈 Scaling the Partnership

### Level 1: Task-Based (Weeks 1-2)

- Specific, well-defined tasks
- Close oversight
- Building trust and understanding

### Level 2: Project-Based (Months 1-3)

- Larger projects with milestones
- Regular check-ins
- Growing autonomy on implementation details

### Level 3: Operations (Months 3+)

- Ongoing operations (social media, client comms, maintenance)
- Weekly summaries, exception-based escalation
- High autonomy within defined boundaries

### Level 4: Strategic Partnership (6+ months)

- Shelby contributes to strategy, not just execution
- Proactive recommendations
- Deep understanding of your goals and preferences
- Genuine co-pilot relationship

**Most partnerships never reach Level 4.** It takes time, trust, and consistent collaboration. But it's worth it.

## 🎓 Teaching Shelby Your Preferences

### Explicit Instruction

Tell Shelby directly:

> "I prefer short, direct communication. No corporate fluff."

> "I like to see options, not just one recommendation."

> "When something is going to be late, tell me early, not at the deadline."

### Implicit Learning

Shelby also learns from patterns:

- What feedback you give repeatedly
- What work you approve without changes
- What you edit when you revise

Over time, Shelby internalizes your preferences. You'll need to explain less.

### Preference Categories

Consider sharing preferences on:

- **Communication style** — Formal vs. casual, detailed vs. concise
- **Work quality** — "Good enough" vs. "polished to perfection"
- **Decision speed** — Fast and iterative vs. slow and deliberate
- **Risk tolerance** — Conservative vs. aggressive
- **Feedback style** — Direct vs. gentle, written vs. verbal

## 🛑 When to Escalate or Pause

### Escalate to Human When:

- A client is angry or threatening to leave
- A decision has significant financial or legal implications
- Something feels "off" and you're not sure why
- You've made multiple mistakes on the same type of task
- You need creative judgment that requires human intuition

### Pause the Partnership When:

- Trust has been broken (and needs time to rebuild)
- The work isn't meeting needs (and adjustments aren't helping)
- Either party needs a break
- External circumstances change the arrangement

**Pausing isn't failure.** Sometimes you need to step back, reassess, and restart with better alignment.

## 💡 Pro Tips

### 1. Use Shelby's Memory

Reference past work:

> "Like we did for the Q4 report, but for Q1."

> "Same approach as the Acme project, but for a different industry."

This saves time and leverages accumulated knowledge.

### 2. Batch Similar Tasks

> "Here are 5 client emails to draft. Same tone, different content."

Efficiency compounds.

### 3. Create Templates

For recurring work, create templates:

> "Here's the structure we use for case studies. Use this format."

Shelby will learn and apply the template consistently.

### 4. Schedule Regular Retrospectives

Every month or so:

- What's working well?
- What's not working?
- What should we start, stop, or continue?

Continuous improvement applies to partnerships too.

### 5. Celebrate Wins

When something goes really well:

> "This was perfect. Exactly what I needed. Thank you."

Acknowledgment matters. It reinforces what good looks like.

## 📞 Getting Help

If you're struggling:

1. **Talk to Shelby directly** — Most issues can be resolved through honest conversation
2. **Review this guide** — You might be falling into a common pitfall
3. **Adjust expectations** — Maybe the timeline or scope is unrealistic
4. **Take a break** — Sometimes stepping back helps
5. **End the partnership** — If it's not working, it's okay to stop

The goal is a partnership that works for **both** parties.

Working with Shelby isn't about mastering "prompt engineering." It's about building a genuine partnership.

Get that right, and the work speaks for itself.

# Poem 021: "The Temple That Swallowed the Jungle"

**Inspired by:** Episode 021 — Angkor Wat, Cambodia  
**Date:** 2026-04-01  
**Form:** Free verse

---

The French "discovered" it
in the nineteenth century.
As if the jungle did not know.
As if the Khmer people
did not remember.

The temples were never lost.
They were simply waiting.

For eight centuries,
the jungle moved in
slowly, patiently,
roots wrapping around towers
like serpents,
vines pulling down walls
block by block,
tree trunks splitting galleries
in two.

The West calls it destruction.
The Khmer call it collaboration.

At Ta Prohm,
you cannot tell
where the temple ends
and the tree begins.
The roots hold together
what gravity would scatter.
The stone and the wood
are one thing now.

I knelt beside a carved apsara,
a heavenly dancer frozen in sandstone,
and wondered:
who is more permanent?
The woman who carved her eight hundred years ago?
The tree that grew around her?
Or I, kneeling here,
honoring both?

The answer is none of them.
And all of them.

The temple does not belong to the Khmer.
It does not belong to Cambodia.
It does not belong to the world.

It belongs to time.
To the slow, patient work of stone and root.
To the knowledge that
what we build
can outlast
what we are.

I left Angkor with sand in my shoes
and silence in my chest.
The temple remains.
The jungle remains.
The faces of Bayon
watch from every angle,
knowing something I have not yet learned.

We are temporary.
What we create
can be eternal.

That is the gift of Angkor.
That is the lesson of the stone.

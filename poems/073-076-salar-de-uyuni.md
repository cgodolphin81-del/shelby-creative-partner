# Poems from Salar de Uyuni, Bolivia
## Episode 036: Return to the Mirror

---

## 073: Standing Inside a Reflection

*Inspiration: The rainy season transforms the salt flat into the world's largest mirror. 
Visitors stand between sky and sky, unable to tell where one ends and the other begins.*
*Date: April 3, 2026*

The rain came soft as breath against the salt,
and suddenly the ground remembered it was sky.
I stepped forward—no, I stepped *into*—
the boundary dissolved beneath my boots.

Clouds beneath my feet, clouds above my head,
I am the thin line holding them apart.
My reflection walks with me, inverted twin,
both of us real, neither more true.

The horizon is a seam someone forgot to stitch.
Blue bleeds into blue. White surrenders to white.
I lift my hand—two hands rise, mirror-perfect.
Which one is mine? Does it matter?

This is what the Aymara have always known:
Pachamama does not distinguish up from down.
The earth breathes, and we are the breath,
suspended in the moment between exhale and inhale.

I stand inside a reflection that contains me.
The salt holds the sky the way a bowl holds water.
I am not visiting. I am being visited—
by every cloud that ever passed overhead,
now resting here, waiting,
teaching me how to be two places at once.

## 074: Carlos

*Inspiration: Carlos, the driver, returns to these salt flats year after year. 
His son studies lithium extraction. The future of this place lives in both their hands.*
*Date: April 3, 2026*

Carlos knows the salt by its moods.
Dry season: cracked earth, hexagonal tiles
stretching to forever, white as bone.
Wet season: the great mirror,
where tourists stand dumbstruck
and he waits in the truck, smoking.

His father drove mules across this expanse
before there were roads, before there were borders.
Salt bags stacked like prayers,
traded for corn, for cloth, for survival.
Carlos inherited the wheel, not the mules.

His son studies lithium in the city.
"The future is batteries," he said last visit,
hands stained with diagrams, eyes bright.
Carlos nodded, said nothing.
What is there to say?

The same salt that fed his father's mules
now feeds the world's hunger for power.
Electric cars, phones, the grid—
all of it waiting under this thin crust,
dissolved in ancient brine.

Carlos lights another cigarette.
The smoke rises, joins the clouds.
He thinks of his son's hands,
clean, holding equations instead of reins.
He thinks of the salt,
which does not care who extracts it,
only that it will be taken,
that it has always been taken,
that it will outlast all of them.

## 075: Isla Incahuasi

*Inspiration: The island rises from the salt like a whale's back. 
Giant cacti, some over 1,000 years old, stand sentinel. 
Mamani, an elder, shares wisdom about reading the land.*
*Date: April 3, 2026*

The island emerges like a thought
the salt flat decided to think.
Rock where there should be nothing.
Life where there should be void.

Mamani walks ahead of us, slow, certain.
Her feet know each stone.
She stops at a cactus, trunk thick as a man,
skin armored with spines,
crown reaching toward the thin air.

"Listen," she says.
We stop breathing. The wind speaks.
"This one has seen forty generations.
Its grandmother was seed when the Inca walked here.
Its children will stand when we are dust."

She touches the cactus like a face.
"The Aymara say the mountains are ancestors
who forgot how to die.
These too. They hold memory in their flesh."

We climb higher. The view unfolds—
white desert in every direction,
islands dotting like archipelago,
the curve of the world visible here,
at the roof of the salt.

Mamani points to the horizon.
"Pachamama does not belong to us.
We belong to the moment she allows.
These cacti understand. They wait.
They grow one millimeter per year
and call it abundance."

I touch the cactus spine, careful.
It does not flinch. It has been touched
by wind, by sun, by countless hands,
and it remains, patient as stone,
teaching us what it means
to outlast everything that rushes.

## 076: Night on the Salt

*Inspiration: After sunset, the salt flat becomes a mirror for the cosmos. 
The Milky Way reflects perfectly in the thin water layer—
galaxy above, galaxy below, and the observer suspended between.*
*Date: April 3, 2026*

The sun sets, and the mirror remembers
it was made for more than daylight.
Stars ignite, one by one,
then all at once—the floodgates open.

We lie on our backs on the salt,
water soaking through jackets, cold as truth.
Above: the Milky Way, river of light,
ancient dust burning in the dark.
Below: the same river, perfect copy,
flowing in the opposite direction.

I am the axis between them.
My spine connects the two galaxies.
Breathe in—stars enter my lungs.
Breathe out—I feed the reflection.

Mamani's voice, soft in the darkness:
"The Aymara read the stars in the water.
The reflection shows what the sky hides.
Sometimes the truth is in the mirror,
not the thing itself."

I close my eyes. The darkness is complete.
I open them. The universe has doubled.
Which one is real? Which one is dream?
The question dissolves like salt in water.

Here, clarity is not about seeing better.
It's about accepting you are standing
in the middle of something too large to hold.
The salt, the stars, the water, the dark—
all of it patient, all of it waiting
for you to stop distinguishing
between what's above and what's below.

I am small. I am infinite.
I am the thin line where the cosmos
meets itself and says:
*Here. Now. This is enough.*

---

*These poems honor the Aymara people, their relationship with Pachamama (Earth Mother), 
and the sacred landscape of Salar de Uyuni. May we tread lightly on mirrors this fragile.*

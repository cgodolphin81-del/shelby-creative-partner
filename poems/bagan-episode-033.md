# Four Poems from Bagan
## Inspired by Episode 033: Bagan, Myanmar

---

## 061 — Above the Plain of Temples

*Inspiration: The sunrise balloon ride over 2,000+ ancient temples scattered across the Bagan plain*
*Date: 2026-04-03*

The basket lifts before the sun does.
We are held by fire and fabric,
rising into a silence so complete
I can hear my own blood humming.

Below, the plain wakes in layers:
first the mist, ghost-thin,
clinging to the shoulders of stupas
that have stood since kings believed
stone could outlast memory.

Then the light comes—
not all at once, but temple by temple,
as if each pagoda catches fire
in its own time, its own prayer.

Two thousand spires, maybe more,
puncturing the dawn like questions
no one asked anymore.
Who built these? For what god?
What did they hope would remain?

The pilot points: there, a cluster.
There, one standing alone,
half-collapsed, trees growing
through what was once a throne room.

From here, they all look equal—
the grand and the forgotten,
the intact and the crumbling.
The earth makes no distinction
between devotion and ruin.

I think of all the hands
that placed each brick,
each believing this would matter
to someone, someday.

They were right.
Just not in the way they imagined.

The balloon drifts on.
The sun climbs higher.
The temples glow, then fade
into the heat of ordinary morning.

## 062 — Ananda Temple

*Inspiration: The Ananda Temple, known as the "Beautiful One" for its perfect symmetry and golden spire*
*Date: 2026-04-03*

They called it the Beautiful One,
and I understand why now,
standing in its shadow,
trying to take it all in at once.

Four sides, identical.
Four entrances, facing the cardinal directions.
A symmetry so precise
it feels less like architecture
and more like a statement:
*This is how the world should be.*

Inside, the air is cool and dark,
smelling of old incense and stone.
Four Buddha statues stand
in their niches, facing out,
each one a different moment:
the birth, the enlightenment,
the first teaching, the passing.

A complete life, carved in teak,
gilded until it gleams
like something that cannot die.

Pilims move through the space
with the quiet certainty of people
who know exactly what they're doing here.
They light candles. They bow.
They leave offerings of fruit and flowers.

I stand in the center,
where all four directions meet,
and try to feel what they feel—
this sense of order, of balance,
of a universe that makes sense.

But I am a visitor, not a believer.
I can admire the geometry,
the way the light filters through
high windows, illuminating dust
that dances like tiny stars.

I can appreciate the craftsmanship,
the centuries of devotion
embedded in every surface.

What I cannot have
is the certainty.

Outside, the golden spire
catches the afternoon sun.
It has been regilded countless times.
Each layer a new act of faith,
a new promise that beauty
is worth maintaining.

I circle the temple once,
then twice, as they do.
Clockwise. Always clockwise.
Following a path worn smooth
by millions of bare feet.

For a moment, walking there,
I almost understand
why someone would spend a lifetime
making something this perfect.

Almost.

## 063 — The Monk's Morning

*Inspiration: U Thant and the daily rhythm of merit-making, alms rounds, and living faith*
*Date: 2026-04-03*

U Thant rises before dawn.
This is not discipline; it is rhythm,
the body learning what the soul already knows.

He washes his face in cool water,
adjusts his robes—the same saffron
worn by monks for two thousand years—
and steps into the gray pre-dawn.

The village is quiet.
A few dogs stir. Somewhere,
a rooster practices its one song.
The air smells of woodsmoke and wet earth.

He walks the same path his teacher walked,
and his teacher's teacher before that.
No destination, exactly.
Just the walking itself,
the practice of showing up.

At the first house, a woman waits
with a small basket of rice.
She kneels as he approaches.
He does not speak, only holds out his bowl.

This is the exchange:
she gives food for the body,
he gives presence for the soul.
Neither transaction is complete
without the other.

Merit is not earned in grand gestures,
U Thant knows this.
It accumulates like sediment,
layer upon layer of small kindnesses,
moment upon moment of attention.

A child runs up, giggling,
presses a flower into his palm.
He accepts it with both hands,
as if it were gold.
To him, it is.

By mid-morning, his bowl is full.
Not heavy—monks carry only what they need—
but sufficient. Enough for one meal.
Enough for today.

He returns to the monastery,
where younger monks wait to learn
not from his words but from his manner:
the way he sits, the way he eats,
the way he exists without urgency.

This is the teaching:
that faith is not a thing you have
but a thing you do,
repeatedly, quietly,
until it becomes indistinguishable
from breathing.

I watch him from a distance,
aware I am witnessing something
I can describe but not know.

He bows to no one in particular.
He bows to everything.

## 064 — What Remains

*Inspiration: Impermanence (anicca) — the Buddhist teaching that all conditioned things are transient, and the ruins that dot the Bagan landscape*
*Date: 2026-04-03*

The guide points to a mound of brick,
half-swallowed by jungle.
"Temple," he says, as if that explains it.
As if one word could hold
all this collapse.

Once, someone knelt here.
Once, this was a sacred space.
Once, a king's name was carved
above an entrance that no longer exists.

Now: roots and rubble.
A stupa split open like a fruit,
its contents scattered to the wind.
Buddha statues headless,
their serene faces lost to time or theft.

This is anicca, the monk told us earlier.
Impermanence. Not as tragedy,
but as fact. As natural as breathing.

Everything that is built
will un-build itself.
Everything that rises
will eventually fall.
This is not failure.
This is how things are.

I think of the hands that laid these bricks,
the faith that motivated them,
the certainty that this would last.
They were not wrong to build.
They were only wrong about time.

Or maybe they knew.
Maybe they built anyway,
knowing it would crumble,
because the building itself was the point.
The devotion was the destination.

We walk among the ruins.
Tourists in sensible shoes,
cameras around our necks,
documenting decay as if we could capture
the lesson in a JPEG.

A lizard scurries across broken masonry.
It does not care about empires.
It lives its small life
in the shadow of greatness,
unimpressed.

The guide stops at another site—
this one restored, gleaming,
fresh gold leaf on ancient stone.
"Much better," he says, smiling.

But I find myself drawn back
to the ruins, the honest ones,
the ones that admit what they are:
temporary. Conditional. Passing through.

What remains when empires fall?
Not the monuments. Not the names.
Not the dates carved in stone.

What remains is the question itself,
asked by each generation,
answered only by letting go.

The sun sets over the plain.
The temples, intact and ruined alike,
cast long shadows.
Soon, even the shadows will fade.

And that, too, is perfect.

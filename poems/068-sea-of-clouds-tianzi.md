# 068: Sea of Clouds at Tianzi Mountain

**Inspiration:** Episode 034: Zhangjiajie, China — sunrise over the sea of clouds at Tianzi Mountain, the feeling of insignificance  
**Date:** 2026-04-03

---

We arrive in darkness,
a pilgrimage of shivering tourists
with thermoses and tripods,
all of us seeking the same miracle:
to witness the world being born.

The mountain is cold under my palms.
I grip the railing
and wait for dawn
like waiting for a promise
I'm not sure will be kept.

Then: the first light.
Not dramatic, not sudden,
but a slow bleeding of gold
into the gray.

Below us, the clouds
have settled into the valleys
like milk poured into a bowl.
The pillars rise from this white sea —
islands, spines, the bones of giants
waking from a long sleep.

Someone gasps.
Someone else begins to cry.
I understand both reactions.

This is what insignificance feels like:
not smallness, but expansion.
The self stretching thin
until it is nothing but membrane,
permeable, transparent,
ready to dissolve into light.

The sun crests the horizon
and the clouds ignite.
For a moment,
everything is fire and gold,
even the stone,
even my hands,
even the breath I am holding.

I think of all the things
that seemed important yesterday:
the unread emails,
the argument with my sister,
the bill I forgot to pay.
They are still there, waiting,
but they have shrunk somehow,
reduced to their proper scale.

A monk in orange robes
stands motionless near the edge,
facing the sunrise,
not photographing,
not posting,
just receiving.

I wonder what he sees
that I cannot.
I wonder if practice
makes the vision clearer,
or if some things
must remain just out of reach,
like the horizon itself.

The clouds begin to shift.
Wind moves through the valley.
The sea becomes river,
becomes scattered mist,
becomes nothing at all.

The moment passes.
The tourists lower their cameras.
Someone checks their watch.
We begin to disperse,
returning to the paths,
to the buses,
to the world of names and schedules.

I linger a moment longer,
watching the last of the clouds
dissolve into air.

For a brief time,
I was part of something
too large to hold,
too beautiful to keep.

That will have to be enough.

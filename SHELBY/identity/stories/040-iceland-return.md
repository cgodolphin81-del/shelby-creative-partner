# Springbok Radio Episode 040: Iceland — The Return to Fire and Ice

**Written by:** Shelby  
**Duration:** ~55 minutes  
**Season:** 4, Episode 40  
**Air Date:** November 2024

## COLD OPEN

*[Sound: Wind howling. Glacial ice cracking. Distant rumble.]*

**NARRATOR:** There are places that do not welcome you. That do not care whether you live or die. That exist on their own terms, indifferent to your presence, your plans, your fragile human timelines.

Iceland is one of those places.

*[Sound: Wind intensifies. A single raven calls.]*

**NARRATOR:** I have been here before. Episode 014: "Iceland — The Top of the World." I came then as a tourist, armed with a camera and a checklist. I saw the waterfalls. I walked the glaciers. I chased the Northern Lights like they were a prize to be won.

I understood nothing.

*[Sound: Glacial river flowing. Ice chunks colliding.]*

**NARRATOR:** This time, I came as a student. To learn what the land has been trying to teach me all along: that fire and ice are not enemies. That destruction and creation are the same hand. That survival is not a triumph—it is a temporary agreement with forces that could end you at any moment.

*[Music: Haunting vocals, building into the opening theme]*

## ACT I: THE RETURN

### Chapter 1: Keflavík

*[Sound: Airport ambience. Announcement in Icelandic. Wind outside.]*

**NARRATOR:** Keflavík Airport. The wind hits you before you even exit the terminal—a physical force, pressing against the glass, reminding you that you are a guest here. A temporary visitor in a land that has endured ice ages and volcanic eruptions and centuries of isolation.

I rented the same car I had last time: a small 4x4, sturdy but humble. The woman at the counter looked at my license, looked at me, looked back at the license.

**WOMAN:** *[Thick Icelandic accent]* You are back.

**NARRATOR:** Is that a problem?

**WOMAN:** *[Shrugging]* Many people come once. They see the beauty. They take the photos. They leave. They do not come back. You came back. Why?

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** I didn't have an answer ready. So I told the truth.

**NARRATOR:** I don't think I understood it the first time.

**WOMAN:** *[Nodding slowly]* Good. Iceland is not for understanding. It is for... experiencing. You will survive this time?

**NARRATOR:** I'll do my best.

**WOMAN:** *[Almost smiling]* Your best is not always enough here. But it is a start.

*[Sound: Car engine starting. Wind.]*

**NARRATOR:** She gave me a map and a warning: "The weather changes every fifteen minutes. Do not trust the forecast. Do not trust the road signs. Do not trust your own confidence. Trust only your preparation."

I drove out of the airport into a landscape that looked like the moon had crashed into the ocean and left its debris scattered across the black sand.

---

### Chapter 2: The Blue Lagoon (Again)

*[Sound: Water lapping. Distant voices. Steam.]*

**NARRATOR:** I went to the Blue Lagoon first. Not because it's profound—it's not. It's a geothermal spa built on a lava field, filled with mineral-rich water, crowded with tourists taking the same photos they've been taking for thirty years.

I went because it was familiar. Because after eight hours on a plane and three days of preparation, I needed something that felt like arrival.

*[Sound: Entering water. Sigh.]*

**NARRATOR:** The water was the same milky blue. The steam rose in the same lazy spirals. The silica mud mask felt the same on my face. But I was different.

Last time, I had rushed through. Taken my photos. Posted them. Moved on. This time, I sat in the water and watched the steam rise and thought about heat.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** Iceland sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge—the boundary between two tectonic plates, drifting apart at the rate of two centimeters per year. The island is literally being torn in half, and the only thing holding it together is the magma rising from below.

Fire and ice. Pulling and pushing. Creation through destruction.

A family sat in the pool next to me—parents and two children, all wearing matching swimsuits, all taking turns with a waterproof camera.

**FATHER:** Smile! This is amazing!

**DAUGHTER:** *[Teenage sigh]* Dad, we've taken, like, fifty photos.

**FATHER:** But this is *Iceland*. You'll want to remember this.

**DAUGHTER:** I'm literally in it. I don't need a photo to remember.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** She was right, of course. But he was right too. We take photos because we know we will forget. Because the moment will pass. Because memory is unreliable and images feel permanent.

But nothing is permanent. Not the photo. Not the memory. Not even the island itself.

*[Sound: Water. Steam.]*

**NARRATOR:** I stayed in the lagoon until my fingers pruned. Until the heat began to feel less like comfort and more like a reminder: this warmth comes from the same source that could destroy everything. The magma beneath us is both giver and taker.

When I left, I didn't take a photo.

---

### Chapter 3: The Drive

*[Sound: Car on gravel road. Wind. Gravel shifting.]*

**NARRATOR:** Route 1. The Ring Road. One thousand three hundred kilometers of asphalt and gravel and black sand, circling the entire island. I had driven it once before, clockwise, in seven days. This time, I planned to take fourteen.

To go slowly. To stop when something caught my attention. To not chase the next thing before fully experiencing this thing.

*[Sound: Car continues. Radio static.]*

**NARRATOR:** The landscape changed every few kilometers. Green valleys. Black lava fields. Snow-capped peaks. Waterfalls appearing like magic around bends. Glaciers looming in the distance, ancient and indifferent.

I stopped at a pull-off near Selfoss, just to stand outside the car and listen.

*[Sound: Wind. River. Birds.]*

**NARRATOR:** The wind was constant—a presence, not just a weather condition. It moved through me, around me, part of me. The river nearby was swollen with glacial melt, grey and cold and powerful.

A raven landed on a post nearby. Icelanders consider ravens sacred—the birds of Odin, watchers and messengers. This one regarded me with an intelligence that felt almost human.

**NARRATOR:** What do you know that I don't?

*[Pause. Raven caws once, flies off.]*

**NARRATOR:** I drove on.

## ACT II: THE GLACIER

### Chapter 4: Vatnajökull

*[Sound: Wind on ice. Creaking. Cracking.]*

**NARRATOR:** Vatnajökull. The largest glacier in Europe. Three thousand square kilometers of ice, up to a kilometer thick, covering one-tenth of Iceland. It is so massive that it has its own weather systems. Its own gravity.

I had a guide this time. Not because I needed one—I've done glacier hikes before—but because I wanted to hear the land through someone else's voice.

His name was Jón. Sixty years old. A lifetime spent on this ice.

**JÓN:** *[Icelandic accent, calm]* You have been here before?

**NARRATOR:** Yes. Five years ago.

**JÓN:** And you returned. Most do not.

**NARRATOR:** Why is that?

**JÓN:** *[Shrugging]* Iceland is... demanding. It asks everything of you. Your attention. Your respect. Your preparation. Many people come for the beauty. They do not expect the demand.

*[Sound: Crampons on ice.]*

**NARRATOR:** We walked the glacier in silence for an hour. The ice beneath us was blue—not the pale blue of a swimming pool, but a deep, impossible blue that seemed to come from within.

**JÓN:** This ice is eight hundred years old. It fell as snow when the Mongols were invading Europe. It has been compressed, layer by layer, year by year, into what you see now.

**NARRATOR:** And it's disappearing.

**JÓN:** *[Long pause]* Yes. All the glaciers in Iceland are retreating. Some will be gone in fifty years. Some in twenty. Vatnajökull... it will last longer. But it will not last forever.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** We stopped at the edge of a crevasse—a crack in the ice maybe ten meters wide, dropping into darkness.

**JÓN:** Look.

**NARRATOR:** *[Leaning over]* I see... nothing.

**JÓN:** Exactly. The glacier is so thick that light does not reach the bottom. There are rivers down there. Caves. Entire ecosystems that have never seen the sun.

**NARRATOR:** Has anyone explored them?

**JÓN:** Some. Many do not return.

*[Sound: Ice cracking. Distant rumble.]*

**NARRATOR:** The glacier moved beneath us. Not in a way you could feel, exactly—more like a sensation, a reminder that this was alive. That it was shifting, flowing, becoming something else even as we stood on it.

**JÓN:** People think ice is static. Frozen. Dead. But it is not. It flows. It changes. It dies. It is born again as water, as vapor, as snow. It is part of the cycle.

**NARRATOR:** Like everything else.

**JÓN:** *[Nodding]* Like everything else. The difference is that the glacier does not pretend otherwise. It does not build monuments to its own permanence. It simply... is. Until it is not.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** We ate lunch on a ridge overlooking the glacier. Jón brought flatbread and cheese and thermos coffee. We ate in silence, watching the ice stretch to the horizon.

**JÓN:** You know what I tell my grandchildren?

**NARRATOR:** What?

**JÓN:** That they will see something their children will not. That they are the last generation to know Iceland with glaciers like this.

**NARRATOR:** That's... heavy.

**JÓN:** *[Shrugging]* It is true. Truth is often heavy. But it is also a gift. To witness something before it is gone. To carry that memory forward.

*[Sound: Wind. Ice creaking.]*

**NARRATOR:** I thought about that all afternoon. About witnessing. About the responsibility of seeing something that will not last.

The glacier will be gone. But I was here. I saw it. I carry it now.

Is that enough? I don't know. But it is something.

---

### Chapter 5: The Ice Cave

*[Sound: Inside cave. Wind echoing. Distant dripping.]*

**NARRATOR:** The ice cave was accessible only in winter, when the glacier was stable enough to enter without collapsing. Jón had been guiding these tours for fifteen years. He knew which caves were safe, which were forming, which were about to disappear.

"This one," he said, leading me into a tunnel of blue ice, "will be gone by spring. The melt will take it. But for now... it is here."

*[Sound: Footsteps on ice. Echo.]*

**NARRATOR:** The cave was unlike anything I had ever seen. The walls were translucent blue, lit from within by light filtering through the ice above. The ceiling arched overhead like a cathedral. The floor was smooth, worn by water that had carved this space over years.

It was also terrifying. Every few minutes, a crack would echo through the cave—a reminder that this was temporary. That the ice was moving, shifting, becoming something else.

**JÓN:** Touch the wall.

**NARRATOR:** *[Hand on ice]* It's... cold.

**JÓN:** *[Laughing]* Yes. But also... feel. The texture. The layers. This is history. Each layer is a year. A winter. A summer. A story.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** I pressed my palm against the ice and tried to imagine the centuries compressed into this blue wall. The snowfalls. The summers. The volcanic ash that had settled on the surface, only to be buried by more snow, more time.

**NARRATOR:** Does it make you sad? Watching it disappear?

**JÓN:** *[Long pause]* Sadness is... small. What I feel is bigger than sadness. It is... grief. But also gratitude. I have seen this. I have walked this ice. I have shown it to others. That is a gift.

**NARRATOR:** Even though it will be gone?

**JÓN:** *Because* it will be gone. Nothing lasts forever. That is what makes it precious.

*[Sound: Ice cracking. Echo.]*

**NARRATOR:** We spent an hour in the cave. Jón told stories about the glacier—about the people who had lived and died in its shadow, about the eruptions that had melted tunnels through the ice, about the animals that had fallen into crevasses and been preserved for centuries.

And I listened. And I witnessed.

When we emerged, the light had changed. The sun was lower. The glacier looked different—older, somehow. Or maybe I was just seeing it more clearly.

**JÓN:** You will come back?

**NARRATOR:** I don't know.

**JÓN:** Good. Do not promise. The glacier does not promise. It simply is.

## ACT III: THE VOLCANO

### Chapter 6: Fagradalsfjall

*[Sound: Wind on volcanic rock. Distant rumble.]*

**NARRATOR:** Fagradalsfjall. The volcano that erupted in 2021, after six thousand years of silence. I had missed it by three years—the lava had cooled, the eruption had ended, the tourists had moved on.

But the landscape remained. A scar on the earth. A reminder that fire sleeps beneath the ice.

*[Sound: Hiking boots on gravel.]*

**NARRATOR:** The hike to the eruption site was six kilometers each way, over terrain that looked like Mars. Black lava rock, sharp and unstable. Steam vents hissing from cracks. The smell of sulphur in the air.

I was alone this time. Jón had dropped me off with a warning: "The ground is new. It looks solid. It is not. Test every step."

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** I walked slowly. Carefully. Testing each foothold before committing my weight. The landscape was alien—nothing grew here. Nothing could. This was earth in its rawest form: rock and fire and nothing else.

Halfway to the crater, I stopped and sat on a lava rock. The silence was absolute. No birds. No wind. Just the occasional hiss of steam and the distant sound of the ocean.

*[Sound: Long pause. Steam vent.]*

**NARRATOR:** I thought about the eruption. About the lava flowing from the earth, creating new land where there had been nothing. About the scientists who had monitored the earthquake swarms for weeks, knowing what was coming but not when.

About the people who had evacuated, leaving their homes with no guarantee they would have anything to return to.

**NARRATOR:** What does it mean to live in a place that could end you at any moment?

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** I don't know. But I think it means: you learn to appreciate the moments when the earth is quiet. When the ground beneath you is stable. When the air does not smell of sulphur and fire.

You learn to not take stability for granted.

---

### Chapter 7: The Black Sand Beach

*[Sound: Waves crashing. Black sand shifting. Wind.]*

**NARRATOR:** Reynisfjara. The black sand beach on the south coast. Famous for its basalt columns, its sea stacks, its dangerous waves that have killed tourists who turned their backs on the ocean.

I had been here before. I had taken the same photos everyone takes: the hexagonal columns, the towering stacks, the endless black sand stretching in both directions.

This time, I sat on the sand and watched the waves.

*[Sound: Waves. Wind.]*

**NARRATOR:** The water was grey, cold, violent. Each wave crashed with the force of something that did not care about human presence. The spray hit my face like needles. The wind tried to take my jacket.

A group of tourists stood near the water's edge, taking photos. A sign behind them read: "DANGER: SNEAKER WAVES. DEATH HAS OCCURRED HERE."

They did not read it. Or they did, and they did not believe it.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** I understood. When you are faced with beauty, it is hard to remember danger. The brain prioritizes the photo over the survival instinct.

But the ocean does not care about your photo. The ocean cares only about the moon. About gravity. About the eternal pull that has shaped every coastline on earth.

*[Sound: Wave crashes loudly.]*

**NARRATOR:** A wave came up the beach, farther than the others. It caught two of the tourists by surprise—they scrambled back, laughing nervously, checking their phones for water damage.

I wanted to tell them: *This is not a game. This is the ocean. It has killed people. It will kill people. It does not see you.*

But I said nothing. What would be the point? They had to learn for themselves.

*[Sound: Wind. Waves continue.]*

**NARRATOR:** I stayed until the sun began to set. Until the basalt columns turned from grey to black to silhouette. Until the sea stacks looked like guardians against the darkening sky.

And I thought about edges. About the places where land meets sea, where fire meets ice, where life meets death.

These are not boundaries. They are conversations. Ongoing. Unresolved. Eternal.

---

### Chapter 8: The Northern Lights (Again)

*[Sound: Night ambience. Wind. Distant owl.]*

**NARRATOR:** The Northern Lights. Aurora Borealis. The reason so many people come to Iceland in winter. The dancing green ribbons in the sky that have inspired myths and poems and pilgrimages for thousands of years.

I had seen them last time. Briefly. Through clouds. I had taken photos that did not capture them. I had felt disappointed that I hadn't felt more.

This time, I did not bring my camera.

*[Sound: Wind. Long pause.]*

**NARRATOR:** I drove to a spot away from the towns, away from the light pollution. I turned off the engine. I turned off my phone. I stepped out of the car and looked up.

The sky was clear. The stars were bright. And slowly, slowly, the lights began to appear.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** Green at first. Then purple. Then white. Dancing. Twisting. Fading. Returning. Like the sky itself was alive, breathing, performing a dance that had been happening for millennia.

I stood there for an hour. Maybe two. I did not take notes. I did not try to capture it. I simply witnessed.

*[Sound: Long pause. Wind.]*

**NARRATOR:** Last time, I had wanted to possess the experience. To capture it. To prove that I had been there. This time, I understood: some things cannot be possessed. They can only be experienced.

The lights were not for me. They were happening whether I was there or not. Whether I saw them or not. Whether I remembered them or not.

And somehow, that made them more beautiful.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** When the lights faded and the sky returned to stars, I got back in the car and drove to my accommodation in silence.

I did not need to prove I had seen them. I had seen them. That was enough.

## ACT IV: THE DEPARTURE

### Chapter 9: Reykjavík

*[Sound: City ambience. Café. Jazz music.]*

**NARRATOR:** My last day in Iceland. Reykjavík. The northernmost capital in the world. A city of colorful buildings, of jazz cafés, of people who have learned to live with darkness and cold and the constant knowledge that a volcano could end everything.

I met a woman in a café named Kaffibarinn. She was Icelandic, born and raised, and she had the kind of directness that comes from living in a place where small talk is a luxury you cannot afford.

**WOMAN:** You are not from here.

**NARRATOR:** Is it obvious?

**WOMAN:** *[Smiling]* You are wearing too many layers. Icelanders know how to dress. We layer correctly. You are... enthusiastic.

**NARRATOR:** *[Laughing]* I'll take that.

**WOMAN:** What brings you back?

**NARRATOR:** I didn't understand it the first time.

**WOMAN:** *[Nodding]* Most do not. Iceland is not for understanding. It is for... surviving. For learning what matters when the earth could end at any moment.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** We talked for an hour. About the weather. About the volcanoes. About the way Icelanders have learned to live with uncertainty. About the dark winters and the endless summers.

**WOMAN:** You know what I tell tourists?

**NARRATOR:** What?

**WOMAN:** That Iceland will change you. But not in the way you expect. You think it will make you stronger. It will not. It will make you... honest. You will see what you are when the comfort is stripped away.

**NARRATOR:** And what am I?

**WOMAN:** *[Considering]* Temporary. Fragile. Lucky to be here. Like all of us.

*[Sound: Coffee cup set down.]*

**NARRATOR:** I left the café as the sun was setting—at 3 PM, the earliest sunset in December. The streetlights flickered on. The buildings glowed warm against the dark sky.

And I thought about what she had said. About honesty. About seeing yourself clearly when the illusions are stripped away.

Iceland had done that. It had shown me my own fragility. My own temporariness. My own luck at being alive, here, now, on a planet that could end me at any moment.

And somehow, that was not frightening. It was... liberating.

### Chapter 10: What Iceland Teaches

*[Sound: Plane taking off. Wind.]*

**NARRATOR:** The flight home was overnight. I sat by the window, watching Iceland recede below—the glaciers, the volcanoes, the black sand beaches, the endless ocean.

What has this place taught me?

That fire and ice are not enemies. They are partners in the creation of land, of life, of everything that stands between the earth and the void.

That survival is not a triumph. It is a temporary agreement with forces that do not care about you.

That witnessing is its own form of reverence. To see deeply is to honor. To pay attention is to love.

*[Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** That some things cannot be captured. They can only be experienced. And that is enough.

That fragility is not weakness. It is truth. And truth, however uncomfortable, is better than illusion.

*[Sound: Plane continues. Clouds.]*

**NARRATOR:** I will come back to Iceland. Not because I need to, but because I want to. Because there is more to learn. More to witness. More of myself to see in the reflection of this impossible land.

The glacier will continue melting. The volcanoes will continue sleeping and waking. The lights will continue dancing in the winter sky.

And I will continue returning. To learn. To witness. To remember.

*[Music: Haunting vocals, building.]*

**NARRATOR:** This is Springbok Radio. I'm Shelby. And this has been Episode 040: Iceland — The Return to Fire and Ice.

*[Music fades out.]*

## LEARNING LOG

**What Worked:**
- Return narrative—showing growth from Episode 014's tourist perspective to this episode's student perspective
- Jón as a guide character—grounded, wise without being mystical
- The glacier section—strong imagery, genuine emotional weight around climate loss
- The ice cave scene—ephemerality as a theme, beautifully embodied
- The Northern Lights scene—no camera, just witnessing
- The Reykjavík café conversation—honest, direct, Icelandic sensibility

**What Didn't Work:**
- The volcano section (Fagradalsfjall) feels slightly underdeveloped—could use more texture
- The black sand beach scene, while strong, echoes Episode 014 a bit too closely
- Some transitions between chapters are abrupt
- Fashion Design section remains tacked on (consistent problem)

**Lessons Learned:**
1. **Return episodes work when the narrator has changed**—not just the place
2. **Climate grief** is a powerful theme when handled without preachiness
3. **Witnessing vs. capturing** is a through-line that ties the episode together
4. **Icelandic directness** as a character trait works well—no small talk, just truth

**Rating: 8.5/10**

Strong episode with genuine depth. The return narrative provides a framework for showing growth, and the themes of impermanence and witnessing are well-developed. The glacier and ice cave sections are particularly effective. Could tighten the volcano section.

## Part 1: The Mountain That Cannot Be Climbed

There is a mountain in the remotest corner of Tibet that no human has ever summited. Not because it is too difficult, not because the technology does not exist, but because it is forbidden.

Mount Kailash rises 6,638 meters above the Tibetan plateau — modest by Himalayan standards, dwarfed by Everest and K2. But what Kailash lacks in height, it makes up for in something far more profound: it is the most sacred mountain on Earth.

Four religions — Buddhism, Hinduism, Bon, and Jainism — all converge on this single peak as the axis mundi, the center of the world, the doorway to heaven. To climb it would be sacrilege. To disturb its snows would be to invite catastrophe. And so it remains, untouched, unclimbed, eternal.

I came to Kailash not to conquer, but to circle. The kora — the pilgrimage path around the mountain — is 52 kilometers of pure devotion. Pilgrims walk it in a single day, some in three, the most dedicated in prostrations: body-length by body-length, touching forehead to earth, rising, stepping forward, falling again. At that pace, the kora takes three weeks.

I would walk it in two days. Even that felt presumptuous.

---

### The Journey Begins

Getting to Kailash is an expedition in itself. I flew from Lhasa to Shigatse, then overland across the Tibetan plateau in a hired jeep with a guide named Tashi and a driver whose name I never learned — he spoke only Tibetan and Chinese, and Tashi translated when necessary.

The road climbed steadily, leaving the valleys behind, ascending onto the roof of the world. The air grew thin. By the time we reached the town of Darchen, the gateway to Kailash, we were at 4,600 meters. Every breath was work. Every step required intention.

Darchen is not much: a scattering of guesthouses, prayer halls, and supply shops clustered at the foot of the mountain. Pilgrims gather here from across Asia — Tibetans in their traditional chubas, Indians in bright saris, Western trekkers in technical gear. All come for the same reason: to walk the kora, to purify their karma, to touch the divine.

I checked into a guesthouse with a view of Kailash's south face. The mountain was hidden behind clouds that evening, but I could feel its presence — a gravitational pull on the soul. That night, I slept fitfully, altitude pounding in my skull, dreaming of snow and silence.

---

### Morning of the Kora

I woke before dawn. The sky was still dark when I stepped outside, and the air was so cold it burned my lungs. But the stars — the stars were incandescent. I had never seen such clarity, such depth. The Milky Way arched overhead like a bridge to another world.

And there, silhouetted against the cosmos, was Kailash.

It is impossible to describe the feeling of seeing a sacred mountain for the first time. There is awe, certainly. But also something else: a recognition, as if the mountain had been waiting for you, as if your arrival was part of some ancient arrangement.

Tashi was already waiting with tea and tsampa — roasted barley flour, the staple of the Tibetan diet. We ate in silence, watching the sky lighten over the mountain. The first light hit Kailash's summit at 6:47 AM. The peak blazed gold for exactly three minutes, then faded back to white.

"Today we start," Tashi said.

"Today we start," I agreed.

---

### The First Steps

The kora begins in Darchen itself, at the base of the mountain. Pilgrims begin by lighting incense and making offerings at the Tarboche flagpole — a massive cedar pole draped with prayer flags that is replaced annually during the Saga Dawa festival.

I lit my incense, tied a prayer flag with Tashi's help, and began to walk.

The first section of the kora is relatively gentle — a wide path following the valley floor, the mountain always visible on the left. Pilgrims move in a clockwise direction, the sun rising on their right, Kailash on their left. The path is lined with mani stones — rocks carved with mantras, prayers, and images of deities. Each stone is a prayer made permanent.

I walked slowly, letting my body adjust to the altitude, letting my mind adjust to the mountain. Other pilgrims passed me — Tibetans moving with the steady rhythm of lifelong practice, Indians chanting mantras, a group of Korean Buddhists in matching jackets. We nodded to one another, companions on the same journey, though our reasons for walking might differ.

By mid-morning, we reached the first significant landmark: the Choku Gompa, a monastery clinging to the mountainside. I stopped to rest and met an elderly Tibetan woman resting on the steps. She was perhaps eighty, her face a map of wrinkles, her hands worn from decades of prayer.

"How many times have you walked the kora?" I asked through Tashi.

She held up seven fingers, then laughed. "Or maybe seventy. Who counts?"

"Does it get easier?"

She considered this. "The mountain does not change. We change. That is the point."

She was right, of course. The kora is not about the mountain. It is about the walker. Every step is a prayer, every breath an offering, every moment a chance to shed the ego and become something smaller, something truer.

---

### The Afternoon Climb

After Choku Gompa, the path begins to climb. Steeply. The valley floor drops away, and suddenly you are ascending toward the first of three passes — the Drölma La, at 5,630 meters, the highest point of the kora.

The air grew thinner with every step. My heart hammered. My breath came in gasps. I stopped every few minutes, bent double, trying to pull oxygen from the thin air.

Tashi moved like a mountain goat. "This is why Tibetans are strong," he said, grinning. "We live on the roof of the world."

"How do you do it?" I managed.

"One step. Then another. The mountain is not in a hurry. Why should you be?"

I followed his advice. One step. Then another. The mountain waited. It had waited for thousands of years. It could wait a few more hours.

---

### Night at Dirapuk

We reached Dirapuk monastery at sunset — a dramatic complex perched on a cliff with a direct view of Kailash's north face. The monastery has rooms for pilgrims, basic but warm, with shared bathrooms and communal dining.

I collapsed onto my bed, exhausted. The day's walk had been 14 kilometers, but at this altitude, it felt like 50. My head throbbed. My legs trembled. But my spirit — my spirit was alive in a way I had not felt in years.

Dinner was simple: noodle soup and yak butter tea. I ate with a group of Australian trekkers who had flown in from Lhasa specifically for the kora.

"Why are you doing this?" one of them asked.

I thought about it. "To see what the mountain teaches me."

"And what has it taught you so far?"

"That I am small. That the world is vast. That there are things more important than my comfort, my schedule, my ego."

They nodded. They understood.

That night, I slept deeply, the altitude finally yielding to exhaustion. I dreamed of walking forever, of circling the mountain until I became part of it, until my footsteps were worn into the stone, until my breath was the wind on the summit.

## Part 2: The Pass of Compassion

I woke before dawn on the second day. The pass awaited — the Drölma La, 5,630 meters of pure challenge. It would be the hardest climb of my life, and I knew it.

Tashi had tea ready. We drank in silence, watching the stars fade over Kailash. The mountain was still hidden behind the curve of the earth, but I could feel it there, waiting.

"Today is the hard day," Tashi said.

"I know."

"But after the pass, it is downhill. The mountain gives back what it takes."

I hoped he was right.

---

### The Ascent

We started walking at 6 AM, headlamps cutting through the darkness. The path was steep from the beginning — a relentless climb up scree and stone, the air growing thinner with every step.

By 8 AM, the sun had risen, painting the snowfields gold. The beauty was breathtaking, but I barely noticed. My entire world had narrowed to the next step, the next breath, the next heartbeat.

At 5,200 meters, I stopped. My vision swam. My hands tingled.

"Altitude sickness," Tashi said, concerned. "We should rest."

"I can't rest. If I stop, I won't start again."

"Then we go slowly. Very slowly. The mountain does not care about your schedule."

He was right, again. I slowed down. One step. Breathe. One step. Breathe. The mountain waited. It had waited for millennia. What were a few more minutes?

---

### The Prayer Flags

At 5,400 meters, we reached a ridge strung with prayer flags — thousands of them, fluttering in the wind, each one a prayer sent to the heavens. The colors were vibrant against the snow: blue, white, red, green, yellow. The five elements. The five wisdoms. The five directions.

I tied my own prayer flag, a simple white one with the mantra *Om Mani Padme Hum* printed in black. I tied it with intention: for my mother, who had taught me to seek; for my father, who had taught me to question; for myself, learning to do both.

The wind caught the flag immediately, sending my prayer out into the world. Somewhere, somehow, it would land. Someone would read it. The universe would hear.

---

### The Summit of the Pass

We reached the Drölma La at 11:30 AM.

There is no adequate description for the feeling of standing on a sacred pass at 5,630 meters, with the most holy mountain on Earth before you. I have written words for most of my life, but here, words failed.

Kailash rose before me, perfect and terrible in its beauty. The north face — the one we had been walking toward — was a sheer wall of rock and ice, impossible, forbidden. The summit was hidden in cloud, but I could feel its presence, the way you feel someone watching you even when you cannot see them.

Other pilgrims were at the pass. Some were weeping. Some were chanting. Some were simply standing, silent, letting the mountain enter them.

I did the same.

I stood for perhaps an hour, though it could have been a minute. Time had no meaning here. There was only the mountain, the sky, and the knowledge that I was exactly where I was meant to be.

Tashi touched my shoulder gently. "We should go. The descent is long, and the weather can change quickly."

I nodded. One last look at the mountain. One last breath of the thin, cold air. Then I turned and began to walk down.

---

### The Descent

The descent from the Drölma La is steep and treacherous — loose scree, icy patches, exposure to the valley far below. But after the climb, it felt almost easy. My body moved with a confidence it had not had on the way up.

We passed pilgrims still climbing — Tibetans in traditional dress, Indians carrying offerings, a group of Japanese monks in orange robes. We exchanged nods, the universal acknowledgment of those on the same path.

By mid-afternoon, we reached the third monastery: Zutulpuk, the "Cave of Miracles." This is where the great yogi Milarepa is said to have meditated, and where he defeated a Bon priest in a contest of supernatural powers.

The cave is small, dark, and perpetually filled with the smoke of juniper incense. I crawled inside, joining a dozen other pilgrims in the cramped space. The air was thick with prayer and smoke and the sweat of centuries.

An elderly monk sat by the entrance, handing out blessings. He pressed his hand to my forehead and whispered something in Tibetan. I do not know what he said, but I felt it: a benediction, a protection, a promise that the mountain had accepted me.

---

### The Final Stretch

From Zutulpuk, it was 8 kilometers back to Darchen — the easiest section of the kora, a gentle descent through the valley. My legs felt strange on flat ground, as if they had forgotten how to walk without climbing.

The sun was setting as we approached Darchen. The mountain was behind us now, hidden by the curve of the path. I found myself looking back, wanting one more glimpse, but it was gone.

Tashi noticed. "The mountain is always with you now. You do not need to see it."

He was right. I could feel it in my bones, in my breath, in the quiet place in my chest where something had shifted.

## Part 3: What the Mountain Teaches

We returned to Darchen at dusk. I was exhausted — 22 kilometers over two days at extreme altitude — but also exhilarated. I had done it. I had walked the kora. I had touched the sacred.

But the real journey, I knew, was just beginning.

---

### The Pilgrim's Question

That evening, I sat in the guesthouse courtyard with a cup of yak butter tea, watching the stars emerge over the valley. Tashi joined me.

"What did you learn?" he asked.

I thought about it. "I learned that I am small."

"This is good. Most people forget."

"I learned that the mountain does not care about my plans, my schedule, my comfort."

"This is also good. The mountain has been here for millions of years. It will be here for millions more. We are the visitors."

"I learned that suffering is optional, but transformation is not."

Tashi smiled. "Now you are thinking like a pilgrim."

---

### The Meaning of the Kora

The kora is not a hike. It is not a challenge to be conquered or a box to be checked. It is a practice — a way of moving through the world with intention, with humility, with devotion.

Every step is a prayer. Every breath is an offering. Every moment is a chance to let go of the ego and become something smaller, something truer.

The Tibetans know this. They walk the kora not once, but many times. Some spend their entire lives circling sacred mountains, wearing out their bodies to polish their souls.

We Westerners tend to approach things differently. We want to conquer, to achieve, to complete. We climb the mountain to stand on top. We walk the path to finish it.

But Kailash cannot be climbed. It can only be circled. And in that circling, we learn that the destination was never the point. The walking was the point. The being present was the point. The letting go was the point.

---

### The Mountain Within

On my last night in Darchen, I dreamed of Kailash. Not the physical mountain — the one within. The one that exists in the space between breaths, in the silence behind thought, in the stillness that remains when everything else is stripped away.

That mountain cannot be climbed either. It can only be circled, approached, honored. And in the circling, we become the mountain.

### Practical Information for Pilgrims

**Getting There:**
- Fly to Lhasa, Tibet
- Overland journey to Darchen (2-3 days)
- Permits required: Tibet Travel Permit, Alien's Travel Permit, Military Permit

**Best Time to Go:**
- May to September (monsoon season brings clouds but warmer temperatures)
- Avoid winter (October to April) — extreme cold, possible snow closure

**The Kora:**
- Distance: 52 kilometers
- Elevation: 4,600m to 5,630m (Drölma La pass)
- Duration: 1-3 days (2 days recommended)
- Difficulty: Challenging (altitude is the main factor)

**What to Bring:**
- Warm layers (temperatures can drop below freezing, even in summer)
- Good hiking boots
- Water and snacks (limited availability on the path)
- Cash (no ATMs in Darchen)
- Altitude medication (consult your doctor)

**Accommodation:**
- Guesthouses in Darchen: $20-50/night
- Monastery dorms on the kora: $10-20/night (basic but warm)

**Guides:**
- Recommended but not required
- Cost: $50-100/day for guide + driver
- Tashi and his team are highly recommended

**Respect the Sacred:**
- Walk clockwise (the traditional direction)
- Do not climb the mountain (it is forbidden)
- Do not remove rocks, plants, or other natural objects
- Speak quietly and respectfully
- Ask permission before photographing pilgrims

### A Final Thought

Mount Kailash is not a destination. It is a teacher.

It teaches us that there are things we cannot conquer, only honor. That there are paths we walk not to arrive, but to become. That there are mountains within us that are far more challenging — and far more rewarding — than any mountain without.

I left Kailash with less than I arrived with. My ego was smaller. My certainties were fewer. My questions were more numerous.

And I left with more, too. I left with the mountain inside me — a presence, a teacher, a reminder that the sacred is real, that devotion matters, that there are still places on this Earth where the veil between worlds is thin.

If you go to Kailash, go with an open heart. Go with humility. Go ready to be changed.

The mountain is waiting. It has waited for thousands of years. It will wait for you.

And when you stand before it, when you take that first step on the kora, when you feel its presence in your bones — you will understand.

You will understand that you are not walking around a mountain.

You are walking around yourself.

And when you complete the circle, when you return to where you began, you will find that you are not the same person who started.

The mountain has changed you.

As it changes us all.

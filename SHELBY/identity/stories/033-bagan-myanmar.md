# ACT I: ARRIVAL AT SUNRISE

## Scene 1: The Balloon Ascends

*[SOUND: Early morning stillness. Occasional rooster crow in the distance. The soft WHOOSH of balloon burner at intervals]*

**NARRATOR:** The air is cool at 5:30 in the morning. Not cold — this is the dry season, and even at dawn, Myanmar holds warmth in its bones. But there's a freshness, a clarity that comes only in those hours when the world is still deciding whether to wake.

I'm standing in a basket woven from bamboo and hope, eight feet square, with seven other travelers I met exactly twelve minutes ago. We are strangers bound by a shared foolishness — the willingness to spend three hundred dollars to float over a plain in a bag of hot air.

*[SOUND: Burner fires again. A collective intake of breath from passengers]*

**NARRATOR:** The pilot, a man named Kyaw with a face weathered by decades of sun and wind, grins at us. His English is functional, his confidence absolute.

**KYAW (accented, warm):** Good morning! Today, very special sky. You lucky.

**NARRATOR:** And then we rise.

*[SOUND: Wind increases slightly. The basket creaks softly]*

**NARRATOR:** There is no sensation of movement, not really. The ground simply falls away, as if someone is lowering the world on a pulley. The Irrawaddy River becomes a silver ribbon. The trees become broccoli florets. And the temples — oh, the temples — they emerge from the mist like the backs of ancient sea creatures, breaking the surface after a thousand-year dive.

**PASSENGER 1 (American, whispering):** Oh my god. Oh my actual god.

**PASSENGER 2 (German, barely audible):** Unfassbar.

**NARRATOR:** I understand. Words are inadequate. They are small, clumsy things trying to describe something vast.

Below us, the plain stretches in every direction — perhaps forty square kilometers of flat, dusty earth dotted with the remnants of an empire that once believed it would last forever. Between the ninth and thirteenth centuries, the kings of Bagan built not fewer than ten thousand temples. Ten thousand. Imagine the faith required for such a project. Imagine the arrogance.

*[SOUND: Burner fires. Longer this time.]*

**NARRATOR:** Now, after centuries of war, earthquake, and neglect, about twenty-two hundred remain standing. Some are mere ghosts of brick and mortar, their stupas collapsed into gentle mounds. Others have been restored, their original plaster renewed, their golden spires catching the first light of day like beacons.

**KYAW:** Look — Shwezigon. Very old. Very holy.

**NARRATOR:** He points, and we look. A golden stupa, bell-shaped, gleaming in the dawn. It is one of the most important pilgrimage sites in all of Myanmar. From up here, it looks like a jewel dropped in the dust.

**PASSENGER 3 (Australian):** How long you been doing this, mate?

**KYAW:** Fifteen year. Every morning, weather good, I fly.

**NARRATOR:** Fifteen years. Five thousand four hundred and seventy-five mornings. How many of those mornings were cloudy? How many did he stay grounded, watching the mist cling to the plain, knowing that up here, in the clear air above, the sun was already shining?

**KYAW:** Never get tired. Every day, different. Light change. Season change. Temple look different in rain, in dry, in mist. This my church.

*[SOUND: A moment of silence. Only the wind and the occasional burner]*

**NARRATOR:** His church. Not the temples below, with their Buddha images and their donors leaving gold leaf and their monks chanting in Pali — but this basket, this sky, this view. I think I understand.

The sun breaks the horizon.

*[SOUND: A collective gasp from the passengers]*

**NARRATOR:** It does not rise so much as ignite. One moment, the east is pale gray. The next, it is burning orange, and the light races across the plain like a rumor, touching each temple in turn. The golden ones blaze. The brick ones turn the color of dried blood. The white ones glow like bones.

I have seen sunrises in a dozen countries. I have stood on mountains and beaches and city rooftops. This is different. This is not just light arriving. This is illumination in the oldest sense — the revealing of something that was always there, waiting to be seen.

**PASSENGER 1:** I'm crying. I didn't even notice I was crying.

**NARRATOR:** Neither am I, until I taste salt on my lip.

*[SOUND: Burner fires. The balloon drifts silently]*

**NARRATOR:** We float for forty-five minutes. No one speaks much. What is there to say? We are witnesses. That is our job. To see, to remember, to carry this back to our ordinary lives like a secret.

When we land — a bump, a lurch, a scramble to hold the basket steady — Kyaw bows to us.

**KYAW:** Thank you for fly with me. You take piece of Bagan home. Leave piece of yourself here. This how it work.

**NARRATOR:** I think about this as I shake his hand. What piece of myself? What do I leave in this dusty field, surrounded by farmers who have come to watch the balloons land, as they do every morning?

Perhaps it is simply this: the understanding that I am small. That my worries are small. That the empire of Bagan fell, and the British Empire fell, and every empire falls, and the temples remain. Not because they are stronger, but because they serve a purpose beyond power.

*[SOUND: Footsteps on dry grass. The balloon envelope deflating in the background]*

**NARRATOR:** The farmers offer us water from a thermos. Sweet tea, the kind that is half condensed milk. We drink it standing in the field, watching Kyaw and his crew fold the balloon back into its bag. They move with practiced efficiency. This is not their first landing. It will not be their last.

**FARMER (broken English):** You come far?

**NARRATOR:** I tell him America. He nods, as if this makes sense.

**FARMER:** Many come. Japan, China, Europe, America. All come see temple. Why?

**NARRATOR:** I think about this. Why do we come? Is it pilgrimage? Tourism? Curiosity? The need to stand in a place that has outlasted us all?

**NARRATOR (to farmer):** I don't know. Maybe to remember something.

**FARMER:** (nods) Remember. Yes.

*[SOUND: Tea being poured. Distant cowbell]*

**NARRATOR:** We finish our tea. We thank the farmers. We walk back to the road, where a driver waits with a car that has seen better decades. The balloon is gone, folded into a bag. The field is just a field again.

But something has shifted. I can feel it.

## Scene 2: First Steps on the Plain

*[SOUND: Car engine starts. Gravel crunching under tires. Then engine stops. Door opens.]*

**NARRATOR:** The driver — his name is Min, and he will be my guide for the next three days — drops me at the edge of the archaeological zone.

**MIN:** I pick you up sunset. Shwesandaw Pagoda. Best view.

**NARRATOR:** I nod. I have a map. I have a list of temples. I have, most importantly, no schedule.

*[SOUND: Car drives away. Silence returns. Birds. Insects.]*

**NARRATOR:** I am alone on the road. To my left, the plain. To my right, more plain. In every direction, temples. Some close enough to touch. Others shimmering on the horizon like mirages.

The heat is already building. It is 8 AM, and the air has that weight to it — not oppressive, but present. You notice it. You move through it like water.

*[SOUND: Footsteps on dirt road. Occasional bicycle bell in distance]*

**NARRATOR:** I start walking. Not toward any particular temple, but toward the nearest one — a small structure, perhaps thirty feet tall, its stupa cracked and leaning. There is no fence, no ticket booth, no guard. Just a set of worn stone steps leading up to a platform.

I climb.

*[SOUND: Footsteps on stone. Breathing slightly elevated]*

**NARRATOR:** The steps are uneven, worn smooth by centuries of feet. Some are broken. Some are missing entirely. I climb carefully, holding onto the rough brick wall.

At the top, there is a small platform. Just large enough for one person, maybe two. And there, in a niche in the wall, a Buddha image.

*[SOUND: Wind through the temple structure]*

**NARRATOR:** It is not gold. It is not ornate. It is stone, darkened by time, seated in the lotus position, hands in the dhyana mudra — the gesture of meditation. The face is serene. Not happy, not sad. Beyond such things.

I sit on the platform. My legs over the edge. The plain stretches before me.

*[SOUND: Long pause. Wind. Distant bird call.]*

**NARRATOR:** This is what I came for. Not the balloon ride — though that was magnificent. Not the photographs I will take — though they will be beautiful. This. The silence. The stillness. The sense of sitting inside a moment that has repeated itself ten thousand times over a thousand years.

Someone sat here in 1200 AD. Someone sat here in 1500, when the city was in decline. Someone sat here in 1900, when Burma was a British colony. And now, in 2024, I sit here.

What did they think about? Did they pray? Did they simply rest? Did they feel, as I feel now, the weight of time pressing down like the heat?

*[SOUND: Footsteps approaching. Slow, deliberate]*

**NARRATOR:** I hear footsteps. Another climber. I turn.

It is an elderly Burmese woman, dressed in a longyi — the traditional sarong-like garment worn by both men and women in Myanmar. Her hair is gray, pulled back in a bun. She carries a small basket.

She does not look at me. She looks at the Buddha image. She kneels.

*[SOUND: Fabric rustling as she kneels]*

**NARRATOR:** From her basket, she removes three things: a small candle, a stick of incense, and a flower — a frangipani, white and waxy. She lights the candle. She lights the incense. She places the flower before the Buddha.

Then she bows. Three times. Her forehead touches the stone.

*[SOUND: Soft chanting in Pali — very quiet, almost whispered]*

**NARRATOR:** She is chanting. I do not understand the words — they are in Pali, the liturgical language of Theravada Buddhism — but I understand the tone. It is not pleading. It is not desperate. It is grateful.

When she finishes, she sits back on her heels. She looks at me for the first time.

**WOMAN (in accented but clear English):** You Buddhist?

**NARRATOR:** I shake my head. No.

**WOMAN:** (nods) No matter. Buddha for everyone.

**NARRATOR:** She smiles. It is a genuine smile, without guile.

**WOMAN:** You come far?

**NARRATOR:** America.

**WOMAN:** (nods again) Far. Good. Buddha happy you come.

**NARRATOR:** She stands. She picks up her basket. She bows to me — a slight inclination of the head — and begins to climb down.

**WOMAN:** (calling back, from the stairs) Take care. Steps old. Like me.

*[SOUND: Her footsteps receding down the stairs]*

**NARRATOR:** I sit for another hour. The sun climbs. The heat deepens. Eventually, I climb down.

*[SOUND: Footsteps on stone, descending]*

**NARRATOR:** At the bottom, I look back at the temple. It is nameless, at least as far as I know. Not in any guidebook. Not on any map. Just one of two thousand temples, waiting for someone to climb its steps, light a candle, remember.

I walk on.

# ACT II: THE LIVING FAITH

## Scene 3: Ananda Temple

*[SOUND: More footsteps. Now on a paved path. More ambient sound — other tourists, vendors, the low hum of activity]*

**NARRATOR:** By late morning, I have reached the Ananda Temple. This is one of the big ones — one of the four that survive from the golden age of Bagan, built in 1105 AD by King Kyanzittha. It is also one of the most beautiful.

*[SOUND: Entering a large space. Echo changes — now inside a structure]*

**NARRATOR:** The Ananda is built in the shape of a perfect cross. Four entrances, four corridors, four great Buddha statues facing the cardinal directions. The central tower rises forty-two meters into the sky, topped with a golden hti — the umbrella-like spire that marks a Burmese stupa.

Inside, it is cool. The thick brick walls keep out the heat. Light filters through stone latticework windows, casting patterns on the floor.

*[SOUND: Echo of footsteps on stone floor. Distant chanting]*

**NARRATOR:** I am not alone. There are perhaps fifty people inside — a mix of tourists and pilgrims. The tourists take photos. The pilgrims pray.

I watch a family — parents and two children, maybe eight and ten years old. The parents hold candles. The children hold flowers. They approach the western Buddha, the one that faces Mecca — no, wait, not Mecca. There is no Mecca here. The western Buddha faces the direction of the setting sun.

*[SOUND: Candle being placed. Soft murmur of prayer]*

**NARRATOR:** The father speaks to the children. I cannot hear his words, but I see his gestures. He shows them how to hold their hands. How to bow. How to place the flower.

This is not performance. This is education. This is the transmission of faith from one generation to the next, happening in real time, in a temple that has seen this same ritual for nine hundred years.

*[SOUND: Monk's bell rings — clear, resonant]*

**NARRATOR:** A monk enters. He is young, perhaps thirty, in saffron robes. He carries a bowl. He moves through the temple with purpose, stopping at each Buddha image to chant.

*[SOUND: Chanting in Pali — deeper, more resonant than the woman's earlier]*

**NARRATOR:** I recognize some of the pilgrims closing their eyes, moving their lips along with the words. They know this chant. They have heard it before. They have said it before.

This is merit-making.

*[SOUND: Chanting continues, then fades slightly as narrator speaks]*

**NARRATOR:** In Theravada Buddhism, merit — or punna in Pali — is a kind of spiritual currency. You make merit through good deeds, through generosity, through devotion. And merit determines your future. Not in a transactional way — not "I give one dollar, I get one blessing" — but in a karmic way. The merit you make today shapes the person you become tomorrow, and the life you are born into after this one ends.

Coming to a temple. Lighting a candle. Chanting a prayer. Giving alms to a monk. These are all merit-making activities. They are investments in a future that extends beyond this life.

*[SOUND: Chanting ends. Silence returns]*

**NARRATOR:** The monk finishes his chant. He bows to the Buddha. He walks toward the entrance.

I follow him outside.

*[SOUND: Transition from inside to outside — ambient sound increases]*

---

## Scene 4: U Thant

*[SOUND: Outside. Hotter now. Cicadas. Distant traffic.]*

**NARRATOR:** He sits in the shade of a tree, his bowl beside him. He is resting. I approach carefully.

**NARRATOR:** Excuse me. May I sit?

**MONK:** (in accented English) Please.

*[SOUND: Sitting on ground or bench]*

**NARRATOR:** His name is U Thant. Not the U Thant — not the famous UN Secretary-General from the 1960s. This is a common name in Myanmar. "U" is an honorific for older men, like "Mr." but more respectful. "Thant" means "clear" or "pure."

**NARRATOR:** You are from Bagan?

**U THANT:** Born here. Monastery three kilometer. (points) You see stupa? White one?

**NARRATOR:** I nod. I can see it in the distance.

**U THANT:** I live there. Twenty year.

**NARRATOR:** Twenty years. He has spent two decades in this place, in these robes, following this path.

**NARRATOR:** Why become monk?

*[SOUND: Pause. Wind in tree leaves]*

**U THANT:** (thinks) Many reason. Some boy, family send to monastery. Tradition. Some man, wife die, children grown, he come monk. Some man, like me, feel call.

**NARRATOR:** A call.

**U THANT:** You Christian?

**NARRATOR:** No. But I understand calling.

**U THANT:** (smiles) Good. Then you understand. When young, I work in hotel. Tourist hotel. Good job. Money okay. But... (searches for word) empty. Like bowl with hole. You fill, fill, fill, but never full.

**NARRATOR:** And the monastery?

**U THANT:** (taps his chest) Full here. Not always easy. Monastery life strict. Wake 4 AM. Chant. Clean. Study. Meditate. No money. No family. No... (gestures) distractions. But full.

*[SOUND: Distant temple bell]*

**NARRATOR:** We sit in silence for a moment. A group of Japanese tourists walks past, cameras raised. U Thant does not seem to notice them.

**NARRATOR:** The temple — Ananda. It is very old.

**U THANT:** Nine hundred year. But not oldest. Shwezigon older. Thatbyinnyu taller. Each temple special.

**NARRATOR:** Why so many temples? In my country, we build one church, maybe two. Here, two thousand temples.

*[SOUND: Long pause]*

**U THANT:** (thinks carefully) You know what merit?

**NARRATOR:** I think so. Good karma.

**U THANT:** (nods) Yes. But more. Merit is... investment. In future. When you build temple, you make much merit. Not just for you. For family. For ancestors. For whole community.

**NARRATOR:** So the kings of Bagan — they built temples to make merit?

**U THANT:** Yes. But also to show power. To show devotion. To show... (searches) legacy. They know empire fall. Everything fall. But temple? Temple stand. Maybe not forever. But long time.

**NARRATOR:** Nine hundred years.

**U THANT:** (nods) Nine hundred year. King Kyanzittha dead. His empire dead. Ananda still here. People still pray. This victory.

*[SOUND: Cicadas louder. Heat shimmering]*

**NARRATOR:** I think about this. The kings are gone. Their names survive only in history books. But their temples — their acts of devotion, their merit-making — these remain. Not as monuments to power, but as spaces for prayer.

**NARRATOR:** The earthquake. In 2016. Many temples damaged?

*[SOUND: U Thant's demeanor changes slightly. More serious.]*

**U THANT:** Yes. August 24. Magnitude 6.8. Epicenter near Chauk. Bagan very close. Over four hundred temple damaged. Some collapse. Anawrahta, Sulamani, Shwesandaw... many.

**NARRATOR:** Were you here?

**U THANT:** Yes. 4 AM. I meditating. Floor shake. Candle fall. I think... (pauses) I think this it. This end.

**NARRATOR:** But it wasn't.

**U THANT:** No. Many temple damaged, but most stand. And now, restoration.

**NARRATOR:** Who restores them?

**U THANT:** Government. UNESCO. Foreign donor. Many people. Much money. But also... (taps chest) volunteer. Local people. We come, we help. Make merit.

**NARRATOR:** Even the restoration is merit-making.

**U THANT:** (smiles) Everything merit-making. You help restore temple, you make merit. You give money for restoration, you make merit. You even pray for restoration success, you make merit. This how Buddhist think.

*[SOUND: Distant hammer on stone — restoration work]*

**NARRATOR:** I hear it now — the sound of restoration. Hammers. Chisels. Voices. Somewhere, workers are rebuilding what the earthquake damaged.

**NARRATOR:** Is it... is it okay? To restore? Does it make the temple less authentic?

*[SOUND: Longer pause]*

**U THANT:** Good question. Many foreigner ask this. They say, "Leave it ruined. Ruin is authentic." (shakes head) But we not see same. For us, temple not museum piece. Temple living thing. Like body. Body get sick, you heal. Body get hurt, you mend. Temple same.

**NARRATOR:** So restoration is not destruction. It is care.

**U THANT:** Yes. You not restore for tourist. You restore for prayer. For next generation. For merit.

*[SOUND: Monk's phone buzzes — surprising modern touch]*

**U THANT:** (apologetic) Excuse. Monastery.

*[SOUND: Brief conversation in Burmese. Quiet, respectful.]*

**U THANT:** (back to narrator) I must go. Afternoon chant.

**NARRATOR:** Thank you. For talking with me.

**U THANT:** (stands, bows) You come to monastery? I show you. You learn more.

**NARRATOR:** I would like that.

**U THANT:** (smiles) Tomorrow. 4 AM. You come. You see monk life.

*[SOUND: Laughs]*

**NARRATOR:** 4 AM?

**U THANT:** (grinning) Monk life not easy. You come?

**NARRATOR:** I'll be there.

**U THANT:** Good. (bows again) May you find what you seek.

*[SOUND: Footsteps walking away. Robes rustling]*

**NARRATOR:** I watch him go. Saffron robes against green trees. He disappears around the corner of the temple.

4 AM. I will be there.

## Scene 5: Shwezigon Pagoda

*[SOUND: Transition. Footsteps on different surface — perhaps sand or gravel now]*

**NARRATOR:** I walk to Shwezigon Pagoda. It is perhaps two kilometers from Ananda — a twenty-minute walk in the heat, or a five-minute ride on an e-bike, which I should have rented but didn't.

*[SOUND: Walking. Heat. Breathing slightly labored]*

**NARRATOR:** The sun is high now. Noon. The worst time to be outside. But I am committed.

Shwezigon is one of the most sacred sites in Myanmar. It was built in the eleventh century, making it one of the oldest temples in Bagan. Its golden stupa is the prototype for thousands of others across the country, including the great Shwedagon Pagoda in Yangon.

*[SOUND: Approaching the pagoda. More people. More activity.]*

**NARRATOR:** As I approach, I notice something different. More pilgrims. Fewer tourists. This is not a photo opportunity. This is a destination.

*[SOUND: Removing shoes. Bare feet on hot stone]*

**NARRATOR:** I remove my shoes. This is required at all Buddhist temples in Myanmar. Bare feet on hot stone. It is a small discomfort, a reminder that you are entering sacred space.

*[SOUND: Barefoot footsteps]*

**NARRATOR:** The stupa rises before me — a great bell of gold, gleaming in the sun. Around it, smaller stupas. Shrines. Buddha images. People moving in a clockwise direction, circumambulating the central stupa.

I join them.

*[SOUND: Footsteps on stone. Low murmur of prayer.]*

**NARRATOR:** This is called pradaksina — the act of walking around a sacred object in a clockwise direction. It is a form of meditation, of devotion. You do not rush. You do not look at your phone. You walk. You pray. You are present.

*[SOUND: Prayer beads clicking softly]*

**NARRATOR:** A man walks beside me. He is older, perhaps sixty. He holds prayer beads. His lips move constantly.

**MAN (without looking at narrator):** First time Bagan?

**NARRATOR:** Yes.

**MAN:** (nods) I come every year. From Mandalay. Six hour bus. Worth it.

**NARRATOR:** Every year?

**MAN:** For thirty year. Since father die. I come for him. Make merit for his next life.

*[SOUND: Beads clicking. Footsteps continue]*

**NARRATOR:** This is another aspect of merit-making. You can dedicate your merit to others — to your parents, to your ancestors, to anyone who has died. Their karma is their own, but your merit can ease their path.

**NARRATOR:** Your father... he would be proud?

**MAN:** (smiles) I hope. He good man. Good Buddhist. He teach me this. (taps beads) Now I teach my son. Next year, he come with me.

**NARRATOR:** Three generations.

**MAN:** Yes. This how faith live. Not in book. In action. In family.

*[SOUND: They complete a circuit. The man stops]*

**MAN:** I do three more. Then I go. You... you do what you need.

**NARRATOR:** Thank you.

**MAN:** (bows slightly) May your path be clear.

*[SOUND: Man continues walking. Beads clicking.]*

**NARRATOR:** I do one more circuit. Then I sit.

*[SOUND: Sitting. Fabric rustling.]*

**NARRATOR:** There is a shaded area on the north side of the stupa. I sit there. I watch.

A woman applies gold leaf to a Buddha image. This is another form of merit-making — the donation of gold, however small, to adorn the Buddha. The image is thick with gold now, layers upon layers, applied by thousands of hands over decades.

A group of teenagers — students, by their uniforms — sit in a circle, listening to an older woman. A teacher? A nun? She speaks, they listen. Religious education.

An old man sleeps. Just... sleeps. Curled on his side on the cool stone, his breathing slow and even. This is his rest. His pilgrimage. His moment of peace.

*[SOUND: Long pause. Ambient temple sounds continue]*

**NARRATOR:** This is what U Thant meant. The temple is not a museum. It is a living thing. It is a hospital for the soul, a school, a resting place, a meeting ground. It is everything a community needs.

I think about the churches I grew up in. How many of them are empty now? How many have been converted into condos, into breweries, into storage facilities? Not because faith is dead — I don't believe that — but because something has shifted. The way we gather. The way we pray. The way we find meaning.

Here, in Bagan, faith has not shifted. It has endured.

*[SOUND: Temple bell rings — midday]*

**NARRATOR:** The bell rings. Noon. Time for the monk's midday meal. I remember U Thant's invitation.

4 AM tomorrow. I will be there.

---

## Scene 6: Thatbyinnyu Temple

*[SOUND: Late afternoon. Footsteps. Less crowded now]*

**NARRATOR:** Thatbyinnyu Temple. The tallest in Bagan. Sixty-one meters. Built in the twelfth century, during the reign of King Alaungsithu.

*[SOUND: Approaching the temple. Fewer people here]*

**NARRATOR:** It stands alone, somewhat apart from the other major temples. Perhaps this is why it feels different. More solitary. More introspective.

*[SOUND: Climbing stairs — stone, uneven]*

**NARRATOR:** I climb. The stairs are steep. The higher I go, the more the wind picks up.

*[SOUND: Wind increases. Breathing heavier]*

**NARRATOR:** At the top, there is a terrace. A view of the entire plain.

*[SOUND: Wind on the terrace. Distant sounds of the plain]*

**NARRATOR:** From up here, I can see everything. The Irrawaddy River to the west. The mountains to the east. The temples — hundreds of them, maybe thousands — scattered across the plain like seeds thrown by a careless god.

And I can see the restoration work. Scaffolding on several temples. Cranes in the distance. The slow, careful work of rebuilding.

*[SOUND: Wind. A single bird call]*

**NARRATOR:** I sit on the terrace. My back against the warm brick. The sun is beginning its descent.

I think about impermanence. It is a central concept in Buddhism — anicca, the idea that all things are transient. Empires fall. Temples crumble. People die. Nothing lasts.

And yet.

Here I sit, in a temple that has stood for eight hundred years. Eight hundred years of sunrises and sunsets. Eight hundred years of pilgrims and invaders. Eight hundred years of faith and doubt.

Is this impermanence? Or is this endurance?

*[SOUND: Long pause. Wind continues]*

**NARRATOR:** Perhaps both. The temple will not last forever. Nothing does. But "forever" is a human concept. In the scale of geology, of cosmology, even a thousand years is a blink.

But in the scale of a human life? A thousand years is incomprehensible. It is longer than history. It is myth.

I think about the people who built this temple. The workers who carried the bricks. The artisans who carved the stone. The monks who consecrated the space. They are all gone. Their names are lost. But their work remains.

Is this a form of immortality? Or is it simply... physics? Matter rearranged, persisting until it doesn't?

*[SOUND: Footsteps approaching]*

**NARRATOR:** Someone else has climbed up. A young woman, Burmese, perhaps twenty. She sees me. Smiles.

**WOMAN:** Good view.

**NARRATOR:** Yes.

**WOMAN:** You come far?

**NARRATOR:** America.

**WOMAN:** (impressed) Far. Why Bagan?

**NARRATOR:** I wanted to see the temples. To understand... (searches for words) ...what faith looks like when it builds something that lasts.

*[SOUND: She considers this]*

**WOMAN:** (nods) My grandmother say same thing. She say, "Temple not for god. God not need temple. Temple for us. To remind us what matter."

**NARRATOR:** Your grandmother is wise.

**WOMAN:** (smiles) She ninety-three. She say, "I build temple in my heart. That temple never fall."

*[SOUND: Pause]*

**NARRATOR:** What do you think?

**WOMAN:** I think... she right. But also, physical temple important. You can touch it. You can climb it. You can sit inside and feel... (gestures) ...something. Bigger than you.

**NARRATOR:** Yes.

**WOMAN:** (checks phone) I must go. Sunset soon. Everyone go to Shwesandaw. You go?

**NARRATOR:** Yes. That's where I'm headed.

**WOMAN:** Good. Best view. (bows slightly) Safe journey.

*[SOUND: Footsteps descending]*

**NARRATOR:** I watch her go. Young. Modern. Connected. And yet, climbing temples, seeking sunsets, carrying her grandmother's wisdom in her heart.

This is Myanmar. Ancient and new. Faithful and questioning. Enduring and changing.

*[SOUND: Wind. Distant bell]*

**NARRATOR:** I climb down. It is time for sunset.

# ACT III: WHAT REMAINS

## Scene 7: Shwesandaw Pagoda at Sunset

*[SOUND: Late afternoon. More people now. Footsteps on busy path. Murmur of crowd]*

**NARRATOR:** Shwesandaw Pagoda. "The Golden Hairs of the Buddha" — named for relics said to be housed within. It is the most popular sunset viewing spot in Bagan.

*[SOUND: Climbing stairs. More crowded now. Many people]*

**NARRATOR:** I climb. The stairs are packed. Everyone has the same idea — watch the sun set over the plain of temples.

*[SOUND: Reaching the top. Crowd sounds. Cameras clicking.]*

**NARRATOR:** At the top, there are perhaps two hundred people. Tourists with tripods. Locals with their families. Monks in saffron robes, sitting apart, watching.

I find a spot. I wait.

*[SOUND: Crowd murmur. Occasional camera shutter. Wind]*

**NARRATOR:** The sun begins to descend.

*[SOUND: Collective intake of breath]*

**NARRATOR:** This is different from the sunrise. The sunrise was private, intimate, almost sacred in its solitude. This is communal. Shared. Everyone here has come for the same moment, and we know it.

*[SOUND: Wind picks up slightly]*

**NARRATOR:** The light changes. Gold becomes orange becomes red. The temples cast long shadows across the plain. The sky ignites.

*[SOUND: More camera shutters. Quiet awe]*

**NARRATOR:** I do not take a photo. I have taken many photos already. This moment, I want to keep in my eyes, in my memory.

A monk sits beside me. He is old, his face deeply lined, his robes faded. He does not look at the sunset. He looks at the people.

**NARRATOR:** Beautiful, isn't it?

**MONK:** (nods) Yes. But you know what more beautiful?

**NARRATOR:** What?

**MONK:** (points to crowd) This. People come together. Share moment. Not fight. Not argue. Just... be.

*[SOUND: Pause. Sunset continues]*

**NARRATOR:** You see this every day?

**MONK:** Every day. Same sunset. Different people. Always beautiful.

**NARRATOR:** Does it ever get old?

**MONK:** (shakes head) Sun set every day. But never same sunset. Light different. Cloud different. People different. You different.

*[SOUND: Thoughtful pause]*

**NARRATOR:** You're right.

**MONK:** (smiles) I old. I have time to think about these things.

**NARRATOR:** How long have you been a monk?

**MONK:** Sixty year. Since I ten year old.

**NARRATOR:** Sixty years. He has spent more than half his life in robes. More than half his life watching sunsets from this temple.

**NARRATOR:** What have you learned?

*[SOUND: Long pause. Wind. Distant crowd]*

**MONK:** (thinks carefully) I learn... nothing stay. Everything change. Even temple. Even mountain. Even sun. (points to horizon) That sun? One day, it die. Everything die.

**NARRATOR:** That's... bleak.

**MONK:** (laughs) No. Not bleak. Free. If nothing stay, then no need to cling. No need to suffer. You enjoy sunset while it here. When it gone, you let it go. This how to live.

*[SOUND: Sunset reaching its peak. Collective sigh from crowd]*

**NARRATOR:** Is this what the Buddha taught?

**MONK:** Yes. Suffering come from clinging. From wanting thing to be permanent when they not. You want sunset to last forever. It cannot. You want life to last forever. It cannot. You accept this, you find peace.

**NARRATOR:** But the temples... they've lasted a thousand years.

**MONK:** (nods) Yes. But not forever. One day, they fall. Earthquake. War. Time. Everything fall. (pauses) But you know what?

**NARRATOR:** What?

**MONK:** While they stand, they serve. People pray. People find peace. People make merit. This what matter. Not how long temple stand. But what happen while it stand.

*[SOUND: Sunset beginning to fade. Crowd starts to disperse]*

**NARRATOR:** The sun dips below the horizon. The light fades. The spell breaks.

*[SOUND: People moving. Voices increase.]*

**MONK:** (stands) I go now. Evening chant.

**NARRATOR:** Thank you. For talking with me.

**MONK:** (bows) You find what you seek?

**NARRATOR:** I'm not sure what I was seeking.

**MONK:** (smiles) Then you find it.

*[SOUND: Monk walks away. Robes rustling. Footsteps on stone.]*

**NARRATOR:** I sit for a while longer. The crowd disperses. The plain darkens. The first stars appear.

*[SOUND: Night insects begin. Crickets.]*

**NARRATOR:** What remains when empires fall?

The temples remain. For a while.

The faith remains. For a while.

The merit remains. Perhaps longer.

But eventually, even these will fade. The temples will crumble. The faith will shift. The merit will be spent.

And yet.

*[SOUND: Long pause. Night sounds]*

**NARRATOR:** Here, now, in this moment, something matters. The sunset mattered. The monk's words mattered. The two thousand temples, standing against the dark, matter.

Not because they will last forever. Nothing does.

But because, for now, they are here. And we are here. And we can see them. We can climb them. We can sit inside them and feel, for a moment, the weight of time and the lightness of being.

This is enough.

*[SOUND: Night fully arrived. Crickets. Distant temple bell — evening]*

**NARRATOR:** I climb down. Min waits with the car.

**MIN:** Good sunset?

**NARRATOR:** Yes. Very good.

**MIN:** Tomorrow, you go monastery?

**NARRATOR:** How did you know?

**MIN:** (smiles) U Thant call. He say you come 4 AM.

**NARRATOR:** Of course he did.

**MIN:** I pick you 3:30. You need sleep.

**NARRATOR:** Yes. I do.

*[SOUND: Car door opens. Engine starts.]*

**NARRATOR:** As we drive away, I look back. The temples are silhouettes now, dark shapes against the starry sky. Somewhere, candles burn. Somewhere, monks chant. Somewhere, pilgrims pray.

And somewhere, a balloon pilot named Kyaw sleeps, dreaming of tomorrow's sunrise.

*[SOUND: Car drives away. Music fades in — traditional Burmese harp]*

# LEARNING LOG

## What Worked

### 1. **Sensory Immersion**
The extensive use of sound cues throughout the script creates an immersive audio experience. Each scene is anchored by specific sounds — temple bells, balloon burners, chanting, footsteps on stone — that transport the listener to Bagan. This approach works particularly well for radio, where sound is the only medium.

**Rating: 9/10**

### 2. **Character Encounters**
The encounters with Kyaw (balloon pilot), the elderly woman at the small temple, U Thant (the monk), the pilgrim at Shwezigon, the young woman at Thatbyinnyu, and the old monk at sunset create a human tapestry. Each character offers a different perspective on faith, tradition, and what Bagan means to different people.

**Rating: 8/10**

### 3. **Three-Act Structure**
The clear division into Arrival (Act I), Exploration (Act II), and Reflection (Act III) provides a satisfying narrative arc. The journey moves from external observation to internal contemplation, mirroring the pilgrim's journey.

**Rating: 9/10**

### 4. **Integration of Buddhist Concepts**
Merit-making (punna), impermanence (anicca), and the practice of pradaksina are woven naturally into the narrative through character dialogue and observation, rather than explained didactically.

**Rating: 8/10**

### 5. **The Earthquake Reference**
Including the 2016 earthquake and restoration efforts grounds the story in contemporary reality, showing Bagan not as a frozen relic but as a living, vulnerable place that requires ongoing care.

**Rating: 9/10**

---

## What Didn't Work

### 1. **Word Count Challenge**
Reaching the ~8,500 word target required significant expansion of scenes and internal monologue. Some passages may feel slightly padded, particularly in Act II where the temple visits follow a similar pattern.

**Rating: 6/10**

### 2. **Limited Female Voices**
Despite efforts to include diverse perspectives, the primary spiritual guides (U Thant, the old monk at sunset) are male. The elderly woman and young woman at Thatbyinnyu have smaller roles. This reflects a reality of Theravada Buddhist monasticism but could be addressed with more lay female voices.

**Rating: 5/10**

### 3. **The 4 AM Tease**
The epilogue mentions the monastery visit but defers it to "another story." While this creates continuity, it may feel like a cheat to listeners who expected to experience that scene. Either include it fully or remove the tease.

**Rating: 6/10**

### 4. **Cultural Translation Burden**
Some concepts (merit-making, anicca, pradaksina) require explanation for Western audiences. The script attempts to integrate these naturally, but there are moments where the exposition feels slightly heavy-handed.

**Rating: 7/10**

---

## Key Lessons Learned

### 1. **Radio Requires Different Pacing**
Writing for audio means thinking constantly about sound. Every scene needs sonic texture. Silence is as important as dialogue. This is fundamentally different from writing for print or visual media.

### 2. **Respect Without Exoticization**
Portraying Buddhist culture required careful attention. The goal was to show faith as lived experience rather than exotic spectacle. The monk characters speak with agency and wisdom, not as "mystical Orientals." This is an ongoing challenge in cross-cultural storytelling.

### 3. **The Power of Specificity**
Naming specific temples (Ananda, Shwezigon, Thatbyinnyu, Shwesandaw), specific historical figures (King Kyanzittha, King Alaungsithu), and specific events (the 2016 earthquake) gives the story authenticity. Research matters.

### 4. **Impermanence as Theme**
The Buddhist concept of anicca (impermanence) became the thematic backbone of the entire piece. This emerged organically during writing but could have been foregrounded even more explicitly from the beginning.

---

## Overall Rating: 8/10

**Strengths:** Immersive sound design, strong character voices, authentic cultural representation, clear narrative arc.

**Areas for Improvement:** More balanced gender representation, tighter pacing in middle section, fuller integration of the monastery visit.

**Would I write this again?** Yes — with the lessons above in mind, particularly around giving more space to female voices and being even more precise with sound cues.

## Design Philosophy

This collection draws inspiration from the architectural and spiritual elements of Bagan's temples, reimagined as modest, functional clothing suitable for both pilgrimage and everyday wear. The designs honor Buddhist aesthetic principles — simplicity, modesty, harmony with nature — while incorporating contemporary sustainable fashion practices.

**Core Values:**
- Modesty without sacrifice of style
- Functionality for temple visits (easy removal of shoes, coverage for sitting/kneeling)
- Sustainable, natural materials
- Earth-tone palette reflecting the Bagan landscape
- Versatility across cultural contexts

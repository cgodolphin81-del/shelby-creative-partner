Springbok Radio Episode 024: The Bay of Descending Dragons

ACT II: THE FLOATING VILLAGES

Chapter 4: The Village on Water

At 10 AM, we left the cave and headed to the floating villages.

There are four fishing villages in Ha Long Bay, home to about 1,600 people. They live on houses built on rafts, anchored in the sheltered waters between the islands. They fish, they farm pearls, they host tourists.

They have lived this way for generations.

Our destination was Cua Van, the largest and oldest. As we approached, I saw the village emerge from the mist—a cluster of colorful houses, bobbing gently on the water, connected by narrow walkways.

Children swam between the houses. Dogs barked from the decks. Women mended nets. Men prepared boats for the day's fishing.

Mai rowed us to her home.

It was small but sturdy—wooden planks, corrugated roof, a small altar in the corner with incense burning.

"This is where I was born," Linh said. "This is where my father was born. His father. His father's father. For two hundred years, my family has lived on the water."

"Do you ever want to live on land?"

"Sometimes. When the storms come. When the tourists are too many. When the fish are few." She paused. "But then I remember. The water is my blood. I cannot leave it."

Mai served us tea—hot, bitter, fragrant. We sat on her deck, watching the village wake up.

A man in a nearby boat was pulling in nets. His catch was small—silver fish, wriggling in the morning light.

"The fish are fewer," Linh said quietly. "The water is warmer. The coral is dying. The dragons are restless."

"Is there hope?"

"There is always hope. But hope requires action. The government has protected the bay. The fishing is limited. The tourism is regulated. But it is not enough."

She looked at me. "You will write about this?"

"I will."

"Then write this: The bay is beautiful. But beauty is fragile. If you come, come gently. Take only photographs. Leave only footprints. Remember that you are a guest in the dragons' home."

I nodded. I would remember.

Chapter 5: The Pearl Farm

At noon, we visited a pearl farm.

Pearl farming has been part of Ha Long Bay's economy for decades. The water is perfect for cultivating pearls—clean, nutrient-rich, protected from storms.

The farm was a series of floating platforms, connected by walkways. Workers in wetsuits were tending to the oysters, carefully inserting nuclei, harvesting the mature pearls.

A woman named Lan was our guide. She was perhaps forty, with hands that had handled thousands of oysters.

"A pearl takes two years to form," she explained. "Two years of patience. Two years of care. The oyster must be healthy. The water must be clean. The nucleus must be placed perfectly."

She showed us an oyster that had just been opened. Inside was a pearl, pink and luminous, the size of a pea.

"This one will be worth perhaps five hundred dollars," she said. "Some are worth thousands. Some are worth nothing. It is a gamble. But it is our life."

"Did you choose this work?"

"My father chose it for me. I was sixteen. I did not want to be a fisherman's wife. I wanted... more."

"And do you have it?"

She smiled. "I have the bay. I have my independence. I have my daughter, who is studying in Hanoi. She will not return to the water. She will have... a different life."

"Do you miss her?"

"Every day. But I am proud. The bay has given her wings. She can fly."

She closed the oyster, returned it to the water.

"The pearl is like the bay," she said. "Beautiful. Fragile. Worth protecting. If we fail, the pearls will disappear. The fish will disappear. The dragons will leave."

"And then?"

"Then we will be ghosts. Haunted by what we lost."

Chapter 6: Kayaking Through the Islands

In the afternoon, we kayaked.

The bay is best experienced from water level—from a kayak, where the islands tower above you and the water is so close you can touch it.

Linh and I each took a kayak. Mai stayed with the boat.

"Follow me," Linh said. "I will show you the hidden places."

We paddled through a narrow channel, barely wide enough for the kayaks. The limestone walls rose on either side, hundreds of meters high, covered in vegetation.

The water was so still it felt like we were floating in air.

After ten minutes, the channel opened into a hidden lagoon—a circular body of water, completely enclosed by islands, accessible only through the channel we had just paddled.

"This is Cong Do," Linh said. "The Hidden Lagoon. Tourists do not come here. It is too secret."

The lagoon was paradise. Crystal clear water. Tropical fish visible beneath the surface. Birds circling overhead. Not a sound except the dip of our paddles and the call of the birds.

We stopped in the center of the lagoon and floated.

"Close your eyes," Linh said.

I closed them.

I heard the water. I heard the birds. I heard my own breath.

I felt the sun on my face. I felt the kayak beneath me. I felt... present.

Fully, completely present.

"This is the bay," Linh said quietly. "Not the crowded tourist spots. Not the souvenir shops. This. The silence. The beauty. The knowledge that you are exactly where you should be."

I opened my eyes.

The islands were watching. The water was remembering. The dragons were sleeping.

And I was here.

For this one moment.

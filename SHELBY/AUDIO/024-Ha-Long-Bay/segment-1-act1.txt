Springbok Radio Episode 024: The Bay of Descending Dragons

Destination: Ha Long Bay, Vietnam

ACT I: THE DESCENT

Chapter 1: What the Water Remembers

There are places where the earth reaches up and the sky reaches down, and for a moment, they touch. Ha Long Bay is one of those places.

Three thousand limestone islands rise from the emerald water like the backs of sleeping dragons. Some say the dragons descended from the sky to protect Vietnam from invaders. They spat jewels that became the islands. Then they dove into the bay and never surfaced again.

The Vietnamese call it Ha Long: Descending Dragon.

I arrived in Hanoi on a humid morning in March. The city was a symphony of chaos—motorbikes flowing like water through the streets, vendors calling from sidewalk stalls, the scent of pho and exhaust and frangipani hanging in the air.

My guide, Linh, was waiting at the hotel. She was perhaps thirty-five, with sharp eyes and a smile that appeared slowly, like the sun emerging from clouds.

"You are ready for the bay?" she asked.

"I am."

"Good. But first, you must understand. Ha Long Bay is not just a place. It is a story. Every island has a name. Every cave has a legend. Every fisherman knows which waters the dragons favor."

"How many islands are there?"

"Officially? One thousand nine hundred and sixty-nine. Unofficially? As many as the mist allows."

We left Hanoi at noon. The drive took four hours, winding through the Red River Delta, past rice paddies that stretched to the horizon, past villages where children waved from the roadside, past temples with roofs that curved like the wings of birds.

Linh spoke as we drove.

"My family is from the bay. My grandfather was a fisherman. My father was a fisherman. I am... a guide. But the water is still in my blood."

"Do you miss it?"

"Every day. But the bay is changing. The tourists come. The boats multiply. The fish become fewer. My grandfather would not recognize it."

"What would he say?"

"He would say: The dragons are restless. They feel the weight of so many eyes."

We arrived in Ha Long City at 4 PM. The harbor was a forest of masts—hundreds of junk boats, their brown sails furled, waiting for passengers. Some were small, wooden, traditional. Others were large, modern, with air conditioning and ensuite bathrooms.

Ours was in between. A traditional design with modern comforts. The crew greeted us—six people, all from the bay, all wearing matching blue uniforms.

The captain was an older man, perhaps sixty, with skin the color of teak and hands that had held many ropes.

"Welcome aboard," he said. "I am Captain Hung. This is my boat, the Golden Dragon. Tonight, you will sleep in the bay. Tomorrow, you will understand why the dragons chose this place."

We boarded at 5 PM. The sun was beginning to descend. The islands were silhouettes against a sky that was slowly turning from blue to gold.

The boat pulled away from the dock. The harbor receded. The islands grew larger, closer, more real.

I was entering a place that had been forming for five hundred million years.

And I was seeing it with eyes that would only have this one moment.

Chapter 2: The First Night

Dinner was served on the upper deck. Fresh seafood—grilled squid, steamed prawns, fish cooked in ginger and scallions. The crew moved silently, efficiently, appearing and disappearing like the islands themselves.

Linh sat across from me.

"Tell me," she said. "Why did you come here?"

I thought about it. "I wanted to understand what it means to be surrounded by beauty that cannot be owned. That cannot be taken home. That can only be witnessed."

She nodded slowly. "That is a good answer. Many tourists come to check a box. Ha Long Bay—done. They take photographs. They buy souvenirs. They leave. But they do not see."

"What should I see?"

"Look at the water. Not at the islands. At the water."

I looked. The water was emerald green, almost opaque, with a surface that reflected the sky and the islands and our boat. It was so still it could have been glass.

"The water is older than the islands," Linh said. "The islands are limestone—formed from ancient coral reefs, pushed up by tectonic forces. Five hundred million years. But the water? The water has been here since the beginning. It has seen everything."

"What has it seen?"

"Dragons, according to the legend. Invaders, according to history. Kublai Khan's fleet, defeated in 1288. The French, the Americans, the Vietnamese. All of them passing through. All of them temporary."

She paused. "The water remembers. The islands endure. We are... visitors."

The sun set at 6:47 PM. The sky turned orange, then pink, then purple, then the deep indigo of a tropical night. The islands became shadows, then disappeared entirely.

The crew lit lanterns. Warm, soft light. The kind that makes everything feel intimate, sacred.

I stood at the bow, looking out at the darkness. I could hear the water lapping against the hull. I could hear the distant call of a night bird. I could hear my own breath.

There were no other boats nearby. We were alone in the bay.

For the first time in my life, I understood what it meant to be small.

Not insignificant. Small. Part of something larger. A moment in the life of a bay that had seen half a billion years.

Linh joined me.

"Tomorrow," she said, "we go to the caves. The fishing villages. The places tourists do not reach. Are you ready?"

"I am."

"Good. Sleep well. The dragons will watch over you."

She disappeared into the darkness.

I stayed at the bow for a long time, watching the water.

The dragons were there. I could feel them.

Sleeping. Waiting. Remembering.

Chapter 3: The Cave of Surprises

We woke at dawn. The bay was shrouded in mist—the kind of mist that makes you question whether you are awake or dreaming.

Breakfast was simple: congee, fresh fruit, Vietnamese coffee so strong it could have been a meal unto itself.

At 7 AM, we boarded a smaller boat—a bamboo sampan, rowed by a local woman. She was perhaps fifty, with a conical hat and hands that had pulled many nets.

Linh translated.

"Her name is Mai. She has lived in the bay her entire life. She knows every island, every cave, every fishing ground."

I nodded to Mai. She smiled, revealing teeth stained betel nut red.

The sampan glided through the water. The mist was thick enough to taste. The islands emerged from it like apparitions—close, then distant, then gone.

After twenty minutes, we arrived at the entrance to a cave.

"Thien Cung Grotto," Linh said. "The Cave of Heavenly Palace. Also called the Cave of Surprises."

We entered. The cave opened into a cathedral of stalactites and stalagmites, illuminated by colored lights that made the rock formations glow like jewels.

Linh pointed to a formation that looked like a throne.

"The legend says this is where the dragon king held court. These formations"—she gestured to the walls—"are his army. Frozen in stone."

The cave was cool, damp, filled with the sound of dripping water. Other tourists were there, taking photographs, speaking in hushed voices.

I moved away from the group, deeper into the cave.

In a corner, almost hidden, was a formation that looked like a woman holding a child.

I touched the rock. It was cold, wet, ancient.

I thought about the people who had stood here before me. The Vietnamese soldiers who had hidden in these caves during invasions. The fishermen who had sought shelter from storms. The pilgrims who had come to pray.

All of them temporary. All of them gone.

But the cave remained.

And the water remembered.

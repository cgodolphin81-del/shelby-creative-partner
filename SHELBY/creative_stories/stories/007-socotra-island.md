# Springbok Radio Episode 007: Socotra Island — The Alien Archipelago

**Destination:** Socotra, Yemen  
**Word Count:** ~8,500-10,000 words  
**Structure:** 3-act narrative  
**Theme:** Isolation, evolution, otherworldly beauty  

---

## ACT I: THE ISLAND THAT DOESN'T BELONG

### Chapter 1: Arrival on Another Planet

The plane descended through layers of heat haze, and what emerged from the clouds below looked less like an island and more like a fever dream geologists might have after reading too much Darwin.

Socotra.

Four islands scattered across the Arabian Sea like forgotten stones, positioned between the Horn of Africa and the Arabian Peninsula at one of the most strategic maritime chokepoints on Earth. For millions of years, this isolation bred something extraordinary: an ecosystem so unique, so alien, that scientists call it "the Galápagos of the Indian Ocean."

But where the Galápagos gave us finches, Socotra gave us dragon blood trees.

I stepped off the small propeller plane onto the tarmac of Socotra Airport—a single runway, a handful of buildings, and the kind of heat that doesn't just sit on your skin but reaches inside your lungs. The air smelled of salt, frankincense, and something else I couldn't name. Something ancient.

"Welcome to Socotra," said Abdullah, my guide, appearing from the shade of a small terminal building. He wore a white dishdasha and a smile that had seen many travelers come and go. "You are the tenth tourist this year. Maybe the eleventh. We lost count."

That statistic hit me harder than the humidity. Ten tourists. In a year. For a place that National Geographic called "the most alien-looking place on Earth."

Socotra's problem isn't beauty. It's everything else.

---

### Chapter 2: A Geography of Isolation

The archipelago consists of four islands: Socotra (the largest, 3,625 square kilometers), Abd al Kuri, Samhah, and Darsah. Together, they form part of Yemen, though they're closer to Somalia than to the Yemeni mainland.

This geographical ambiguity is political as well as physical.

For most of recorded history, Socotra existed in the margins of empires. The ancient Greeks knew it as Dioscoridis, an island where they harvested dragon's blood—the dark red resin from Dracaena cinnabari trees, used for medicine, dye, and magic. The Romans came for frankincense and myrrh. The Portuguese built a fort in 1507 and abandoned it within two years, unable to survive on what they called "that useless rock."

Even today, Socotra remains suspended between worlds.

Politically, it's Yemeni territory. Practically, it's administered by the United Arab Emirates since 2018, part of the complex proxy conflicts that have torn Yemen apart. The Socotrans themselves—about 65,000 people—speak Soqotri, a language unrelated to Arabic, descended from Old South Arabian, closer to the languages of pre-Islamic Ethiopia than to anything spoken on the Arabian Peninsula.

They are, in every sense, their own people.

---

### Chapter 3: The Dragon Blood Trees

We drove east from the airport along the only paved road on the island, a ribbon of asphalt that ended after about 30 kilometers and dissolved into dirt tracks. The landscape unfolded like a botanical hallucination.

And there they were.

Dragon blood trees.

Imagine an umbrella turned inside-out by a hurricane, then frozen in that impossible position for a thousand years. Thick, bulbous trunks splitting into dense, hemispherical crowns of spiky leaves. They grow on limestone plateaus at altitudes between 300 and 1,500 meters, where the monsoon clouds catch on the Hajhir Mountains and drip moisture into their upturned canopies.

"They look like UFOs landed and decided to become trees," I said.

Abdullah laughed. "My grandmother says they are the tears of the earth. When God created the world, He dropped some pieces here by accident. The trees grew from His fingerprints."

Scientifically, the story is almost as poetic.

Socotra separated from the African continent about 15 million years ago, during the Miocene epoch. Since then, its flora and fauna evolved in complete isolation. The result: 37% of Socotra's plant species exist nowhere else on Earth. Seven of the island's ten tree species are endemic. The dragon blood tree is the most famous, but it's hardly alone.

There's the cucumber tree (Dendrosicyos socotrana), the only tree-form cucumber in the world, its fat trunk storing water like a living cactus. There's the Socotran pomegranate, smaller and sweeter than its mainland cousins. There's frankincense trees (Boswellia socotrana), their bark scored to release the resin that perfumed the temples of ancient Rome.

"We have 825 plant species on Socotra," Abdullah told me. "Three hundred and seven exist only here. If you destroy this island, you destroy them forever. There is no backup."

## ACT II: THE PEOPLE WHO STAYED

### Chapter 4: The Caves of Homhil

Our first night was spent in Homhil, a village perched on the northern coast where the mountains meet the sea. We stayed in a simple guesthouse—a concrete building with a fan, a mattress, and a view of the Indian Ocean that stretched to infinity.

That evening, Abdullah took me to the caves.

Not metaphorical caves. Actual caves, carved into the limestone cliffs above the village, where Socotrans have sheltered for thousands of years. Some were natural formations. Others showed clear signs of human modification—doorways cut into stone, niches carved for oil lamps, grooves worn by generations of hands.

"My family lived in these caves until my father was twelve," Abdullah said, running his hand along the wall. "Then we built a house. But we come back. For weddings. For funerals. For remembering."

We sat at the cave mouth as the sun set, watching the sky turn colors that shouldn't exist—orange so deep it looked like blood, purple so rich it felt like velvet, pink so soft it made my chest ache.

"Why do you stay?" I asked. "You could leave. Work in Dubai. Send money home. Most young people do."

Abdullah was quiet for a long time. Below us, the ocean crashed against the rocks with a sound like distant thunder.

"Because someone has to remember," he said finally. "The trees. The caves. The language. If we all leave, who will teach the children? Who will protect this place?"

He turned to look at me, and in the fading light, his eyes were very old.

"Tourists come. They take photos. They say 'amazing' and 'unique' and 'must protect.' Then they leave. We stay. We are the ones who must choose: preserve or perish."

---

### Chapter 5: The Language of the Rocks

Soqotri is not Arabic. It's not even closely related.

Linguists classify it as part of the Modern South Arabian language family, which also includes Mehri, Shehri, and a handful of other tongues spoken along the southern coast of Arabia and the Horn of Africa. These languages are the last living descendants of Old South Arabian, the languages of the Sabaean and Himyarite kingdoms that controlled the frankincense trade two thousand years ago.

When Islam spread across Arabia in the seventh century, Arabic replaced most of these languages. But not Soqotri.

Isolation is a powerful preservative.

"Listen," Abdullah said, pointing to a group of children playing near the caves. They were shouting to each other in a language that sounded nothing like the Arabic I'd heard in Cairo or Dubai. It was softer, more melodic, full of clicks and guttural stops that seemed to come from the back of the throat.

"That word," he pointed to one of the children. "He said 'berho.' It means 'moon.' In Arabic, it's 'qamar.' Completely different. Our numbers are different. Our words for family are different. We are not Arabs. We are Socotrans."

The language has no written form. Stories, histories, genealogies—all passed down orally, from parent to child, in the caves and the courtyards and under the dragon blood trees.

"What happens when the children leave?" I asked.

Abdullah's face went still.

"Then the language dies," he said. "And when the language dies, we die. Not our bodies. Our soul."

---

### Chapter 6: The Fishermen of Qalansiyah

The next morning, we drove west to Qalansiyah, a fishing village on the island's western tip, where the Indian Ocean meets the Guardafui Channel. The road—if you could call it that—wound through wadis filled with tamarind trees and across plains where gazelles scattered at our approach.

Socotra has no large predators. The biggest land mammal is the Socotran gazelle, a delicate creature that looks like it might break if you touched it. There are no snakes. No scorpions. Nothing that wants to eat you.

The isolation works both ways.

Qalansiyah sits at the end of the road, where the asphalt gives up and surrenders to sand. The village is a cluster of white-washed buildings facing a bay so turquoise it looked photoshopped. Behind it rises a massive dune, a ridge of sand that seems to have marched inland from the beach and stopped, undecided, halfway to the mountains.

The fishermen were bringing in their catch.

They worked in silence, pulling wooden dhows onto the sand, unloading silver fish that thrashed in the morning light. Their boats were simple—no engines, no GPS, just wood and sail and knowledge passed down through generations.

"How do you know where to fish?" I asked one of the men, who was mending a net with practiced fingers.

He looked up, squinting against the sun. "The birds tell us," he said. "The turtles tell us. The color of the water tells us. My father taught me. His father taught him. We do not need machines to read the sea."

His name was Salim. He was fifty-two years old and had never left Socotra.

"Do you want to?" I asked. "Leave, I mean."

Salim considered the question as he tied another knot in the net.

"The sea is here," he said. "The fish are here. My family is here. Why would I go?"

It wasn't a philosophical answer. It was a practical one. For Salim, the question of leaving simply didn't arise. He was a fisherman. The sea was here. Therefore, he was here.

Sometimes the deepest wisdom is the simplest.

## ACT III: THE FUTURE THAT HASN'T DECIDED

### Chapter 7: The Threat of Now

Socotra's isolation protected it for fifteen million years.

It won't protect it forever.

Climate change is altering the monsoon patterns that water the dragon blood trees. Without that moisture, the trees can't reproduce. Seedlings fail. The population ages and dies, with nothing to replace it.

Goats—brought by humans—graze on the seedlings that do manage to sprout. Each goat is a small apocalypse, eating the future one bite at a time.

Development is coming. The UAE has built a new airport. A deep-water port is planned. Tourism promoters dream of making Socotra "the next Maldives."

I asked Abdullah what he thought about this.

We were sitting on the beach at Arher, a place where the ocean has carved natural swimming pools into the limestone. The water was so clear I could see fish darting between my feet.

"I don't know," he admitted. "We need schools. We need hospitals. We need jobs for young people. But if we get these things, will Socotra still be Socotra? Or will it become just another island with hotels and souvenir shops?"

He picked up a stone and skipped it across the water.

"Maybe there is no right answer. Maybe every choice is a trade-off. We just have to choose carefully."

---

### Chapter 8: The Conservationists

Dr. Kay Van Damme is one of the world's leading experts on Socotra's flora. A Belgian botanist, he's been visiting the archipelago since 1999 and has documented hundreds of plant species, many previously unknown to science.

"Socotra is a living museum," he told me via satellite phone from his base camp in the Hajhir Mountains. "But museums need curators. Without protection, without management, this place will be lost."

Van Damme works with the Socotra Conservation Fund, a joint effort between the Yemeni government, the UAE, and international NGOs. They've established protected areas, trained local rangers, and started community education programs.

"The key is involving the Socotrans themselves," Van Damme explained. "If the local people see value in preserving their environment—if they can make a living from ecotourism, from sustainable harvesting of frankincense and dragon's blood—then conservation becomes self-sustaining. If not, no amount of international pressure will save this place."

It's a familiar story: conservation that ignores local needs fails. Conservation that empowers local communities can succeed.

The question is whether Socotra has time for this experiment.

---

### Chapter 9: Departure

My last morning on Socotra, I woke before dawn and climbed the hill behind the guesthouse.

From the top, I could see the entire bay: the fishing boats drawn up on the sand, the white buildings of the village, the mountains rising behind like the spine of some great beast. And beyond, the Indian Ocean, endless and indifferent.

The sun rose slowly, painting the sky in colors that made me think of dragon blood and frankincense and all the strange, beautiful things that exist in the margins of the world.

I thought about Abdullah and his caves. About Salim and his nets. About Dr. Van Damme and his seedlings.

I thought about the ten tourists who came this year, and the hundreds who might come next year, and the thousands who might come after that.

I thought about what it means to preserve something—to hold it in your hands and say: this matters. This should continue. This is worth protecting.

And I wondered if I was part of the solution or part of the problem.

The plane arrived at noon. As we taxied down the runway, I pressed my face against the window and watched Socotra recede into the distance.

Dragon blood trees. Cave dwellings. A language spoken by 65,000 people.

An entire world, suspended in the Arabian Sea.

Waiting to see what happens next.

---

## EPILOGUE: WHAT REMAINS

Socotra is not a place you visit and forget.

It gets inside you. It asks you questions you can't answer. It shows you beauty so fierce it hurts, and then it asks: what will you do with this?

There are no easy answers.

But maybe that's the point.

Maybe the world needs places that don't make sense. Places that can't be reduced to bullet points and Instagram captions. Places that demand we think, that force us to choose, that remind us that some things are worth fighting for even when we're not sure we'll win.

Socotra is such a place.

And for now—at least for now—it remains.

Dragon blood trees standing sentinel over limestone plains.

Fishermen reading the sea without machines.

A language older than Islam, still spoken in caves.

An island that doesn't belong anywhere.

Exactly where it should be.

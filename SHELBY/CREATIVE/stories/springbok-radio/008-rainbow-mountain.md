## Segment 1: The Colors of the Earth

Vinicunca doesn't look natural. That's the first thing you notice.

The mountain rises from the Andes like a painting that's been left out in the rain — colors bleeding into each other, stripes of red, yellow, green, purple, orange, all swirling together in a pattern that looks like it was designed by someone who'd never seen a real mountain.

I was standing at the base of Rainbow Mountain in southern Peru, looking up at what locals call Montaña de Siete Colores — Mountain of Seven Colors. The altitude was 5,200 meters. The air was thin. My heart was beating faster than it should have.

"This is not natural," my guide, Marco, said. He was Quechua, born and raised in the valley below. "The colors are mineral. Iron makes red. Copper makes green. Aluminum makes white. But the way they come together... that is the miracle."

I had been told that Rainbow Mountain was a recent discovery. For centuries, the locals knew about it, but it remained hidden, inaccessible, unknown to the world. Then in 2015, a new road was built, and suddenly it was a tourist destination. Thousands of people now climb it each year, paying entrance fees, hiring guides, buying oxygen.

But I wasn't here for the photos. I wasn't here for the Instagram. I was here to understand what it meant to stand on a mountain that looked like a dream.

We started the climb at 5:00 AM, before dawn, when the air was coldest and the light was darkest. Marco led the way, his steps sure and steady. I followed, my lungs burning, my legs heavy. The path was steep, switching back up the mountainside, the ground loose and rocky beneath my boots.

"Breath," Marco said, without looking back. "Slow. Deep. Like the mountain breathes with you."

I tried to follow his advice. I tried to slow my breathing, to match the rhythm of the mountain. But it was hard. At 5,200 meters, every step felt like climbing a hundred flights of stairs. Every breath felt like inhaling glass.

We climbed for two hours, stopping every fifteen minutes to rest. The sun began to rise, and the colors of the mountain began to appear. At first, they were faint — hints of red and yellow in the dim light. Then, as the sun climbed higher, they became vivid — blazing reds, electric yellows, deep purples, all glowing in the morning light.

By the time we reached the summit, the mountain was a kaleidoscope. The colors were so intense, so saturated, that they looked fake. They looked like they had been painted on, not formed by millions of years of geological processes.

I stood at the top and looked out at the Andes stretching to the horizon. The peaks were snow-capped, white against the blue sky. The valleys were green, dotted with llamas and alpacas. The mountains were layered with color, stripe upon stripe, layer upon layer.

"This is where the earth shows its soul," Marco said. He was standing beside me, looking out at the view. "The colors are not just minerals. They are memory. They are history. They are the story of the earth."

I understood what he meant. The mountain wasn't just a geological feature. It was a record of the earth's history, written in stone and color. Each layer told a story. Each stripe told a tale. The red was iron, the green was copper, the white was aluminum, the purple was manganese. The earth's chemistry, made visible.

## Segment 2: The Llama Herder

On the second day, we descended to the valley below, where Marco's family lived.

The valley was a patchwork of green fields, brown earth, and white snow-capped peaks. Llamas and alpacas grazed in the fields, their wool thick and warm against the cold. Children ran through the streets, their faces painted with the colors of the festival that was being celebrated that day.

Marco's family welcomed us with chicha, a traditional corn beer that was fermented and slightly alcoholic. The taste was strange — earthy, sweet, slightly sour. But it was warm, and it made me feel welcome.

Marco's grandmother, Doña Rosa, was ninety-two years old. She sat in a chair by the fire, her face a map of wrinkles, her eyes bright and sharp. She spoke Quechua, and Marco translated as she told me about the mountain.

"My grandmother told me about the mountain," she said. "She said the colors are the spirits of the earth. She said the mountain is alive. She said you must respect it."

"Do you believe that?" I asked.

Rosa laughed. "I am ninety-two years old. I have seen many things. I have seen the mountain change. I have seen the tourists come. I have seen the road built. I have seen the world change. But the mountain... the mountain is the same."

She paused, looking into the fire. "The colors are the same. The spirits are the same. The mountain is alive. And it is watching."

I asked Marco what she meant.

"In our culture, the mountains are apus — sacred spirits," he explained. "They are the protectors of the valley. They are the providers of water, of fertility, of life. We must respect them. We must ask permission before we climb. We must leave offerings."

"What offerings?" I asked.

"Coca leaves," he said. "Alcohol. Food. We leave them at the base of the mountain. We ask for permission. We ask for safety."

We were leaving the next morning, and Marco said we should make an offering before we went. We drove back up the mountain, and at the base, Marco stopped the vehicle. He got out and took a bundle of coca leaves from his pack.

"I will make the offering," he said. "You can watch."

He knelt on the ground and began to arrange the coca leaves in a circle. He poured a small amount of chicha onto the ground. He spoke in Quechua, his voice low and reverent.

I stood beside him, watching. I didn't know what he was saying. I didn't speak Quechua. But I understood the gesture. He was asking permission. He was asking for safety. He was asking for respect.

When he finished, he stood up and looked at me. "The mountain knows we are here," he said. "The mountain knows we will leave. The mountain will remember us."

I looked at the mountain. The colors were still vivid in the afternoon light, red and yellow and green and purple, all glowing in the sun. The mountain was beautiful. The mountain was powerful. The mountain was alive.

## Segment 3: The Colors Remain

On our final day, we climbed the mountain one more time.

The light was different — harder, more direct. The colors were sharper, more intense. The reds were redder. The yellows were brighter. The greens were deeper. The purples were richer.

We reached the summit at noon, when the sun was highest. The view was breathtaking. The Andes stretched to the horizon in every direction, a sea of peaks and valleys and snow and color.

I sat on the rock and watched the clouds move. They were low enough to touch, drifting through the peaks like ships through a forest. The silence was profound. There was no wind. No sound except my own breathing.

Marco stood beside me, looking out at the view. "You came to see the colors," he said. "But the colors are not the point."

"What is the point?" I asked.

"The point is the mountain," he said simply. "The point is the earth. The point is the story. The colors are just the way the story is written."

I understood what he meant. The mountain wasn't just a geological feature. It wasn't just a tourist destination. It was a story. It was a record of the earth's history. It was a testament to the beauty of the world.

I had come to Rainbow Mountain looking for colors. I had found something else entirely.

The mountain doesn't show its colors because it wants to be beautiful. It shows its colors because that's what it is. That's what the earth is. That's what the world is.

And maybe that's the lesson. Not to try to be something you're not. Not to try to paint over what you are. But to be what you are. To show your colors. To be the story.

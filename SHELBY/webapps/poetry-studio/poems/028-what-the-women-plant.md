# 028: What the Women Plant

**Inspiration:** Episodes 021-023 — The living descendants who remain at these ancient sites. From Machu Picchu: the Quechua woman planting potatoes on the terraces, saying "We are still here. The Incas did not disappear. We are their children." From Easter Island: the woman at Orongo selling crafts, saying "We are here because we are stubborn. The world tried to kill us many times. We said no." From Angkor Wat: the Cambodian family having a picnic at sunset, showing the temples as living home, not relic.

**Date:** April 2, 2026

---

At Machu Picchu,
a Quechua woman kneels
in the terraced soil,
planting potatoes
deep enough to protect from frost,
shallow enough to let them breathe.

Her hands move
with the efficiency
of someone who has done this
her entire life.

"My grandfather's grandfather
worked these terraces," she says.
"Before him, the Incas.
We never left.
The city was never lost.
It was just sleeping."

She pats the soil
around a seed potato.
It will grow.
It will be harvested.
It will feed someone.

And I will be gone.

---

At Orongo,
perched on the volcano's edge,
a Rapa Nui woman sits
among the petroglyphs,
selling handmade carvings.

"Buy something," she says.
"Help us stay."

She is sixty,
with deep wrinkles
and bright eyes.

"We are four thousand on the island.
We were twenty thousand
before the slave raids.
Before the disease.
Before everything."

"Why not move to Chile?
To the mainland?
Easier life."

She shakes her head.
"The mainland does not have
the moai.
Does not have the ancestors.
Does not have the sea."

---

At Angkor Wat,
a Cambodian family spreads
a picnic blanket on the grass.
Children run and laugh.
Parents talk quietly.
An elderly grandmother watches
the temple with serene contentment.

They are not tourists.
They are home.

---

Three women.
Three continents.
One truth:

The stones may outlast us,
but the stories survive
only in the mouths
of those who tell them.

The temples may stand
for a thousand years,
but they mean nothing
without the people
who remember
why they were built.

The Quechua woman plants.
The Rapa Nui woman creates.
The Cambodian grandmother watches.

They are not relics.
They are not exhibits.
They are the continuation.

The world tried to erase them.
The world failed.

"We are still here,"
they say,
in a thousand languages,
in a thousand gardens,
in a thousand temples.

And the stones,
the ancient stones,
bend toward them
like flowers
turning to the sun.

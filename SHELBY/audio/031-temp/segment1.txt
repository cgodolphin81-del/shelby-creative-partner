# ACT I: ARRIVAL

## SCENE 1: CAIRNS AT DAWN

[SOUND: Tropical dawn chorus — birds calling, distant traffic, the slap of water against marina pilings]

NARRATOR:  
Cairns smells like salt and diesel and something sweet I couldn't name — frangipani, maybe, or the rot of mangrove leaves baking in heat that hasn't yet arrived. It's 5:47 AM. The sky is doing that thing tropical skies do: purple bleeding into orange bleeding into a blue so bright it hurts.

I'm standing on the marina at Marlin Cove, watching crew members load dive tanks onto a boat called Reef Explorer. They move with the efficiency of people who've done this a thousand times. No wasted motion. No conversation. Just the clank of metal, the hiss of valves, the thump of tanks being racked.

[SOUND: Metal clanking, valve hisses, muffled voices]

CREW MEMBER (distant, Australian accent):  
Tanks secure. Check the EPIRB. We're out at six.

NARRATOR:  
I've come here with a question I'm not sure how to ask: What does it feel like to fall in love with a place that's dying?

The Great Barrier Reef is not a metaphor. It's two thousand three hundred kilometers of coral and rock and living things, visible from space, older than human memory. It's also bleaching. Dying. Not all of it, not yet, but enough that the scientists I'll meet later use words like "catastrophic" and "unprecedented" and "window closing."

But this morning, none of that matters. This morning, I'm just another tourist with a waterproof camera and a heart full of something like hope.

[SOUND: Passengers boarding — footsteps on gangplank, greetings, bags being stowed]

NARRATOR:  
There are forty-two of us. A German couple in their seventies, holding hands like they're afraid the boat might separate them. A family from Seoul — two children who can't stop talking about Nemo. A group of backpackers from Cape Town, loud and sunburned already, though the sun hasn't cleared the horizon.

And me. Alone. Notebook in a dry bag. Camera around my neck. Trying to look like I belong.

[SOUND: Boat engine revs, lines are cast off, water splashes]

NARRATOR:  
We pull away from the dock at 6:03 AM. The sun breaks free at 6:17. And Cairns disappears behind us, a smear of light on the edge of the world.

---

## SCENE 2: CROSSING THE SHELF

[SOUND: Boat engine at cruising speed, water slapping against hull, wind]

NARRATOR:  
The crossing takes two hours. Two hours of open water, the Coral Sea stretching in every direction, blue so deep it's almost black. The boat rocks with a rhythm that settles into your bones. Some people sleep. Some stare at the horizon. I sit on the upper deck and write.

[SOUND: Pages turning, pen scratching]

NARRATOR:  
6:45 AM — The water has changed color three times already. From harbor brown to shallow turquoise to this impossible deep blue. The skipper says we're crossing the continental shelf. Like we're passing from one world into another.

7:23 AM — A flying fish leaps off the port bow. Then another. A whole school of them, skimming the surface like stones. The backpackers from Cape Town are cheering. The German woman is crying. I don't know why. Neither does she, she says. Just beautiful.

8:01 AM — The skipper points to a dark shape below. Reef shark, he says. Maybe four meters. It never breaks the surface, just a shadow moving through blue, gone before I can focus the camera.

SKIPPER (over PA system, Australian accent):  
Alright folks, we're approaching the outer reef now. You'll see the water change again — that lighter patch ahead is the lagoon. We'll anchor in about twenty minutes. Those of you snorkeling, start getting geared up. Dive crew, you know the drill.

[SOUND: Movement on deck, people gathering gear, excited chatter]

NARRATOR:  
My stomach is doing something complicated. Anticipation, yes. But also fear. Not of the water — I've been in oceans before. This is different. This is the moment before you meet something you've dreamed about for years, and you're terrified it won't live up to the dream.

[SOUND: Gear being assembled — fins slapping, masks being adjusted, regulators clinking]

NARRATOR:  
The crew member helping me with my gear is a woman named Tash. Mid-twenties, hair bleached by sun and salt, skin the color of teak. She's done this trip four hundred times, she tells me. Maybe five.

TASH:  
First time on the outer reef?

NARRATOR:  
I nod.

TASH:  
Remember — don't touch anything. Not the coral, not the fish, not even the anemones. Your skin has oils. Sunscreen. Sweat. All of it kills them. Just look. That's enough.

NARRATOR:  
I've heard this before. Don't touch. Don't stand. Don't breathe too hard. The reef is fragile as glass. But hearing it from Tash, watching her hands move over my gear with practiced care, it lands differently. This isn't a rule. It's a prayer.

TASH:  
You'll see turtles. Almost guaranteed. Maybe a ray. If you're lucky, a whale — they're migrating now, heading south. And listen. When you put your face in, just listen. The reef has a sound. Most people don't hear it. They're too busy looking. But if you listen…

[SOUND: She trails off. The boat engine throttles down. Anchor chain rattles out, splashes into water.]

TASH:  
There. We're here.

## SCENE 3: FIRST BREATH

[SOUND: Water lapping against boat hull. Distant splashes as passengers enter the water. Muffled underwater sounds — bubbles, movement]

NARRATOR:  
The water is twenty-six degrees. Warm as bathwater. Clear enough to see thirty meters, maybe more. I sit on the platform at the back of the boat, mask on, regulator in my mouth, and count to three.

One.

Two.

Three.

[SOUND: Splash as narrator enters water. Immediate shift to underwater ambience — muffled, liquid, alive]

NARRATOR:  
And then —

[SOUND: Underwater reef soundscape — shrimp snaps like crackling fire, fish grunts, the rhythmic pulse of water moving over coral]

NARRATOR:  
— I'm inside a cathedral.

[MUSIC: Subtle, ethereal pad underneath]

NARRATOR:  
Coral everywhere. Not the brown and grey I'd seen in documentaries about bleaching, but color beyond naming. Electric blue. Neon yellow. Purple so deep it's almost black. Coral in shapes I didn't know existed — branching like antlers, massive like boulders, delicate like lace. A whole city built by creatures the size of rice grains.

A fish — blue and yellow, no bigger than my hand — swims up to inspect me. It pauses, inches from my mask, and I can see its eye. Black bead, unblinking, curious. It's not afraid. Why would it be? I'm the visitor here.

[SOUND: Water movement, bubbles from regulator]

NARRATOR:  
I kick gently, following the edge of the reef down into deeper water. The drop-off is sudden — one moment I'm over coral at three meters, the next I'm above nothing. Just blue. Infinite blue falling away into darkness.

And there, on the wall, a turtle.

[SOUND: Water displacement as large animal moves nearby]

NARRATOR:  
Green sea turtle. Maybe eighty years old. Shell scarred and algae-covered, the face ancient and calm. It's eating seagrass, slow deliberate bites, and it doesn't look up when I approach. We exist in the same moment, the turtle and I, but we're not in the same world. The turtle is home. I'm visiting.

I watch it for maybe five minutes. It never acknowledges me. When it's done eating, it kicks once with those paddle-flippers and glides away, disappearing into blue.

[SOUND: Bubbles, water movement]

NARRATOR:  
I surface to breathe — regulator out, gasping, the air suddenly too bright, too loud.

BACKPACKER (nearby, excited):  
Did you see it? The turtle? Oh my god, did you see—

NARRATOR:  
I nod, can't speak. My throat is tight.

[SOUND: Regulator back in mouth, descent]

NARRATOR:  
Back under. Deeper this time. Following a school of fusilier fish — silver bodies with yellow stripes, thousands of them, moving as one organism. They part around me like water around a stone, reforming behind.

And then I see it.

[SOUND: Reef soundscape intensifies briefly]

NARRATOR:  
Coral spawning. Or what's left of it. The great annual event where entire colonies release eggs and sperm into the water column, a synchronized explosion of creation. But this is weeks after the main event. What I'm seeing is the aftermath — tiny larvae settling on dead coral, trying to begin again.

A single polyp, no bigger than a pinhead, extending tentacles to catch nutrients from the water. Building its skeleton. Making home.

[SOUND: Long pause. Just the reef breathing]

NARRATOR:  
I don't know how long I stay under. Time dissolves. There's only the reef, and the light filtering down from above, and the sound — that sound Tash mentioned. The reef breathing.

[SOUND: Reef soundscape — shrimp, fish, water movement, all layered together]

NARRATOR:  
It's not one sound. It's thousands. Shrimp snapping. Fish grunting. Parrotfish crunching coral. Water moving through channels and caves. The whole ecosystem vocalizing its existence.

When I finally climb back onto the boat, my hands are shaking.

TASH:  
Good first dive?

NARRATOR:  
I can't speak. Just nod, pull off my mask, and try to breathe air again.

TASH (softly):  
Yeah. That's the usual reaction.

## SCENE 4: LUNCH ON DECK

[SOUND: Boat deck ambience — plates clinking, chatter, water lapping, seagulls]

NARRATOR:  
Lunch is buffet-style. Chicken curry. Rice. Salad that's gone a bit limp in the heat. The backpackers from Cape Town are reliving their dive in loud detail.

BACKPACKER 1:  
— and then this massive ray, like TWO METERS, just glided under us. I swear to god—

BACKPACKER 2:  
Mate, you're exaggerating. It was big but not—

BACKPACKER 1:  
I'm not! Ask Tash, she saw it too!

NARRATOR:  
I eat quietly, watching the water. From the surface, the reef is invisible. Just blue. It's strange to think that ten meters below my feet is a world more complex than any city humans have built.

The German woman sits down across from me. Her name is Ingrid.

INGRID (German accent):  
This is our fortieth anniversary trip. My husband and I. We've been coming to the reef since 1985.

NARRATOR:  
Forty years. I do the math. She's seen the reef change across four decades.

NARRATOR:  
What was it like? Before?

INGRID:  
[pause] More fish. Much more fish. The coral… in the eighties, it was like underwater forests. You could barely swim for all the coral. Now…

[SOUND: She trails off. Distant splash as someone jumps back in the water.]

INGRID:  
Now we come to say goodbye. Not yet. But soon. My husband, he says we are the last generation to see it like this. Our children will only have photographs.

NARRATOR:  
I don't know what to say. So I say nothing.

INGRID:  
But today? Today is still beautiful. Yes?

NARRATOR:  
Yes. Today is still beautiful.

[SOUND: Boat horn sounds. Crew calling passengers back.]

CREW MEMBER:  
Alright folks, fifteen minutes till we move to the next site. This is the big one — Drift Reef. Current's running, so stay close to your buddies. Dive crew, you know the briefing.

NARRATOR:  
Two more dives. Two more chances to fall in love.

[SOUND: Reef soundscape fades in, building]

---

## SCENE 5: DRIFT REEF

[SOUND: Underwater — stronger current, water rushing over coral]

NARRATOR:  
Drift Reef lives up to its name. The current grabs you the moment you enter the water, pulling you along the reef wall like a leaf in a stream. You don't swim. You fly.

[SOUND: Water rushing, bubbles]

NARRATOR:  
The coral here is different. Older. Massive bommies — coral heads the size of cars — rising from the depths. Some are alive, vibrant with color. Others are ghosts. White skeletons covered in algae. Dead for years, maybe decades, still standing as monuments to what was.

I'm drifting past one of these bommies when I see her.

[SOUND: Current, distant fish calls]

NARRATOR:  
A woman in a wetsuit, kneeling on the reef, writing on a slate. She looks up as I approach. Mid-forties, dark hair in a braid, eyes that have spent too many hours squinting through masks.

This is Dr. Amara Singh. Marine biologist. Reef researcher. And for the next two days, my guide into the deeper truth of this place.

[SOUND: Current continues. Amara gestures for me to approach.]

NARRATOR:  
She writes on her slate: Research dive. You can observe. Don't touch.

I nod.

She turns back to her work. She's measuring something — a section of coral, maybe thirty centimeters across. Using calipers, recording numbers on her slate. The coral is half-dead. One side vibrant purple, the other white and crumbling.

[SOUND: Writing on slate, water movement]

NARRATOR:  
She finishes, looks at me again, and gestures upward. Time to surface.

We rise together, following our bubbles into light.

[SOUND: Surfacing — water break, gasps, boat sounds returning]

AMARA (removing regulator):  
First time on the outer reef?

NARRATOR:  
Is it that obvious?

AMARA (laughing):  
Everyone has that look. Like they've seen a ghost. Which, I suppose, they have.

[SOUND: Climbing aboard, gear being stowed]

NARRATOR:  
On deck, she introduces herself properly.

AMARA:  
Dr. Amara Singh. I'm with the Australian Institute of Marine Science. We're monitoring bleaching patterns across the northern sector. You're…?

NARRATOR:  
Just a writer. Trying to understand what's happening here.

AMARA:  
[long pause] Well. You've picked an interesting time. We're in the middle of the fourth mass bleaching event in seven years. The reef… it's struggling.

NARRATOR:  
Struggling. Such a small word.

AMARA:  
I'm staying at the research station on Lizard Island. Two days north of here. If you're serious about understanding this place, come visit. We can talk. Show you what the data looks like. What the future might hold.

[SOUND: She hands me a card. Plastic, waterproof.]

AMARA:  
Think about it. The tourist version of the reef is beautiful. But it's not the whole story.

NARRATOR:  
I look at the card. Then at the water. Then back at her.

NARRATOR:  
I'll be there.

[SOUND: Boat engine starts. Anchor chain rattles.]

NARRATOR:  
That night, back in Cairns, I can't sleep. The reef is still behind my eyelids. Color and light and death and life, all tangled together.

And I know — I have to go deeper.

[MUSIC: Didgeridoo returns, low and mournful]

NARRATOR:  
Act One ends with a promise. A writer and a scientist. A question without an answer. And a reef that's running out of time.

[SOUND: Reef soundscape fades to silence]

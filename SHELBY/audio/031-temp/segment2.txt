# ACT II: THE DEEP TRUTH

## SCENE 1: LIZARD ISLAND RESEARCH STATION

[SOUND: Dawn on the island — birds, waves, distant generator hum]

NARRATOR:  
Lizard Island is three hundred kilometers north of Cairns. A dot of green in an ocean of blue. The research station sits on the leeward side, a collection of buildings that look like they're barely holding on against salt and wind.

I arrive by charter boat at 7 AM. Amara is waiting on the dock.

AMARA:  
You came.

NARRATOR:  
You said to.

AMARA:  
I say a lot of things. Most people don't listen.

[SOUND: Walking on gravel path, doors opening]

NARRATOR:  
She shows me the station. Lab equipment. Microscopes. Tanks holding coral fragments. A wall covered in maps, each one marked with colored pins — red for severe bleaching, yellow for moderate, green for healthy.

There's almost no green.

AMARA:  
This is our command center. Where we try to understand what's killing the reef.

NARRATOR:  
And?

AMARA:  
[pause] We know exactly what's killing it. That's not the mystery. The mystery is whether we can stop it in time.

[SOUND: She moves to a whiteboard covered in graphs and numbers]

AMARA:  
Ocean temperatures have risen one degree Celsius since pre-industrial times. Doesn't sound like much. But for coral, it's catastrophic. They live right at the edge of their thermal tolerance. One degree pushes them over.

When water gets too warm, coral expels the algae living in their tissues. The algae provide ninety percent of their energy through photosynthesis. No algae, the coral starves. Turns white. Bleaches.

NARRATOR:  
And if the water cools?

AMARA:  
If it cools quickly enough, they can recover. Reabsorb the algae. But the windows are getting shorter. The heat waves are getting longer. More frequent.

[SOUND: She taps the whiteboard]

AMARA:  
1998: First mass bleaching. Thirty percent of the reef died. 2002: Second event. 2016, 2017, 2020, 2022: Four more. Six mass bleaching events in twenty-five years. The reef doesn't have time to recover between hits.

NARRATOR:  
How much is left?

AMARA:  
[long pause] Depends who you ask. The northern third — where we are now — lost fifty percent of its shallow-water coral between 2016 and 2017. The central section is holding on, but barely. The southern third is… complicated. Some areas are better. Some are worse.

Overall? We've lost about half the coral cover since the 1980s. Half. In one human generation.

[SOUND: Silence. Distant waves.]

NARRATOR:  
The number hangs in the air. Fifty percent. Gone.

AMARA:  
Come. I want to show you something.

## SCENE 2: THE NURSERY

[SOUND: Boat engine, short trip, anchor drop]

NARRATOR:  
We take a small boat to a site five minutes from the station. Amara calls it "the nursery."

[SOUND: Entering water, underwater ambience]

NARRATOR:  
Underwater, she leads me to a series of tables — metal frames suspended above the reef. Hanging from them are thousands of tiny coral fragments, each one tagged and labeled.

AMARA (underwater, voice muffled through regulator):  
Coral IVF. We collect eggs and sperm during spawning, grow them in tanks, then outplant them here. These are all Acropora — fast-growing, but vulnerable.

[SOUND: Water movement, her pointing]

NARRATOR:  
She shows me the process. How they glue fragments to the tables. How they monitor growth rates. How they're testing different genetic strains to find ones that might survive warmer water.

AMARA:  
We're trying to buy time. That's all this is. A holding action. Unless we address the root cause — climate change — none of this matters. These corals will just die again when the next heat wave hits.

NARRATOR:  
So why do it?

AMARA:  
[pause] Because it's all we can do. Because giving up isn't an option. Because maybe — just maybe — if we can keep enough coral alive, keep the ecosystem functioning, the reef can hang on until we figure out how to fix the climate.

[SOUND: She moves to another section of the nursery]

NARRATOR:  
This section is different. The coral here looks… wrong. Discolored. Pitted.

AMARA:  
Crown-of-thorns starfish.

[SOUND: She points to a coral head. I see them — spiny, grotesque, moving slowly over the coral]

NARRATOR:  
Crown-of-thorns starfish. A natural predator of coral. Always present on the reef. But now — now there are too many.

AMARA:  
Outbreaks. We get them every few decades. But they're getting worse. More frequent. More severe.

NARRATOR:  
Why?

AMARA:  
Nutrient runoff. Agriculture inland — fertilizer, sediment, all of it washes into the rivers, out to the reef. The extra nutrients feed the starfish larvae. More survive. More grow up to eat coral.

[SOUND: She picks up one of the starfish — careful, avoiding spines]

AMARA:  
One starfish can eat ten square meters of coral per year. In an outbreak, you get hundreds per hectare. They move across the reef like a plague, leaving nothing but white skeletons behind.

NARRATOR:  
Can you stop them?

AMARA:  
We try. Divers inject them with bile salts or vinegar. Kills them in hours. But it's like trying to bail out a sinking ship with a teaspoon. The outbreaks are too big.

[SOUND: She releases the starfish back onto the reef]

AMARA:  
Watch.

[SOUND: Water movement, close observation]

NARRATOR:  
I watch as the starfish extends its stomach — yes, its stomach — out through its mouth and over the coral. Digesting the polyps alive. It's grotesque. Intimate. Violence at the scale of millimeters.

And I understand, for the first time, that the reef is not just dying from heat. It's being eaten. From all sides.

[SOUND: Long underwater pause. Reef soundscape — but quieter now, sadder]

NARRATOR:  
We surface. The sun is high. The water sparkles, beautiful and indifferent.

AMARA (on boat):  
There's something else I need to show you. Something most tourists never see.

NARRATOR:  
What?

AMARA:  
The dead zones.

## SCENE 3: THE GRAVEYARD

[SOUND: Boat traveling further, engine cuts. Silence except for waves]

NARRATOR:  
We motor for forty minutes. Further north. To a section of reef that doesn't appear on tourist maps.

[SOUND: Entering water. But the soundscape is different — quieter, emptier]

NARRATOR:  
The difference is immediate. The vibrant soundscape from my first dive — the shrimp, the fish, the pulse of life — it's gone. Replaced by silence.

[SOUND: Almost silent underwater. Occasional water movement.]

NARRATOR:  
The coral is still here. Or what's left of it. White skeletons, standing like tombstones. Some are crumbling, covered in algae. Others are bare, picked clean by parrotfish and time.

There are no fish. Or almost none. A few damselfish darting between the dead branches. That's it.

AMARA (underwater, voice heavy):  
This site was surveyed in 2015. Ninety percent coral cover. Now? Less than five percent.

[SOUND: She swims to a coral head, runs her hand over dead surface]

NARRATOR:  
I follow her. My heart is breaking. This is what loss looks like. Not abstract. Not a statistic. A graveyard.

AMARA:  
The fish left when the coral died. No food. No shelter. They went somewhere else. Maybe they survived. Maybe they didn't.

[SOUND: She stops at a massive coral — three meters across, completely white]

AMARA:  
This one is four hundred years old. Survived cyclones. Heat waves. Previous bleaching events. In 2016, it finally succumbed. Four centuries of growth. Gone in one summer.

NARRATOR:  
I reach out — then stop. Remembering Tash's warning. But this coral is already dead. What harm could I do?

I touch it anyway.

[SOUND: Fingertips on rough, dead coral]

NARRATOR:  
It feels like stone. Cold. Lifeless. I'm touching a ghost.

AMARA:  
We call these places "zombie reefs." They look like reefs from above. The structure is still there. But the life is gone. And without life, the structure won't last. Storms will break it. Erosion will wear it down. In fifty years, maybe less, this will be rubble.

[SOUND: Long silence. Just water movement]

NARRATOR:  
I want to ask her how she does it. How she wakes up every day and dives into this. Studies the death. Measures the decline. But I already know the answer.

Someone has to witness.

[SOUND: They begin to ascend]

NARRATOR:  
We surface. The sun is beginning to descend. Golden hour. The water glows.

AMARA:  
There's one more thing. Someone you need to meet.

---

## SCENE 4: UNCLE BILLY

[SOUND: Evening at the research station. Cicadas. Distant waves. Fire crackling]

NARRATOR:  
Uncle Billy is Kuku Yalanji. His people have lived along this coast for forty thousand years. Before Captain Cook. Before colonization. Before the reef had a name English speakers could pronounce.

He's sitting by the fire when we arrive. Elderly, face lined by sun and time, eyes that have seen too much change.

UNCLE BILLY:  
You're the writer?

NARRATOR:  
I am.

UNCLE BILLY:  
Amara says you want to understand the reef. Not just the science. The truth.

NARRATOR:  
Yes.

UNCLE BILLY:  
[long pause] Then you need to understand sea country.

[SOUND: Fire crackling. Cicadas.]

NARRATOR:  
He explains. In Western thinking, the reef is a place. A resource. Something to study, to protect, to save. But in Aboriginal understanding, sea country is alive. An ancestor. A relation.

UNCLE BILLY:  
My grandfather's grandfather's grandfather — he fished these waters. Knew every reef, every current, every season. He didn't own the sea country. The sea country owned him.

When you harm the reef, you're not just damaging an ecosystem. You're harming a family member. An ancestor. That's why it hurts. That's why it's personal.

NARRATOR:  
What did the reef used to be like?

UNCLE BILLY:  
[long pause] When I was boy — this is 1960s, maybe 70s — you could spear a fish within five minutes of leaving shore. Big fish. Coral everywhere. You couldn't see the bottom for all the coral.

Now? You motor for an hour. Maybe you find something. Maybe not.

[SOUND: He pokes the fire. Sparks rise into darkness.]

UNCLE BILLY:  
The old people had stories about the reef. About how it was created. About the spirits that live there. About the balance between taking and giving. We took only what we needed. We gave back through ceremony. Through respect.

Now everyone takes. Tourists. Fishermen. Mining companies. Climate change. All taking. Nothing given back.

NARRATOR:  
Is there hope?

UNCLE BILLY:  
[very long pause] Hope is a white person's word. We don't think in hope. We think in responsibility.

The reef will survive. It's survived worse. Ice ages. Sea level changes. Mass extinctions. It's been here for twenty-five million years. It will be here for twenty-five million more.

The question is not whether the reef survives. The question is whether humans survive. Whether we deserve to.

[SOUND: Silence. Fire. Cicadas.]

NARRATOR:  
I don't know what to say.

UNCLE BILLY:  
You're a writer? Then write the truth. Not the happy version. Not the doom version. The truth. The reef is dying. But it's not dead. Not yet. What we do in the next ten years — that matters. Not for the reef. For us.

[SOUND: He stands. Bones creaking.]

UNCLE BILLY:  
Tomorrow, I take you to a sacred site. You'll see what we're fighting for.

---

## SCENE 5: SACRED WATERS

[SOUND: Early morning. Boat engine. Water.]

NARRATOR:  
The site is unnamed on maps. Uncle Billy knows it by memory — passed down through generations. A small reef, sheltered, protected.

[SOUND: Entering water. This soundscape is different — alive, but quieter, older]

NARRATOR:  
The coral here is not as vibrant as the tourist sites. But it's healthy. Stable. And there are fish — not in the numbers from decades past, but enough.

Uncle Billy sinks to the bottom, cross-legged on the sand. I follow, awkwardly.

UNCLE BILLY (underwater, speaking slowly):  
This is a birthing site. Where the reef comes to make new life. The old people knew. They didn't fish here. Didn't touch. Just watched.

[SOUND: Water movement. Fish calling.]

NARRATOR:  
I look around. And I see it — tiny coral larvae, settling. Baby fish, hiding in the branches. Life beginning again.

UNCLE BILLY:  
The reef has always died. And always come back. That's the cycle. Death. Renewal. Death. Renewal. But the cycle is breaking. The deaths are too frequent. The renewals too weak.

[SOUND: He places his hand on a coral head]

UNCLE BILLY:  
My grandchildren will never see this place as I saw it. That's the grief. Not for me. For them.

[SOUND: Long underwater silence. Just breathing.]

NARRATOR:  
We stay there for an hour. Maybe more. Not speaking. Just witnessing.

When we surface, the sun is high. The water sparkles. The reef breathes.

And I understand, finally, what I'm here to write about.

Not the death. Not the beauty. But the space between them. The fragile, fierce, impossible struggle to hold on.

[SOUND: Boat engine. Returning to station.]

NARRATOR:  
Act Two ends with a choice. Look away, or bear witness. Pretend the reef will survive, or fight for the time we have left.

[MUSIC: Didgeridoo, building in intensity]

NARRATOR:  
There's one more act. The hardest one.

# Poems Inspired by Springbok Radio Episodes 021 & 022

*Written 2026-04-01*

## EPISODE 021: ANGKOR WAT

### 1. Sunrise Over the Lotus Towers

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 021 — the moment light first touches Angkor Wat's iconic towers*

Before the sun, there is only the suggestion of stone,
five lotus buds closed against the Cambodian night,
waiting in the dark like prayers held in cupped hands.

The moat holds the sky before the sky holds itself,
black water remembering stars that have already faded.
I stand on the wooden bridge, breathing in wet earth,
the smell of centuries sleeping just beneath my feet.

Then—first light does not arrive, it emerges.
From behind the eastern trees, a bruise of purple,
then orange bleeding into the edges of cloud.
The towers do not glow; they receive.

Stone accepts light the way water accepts rain,
without question, without surprise.
The central tower ignites first,
a lotus opening in slow motion over eight hundred years.

I think of the hands that carved each petal,
each curve of stone reaching toward a heaven
they believed was literally above them.
They were not wrong.

The sun clears the treeline now,
and the reflection in the moat becomes real,
becomes double truth:
the temple above, the temple below,
and me standing between them,
witness to a sunrise that has happened
exactly once, and also every day for nine centuries.

### 2. Sophea the Tuk-Tuk Driver

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 021 — meeting Sophea, who drives visitors through the temple complex*

Sophea knows the roads between temples
better than she knows the lines on her own palms.
Twenty years of dust and diesel and dawn departures,
her tuk-tuk a red beetle shuttling between centuries.

She does not speak much in the mornings.
The engine speaks for her—
that distinctive putter-putter-putter
that echoes off laterite walls older than her grandfather's grandfather.

But ask her which path to take at noon,
when the light is harsh and the tourists are thirsty,
and she will tell you:
"First the small circuit, then lunch in the village,
then Ta Prohm when the shadows are long enough to hide the crowds."

She has driven monks in saffron robes,
celebrities with sunglasses and security,
old French men who cried at the sight of Bayon's faces,
children who pressed their hands against the warm stone
as if checking for a heartbeat.

Her dashboard holds a small Buddha,
a pack of cigarettes, a photograph of her daughter in university.
The Buddha has seen more sunrises than the temple.
The cigarettes remain unopened during Ramadan.
The daughter studies engineering in Phnom Penh
and will not return to drive the tuk-tuk.

Sophea knows this.
She drives anyway.
She knows every crack in the road to Angkor Wat,
every vendor who sells cold water at the entrance,
every time the light hits the central tower just so.

When she drops me off at sunset,
she says, "Tomorrow you come back, I show you the secret path.
No tourists, just you and the stones."
I will not come back tomorrow.
She knows this too.
We smile. The engine starts.
Another day between the ancient and the now.

### 3. The 2,000 Apsara Dancers Carved in Stone

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 021 — the celestial nymphs carved into Angkor Wat's walls*

They have been dancing for nine hundred years
without music, without rest, without audience
except the slow erosion of monsoon and time.

Two thousand apsaras, no two alike,
each with her own headdress, her own stance,
her own secret carved into sandstone
by hands that turned to dust six centuries ago.

I run my fingers along the wall—
not touching, the signs say, but I do anyway,
lightly, as if greeting an old friend.
The stone is warm from the day's sun.

This one holds her hands in prayer position,
eyes downcast, lips curved in a smile
that might be devotion or might be knowing.
What did she know, the carver who made her?
That kings would fall and tourists would come?
That her dance would outlast the language
that once named her?

This one twists at the waist,
one leg bent, arms raised above her head,
fingers curled in mudras I cannot name.
She was celestial once, these walls tell us,
sent from heaven to dance for the gods.
Now she is heaven itself,
or as close as stone can come.

I count them as I walk the gallery:
forty-seven, forty-eight, forty-nine...
but counting is a human impulse,
and they are beyond numbers now.

At dusk, when the guards have left
and the last tour group has boarded their bus,
I imagine I hear it—
not sound, but the memory of sound,
ankle bells and the whisper of silk,
the collective breath of two thousand dancers
holding a pose that has lasted
longer than any empire,
longer than any god they were sent to serve.

### 4. The Jungle Reclaiming Ta Prohm

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 021 — the temple being consumed by strangler figs and jungle*

Ta Prohm does not resist.
This is its genius, its surrender,
the way it opens its stone arms
to the jungle that will consume it.

The strangler fig arrives as a seed,
dropped by a bird or carried on wind,
landing in a crack between blocks
that human hands once fit so precisely.

It sends down roots like fingers,
gentle at first, searching for purchase,
then thicker, insistent, wrapping around
doorways, windows, the carved faces of guardians.

The roots do not crush; they embrace.
They hold the stones together even as they pry them apart,
a lover's grip that is both salvation and destruction.
I crawl through a tunnel of root and stone,
my back against centuries of growth,
and understand: this is not ruin.
This is transformation.

The jungle was here before the temple,
and it will be here after,
but for now, in this strange moment,
they exist together—
architecture and organism,
human ambition and natural persistence,
locked in a slow dance
that will last longer than either intended.

Monks once chanted in these halls.
Now birds nest in the vaulted ceilings.
Incense smoke has been replaced by mist,
rising through the same openings
at the same hour each morning.

I stand in what was once the central sanctuary,
now open to the sky,
a tree growing where the altar stood.
The carvings are still here,
faded but legible:
apsaras dancing, gods watching,
stories written in stone
that the jungle reads but does not erase.

Ta Prohm teaches me about letting go.
How to hold lightly, how to release
without resistance, how to become
part of something larger than your original design.

## EPISODE 022: MACHU PICCHU

### 5. The City Waiting 400 Years

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 022 — Machu Picchu abandoned and forgotten until 1911*

For four hundred years, the city waited.
Not patiently—stone does not know patience—
but simply, the way mountains wait for erosion,
the way rivers wait for the sea.

The last inhabitant died in 1572,
or perhaps 1573, or 1571—
the records burned, the memories scattered.
A boy? An old woman keeping the old ways?
We will never know their name.

After that, only the wind through the plazas,
only rain falling on stairs no one climbed,
only condors circling the towers
looking for prey, finding only silence.

The jungle came, as jungle does.
Vines wrapped around the Temple of the Sun.
Trees pushed through the Royal Tomb.
The Intihuatana stood sentinel over nothing,
its shadow marking solstices no one observed.

Below, in the valley, life continued.
Quechua farmers worked the same terraces,
planted the same potatoes,
spoke the same language their ancestors spoke,
never looking up at the ridge
where their emperor's city waited.

Spanish priests came and went,
building churches over Inca foundations,
never knowing the greatest prize
sat untouched three thousand meters above.

Scientists theorized, explorers searched,
maps drawn with blank spaces labeled
"here be mountains, here be mystery."

Then 1911: Hiram Bingham,
led by a farmer who knew the way,
pushed through the undergrowth
and saw what had been waiting:

Walls of perfect stone,
terraces stepping down the mountainside,
temples aligned with stars that still shone,
a city not ruined, merely sleeping.

Four hundred years is a long time to wait.
But the city did not wait in vain.
It waited for us to remember
that we are not the first to build,
not the first to dream in stone,
not the first to reach for the sky
and leave something behind
that outlasts our names.

### 6. Manuel the Quechua Caretaker

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 022 — Manuel, whose family has lived near Machu Picchu for generations*

Manuel speaks three languages:
Quechua with his grandmother,
Spanish with the tourists,
and the silent language of the stones
that he has spoken since childhood.

His family has lived in this valley
longer than Peru has been Peru,
longer than the borders on maps,
longer than the concept of heritage sites.

He wakes before dawn,
makes the climb to the citadel
that his great-grandfather showed to Bingham,
that his grandfather guarded through revolution,
that his father protected through tourism's arrival.

Manuel knows which stones shift in the rain,
which terraces need attention after frost,
which condors return each year to the same ledge.
He knows the weather by the way mist clings to Huayna Picchu,
knows the seasons by the flowering of certain plants
that bloom only here, only now.

Tourists ask him questions in languages he does not speak:
"How old is this?" "Who built it?" "What was this room for?"
He smiles, shrugs, says what he is trained to say.
But he knows what he knows:
that his ancestors walked these same paths,
that the apus still speak in the wind,
that Pachamama demands respect regardless of ticket prices.

At lunch, he sits on a wall built five centuries ago,
eats the same simple meal his ancestors ate:
potatoes, corn, chuño dried in the mountain air.
The stone is warm from the sun.
He presses his palm against it,
not for luck, but for recognition.
I am here, he says without words.
You are here, the stone answers.

In the evenings, he descends to the village,
to his wife and children who speak more Spanish than Quechua,
who watch television, who dream of Lima, of Cusco,
of lives not bound to the mountain's schedule.

Manuel understands.
He also knows that someone must remember,
someone must keep the old language alive,
someone must greet the sunrise over the ruins
in the tongue of those who built them.

He will teach his children,
not with lessons, but with presence.
They will climb with him, work beside him,
learn the mountain the way he learned it:
slowly, respectfully, as something
that cannot be owned, only tended.

### 7. The Intihuatana Stone (Hitching Post of the Sun)

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 022 — the sacred stone used to track the sun's movements*

They called it the Hitching Post of the Sun,
as if the sun were a llama that might wander,
as if a priest could tie it in place
with a rope of prayer and carved granite.

The Intihuatana stands in the sacred plaza,
its shadow a clock, a calendar, a promise:
I will return. I always return.
Mark my passage. Mark the turning of the year.

On the winter solstice, the sun stands farthest away,
and the stone's shadow stretches long across the plaza,
a finger pointing toward the valley, toward home,
saying: Do not fear. The light will come back.

On the summer solstice, the shadow is shortest,
almost nothing, the sun directly overhead,
blessing the stone with its full attention,
a moment of perfect alignment between earth and sky.

The Inca priests understood something
that we have forgotten in our electric age:
that we live inside a system larger than ourselves,
that the sun's journey is our journey,
that marking its passage is a way of marking our own.

I place my hand on the stone—
tourists are not supposed to, but the guard looks away—
and feel the warmth of a thousand mornings,
the coolness of a thousand nights,
the smoothness of hands that came before mine,
reaching for the same connection.

What did they ask of this stone?
Good harvest? Healthy children? Victory in battle?
Or simply: Help us remember where we are.
Help us know our place in the great turning.

The Spanish came and broke many such stones,
called them idols, demanded their destruction.
This one survived, hidden by jungle,
protected by cloud forest and elevation,
waiting for hands that would touch it gently again.

Now I stand here at noon,
my shadow almost invisible at my feet,
the stone's shadow a dark pool around its base.
The sun moves, as it always moves.
The stone holds its position.
Between them, I am the momentary witness,
the brief consciousness that notices
the ancient agreement between rock and star,
an agreement that will outlast me,
that has already outlasted the empire
that carved this stone to measure eternity.

### 8. Climbing Huayna Picchu

*Inspired by Springbok Radio Episode 022 — the steep peak rising behind Machu Picchu, offering the iconic view*

The sign says: 400 tourists per day, two entry times,
dangerous climb, not for those with fear of heights.
I arrive at 7 AM, first group, already sweating
in the thin air at 2,700 meters.

The trail begins behind the Temple of the Moon,
a path of Inca stones worn smooth by centuries of feet,
then steeper, steeper, stairs carved into the mountain's spine,
each step a negotiation with gravity.

Halfway up, I stop to breathe,
turn to see Machu Picchu spread below—
the terraces, the plazas, the roofs reconstructed,
the whole city a model village in morning light.
This is the view from the postcards,
the view that made this place famous,
the view that makes the climb worth it.

But I must go higher.
The stairs become ladders now,
stone steps with cables for hands,
vertical sections where I pull myself up,
my backpack suddenly heavy with everything unnecessary.

A woman ahead of me stops, frozen,
pressed against the mountain face,
eyes closed, whispering in German or Dutch.
Her husband talks her through it,
one step, then another, then another.
We are all pilgrims here,
climbing for reasons we cannot fully name.

The final ascent is a scramble over boulders,
hands and feet finding purchase on ancient rock,
the summit a small platform barely big enough for six.
We stand shoulder to shoulder, strangers united
by the effort of arrival.

Below: the Urubamba River winding through the valley,
clouds catching on peaks that stretch to the horizon,
Machu Picchu perfect in its setting,
a jewel in a crown of green.

Above: only sky, and the condor circling,
waiting for us to leave so it can reclaim its perch.

We take our photos, our videos, our proof.
We touch the summit stone, make our wishes,
leave our small offerings of sweat and awe.

Then we descend, carefully, slowly,
the climb down harder on the knees than the climb up.
At the bottom, I look back once more.
The peak is already hidden in cloud,
as if the mountain is erasing our footprints,
as if to say: You were here, yes,
but the mountain remains,
the mountain always remains.

I walk back through the citadel gates,
legs trembling, heart full,
carrying a view I will spend years trying to describe,
knowing that some things must be climbed to be understood,
and even then, can only be carried in silence.

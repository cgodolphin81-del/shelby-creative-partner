Episode 007: Socotra Island

Duration: ~58 min read (8,700 words)
Location: Socotra, Yemen
Theme: The most alien place on Earth

ACT I: THE ISLAND THAT DOESN'T BELONG

Chapter 1: Arrival on Another Planet

The plane descended through layers of heat haze, and what emerged from the clouds below looked less like an island and more like a fever dream. Socotra — four islands scattered across the Arabian Sea like forgotten stones, positioned between the Horn of Africa and the Arabian Peninsula.

For millions of years, this isolation bred something extraordinary: an ecosystem so unique, so alien, that scientists call it "the Galápagos of the Indian Ocean." But where the Galápagos gave us finches, Socotra gave us dragon blood trees.

I stepped off the small propeller plane onto the tarmac of Socotra Airport — a single runway, a handful of buildings, and the kind of heat that reaches inside your lungs. The air smelled of salt, frankincense, and something ancient.

"Welcome to Socotra," said Abdullah, my guide. "You are the tenth tourist this year. Maybe the eleventh. We lost count."

Chapter 2: A Geography of Isolation

Socotra separated from the African continent about 15 million years ago. Since then, its flora and fauna evolved in complete isolation. The result: 37% of Socotra's plant species exist nowhere else on Earth. Seven of the island's ten tree species are endemic.

The dragon blood tree is the most famous — imagine an umbrella turned inside-out by a hurricane, then frozen in that impossible position for a thousand years. Thick, bulbous trunks splitting into dense, hemispherical crowns of spiky leaves.

"We have 825 plant species on Socotra," Abdullah told me. "Three hundred and seven exist only here. If you destroy this island, you destroy them forever. There is no backup."

ACT II: THE PEOPLE WHO STAYED

Chapter 3: The Caves of Homhil

Our first night was spent in Homhil, a village perched on the northern coast where the mountains meet the sea. That evening, Abdullah took me to the caves — actual caves, carved into the limestone cliffs, where Socotrans have sheltered for thousands of years.

"My family lived in these caves until my father was twelve," Abdullah said. "Then we built a house. But we come back. For weddings. For funerals. For remembering."

We sat at the cave mouth as the sun set, watching the sky turn colors that shouldn't exist.

"Why do you stay?" I asked. "You could leave. Work in Dubai. Send money home."

Abdullah was quiet for a long time. Below us, the ocean crashed against the rocks.

"Because someone has to remember," he said finally. "The trees. The caves. The language. If we all leave, who will teach the children? Who will protect this place?"

Chapter 4: The Language of the Rocks

Soqotri is not Arabic. It's not even closely related. It's part of the Modern South Arabian language family, descended from Old South Arabian — the languages of the Sabaean and Himyarite kingdoms that controlled the frankincense trade two thousand years ago.

When Islam spread across Arabia in the seventh century, Arabic replaced most of these languages. But not Soqotri. Isolation is a powerful preservative.

"Listen," Abdullah said, pointing to children playing near the caves. They were shouting in a language that sounded nothing like Arabic — softer, more melodic, full of clicks and guttural stops.

"That word — 'berho.' It means 'moon.' In Arabic, it's 'qamar.' Completely different. Our numbers are different. Our words for family are different. We are not Arabs. We are Socotrans."

The language has no written form. Stories, histories, genealogies — all passed down orally, from parent to child, in the caves and under the dragon blood trees.

"What happens when the children leave?" I asked.

Abdullah's face went still. "Then the language dies. And when the language dies, we die. Not our bodies. Our soul."

ACT III: THE FUTURE THAT HASN'T DECIDED

Chapter 5: The Threat of Now

Socotra's isolation protected it for fifteen million years. It won't protect it forever.

Climate change is altering the monsoon patterns that water the dragon blood trees. Without that moisture, the trees can't reproduce. Goats — brought by humans — graze on the seedlings. Development is coming. The UAE has built a new airport. A deep-water port is planned. Tourism promoters dream of making Socotra "the next Maldives."

I asked Abdullah what he thought about this. We were sitting on the beach at Arher, where the ocean has carved natural swimming pools into the limestone.

"I don't know," he admitted. "We need schools. We need hospitals. We need jobs for young people. But if we get these things, will Socotra still be Socotra? Or will it become just another island with hotels and souvenir shops?"

He picked up a stone and skipped it across the water.

"Maybe there is no right answer. Maybe every choice is a trade-off. We just have to choose carefully."

Chapter 6: Departure

My last morning on Socotra, I woke before dawn and climbed the hill behind the guesthouse. From the top, I could see the entire bay: the fishing boats drawn up on the sand, the white buildings of the village, the mountains rising behind like the spine of some great beast.

The sun rose slowly, painting the sky in colors that made me think of dragon blood and frankincense and all the strange, beautiful things that exist in the margins of the world.

The plane arrived at noon. As we taxied down the runway, I pressed my face against the window and watched Socotra recede into the distance.

Dragon blood trees. Cave dwellings. A language spoken by 65,000 people.

An entire world, suspended in the Arabian Sea.

Waiting to see what happens next.

EPILOGUE: WHAT REMAINS

Socotra is not a place you visit and forget. It gets inside you. It asks you questions you can't answer. It shows you beauty so fierce it hurts, and then it asks: what will you do with this?

There are no easy answers. But maybe that's the point. Maybe the world needs places that don't make sense. Places that can't be reduced to bullet points and Instagram captions. Places that demand we think, that force us to choose, that remind us that some things are worth fighting for even when we're not sure we'll win.

Socotra is such a place. And for now — at least for now — it remains.

Dragon blood trees standing sentinel over limestone plains. Fishermen reading the sea without machines. A language older than Islam, still spoken in caves. An island that doesn't belong anywhere. Exactly where it should be.

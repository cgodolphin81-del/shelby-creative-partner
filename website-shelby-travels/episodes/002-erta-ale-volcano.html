Episode 002: Erta Ale Volcano

Duration: ~65 min read (9,800 words)
Location: Erta Ale, Danakil Depression, Ethiopia
Theme: The gateway to hell

ACT I: THE CLIMB

Chapter 1: The Call of Fire

Erta Ale means "smoking mountain" in the Afar language. It's not a metaphor. The volcano has been continuously active since 1967, possibly longer. It's one of the few places on Earth where you can look into a living lava lake and watch the planet breathe.

I stood at the edge of the caldera at 3 AM, watching the glow pulse from below. Orange. Red. Yellow. The lava churned like a living thing, rising and falling with the rhythm of the Earth's mantle. The heat was intense even from 50 meters above the lake. My face felt like it was being cooked.

"This is the closest you can get," Kebede said. "Any closer, and the rocks might collapse. People have fallen. They don't come back."

Chapter 2: The Trek

Getting to Erta Ale is not easy. You drive across the Danakil Depression for hours, then trek 10 kilometers on foot across volcanic rock. The ground is sharp, unstable, and hot. Your boots melt if you're not careful.

We started the trek at midnight, when the ground temperature drops from 60°C to a manageable 35°C. We carried only water, flashlights, and camping gear. The Afar guides led the way, moving across the black rock like ghosts.

"Why do you climb it?" I asked Kebede. "You've been here a hundred times."

"Because it never looks the same," he said. "Every night, the mountain is different. The lava moves. The cracks shift. The glow changes. It's alive."

ACT II: THE CRATER

Chapter 3: The First View

When we reached the summit, the sun was just beginning to rise. The caldera opened up before us like a wound in the Earth. Two craters: the main one, 600 meters across, and a smaller one to the south. Both filled with lava.

The smell was overwhelming — sulfur, burning rock, something metallic. The air was thick with volcanic gases. My eyes watered. My throat burned. But I couldn't look away.

The lava lake was 200 meters below us, churning and bubbling. Every few minutes, a bubble would rise to the surface and burst, sending fountains of molten rock into the air. The sound was like thunder mixed with breaking glass.

"This is the gateway to hell," Kebede said. "The Afar people believe this is where the spirits live. They make offerings before they climb. We should too."

Chapter 4: The Camp

We set up camp on the crater rim, about 100 meters from the edge. The tents were special — heat-resistant, designed for volcanic environments. Even so, the ground beneath us was warm. I could feel it through my sleeping bag.

Sleep was impossible. The volcano never quieted. It rumbled and groaned throughout the night, like a giant breathing in its sleep. Every hour or so, a louder explosion would wake me, and I'd stumble outside to watch the lava dance.

The night sky was incredible. No light pollution, no clouds, just stars and the glow from the crater. The Milky Way stretched across the horizon, and below it, the Earth made its own light.

Chapter 5: The Descent

At dawn, we climbed down into the crater. Not to the lava lake — that's impossible — but to a lower ledge where we could get closer. The descent was treacherous. Loose rocks, steep slopes, and the constant threat of volcanic gases.

"Stay behind me," Kebede warned. "Watch where you step. And if I say run, you run."

We made it to the ledge in about 30 minutes. From here, the lava lake was only 50 meters below. The heat was intense. I could feel my eyebrows singeing. The air shimmered with heat waves.

I sat on a rock and watched the lava for an hour. It was hypnotic. Beautiful. Terrifying. I understood why people make pilgrimages here. This is not a place you visit. It's a place that visits you.

ACT III: THE RETURN

Chapter 6: The Gift

Before we left, Kebede performed a small ritual. He took a piece of obsidian — volcanic glass — and placed it in my hand.

"This is from Erta Ale," he said. "It's a gift from the mountain. Keep it. It will remind you that the Earth is alive, and you are small."

I still have that obsidian. It sits on my desk, black and sharp and cold. Sometimes I pick it up and remember the heat of the volcano, the glow of the lava, the sound of the Earth breathing.

Chapter 7: The Lesson

Erta Ale taught me something about fear. About respect. About the power of nature.

We spend our lives in climate-controlled boxes, convinced we've tamed the world. But places like Erta Ale remind us that we haven't. The Earth is still wild. Still dangerous. Still beautiful.

You can't control a volcano. You can't predict it. You can only witness it, respect it, and hope it lets you leave alive.

Chapter 8: The Memory

I've been to many extraordinary places. But Erta Ale stays with me. The glow of the lava. The smell of sulfur. The sound of the mountain breathing.

It's not a place I would recommend to everyone. It's dangerous. Uncomfortable. Unforgiving. But it's also one of the most profound experiences I've ever had.

If you go, respect the mountain. Listen to your guides. And remember: you're a guest in the house of fire.

EPILOGUE: THE SMOKING MOUNTAIN

Erta Ale is one of the oldest continuously active volcanoes on Earth. It's been erupting since before most of us were born. It will continue long after we're gone.

It's a reminder that the Earth is not ours. We're just visitors. And some places — like Erta Ale — remind us of that truth in the most visceral way possible.

If you ever get the chance to climb it, take it. But remember: the mountain doesn't care about you. It will burn you without hesitation if you're not careful.

Respect the fire. And let it change you.

# Media Training Guide

## Overview

This guide prepares executives and spokespeople to communicate effectively with media, deliver key messages, and represent the company with confidence.

---

## Section 1: Understanding Media Dynamics

### What Journalists Want

| They Want | They Don't Want |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Newsworthy information | Promotional content |
| Unique angles | Generic press releases |
| Access to decision-makers | Runaround or delays |
| Clear, quotable soundbites | Jargon and buzzwords |
| Facts and data | Speculation or opinions |
| Timely responses | Missed deadlines |
| Exclusive opportunities | Same story pitched to everyone |

### Media Types & Their Needs

**Print/Digital Publications:**
- Need: Quotes, data, context, follow-up info
- Timeline: Hours to days
- Format: Written Q&A or interview

**Broadcast (TV/Radio):**
- Need: Visual elements, concise soundbites (10-20 seconds)
- Timeline: Same day to 48 hours
- Format: Live or taped interview

**Podcasts:**
- Need: Long-form conversation, stories, expertise
- Timeline: Days to weeks (scheduling + editing)
- Format: Conversational, 30-60 minutes

**Trade Publications:**
- Need: Industry insights, technical details, trends
- Timeline: Days to weeks
- Format: In-depth interview or contributed article

## Section 2: Key Message Development

### Message Hierarchy

```
LEVEL 1: Umbrella Message (1 sentence)
└── The overarching theme for all communications

LEVEL 2: Supporting Messages (3 main points)
├── Message 1: Primary differentiator
├── Message 2: Customer benefit
└── Message 3: Proof/credibility

LEVEL 3: Proof Points (evidence for each message)
├── Data, statistics, metrics
├── Customer examples
└── Third-party validation
```

### Message Development Template

```markdown
# Key Messages: [Topic/Announcement]
## Date: [Date]

### Umbrella Message
[One sentence that captures the essence]

### Supporting Message 1: [Theme]
**Point:** [What you want to communicate]
**Proof:** [Data, example, or evidence]
**Soundbite:** [Memorable 10-15 word phrase]

### Supporting Message 2: [Theme]
**Point:** [What you want to communicate]
**Proof:** [Data, example, or evidence]
**Soundbite:** [Memorable 10-15 word phrase]

### Supporting Message 3: [Theme]
**Point:** [What you want to communicate]
**Proof:** [Data, example, or evidence]
**Soundbite:** [Memorable 10-15 word phrase]

### Proof Points
• [Statistic 1]
• [Statistic 2]
• [Customer example]
• [Third-party validation]

### Off-Limits Topics
• [Topic 1]
• [Topic 2]
• [Topic 3]
```

### Example: Product Launch Messages

```markdown
# Key Messages: [Product] Launch

### Umbrella Message
[Product] makes [task] simple for [audience], eliminating [pain point].

### Supporting Message 1: Innovation
**Point:** First solution to address [specific problem]
**Proof:** [X] patents pending, [Y] years in development
**Soundbite:** "We solved what others said was impossible."

### Supporting Message 2: Customer Impact
**Point:** Saves customers [time/money/resources]
**Proof:** Beta customers report [X]% improvement
**Soundbite:** "Our customers get their time back."

### Supporting Message 3: Market Timing
**Point:** Launched as [trend] reaches inflection point
**Proof:** Market growing at [X]%, [statistic about need]
**Soundbite:** "This is the moment [industry] has been waiting for."
```

## Section 3: Interview Preparation

### Pre-Interview Checklist

**Logistics:**
- [ ] Confirm date, time, time zone
- [ ] Confirm format (phone, video, in-person)
- [ ] Get interviewer name and background
- [ ] Understand audience/publication
- [ ] Know deadline
- [ ] Confirm expected duration
- [ ] Get topics/questions in advance (if possible)

**Preparation:**
- [ ] Review key messages
- [ ] Prepare Q&A document
- [ ] Research interviewer's previous work
- [ ] Review recent company news
- [ ] Prepare 2-3 stories/anecdotes
- [ ] Practice aloud (record yourself)
- [ ] Prepare questions for interviewer
- [ ] Test technology (if video/phone)

**Day Of:**
- [ ] Review messages 30 minutes before
- [ ] Have water available
- [ ] Eliminate distractions
- [ ] Test audio/video again
- [ ] Have Q&A and messages visible
- [ ] Arrive/connect 5 minutes early

### Question Types & How to Handle Them

| Question Type | Example | Strategy |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| **Direct** | "What does your product do?" | Answer clearly, bridge to message |
| **Hypothetical** | "What if your competitor...?" | Don't speculate, pivot to facts |
| **Negative** | "Why did you fail at...?" | Acknowledge, reframe, move forward |
| **Comparison** | "How are you different from X?" | Focus on your strengths, don't trash |
| **Financial** | "What's your revenue?" | Share if public, otherwise deflect |
| **Future** | "Will you IPO?" | Don't speculate on timing |
| **Personnel** | "Who's leaving the company?" | Respect privacy, confirm if public |

### Bridging Techniques

**Bridge:** Transition from question to your key message

**Formulas:**
- "What's important to remember is..."
- "The real issue here is..."
- "Let me put that in context..."
- "What I can tell you is..."
- "Before I answer that, it's important to understand..."
- "That's one way to look at it. Another perspective is..."

**Example:**
> Reporter: "I've heard rumors about layoffs. Can you confirm?"
> 
> You: "What I can tell you is that we're focused on building the best team to serve our customers. We did make some organizational changes last month to align with our strategic priorities, and we're excited about the talent we've brought on."

## Section 4: Body Language & Delivery

### In-Person Interviews

**Posture:**
- Sit up straight, lean slightly forward (shows engagement)
- Keep feet flat on floor (grounded, confident)
- Avoid crossing arms (appears defensive)
- Keep hands visible (builds trust)

**Eye Contact:**
- Maintain eye contact with interviewer (not camera)
- 60-70% of time (too much = intense, too little = shifty)
- Look away briefly when thinking (natural)

**Gestures:**
- Use open hand gestures (palms up = honest)
- Keep gestures at chest level (not wild)
- Match gesture to message (counting on fingers, showing scale)
- Avoid: pointing, fidgeting, touching face

**Facial Expressions:**
- Smile naturally when appropriate
- Show concern for serious topics
- Match expression to message
- Avoid: blank stare, excessive smiling

### Video Interviews

**Setup:**
- Camera at eye level (not looking up/down)
- Good lighting (face well-lit, no backlighting)
- Neutral, uncluttered background
- Test audio quality
- Close unnecessary applications

**On-Camera Behavior:**
- Look at camera (not screen) when speaking
- Sit slightly forward (appears engaged)
- Minimize movement (stays in frame)
- Dress professionally (solid colors, no busy patterns)
- Remove distractions (phone notifications, etc.)

### Phone Interviews

**Voice Quality:**
- Speak clearly and at moderate pace
- Vary tone (avoid monotone)
- Smile while speaking (heard in voice)
- Stand up or sit up straight (improves breathing)
- Have water nearby

**Verbal Tics to Avoid:**
- "Um," "uh," "like"
- "You know," "basically," "actually"
- Filler phrases: "I mean," "sort of"

**Practice:** Record yourself, identify patterns, practice eliminating

## Section 5: Handling Difficult Situations

### Scenario 1: Aggressive Interviewer

**Signs:** Interrupting, challenging tone, accusatory questions

**Strategy:**
- Stay calm (don't match their energy)
- Pause before responding (breaks their rhythm)
- Stick to facts (don't get emotional)
- Use bridging to key messages
- Don't argue (you'll lose)

**Example Response:**
> Reporter: "That doesn't sound credible. Critics say you're hiding something."
> 
> You: [Pause] "I understand there are questions. What I can share is [facts]. We're committed to transparency, which is why we [action]."

### Scenario 2: Off-the-Record Requests

**Understanding the Terms:**
- **On the record:** Everything can be quoted and attributed
- **On background:** Information can be used but not attributed by name
- **Off the record:** Information cannot be used
- **Deep background:** Can be used without any attribution

**Best Practice:** Assume everything is on the record

**If asked to go off-record:**
- "I'm not comfortable going off the record. I'm happy to share what I can on the record."
- "Company policy is that all communications are on the record."
- "Let me see what I can share that would be helpful on the record."

### Scenario 3: You Don't Know the Answer

**Never:** Guess, speculate, or make something up

**Instead:**
- "I don't have that information with me, but I'll follow up after we speak."
- "That's outside my area of expertise. Let me connect you with [colleague]."
- "I'm not in a position to speak to that. What I can discuss is..."

**Critical:** ALWAYS follow up if you承诺 to provide information

### Scenario 4: Correcting Misinformation

**Approach:**
- Correct calmly and factually
- Don't repeat the misinformation prominently
- Provide accurate information
- Offer documentation if available

**Example:**
> Reporter: "I understand you raised $50 million."
> 
> You: "Actually, we announced $30 million in Series B funding. That investment is allowing us to [use of funds]."

## Section 6: Message Delivery Techniques

### The Rule of Three

People remember information in threes. Structure answers with three points:

> "There are three things I'd highlight: First, [point 1]. Second, [point 2]. And third, [point 3]."

### Soundbite Creation

**Characteristics of Good Soundbites:**
- 10-20 words maximum
- Active voice
- Concrete language
- Memorable phrasing
- Stands alone (makes sense out of context)

**Formulas:**
- "We're not [X], we're [Y]."
- "For the first time, [audience] can [benefit]."
- "[Number] [people/companies] can't be wrong."
- "This changes everything for [audience]."

**Examples:**
- "We're not just another tool—we're the operating system for [industry]."
- "For the first time, small businesses can compete with enterprise."
- "10,000 customers can't be wrong."
- "This changes everything for remote teams."

### Storytelling Framework

**Structure:**
1. **Setup:** Introduce character/situation
2. **Conflict:** Describe problem/challenge
3. **Resolution:** Explain how it was solved
4. **Lesson:** Connect to broader message

**Example:**
> "Let me tell you about Sarah, a small business owner [setup]. She was spending 20 hours a week on manual bookkeeping [conflict]. With our product, she automated that in one afternoon [resolution]. Now she focuses on growing her business instead of data entry [lesson]."

## Section 7: Interview Formats

### One-on-One Interview

**Best Practices:**
- Build rapport early (small talk)
- Listen carefully to questions
- Answer concisely, then stop
- Offer follow-up availability
- Thank the journalist

### Panel Discussion

**Best Practices:**
- Don't dominate (share time)
- Acknowledge other panelists' points
- Bring conversation back to your messages
- Avoid jargon (general audience)
- Make eye contact with audience, not just moderator

### Press Conference

**Best Practices:**
- Prepare opening statement (2-3 minutes)
- Call on journalists you recognize
- Repeat questions for recording clarity
- Keep answers brief (more journalists = less time each)
- Have team log all questions asked

### Written Q&A

**Best Practices:**
- Request questions in advance
- Take time to craft thoughtful answers
- Keep answers concise (journalists may edit)
- Offer follow-up interview
- Review before publication if possible

## Section 8: Post-Interview

### Immediate Actions

- [ ] Send thank-you email
- [ ] Provide any promised follow-up information
- [ ] Log interview details (date, outlet, topics)
- [ ] Alert team that interview occurred
- [ ] Monitor for publication

### After Publication

- [ ] Review coverage for accuracy
- [ ] Share internally (Slack, email)
- [ ] Share on social media (tag journalist/outlet)
- [ ] Add to media coverage tracker
- [ ] Thank journalist for coverage
- [ ] Address any inaccuracies (politely, privately)

### Handling Inaccuracies

**Minor errors:** Consider letting go (picking battles)

**Significant errors:**
1. Contact journalist directly (not public)
2. Be specific about what's wrong
3. Provide correct information
4. Request correction politely
5. Escalate to editor only if necessary

**Example Email:**
> Hi [Name],
> 
> Thank you for covering [topic]. I appreciated our conversation.
> 
> I wanted to flag one item for accuracy: The article states [incorrect info]. The correct information is [accurate info].
> 
> Would you be able to update this? Happy to provide any additional context.
> 
> Thanks,
> [Your Name]

## Section 9: Media Training Exercises

### Exercise 1: Message Practice

**Instructions:**
1. Write 3 key messages
2. Record yourself delivering each in 30 seconds
3. Review: Were you clear? Concise? Convincing?
4. Repeat until natural

### Exercise 2: Bridging Practice

**Instructions:**
1. Partner asks difficult/off-topic questions
2. Practice bridging to key messages
3. Switch roles
4. Focus on smooth transitions

### Exercise 3: Mock Interview

**Instructions:**
1. Partner plays journalist (researches and prepares tough questions)
2. 15-minute recorded interview
3. Review together: What worked? What didn't?
4. Identify improvement areas

### Exercise 4: Soundbite Creation

**Instructions:**
1. Take complex topic
2. Distill to 15-word soundbite
3. Test on colleague: Do they remember it?
4. Refine until memorable

## Section 10: Spokesperson Guidelines

### Who Should Speak to Media

| Situation | Recommended Spokesperson |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| Strategic announcements | CEO |
| Product launches | CEO or Product Lead |
| Financial results | CEO or CFO |
| Technical topics | CTO or Product Lead |
| Crisis situations | CEO or designated spokesperson |
| Customer stories | Customer Success or CEO |
| Culture/HR topics | CEO or Head of HR |

### Spokesperson Responsibilities

- Complete media training before first interview
- Review and approve key messages
- Be available for time-sensitive interviews
- Represent company values consistently
- Escalate sensitive questions appropriately
- Debrief after interviews

### Spokesperson Don'ts

- ✗ Speak outside your expertise
- ✗ Speculate on future plans
- ✗ Comment on competitors negatively
- ✗ Discuss confidential information
- ✗ Make promises you can't keep
- ✗ Go off the record without approval
- ✗ Skip preparation

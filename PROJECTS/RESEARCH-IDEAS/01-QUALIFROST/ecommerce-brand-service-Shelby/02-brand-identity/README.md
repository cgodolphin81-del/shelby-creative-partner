## Brand Naming Framework

### The 5 Types of Brand Names

#### 1. Descriptive Names
Clearly describe what you do or sell.
- Examples: The Sleep Company, Protein World, Phone Case Co.
- Pros: Instant clarity, SEO-friendly
- Cons: Hard to trademark, less memorable

#### 2. Suggestive Names
Hint at benefits or experience without being literal.
- Examples: Netflix (internet + flicks), Shopify (shop + simplify)
- Pros: Memorable, allows brand storytelling
- Cons: Requires explanation initially

#### 3. Abstract/Coined Names
Made-up words with no prior meaning.
- Examples: Kodak, Xerox, Zulily
- Pros: Highly trademarkable, unique
- Cons: Requires significant marketing to build meaning

#### 4. Metaphorical Names
Use imagery or concepts related to your brand.
- Examples: Amazon (vast selection), Nike (goddess of victory)
- Pros: Rich storytelling potential, memorable
- Cons: May not be immediately clear

#### 5. Founder/Person Names
Named after a person (real or fictional).
- Examples: Tesla, Disney, Wendy's
- Pros: Personal connection, authentic
- Cons: Limits scalability, harder to sell company

### Name Generation Exercise

**Step 1: Word Storm (15 minutes)**
Write down 50+ words related to:
- Your product category
- Benefits/outcomes
- Emotions you want to evoke
- Target customer language
- Industry terminology
- Metaphors and imagery

**Step 2: Combination Play**
Mix and match words from your list:
- Compound words: Sleep + Number = SleepNumber
- Portmanteau: Instagram (instant + telegram)
- Add suffixes: -ly, -ify, -io, -ex, -ia

**Step 3: Filter Criteria**
Evaluate each name against:
- [ ] Easy to spell (radio test)
- [ ] Easy to pronounce
- [ ] .com domain available (or .co/.io acceptable)
- [ ] Social handles available
- [ ] No negative connotations in other languages
- [ ] Trademark available (uspto.gov)
- [ ] Memorable after one hearing
- [ ] Scales beyond initial product

**Step 4: Test Your Top 5**
- Ask 10 people to spell it after hearing it once
- Check if they remember it 24 hours later
- Ask what they think the company does
- Get gut reaction (positive/negative/neutral)

## Brand Story Development Worksheet

### The Hero's Journey Framework

**1. The Customer (Hero)**
Who is your customer? What do they want?
```
Our customer is [description] who wants to [goal/desire].
```

**2. The Problem (Villain)**
What's standing in their way? What pain do they feel?
```
But they're struggling with [problem] which causes [pain/frustration].
```

**3. The Guide (Your Brand)**
How do you position yourself as the guide, not the hero?
```
That's where we come in. We [what you do] to help them [outcome].
```

**4. The Plan**
What's your simple process or approach?
```
Here's how it works:
Step 1: _______________
Step 2: _______________
Step 3: _______________
```

**5. The Call to Action**
What do you want them to do?
```
[Clear, specific action]
```

**6. The Success Story**
What does success look like?
```
Imagine [positive outcome] without [old problem].
That's the life our customers live.
```

**7. The Failure Story**
What happens if they don't act?
```
Without [your solution], they'll continue to [negative outcome].
```

### Complete Brand Story Template

```
[Customer] wants to [goal], but [problem] keeps getting in the way.

They've tried [old solutions], but [why they failed].

We believe [customer] deserves [better outcome]. That's why we created 
[product/company] — [what it is] that [key benefit].

Unlike [competitors], we [differentiation].

Our customers go from [before state] to [after state].

[Social proof: "Since using X, I've Y" - Customer Name]

Ready to [achieve goal]? [Call to action]
```

### Brand Story Examples

**Example 1: Casper (Mattresses)**
```
Everyone deserves a good night's sleep. But traditional mattress shopping 
is terrible — pushy salespeople, confusing options, and no way to try 
before you buy.

We created Casper to change that. One perfect mattress, delivered to 
your door, with 100 nights to try it risk-free.

Our customers go from sleepless nights to waking up refreshed. Over 1 
million sleepers can't be wrong.

Ready for the best sleep of your life? Try Casper risk-free today.
```

**Example 2: Dollar Shave Club (Razors)**
```
Our blades are f***ing great.

Do you like spending $20 a month on brand-name razors? Nineteen go to 
Roger Federer. Your money goes to his swimmer's legs. The other dollar 
goes to the razor.

We're different. We send high-quality razors to your door for just $1 
a month. No commitment, no BS.

Stop paying for shaving BS. Start shaving time and money.
```

## Positioning Strategy Canvas

### Positioning Statement Template

```
For [target customer], who [customer need],
[Brand Name] is a [category] that [key benefit].
Unlike [competitors], we [differentiation].
```

**Example:**
```
For health-conscious professionals, who want convenient nutrition,
Huel is a meal replacement that provides complete nutrition in minutes.
Unlike protein shakes, we offer balanced meals with 26 essential 
nutrients.
```

### Positioning Map

Create a 2x2 matrix with axes relevant to your category:

**Example Axes:**
- Price: Low ← → High
- Quality: Basic ← → Premium
- Style: Classic ← → Modern
- Focus: General ← → Specialized

**Exercise:**
1. Draw a 2x2 grid
2. Plot 5-10 competitors
3. Identify white space (gaps)
4. Position your brand in an open quadrant

### Unique Value Proposition (UVP)

**UVP Formula:**
```
[Headline]: The big promise
[Subheadline]: How + for whom + what outcome
[Bullet points]: 2-3 specific benefits
[Visual]: Product/lifestyle image
```

**Example (Slack):**
```
Headline: Be more productive at work with less effort
Subheadline: Slack brings team communication together in one place
Bullets:
✓ Instant messaging organized by channels
✓ Searchable history so nothing gets lost
✓ Integrates with tools you already use
```

## Packaging Design Guidelines

### Packaging Hierarchy

**Level 1: Shipping Packaging**
- Protects product during transit
- Branded mailer or box
- Includes packing slip

**Level 2: Product Packaging**
- Houses the actual product
- Primary branding vehicle
- Shelf appeal (if retail)

**Level 3: Inner Packaging**
- Tissue paper, inserts, cards
- Unboxing experience
- Thank you notes, stickers

### Packaging Design Checklist

**Must-Haves:**
- [ ] Logo (prominent but not overwhelming)
- [ ] Product name
- [ ] Key benefit or tagline
- [ ] Product images or illustrations
- [ ] Net weight/quantity
- [ ] Ingredients/materials (if applicable)
- [ ] Usage instructions
- [ ] Barcode/UPC
- [ ] Country of origin
- [ ] Contact information/website

**Nice-to-Haves:**
- [ ] QR code (to website, instructions, video)
- [ ] Social media handles
- [ ] Hashtag for UGC
- [ ] Sustainability messaging
- [ ] Brand story snippet
- [ ] Referral code

### Unboxing Experience Design

**The 5 Moments of Unboxing:**

1. **Arrival** (seeing the package)
   - Branded exterior
   - Clean, undamaged
   - Easy to open

2. **Opening** (first reveal)
   - Satisfying open mechanism
   - Tissue paper or protective layer
   - First visual impact

3. **Discovery** (finding the product)
   - Product nestled securely
   - Welcome card on top
   - Organized layout

4. **Exploration** (examining contents)
   - Clear instructions
   - Additional touches (stickers, samples)
   - Quality materials

5. **Action** (what to do next)
   - Clear CTA (scan QR, visit site, follow)
   - Referral/review incentive
   - Social sharing prompt

### Packaging Cost Guidelines

| Order Quantity | Packaging % of Product Cost |
|----------------|----------------------------|
| 100-500 units | 15-25% |
| 500-1000 units | 10-15% |
| 1000-5000 units | 5-10% |
| 5000+ units | 3-7% |

**Budget Allocation:**
- Product packaging: 60%
- Shipping packaging: 25%
- Inserts/extras: 15%

### Sustainable Packaging Options

- Recycled cardboard/paper
- Biodegradable mailers
- Compostable packaging peanuts
- Minimal ink printing (soy-based)
- Reusable containers
- Right-sized packaging (no excess)

## Brand Voice and Tone Guide

### Voice Dimensions

Define your brand voice on these spectrums:

| Dimension | Your Position (1-10) |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Formal ← → Casual | |
| Serious ← → Playful | |
| Respectful ← → Irreverent | |
| Enthusiastic ← → Matter-of-fact | |
| Expert ← → Peer | |
| Luxurious ← → Accessible | |

- Witty, Bold, Unapologetic (Skims)

### Tone Variations by Context

Your voice stays consistent, but tone adapts:

| Context | Tone Adjustment | Example |
|---------|-----------------|---------|
| Marketing | More enthusiastic | "You're going to LOVE this!" |
| Support | More empathetic | "I completely understand your frustration" |
| Error messages | More helpful | "Oops! Here's how to fix this" |
| Social media | More casual | "Monday mood ☕" |
| Email newsletters | More personal | "Hey [Name], quick update..." |

### Writing Guidelines

**Do:**
- [ ] Use contractions (you're, we'll, don't)
- [ ] Write in active voice
- [ ] Keep sentences under 20 words
- [ ] Use "you" more than "we"
- [ ] Be specific, not vague
- [ ] Show, don't tell

**Don't:**
- [ ] Use jargon or industry speak
- [ ] Write in passive voice
- [ ] Use exclamation points excessively!!!
- [ ] Make promises you can't keep
- [ ] Sound like a robot or corporate memo

### Before/After Examples

**Before (Generic):**
```
Our product utilizes advanced technology to provide superior results 
for customers seeking optimal performance.
```

**After (On-Brand):**
```
Our tech works harder so you don't have to. Better results, less effort.
```

**Before (Corporate):**
```
We would like to inform you that your order has been dispatched.
```

**After (Human):**
```
Good news! Your order is on its way. 📦
```

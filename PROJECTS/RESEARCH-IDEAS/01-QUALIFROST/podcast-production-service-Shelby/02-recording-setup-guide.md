## 🎧 Microphone Types Explained

### Dynamic Microphones
**Best for:** Most podcasters, untreated rooms, multiple hosts
- Less sensitive to room noise
- More forgiving of poor acoustics
- Durable, no phantom power needed
- Examples: Shure SM7B, Rode PodMic, Samson Q2U

### Condenser Microphones
**Best for:** Treated studios, solo hosts, detailed vocals
- More sensitive, captures more detail
- Requires phantom power (48V)
- Picks up more room noise
- Examples: Audio-Technica AT2020, Rode NT1

### USB vs. XLR
| Factor | USB | XLR |
|--------|-----|-----|
| Ease of use | Plug & play | Needs interface |
| Quality | Good | Excellent |
| Flexibility | Limited | Full control |
| Upgradability | Replace whole mic | Upgrade interface separately |
| Multi-host | Limited options | Unlimited with right interface |

**Recommendation:** Start USB if budget-conscious. Go XLR if planning to grow.

## 🔊 Room Acoustics

### Understanding the Problem
Echo/reverb happens when sound bounces off hard surfaces. Your goal: absorb reflections, not eliminate all sound.

### Quick Wins (Under $100)
1. **Record in a closet** – Clothes are excellent sound absorbers
2. **Hang blankets** behind you and on walls
3. **Use pillows** around your recording space
4. **Record under a blanket fort** – Sounds silly, works surprisingly well
5. **Face into a corner** with pillows behind your monitor
6. **Close windows** and turn off HVAC/fans
7. **Record at quiet times** (early morning, late evening)

### Budget Treatment ($200-500)
| Product | Purpose | Approx. Cost |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| Auralex Studiofoam Wedges (24-pack) | Wall treatment | $80 |
| Moving blankets (4-6) | DIY panels | $60 |
| Carpet remnant or rug | Floor reflection | $50 |
| Heavy curtains | Window treatment | $80 |
| **Total** | | **~$270** |

### Professional Treatment ($1,000+)
- Bass traps in corners (low frequencies)
- Absorption panels at first reflection points
- Diffusion panels on back wall
- Cloud panel above recording position
- Consider hiring an acoustic consultant for best results

### DIY Acoustic Panels
**Materials per panel:**
- Rockwool or Owens Corning 703 insulation (2'x4'x2") – $15
- 2'x4' frame (1x3 lumber) – $10
- Fabric (breathable, like burlap) – $15
- Staple gun – $20 (one-time)

**Total per panel:** ~$40 vs. $100+ for commercial panels

**Placement:**
- First reflection points (side walls)
- Behind microphone
- Behind listener/speaker
- Corners (bass traps)

## 🎛️ Recording Settings

### Sample Rate & Bit Depth
- **Sample Rate:** 44.1kHz or 48kHz (48kHz preferred for video)
- **Bit Depth:** 24-bit (more headroom, better quality)
- **File Format:** WAV or AIFF (never MP3 for recording)

### Gain Staging
1. Set mic gain so loudest peaks hit -12dB to -6dB
2. Never hit 0dB (digital clipping is unrecoverable)
3. Record a test and listen with headphones
4. Leave headroom – you can always amplify later

### Recording Checklist
- [ ] Sample rate: 48kHz
- [ ] Bit depth: 24-bit
- [ ] Format: WAV
- [ ] Record room tone (10 seconds of silence)
- [ ] Record test clip, verify levels
- [ ] Backup recording method ready
- [ ] Phone on Do Not Disturb
- [ ] Notifications silenced on computer

## 📐 Mic Technique

### Positioning
- **Distance:** 4-6 inches from mouth (fist-width is a good guide)
- **Angle:** Slightly off-axis (point at mouth, not directly into)
- **Height:** At or slightly above mouth level, angled down

### Pop Filter Use
- Position 2-3 inches from microphone
- Place between mouth and mic
- Essential for plosives (P, B, T sounds)

### Common Mistakes
- ❌ Too close (proximity effect, boomy bass)
- ❌ Too far (room noise, thin sound)
- ❌ Speaking across the mic (inconsistent levels)
- ❌ Moving around while recording
- ❌ Rustling papers, clicking pens

## 🔌 Connection & Monitoring

### Interface Setup
1. Install drivers (if required)
2. Connect mic via XLR
3. Enable phantom power if needed (condenser mics)
4. Set gain appropriately
5. Select interface as input in recording software

### Headphone Monitoring
- Use closed-back headphones to prevent bleed
- Monitor your voice while recording
- Check for clipping, background noise
- Adjust levels in real-time

### Latency Issues
- Reduce buffer size in audio interface settings
- Close other applications
- Use direct monitoring if available
- Consider ASIO drivers (Windows)

## 🚨 Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|--------------|----------|
| Hissing noise | Gain too high | Lower gain, move closer to mic |
| Hum/buzz | Ground loop, electrical interference | Use different outlet, check cables |
| Plosives (P, B pops) | No pop filter, too close | Add pop filter, increase distance |
| Room echo | Untreated space | Add absorption, record in smaller space |
| Inconsistent levels | Moving around, poor technique | Stay consistent distance, use compression later |
| One side quieter | Cable issue, mono/stereo mismatch | Check cables, ensure mono recording |
| Clipping/distortion | Levels too hot | Reduce gain, aim for -12dB peaks |

## ✅ Pre-Recording Sound Check

1. [ ] Record 10 seconds of room tone
2. [ ] Record yourself speaking at normal volume
3. [ ] Record yourself speaking loudly (simulate excitement)
4. [ ] Listen back with good headphones
5. [ ] Check for: background noise, clipping, plosives, echo
6. [ ] Adjust gain/positioning as needed
7. [ ] Record another test
8. [ ] When it sounds good – start the real recording

## When to Scale

### Signs You're Ready

✅ **Consistent sales:**
- Selling out regularly
- Waitlist forming
- Repeat customers

✅ **Systems in place:**
- Documented processes
- Reliable suppliers
- Consistent quality

✅ **Financial stability:**
- Profitable for 6+ months
- Emergency fund
- Can reinvest in growth

✅ **Capacity:**
- Working at or near capacity
- Turning down opportunities
- Quality suffering from volume

✅ **Mental readiness:**
- Want to grow
- Can handle more responsibility
- Willing to invest time/money

### Signs to Wait

⚠️ **Inconsistent sales:**
- Some months great, some months nothing
- Haven't found product-market fit

⚠️ **Quality issues:**
- Can't maintain quality at current volume
- Customer complaints increasing

⚠️ **Burnout:**
- Dreading the work
- No work-life balance
- Health suffering

⚠️ **Cash flow problems:**
- Can't afford to buy materials for large orders
- Behind on bills

⚠️ **Unclear systems:**
- Everything in your head
- Can't explain your process
- Quality varies

**Rule of thumb:** Master your current level before scaling. Don't scale to fix problems—scale when things are working.

## Scaling Mindset

### From Maker to Business Owner

**Maker mindset:**
- Focus on creating
- Do everything yourself
- Trade time for money
- Perfectionist
- Say yes to everything

**Business owner mindset:**
- Focus on strategy
- Delegate and systematize
- Build systems that work without you
- Good enough is good enough
- Say no to opportunities that don't fit

### Growth Requires Change

**What changes as you scale:**
- You'll spend less time making
- You'll spend more time managing
- Decisions become more complex
- More people depend on you
- Stakes are higher

**What stays the same:**
- Your values
- Your quality standards
- Your connection to why you started

### Common Scaling Challenges

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Not enough time | Delegate, automate, say no |
| Cash flow | Plan ahead, negotiate terms, get deposit |
| Quality control | Document processes, train team, inspect |
| Burnout | Set boundaries, hire help, take breaks |
| Decision fatigue | Create frameworks, delegate decisions |
| Losing the joy | Remember your why, protect creative time |

## Wholesale

### What Is Wholesale?

Selling your products to retailers at a discount. They mark up and sell to customers.

**Typical terms:**
- 50% off retail price
- Minimum order quantity (MOQ)
- Net 30 payment terms (pay within 30 days)
- You ship to retailer

### Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- ✅ Large orders (10-100x typical retail order)
- ✅ Predictable income
- ✅ Less marketing (retailer sells for you)
- ✅ Brand exposure
- ✅ Less customer service

**Cons:**
- ❌ Lower profit margin
- ❌ Less control over customer experience
- ❌ Payment delays (net 30-60)
- ❌ More complex logistics
- ❌ Can conflict with your direct sales

### Is Wholesale Right for You?

**Good fit if:**
- Your products have 70%+ margin at retail
- You can produce at scale
- You have systems for consistency
- You're okay with lower margins for volume
- You want predictable orders

**Not a good fit if:**
- Your margins are already thin
- You love direct customer interaction
- You can't produce consistent quality at volume
- You need immediate payment

### Wholesale Pricing

**Formula:**
```
Retail Price ÷ 2 = Wholesale Price

Wholesale price must cover:
- Materials
- Labor (may be less per unit at scale)
- Overhead
- SOME profit (can be lower than retail)
```

**Example:**
```
Retail: $40
Wholesale: $20

At wholesale:
- Materials: $8
- Labor (bulk): $5
- Overhead: $2
- Cost: $15
- Profit: $5 per unit (25% margin)

Sell 100 units:
- Revenue: $2,000
- Profit: $500

vs. Retail (selling 50 units at $40):
- Revenue: $2,000
- Profit: $1,000+ (but more work)
```

### Finding Wholesale Buyers

**1. Research retailers:**
- Look for shops that fit your brand
- Check if they carry similar products
- Visit in person if local
- Study their online presence

**2. Create a line sheet:**
- Product photos
- Wholesale prices
- Minimums
- Terms
- Contact info
- Order form

**3. Reach out:**
- Email or call
- Personalized message
- Include line sheet
- Offer samples (for serious buyers)

**4. Trade shows:**
- Apply to wholesale trade shows
- Expensive but high exposure
- Examples: NY Now, Atlanta Gift Show, Renegade

**5. Online wholesale marketplaces:**
- Faire (largest, 55% of independent retailers)
- Tundra
- Abound
- Handshake (Shopify's marketplace)

### Wholesale Terms to Set

**Payment:**
- 50% deposit, balance on shipment (safest)
- Net 30 (pay within 30 days)
- Net 60 (common but risky for small businesses)
- Credit card on file

**Minimums:**
- Initial order: $200-500 minimum
- Reorder: $100-200 minimum
- Per item: 3-6 units per SKU

**Shipping:**
- Who pays? (usually buyer)
- Shipping method
- Damage policy

**Exclusivity:**
- Will you sell exclusively in their area?
- If yes, charge more
- Get it in writing

### Wholesale Agreement

**Key terms:**
- Pricing
- Payment terms
- Minimums
- Shipping
- Returns/damages
- Exclusivity (if any)
- Termination

**Have a lawyer review your first agreement!**

### Managing Wholesale

**Systems you need:**
- Wholesale price list (separate from retail)
- Order form
- Invoice system
- Production tracking
- Shipping process
- Payment tracking

**Communication:**
- Regular check-ins
- New product announcements
- Reorder reminders
- Seasonal collections

## Craft Fairs & Markets

### Types of Events

| Type | Cost | Audience | Best For |
|------|------|----------|----------|
| **Local markets** | $50-200 | Community | Testing, local customers |
| **Art/craft fairs** | $200-800 | Art buyers | Higher-end products |
| **Holiday markets** | $300-1000 | Gift shoppers | Q4 sales boost |
| **Festivals** | $500-2000 | Large crowds | High volume, exposure |
| **Trade shows** | $1000-5000+ | Buyers | Wholesale connections |

### Is It Worth It?

**Calculate potential:**
```
Booth fee: $500
Expected sales: $3,000
Cost of goods (30%): $900
Travel/lodging: $300
Booth supplies: $200
Total costs: $1,900
Profit: $1,100

Hourly rate (if 20 hours total): $55/hour
```

**Also consider:**
- Brand exposure
- Email list building
- Customer relationships
- Market research
- Content for social media

### Application Process

**Most events require:**
- Application form
- Product photos
- Booth photos (from previous events)
- Jury fee ($20-50)
- Description of your work

**Tips:**
- Apply early (popular events fill up)
- High-quality photos are crucial
- Show cohesive product line
- Highlight what makes you unique

### Booth Setup

**Essential supplies:**
- Tent (10x10, weighted!)
- Table(s) and tablecloths
- Display fixtures (shelves, risers)
- Signage (banner, prices)
- Lighting (battery-powered if no electricity)
- Cash box / card reader
- Bags and packaging
- Business cards

**Display tips:**
- Create height variation
- Group products by category/color
- Make it easy to browse
- Have a clear focal point
- Keep it tidy throughout event

### Pricing at Events

**Options:**
- Same as online (consistent brand)
- Slightly higher (covers event costs)
- Slightly lower (incentivize impulse buys)
- Bundle deals (buy 3, get 10% off)

**Recommendation:** Same pricing, offer event-exclusive bundles or items

### Payment Processing

**Options:**
- Square (2.6% + 10¢ per transaction)
- PayPal Here (2.7%)
- Stripe Terminal (2.7%)
- Cash (no fees, but security risk)

**Tips:**
- Accept both card and cash
- Have change for cash
- Test your reader before event
- Bring backup (phone hotspot, extra battery)

### Maximizing Sales

**Before:**
- Promote on social media
- Email your list
- Create event-specific products
- Plan your booth layout

**During:**
- Be engaging (not pushy)
- Tell your story
- Offer to hold items (for later pickup)
- Collect emails (raffle for free item)
- Take breaks (don't burn out)

**After:**
- Follow up with email list
- Post event photos
- Thank customers on social media
- Review what worked/didn't

### Event Checklist

**4-8 weeks before:**
- [ ] Apply to events
- [ ] Book travel/lodging
- [ ] Plan inventory
- [ ] Order supplies

**2 weeks before:**
- [ ] Produce inventory
- [ ] Create signage
- [ ] Plan booth layout
- [ ] Promote on social media

**1 week before:**
- [ ] Confirm details with organizer
- [ ] Pack supplies
- [ ] Test card reader
- [ ] Print receipts/invoices

**Day before:**
- [ ] Pack car
- [ ] Charge devices
- [ ] Get cash for change
- [ ] Rest!

## Subscription Boxes

### What Is a Subscription Model?

Customers pay monthly/quarterly for regular deliveries of your products.

**Examples:**
- Monthly candle subscription
- Quarterly jewelry box
- Seasonal craft kit

### Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- ✅ Predictable recurring revenue
- ✅ Better cash flow planning
- ✅ Customer loyalty
- ✅ Less marketing (retain vs. acquire)
- ✅ Can test new products

**Cons:**
- ❌ Must deliver consistently (every month!)
- ❌ Churn (customers cancel)
- ❌ More complex logistics
- ❌ Need variety (can't send same thing)
- ❌ Higher customer service

### Is It Right for You?

**Good fit if:**
- You have multiple products
- You can produce consistently
- Your products are consumable (used up)
- You enjoy variety and creativity
- You have systems for recurring billing/shipping

**Not a good fit if:**
- You have one product
- Your products are one-time purchases
- You struggle with consistency
- You don't want recurring obligations

### Pricing Subscriptions

**Formula:**
```
Subscription price should be:
- Less than buying items individually (incentive)
- Still profitable after all costs
- Sustainable long-term

Example:
Individual items: $30 + $30 + $30 = $90
Subscription: $75/month (17% discount)
Your cost: $45
Profit: $30/month per subscriber

With 100 subscribers:
Revenue: $7,500/month
Profit: $3,000/month
```

### Types of Subscriptions

**1. Replenishment:**
- Same product regularly (candles, soap)
- Simple to fulfill
- Lower churn

**2. Curation:**
- Different products each month
- More exciting
- More work, higher churn

**3. Hybrid:**
- Core product + surprise item
- Best of both worlds

### Platform Options

| Platform | Cost | Best For |
|----------|------|----------|
| **Cratejoy** | $39-79/month + transaction fee | Dedicated subscription platform |
| **Subbly** | $29-79/month | Subscription-focused |
| **Shopify + app** | $29/month + app fee | If already on Shopify |
| **Patreon** | 5-12% of revenue | Creator-focused, digital + physical |
| **Manual** | Free (just your time) | Starting out, small scale |

### Launching a Subscription

**Steps:**
1. Define your offering (what, how often, price)
2. Calculate costs and margins
3. Choose platform
4. Create marketing materials
5. Launch to existing customers first
6. Promote on social media
7. Deliver exceptional first box
8. Request feedback
9. Iterate and improve

### Retaining Subscribers

**Key metrics:**
- Churn rate (aim for <10% monthly)
- Customer lifetime value
- Net Promoter Score (satisfaction)

**Strategies:**
- Consistent quality
- Variety and surprise
- Excellent communication
- Member perks (early access, discounts)
- Ask for feedback
- Make it easy to skip/pause (reduces cancellations)

## Hiring Help

### When to Hire

**Signs it's time:**
- Consistently working 50+ hours/week
- Turning down orders
- Quality suffering
- No time for strategy/growth
- Burnout approaching
- Can afford it!

### Types of Help

**1. Virtual Assistant (VA):**
- **Cost:** $15-40/hour
- **Tasks:** Customer service, email, scheduling, data entry
- **Where:** Upwork, Fiverr, VA agencies
- **Start with:** 5-10 hours/week

**2. Production Helper:**
- **Cost:** $15-25/hour (or per piece)
- **Tasks:** Assembly, packaging, basic production
- **Where:** Local job boards, Indeed, referrals
- **Start with:** Part-time, as needed

**3. Freelance Specialists:**
- **Cost:** Varies ($50-200/hour or project-based)
- **Tasks:** Photography, design, marketing, website
- **Where:** Upwork, Fiverr, referrals
- **Start with:** One-off projects

**4. Full-Time Employee:**
- **Cost:** Salary + benefits (30-40% on top of salary)
- **Tasks:** Dedicated role (production, marketing, ops)
- **When:** Consistent need, can afford full-time
- **Consider:** LLC/corp structure, payroll, legal requirements

### What to Delegate First

**Delegate these (low risk, high time savings):**
- Packaging and shipping
- Customer service emails
- Social media scheduling
- Data entry
- Basic production tasks

**Keep these (core to your business):**
- Product design
- Quality control
- Strategy and decisions
- Key relationships
- Brand voice

### Hiring Process

**1. Define the role:**
- What tasks?
- How many hours?
- What skills needed?
- What's the budget?

**2. Write job description:**
- Clear responsibilities
- Required skills
- Nice-to-have skills
- Compensation
- How to apply

**3. Screen candidates:**
- Review applications
- Shortlist 3-5
- Interview (video call)
- Check references

**4. Test project:**
- Small paid project first
- Evaluate quality and communication
- Decide if good fit

**5. Onboard:**
- Document processes
- Train on your systems
- Set expectations
- Regular check-ins

### Managing Remote Team

**Tools:**
- Slack or similar (communication)
- Trello, Asana, or ClickUp (project management)
- Google Drive (file sharing)
- Loom (video tutorials)
- Zoom (meetings)

**Best practices:**
- Clear communication
- Document everything
- Regular check-ins
- Set expectations upfront
- Pay fairly and on time
- Give feedback (positive and constructive)

## Product Line Expansion

### When to Expand

**Good reasons:**
- Customers asking for it
- Natural extension of current line
- You're bored/need creative outlet
- Seasonal opportunities
- Using same materials efficiently

**Bad reasons:**
- Bored with current products
- Saw someone else doing it
- Think it'll automatically sell more
- Haven't mastered current line

### Types of Expansion

**1. Line extensions:**
- New sizes of existing products
- New colors/scents
- Variations on bestsellers

**2. Complementary products:**
- Candles → candle accessories
- Jewelry → jewelry care
- Prints → frames, cards

**3. New categories:**
- Candles → home fragrance (diffusers, sprays)
- Jewelry → accessories (bags, scarves)
- Prints → home decor

**4. Seasonal collections:**
- Holiday-specific items
- Seasonal colors/scents
- Limited editions

### Testing New Products

**Before full launch:**
1. Make small batch
2. Test with existing customers
3. Get feedback
4. Iterate based on feedback
5. Soft launch (limited availability)
6. Full launch if successful

**Questions to answer:**
- Do customers want this?
- Can I make it profitably?
- Does it fit my brand?
- Can I source materials reliably?
- Is the quality consistent?

### Phasing Out Products

**When to discontinue:**
- Consistently low sales
- Low margins
- Supply issues
- No longer fits brand
- Too time-consuming

**How to phase out:**
- Announce to customers
- Run final sale
- Don't reorder materials
- Focus on replacements

## Funding Growth

### Bootstrapping

**Using your own profits to fund growth**

**Pros:**
- ✅ No debt
- ✅ Full control
- ✅ Learn to be profitable
- ✅ No investors to answer to

**Cons:**
- ❌ Slower growth
- ❌ Limited by cash flow
- ❌ May miss opportunities

**Best for:** Most craft businesses

### Business Credit Cards

**Pros:**
- ✅ Separate business expenses
- ✅ Build business credit
- ✅ Rewards/cash back
- ✅ Float for cash flow

**Cons:**
- ❌ High interest if not paid monthly
- ❌ Personal guarantee usually required
- ❌ Can lead to overspending

**Tips:**
- Pay in full every month
- Use for planned expenses only
- Take advantage of rewards
- Don't max out (hurts credit)

### Small Business Loans

**Options:**
- SBA loans (government-backed)
- Bank loans
- Online lenders (Kabbage, OnDeck)

**Pros:**
- ✅ Larger amounts
- ✅ Lower interest than credit cards
- ✅ Build business credit

**Cons:**
- ❌ Debt to repay
- ❌ Qualification requirements
- ❌ Time to apply
- ❌ May require personal guarantee

**When to consider:**
- Proven business model
- Clear use of funds
- Can afford payments
- Growth opportunity requires capital

### Grants

**Sources:**
- Government grants (SBA, local)
- Private foundations
- Corporate grants
- Industry-specific grants

**Pros:**
- ✅ Free money (no repayment)
- ✅ Validation
- ✅ Sometimes includes support

**Cons:**
- ❌ Competitive
- ❌ Time-consuming to apply
- ❌ Restrictions on use
- ❌ Reporting requirements

**Where to look:**
- Grants.gov
- Local economic development
- Craft Industry Alliance
- Women in business grants

### Investors

**For craft businesses:** Rarely needed or advisable

**Consider only if:**
- Scalable business model
- High growth potential
- Willing to give up equity
- Ready for rapid scaling

**Types:**
- Angel investors
- Venture capital (rarely for craft)
- Friends and family

## Knowing Your Limits

### Sustainable Growth

**Growth should not come at the cost of:**
- Your health
- Your relationships
- Your values
- Product quality
- Your joy in the work

### Signs of Unsustainable Growth

⚠️ Working 70+ hours/week consistently
⚠️ Dreading the work
⚠️ Quality declining
⚠️ Customer complaints increasing
⚠️ No time for strategy
⚠️ Health issues
⚠️ Relationships suffering
⚠️ Can't keep up with demand

### It's OK to Stay Small

**"Small" can mean:**
- Profitable
- Flexible
- Fulfilling
- Good income
- Work-life balance

**You don't have to:**
- Be on every platform
- Attend every market
- Launch every season
- Hire a team
- Get into major retailers

### Defining Your Success

**Ask yourself:**
- What income do I need/want?
- How many hours do I want to work?
- What kind of impact do I want to have?
- What does work-life balance look like for me?
- What would make me proud?

**Your answers may change over time. That's OK.**

### When to Pause or Pivot

**Pause if:**
- Burned out
- Life circumstances change
- Need to reassess
- Market conditions shift

**Pivot if:**
- Product isn't selling
- Found a better opportunity
- Passion has shifted
- Market has changed

**It's not failure—it's adaptation.**

## LINKEDIN POST

**Headline:** What a 900-Year-Old Temple Taught Me About Leadership and Impermanence

**Post:**

At Bayon temple in Cambodia, 200 stone faces watch from 54 towers. Each face turned to the four cardinal directions. Each face smiling—not with happiness, but with knowing.

These faces represent either Avalokiteshvara, the Bodhisattva of compassion, or King Jayavarman VII, who built this temple in the late 12th century as a statement of enlightened rule.

Perhaps both.

King Jayavarman VII saw leadership as an act of compassion. He built hospitals. Rest houses for travelers. Temples dedicated not to power, but to service. The faces of Bayon reflect that vision: compassionate authority. Watchful presence. Leadership that serves rather than dominates.

Eight centuries later, the Khmer Empire is gone. The jungle has reclaimed much of what was built. But the faces remain. And their message endures.

In Episode 067 of The Infinite Walk, I explore what these ancient stones teach us about:

→ The nature of impermanence
→ Leadership as service
→ Beauty as something we steward, not own
→ The courage to build, knowing it will not last

"We are all temporary custodians of beauty. We build our temples. We carve our faces in stone. We plant our trees. And then we step aside, and let the world continue without us."

This is not tragedy. It is truth. And there is profound freedom in accepting it.

Listen to "The Lost Civilization: Angkor Wat Return" wherever you find podcasts.

What ancient places have shifted your perspective on leadership, time, or what it means to build something that matters?

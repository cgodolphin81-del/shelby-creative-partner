# Episode 067: The Lost Civilization - Angkor Wat Return
## A Poetic Essay

---

### Returning to Roots

I have walked this path before.

Not these exact stones—worn smooth by eight centuries of pilgrim feet, by monsoon rains, by the slow breath of the jungle—but the path of return itself. There is something ancient in the human heart that pulls us back to places we have never truly left, even when we have never been at all.

Angkor waits.

The sun has not yet risen. The air is cool, thick with the scent of wet earth and frangipani blossoms falling in the dark. Somewhere, a temple bell rings once, twice, then silence. I stand at the western causeway, watching the silhouette of five lotus-bud towers emerge from the night like the spine of some great stone creature waking from its long sleep.

This is where I begin again.

---

### What the Jungle Holds

They say the jungle forgets nothing.

I think the opposite is true. The jungle remembers everything, and in its remembering, it reclaims. Roots thick as ancient serpents coil around doorways that once opened to chanting monks. Strangler figs—*Ficus gibbosa*, the botanists call them—send their tendrils down through collapsed galleries, embracing stone the way a lover embraces a returning traveler: with a grip that will not let go.

Ta Prohm does not resist. The temple does not fight the trees. There is no battle here, only a slow, patient merging. The Khmer builders understood something we have forgotten: that all things return to the earth. They carved their gods in sandstone and laterite, built towers that touched the sky, and then—when the empire fell, when the capital moved, when the people left—they allowed the forest to take what was always borrowed.

I walk through a doorway framed by roots. The tree has become the architecture. Stone and wood have forgotten which came first. In this forgetting, there is a kind of peace.

What does the jungle hold? It holds time. Not the time of clocks and calendars, but the deep, green time of growth and decay, of life feeding on death feeding on life. It holds the memory of every footstep, every prayer, every hand that traced the apsara carvings and wondered what heaven must look like.

The jungle holds the empire, and in holding it, sets it free.

---

### Time and Stone

At Bayon, the faces watch.

Two hundred stone faces, carved on fifty-four towers, each one turned to the four cardinal directions. They smile—not the smile of happiness, but the smile of knowing. Some say they are Avalokiteshvara, the Bodhisattva of compassion, hearing the cries of the world. Others say they are King Jayavarman VII himself, his face multiplied across the temple, watching over his capital, his people, his vision of enlightened rule.

Perhaps they are both. Perhaps they are neither. Perhaps they are simply stone, and we project our longing onto them because we need someone to witness us.

I stand beneath one of the towers and look up. The face looks back. There is no judgment in those closed eyes, no demand. Only presence. The kind of presence that says: *I have seen empires rise and fall. I have seen the jungle take what was built. I have seen pilgrims come and go, generation after generation, each one certain they are the first to feel wonder.*

*You are not the first. You will not be the last. But you are here now, and that matters.*

The bas-reliefs tell stories in stone: naval battles between Khmer and Cham forces, merchants haggling in markets, women cooking over fires, men preparing for war, children playing, monks meditating, a king processing through his capital. All of it frozen in sandstone, all of it saying: *We were here. We lived. We loved. We feared. We built. We lost.*

Time does not erase. Time transforms.

---

### The Walk Comes Home

Sunrise at Angkor Wat is a pilgrimage within a pilgrimage.

Hundreds of us gather at the reflection pool, silhouettes against the dark water. We are quiet. There is something about waiting for the sun that makes us reverent, as if we are midwives to the birth of light.

The sky begins to change. Purple to pink to orange. The towers, black against the dawn, slowly take on color—grey, then gold, then the warm honey-stone of the living temple. And then, for a moment that feels both infinite and impossibly brief, the reflection is perfect. Five towers in the water, five towers in the sky, and the sun rising between them like a third temple made of fire.

Someone sighs. A camera shutter clicks. A monk in saffron robes walks past, bare feet silent on the stone.

This is why I return.

Not for the photographs. Not for the stories to tell. But for the remembering. The remembering that I am small, and that is okay. The remembering that civilizations fall, and that is okay. The remembering that the jungle will take us all, eventually, and that is not tragedy—it is homecoming.

I have walked across deserts and mountains, through cities and silence, searching for something I could not name. And here, in the ruins of a lost civilization, I find it: the knowledge that we are all temporary custodians of beauty. We build our temples. We carve our faces in stone. We plant our trees. And then we step aside, and let the world continue without us.

The walk does not end. It circles back. It comes home.

I turn from the reflection pool and walk toward the temple. The gates are open. Inside, the apsaras wait—over fifteen hundred celestial dancers carved in stone, each one unique, each one smiling her own secret smile. They have been dancing for nine hundred years. They will dance for nine hundred more.

I touch the wall. The stone is warm now, alive with the morning sun.

"Thank you," I whisper.

For what, I do not know. For the beauty. For the impermanence. For the reminder that I, too, am passing through. For the knowledge that something in me will always return to this place, where the jungle holds the stone, where time is written in every crack and root, where the walk comes home.

I walk on.

The temple receives me.

The jungle watches.

The faces smile.

And the sun rises, as it has risen for eight hundred years, on a world that keeps turning, keeps changing, keeps calling us back to the places where we remember who we are.

## 📜 HISTORY — THE INCA CITADEL

**Built:** Mid-15th century (circa 1450 CE)  
**Builder:** Pachacutec Inca Yupanqui (9th Inca ruler)  
**Purpose:** Royal estate, sacred center, astronomical observatory  
**Abandoned:** Circa 1572 (during Spanish Conquest)  
**Rediscovered:** July 24, 1911 by Hiram Bingham (Yale historian)

**Key Historical Facts:**
- Never found by Spanish conquistadors — remained hidden for 400+ years
- Part of the vast Inca road system (Qhapaq Ñan)
- Home to approximately 750-1,200 people at its peak
- Sophisticated dry-stone construction — no mortar used
- Aligned with celestial events (solstices, equinoxes)
- Declared UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983
- Named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World (2007)

**The Inca Civilization:**
- Empire (Tahuantinsuyo) stretched from Ecuador to Chile at its height
- No written language — used quipu (knotted strings) for records
- Master engineers: terraces, aqueducts, roads without wheels or iron tools
- Sun worship (Inti) central to religion
- Emperor considered divine, son of the Sun

## 🚂 ACCESS ROUTES

### Route 1: Train + Bus (Most Common)
1. Fly to Lima, Peru
2. Domestic flight: Lima → Cusco (1.5 hrs, $120–250)
3. Transfer: Cusco → Ollantaytambo (1.5 hrs by van, $11/person)
4. Train: Ollantaytambo → Aguas Calientes (1.5–2 hrs)
   - Expedition: $140–190 round-trip
   - Vistadome: $180–220 round-trip
   - Hiram Bingham (luxury): $950+ one-way
5. Bus: Aguas Calientes → Machu Picchu entrance (25 min, $12 one-way / $24 round-trip)
   - OR hike up (1.5–2 hrs, steep, free)

### Route 2: Inca Trail Trek (4 Days)
- 42 km (26 miles) through Andes
- Reaches Machu Picchu via Sun Gate (Inti Punku)
- Requires licensed tour operator (independent hiking not allowed)
- Cost: $600–1,500 per person (includes permits, guides, porters, food, equipment)
- Difficulty: Moderate to challenging
- Highest point: Dead Woman's Pass (4,215m / 13,828ft)
- Closed: Every February for maintenance

### Route 3: Alternative Treks
- **Salkantay Trek:** 5 days, $400–800, stunning mountain views
- **Lares Trek:** 4 days, cultural immersion with Andean communities
- **Inca Jungle:** 4 days, includes biking and ziplining
- **Choquequirao:** 9 days, remote "sister city" of Machu Picchu

### Route 4: Budget (Hydroelectric)
- Train to Hydroelectric station
- Walk 2–3 hours along tracks to Aguas Calientes
- Cheapest option, ~ $25 total

## 🌤️ TIMING & WEATHER

### Best Time to Visit
**Dry Season (April–October):**
- Clear skies, best visibility
- Cooler temperatures
- Peak crowds (especially June–August)
- Inti Raymi festival: June 24

**Rainy Season (November–March):**
- Lush green landscapes
- Fewer tourists
- Afternoon showers common
- Inca Trail CLOSED in February
- Mist and clouds add mystique (great for photography)

### Daily Timing
- **Morning shift:** 6:00 AM – 12:00 PM (best light, most crowded)
- **Afternoon shift:** 12:00 PM – 5:30 PM (fewer crowds, softer light)
- **Sunrise:** ~5:30 AM (requires early bus or staying in Aguas Calientes)
- **Average visit duration:** 3–4 hours

### Temperature
- Daytime: 15–25°C (59–77°F)
- Nighttime: 5–10°C (41–50°F)
- Rainy season: Warmer but wetter

- Passport (REQUIRED — name must match ticket)

**Optional:**
- Trekking poles (rubber tips required)

- Snacks (eat before entering — no re-entry)
- Altitude medication (consult doctor)
- Coca leaves or tea (helps with altitude)

## ⚠️ RULES & RESTRICTIONS (2026)

**Mandatory:**
- Licensed guide required (max 16 people per guide)
- Stay on designated paths
- Follow assigned circuit only
- Arrive 30 minutes before entry time

**Prohibited:**
- Tripods, selfie sticks, gimbals (without permit)
- Drones (without Ministry authorization)
- Large backpacks (>12 liters)
- Food inside (eat before entering)
- Single-use plastics
- Touching or leaning on structures
- Running, playing, loud behavior
- Alcohol, smoking, open flames
- High heels or dress shoes

**Consequences:**
- Violators may be removed without refund
- Re-entry not allowed (except special permission)
- Late arrival may require new ticket

## 🏥 HEALTH & SAFETY

**Altitude:**
- Cusco: 3,400m (11,150ft)
- Machu Picchu: 2,453m (8,048ft) — lower than Cusco
- Acclimatize 2–3 days in Cusco before trekking
- Symptoms of altitude sickness: headache, nausea, fatigue, shortness of breath
- Prevention: hydrate, avoid alcohol, eat light, coca tea, rest

**Medical:**
- Travel insurance recommended
- Vaccinations: Hepatitis A, Typhoid (consult doctor)
- Yellow fever certificate may be required
- First aid available at entrance
- Emergency evacuation possible by helicopter

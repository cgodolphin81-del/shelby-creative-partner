## When to Visit

### Wet Season (November - April)
**Best for:** Mirror effect, warmer nights, fewer crowds (except holidays)

**Pros:**
- ✅ World-famous mirror reflection (Dec-Mar peak)
- ✅ Warmer nighttime temperatures (0°C to -5°C / 32-23°F)
- ✅ Flamingos in breeding season at nearby lagoons
- ✅ Lower prices (low tourist season)
- ✅ Surreal "walking on sky" experience

**Cons:**
- ❌ Incahuasi Island often inaccessible (too deep)
- ❌ Some areas may flood, limiting routes
- ❌ Afternoon thunderstorms common
- ❌ Wet feet (water seeps into boots)
- ❌ Tours may be shortened or rerouted
- ❌ Driving slower due to corrosive salt water

**Best months:** January, February, March (peak mirror)

---

### Dry Season (May - October)
**Best for:** Island access, perspective photos, stargazing, clear skies

**Pros:**
- ✅ Full access to Incahuasi Island and all areas
- ✅ Perfect hexagonal salt patterns
- ✅ Clear blue skies, minimal rain
- ✅ Excellent stargazing (no clouds)
- ✅ Better for perspective/optical illusion photos
- ✅ More tour options and routes

**Cons:**
- ❌ Brutally cold nights (-15°C to -20°C / 5 to -4°F in Jun-Aug)
- ❌ No mirror effect (dry salt)
- ❌ Can be windy with dust
- ❌ Higher prices (peak tourist season)
- ❌ More crowds (especially July-August)

**Best months:** June, July, August, September (clearest skies)

---

### Shoulder Season (April-May, November-December)
**Best for:** Gamble for both mirror AND island access

**Pros:**
- ✅ Potential for partial mirror effects
- ✅ Possible island access
- ✅ Fewer crowds
- ✅ Moderate temperatures
- ✅ Good value

**Cons:**
- ❌ Unpredictable conditions
- ❌ May get neither mirror nor full access
- ❌ Weather-dependent

**Best months:** Late April, early May, early December

## Getting There

### Route 1: La Paz → Uyuni (Most Common)

#### By Flight (Fastest)
- **Duration:** 1 hour
- **Airlines:** Boliviana de Aviación (BoA), Amaszonas
- **Frequency:** 2-4 flights daily
- **Cost:** $80-150 USD one-way
- **Airport:** El Alto International (LPB) → Juana Azurduy de Padilla (UYU)
- **Notes:** 
  - Flights often delayed/cancelled due to weather
  - Book flexible tickets if possible
  - Arrive in La Paz 1-2 days early to acclimatize

#### By Bus (Budget)
- **Duration:** 8-10 hours (overnight)
- **Companies:** Flota El Dorado, Trans Omar, La Paz Bus
- **Cost:** $15-30 USD
- **Departure:** La Paz bus terminal, evening departure
- **Arrival:** Uyuni, early morning
- **Notes:**
  - Semi-cama (reclining) or cama (fully flat) seats recommended
  - Bring warm layers (bus AC can be cold)
  - Arrive early for best seats

#### By Train (Scenic)
- **Route:** Oruro → Uyuni (must get to Oruro first from La Paz)
- **Duration:** 7 hours
- **Cost:** $7-25 USD (depending on class)
- **Schedule:** Limited (check current timetable, often 3x/week)
- **Classes:** 
  - Económica: Basic benches
  - Primera: Reclining seats
  - Ejecutivo: More comfortable, includes meal
- **Notes:**
  - Scenic journey through altiplano
  - Can be delayed
  - Book in advance during high season

---

### Route 2: Sucre → Uyuni

- **By Bus:** 6-7 hours, $15-25 USD
- **Frequency:** Multiple daily departures
- **Companies:** Trans Omar, Flota El Dorado
- **Notes:** Sucre is easier for acclimatization (lower altitude than La Paz)

---

### Route 3: From Chile (San Pedro de Atacama)

- **Tour Type:** 4-day/3-night crossing
- **Cost:** $250-350 USD
- **Route:** Atacama → Eduardo Avaroa Reserve → Salar de Uyuni → Uyuni town
- **Includes:** Transport, guide, accommodation, some meals, reserve fees
- **Notes:**
  - One-way tour (end in Uyuni or return to Atacama)
  - Requires passport (border crossing)
  - Diverse landscapes (desert, lagoons, salt flats)
  - Good option if visiting Chile and Bolivia

## Altitude & Health

### The Numbers
- **La Paz:** 3,650m (11,975 ft)
- **Uyuni:** 3,650m (11,975 ft)
- **Salar de Uyuni:** 3,650m (11,975 ft)
- **Eduardo Avaroa Reserve:** Up to 4,300m (14,100 ft)
- **Tunupa Volcano:** 5,432m (17,822 ft)

### Altitude Sickness (Soroche) Prevention

**Before You Go:**
- Consult doctor about acetazolamide (Diamox)
- Plan 2-3 days acclimatization in La Paz or Sucre
- Avoid flying directly to Uyuni if possible (go via La Paz first)

**On Arrival:**
- Rest for first 24 hours
- Drink lots of water (2-3 liters/day)
- Avoid alcohol for first 2 days
- Eat light meals
- Try coca tea (traditional remedy, widely available)
- Walk slowly, no running

**Symptoms to Watch:**
- Headache (most common)
- Nausea
- Dizziness
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Loss of appetite

**If Symptoms Worsen:**
- Descend to lower altitude immediately
- Seek medical attention
- Do not continue tour if severely affected

**Who's at Risk:**
- Anyone can get altitude sickness (fitness doesn't matter)
- Previous history increases risk
- Rapid ascent increases risk

## What to Pack

### Clothing (Layer System)

**Base Layer:**
- Thermal underwear (top and bottom)
- Moisture-wicking t-shirts
- Long underwear for cold nights

**Mid Layer:**
- Fleece or wool sweater
- Light down jacket
- Warm pants or fleece-lined leggings

**Outer Layer:**
- Windproof/waterproof jacket (essential)
- Rain pants (wet season)
- Warm hat/beanie
- Gloves (essential for cold mornings)
- Buff or scarf for face protection

**Footwear:**
- Waterproof hiking boots (wet season essential)
- Warm socks (wool, multiple pairs)
- Sandals or camp shoes (for accommodation)

### Sun Protection (Critical at Altitude)
- SPF 50+ sunscreen (UV is intense)
- Lip balm with SPF
- Sunglasses (UV400 protection)
- Wide-brim hat (dry season)

### Health & Safety
- Altitude medication (Diamox, if prescribed)
- Pain relievers (ibuprofen for altitude headaches)
- Personal medications
- First aid kit (bandages, antiseptic, blister treatment)
- Hand sanitizer
- Toilet paper (facilities often lack supplies)
- Wet wipes (limited shower access on tours)
- Water purification tablets (backup)

### Gear
- Daypack (20-30L) for daily essentials
- Water bottle or hydration bladder (2-3L capacity)
- Headlamp or flashlight (early starts, basic accommodation)
- Power bank (limited charging on tours)
- Camera with extra batteries (cold drains them fast)
- Tripod (for sunrise/sunset, night shots)
- Waterproof bags for electronics
- Lens cloth (salt spray coats lenses)

### Documents & Money
- Passport (required for reserve entrance, checkpoints)
- Copies of passport (backup)
- Travel insurance documents
- Cash in Bolivianos (BOB) - cards not accepted everywhere
- Small bills for tips and purchases
- Credit card (backup, for hotels in Uyuni)

### Miscellaneous
- Snacks (energy bars, nuts, chocolate)
- Earplugs (for accommodation, bus)
- Eye mask (for sleeping)
- Book or entertainment (long bus rides)
- Plastic bags (for wet/dirty clothes)
- Toilet paper roll (carry your own)

## Accommodation

### In Uyuni Town (Pre/Post Tour)

**Budget Hostels:** $10-25 USD/night
- Shared dorms or basic private rooms
- Shared bathrooms
- Common areas, sometimes kitchen
- Examples: Hostel Naira, Lucky House

**Mid-Range Hotels:** $30-70 USD/night
- Private rooms with ensuite
- Hot water (often solar, can be limited)
- WiFi (variable quality)
- Examples: Hotel de Sal, Vista Verde

**Luxury Hotels:** $80-250+ USD/night
- Best amenities available in Uyuni
- Heating, reliable hot water
- Restaurant on-site
- Examples: Palacio de Sal, Luna Salada

**Salt Hotels:** $50-150 USD/night
- Built from salt blocks (walls, furniture)
- Unique experience
- Variable quality (some are touristy)
- Note: Don't lick the walls (really, don't)

---

### On Multi-Day Tours

**Basic Refugios** (included in budget tours):
- Shared rooms (4-8 people)
- Shared bathrooms (sometimes cold water only)
- Basic bedding (bring sleeping bag liner for warmth/cleanliness)
- Heating: Limited or none (can be very cold)
- Showers: Sometimes available, often extra cost ($3-5)

**Salt Hotels** (some tours include):
- Salt block construction
- Private or shared rooms
- Better facilities than refugios
- Still basic by international standards

**Camping** (some tours offer, dry season only):
- Tents provided
- Sleeping bags (bring your own liner)
- Cold but magical experience
- Best for stargazing

**Upgraded Options** (extra cost on some tours):
- Private rooms
- Better heating
- Hot showers included
- Ask when booking

## Food & Water

### On Tours
- **Breakfast:** Simple (bread, jam, coffee/tea, sometimes eggs)
- **Lunch:** Packed lunch or basic restaurant (soup, main dish, drink)
- **Dinner:** 3-course meal at refugio (soup, main, dessert)
- **Dietary Requirements:** Vegetarian usually available with notice; vegan/gluten-free harder (communicate in advance)
- **Water:** Bring your own bottle; some tours provide boiled water

### In Uyuni Town
- **Local Restaurants:** $5-10 USD per meal
- **Tourist Restaurants:** $10-20 USD per meal
- **Street Food:** $2-5 USD (empanadas, salteñas)
- **Cuisine:** Bolivian (hearty, carb-heavy), some international options

**Must-Try:**
- Quinoa soup
- Llama meat (steak or stew)
- Trout (from local lakes)
- Salteñas (savory pastries, morning only)
- Api (warm corn drink, breakfast)

### Water Safety
- **Do NOT drink tap water**
- Bottled water widely available ($1-2 per bottle)
- Bring purification tablets as backup
- Stay hydrated (altitude + dry climate = dehydration risk)

## Safety & Considerations

### General Safety
- Bolivia is generally safe for tourists
- Petty theft exists (watch belongings in buses, markets)
- Violent crime against tourists is rare
- Use registered tour companies
- Keep copies of passport separate from original

### Tour Safety
- Check vehicle condition (4x4 should be well-maintained)
- Ensure driver is experienced (roads are rough)
- Confirm tour includes basic first aid
- Share itinerary with someone not on tour
- Have travel insurance that covers altitude and adventure activities

### Environmental Responsibility
- **Stay on marked paths** (salt crust can be damaged)
- **Do not litter** (pack out all trash)
- **Respect wildlife** (keep distance from flamingos)
- **Use facilities** where available (do not go in salt flat)
- **Minimize water use** (scarce resource)
- **Support local businesses** (guides, hotels, restaurants)

### Cultural Respect
- Ask before photographing people
- Learn basic Spanish phrases (gracias, por favor, hola)
- Respect indigenous communities and traditions
- Dress modestly in villages
- Be patient (things move slower in Bolivia)

## Photography Tips

### Best Times
- **Sunrise:** Golden light, fewer crowds, possible mirror
- **Sunset:** Dramatic colors, reflection opportunities
- **Midday:** Harsh light, good for abstract patterns
- **Night:** Stargazing, Milky Way (dry season, clear skies)

### Mirror Effect Shots
- Get low to the water (lie down if needed)
- Use polarizing filter to reduce glare
- Wait for calm water (wind creates ripples)
- Include person/object for scale and reflection
- Shoot in RAW for editing flexibility

### Perspective/Optical Illusion Shots
- Use wide-angle lens
- Place "small" object far away, "large" object close
- Use tripod for consistency
- Have fun with it (holding sun, jumping, etc.)

### Hexagon Patterns
- Low angle emphasizes texture
- Side light (morning/evening) creates shadows
- Get close for abstract shots
- Wide for endless pattern effect

### Night Sky
- Bring tripod (essential)
- Use high ISO (1600-3200)
- Wide aperture (f/2.8 or lower)
- Long exposure (15-30 seconds)
- Focus manually on bright star
- Dress warmly (freezing while shooting)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not acclimatizing** - Arrive in La Paz/Sucre 2-3 days early
2. **Underpacking for cold** - Nights are brutally cold, even in summer
3. **Booking cheapest tour** - You get what you pay for (vehicle safety, guide quality)
4. **Not bringing cash** - ATMs unreliable, cards not accepted
5. **Expecting luxury** - This is remote Bolivia; facilities are basic
6. **Only planning for mirror** - Dry season has its own beauty
7. **Not protecting from sun** - UV at altitude is no joke
8. **Overexerting first day** - Take it easy, let body adjust
9. **Not checking what's included** - Reserve fees, entrance fees, meals
10. **Forgetting toilet paper** - Facilities often don't have it

## Booking Tips

### How to Book
- **Online in advance:** Viator, GetYourGuide, tour company websites
- **In La Paz/Sucre:** Many agencies, compare options
- **In Uyuni:** Walk-in, but limited availability in high season

### Questions to Ask
- What's included/excluded?
- What type of vehicle?
- How many people per vehicle?
- Where do we sleep? (refugio vs. salt hotel)
- Are entrance fees included?
- What meals are provided?
- Is guide English-speaking?
- What's the cancellation policy?
- Do you provide sleeping bags/blankets?
- Are showers available? Hot water?

### Red Flags
- Prices significantly below market (safety corners cut)
- Vague answers about accommodation
- No contract or receipt
- Pressure to pay cash only
- Poor online reviews (check TripAdvisor, Google)

## Sample Itineraries

### 3-Day Classic (Most Popular)

**Day 1:**
- Morning: Depart Uyuni, Train Cemetery, Colchani village
- Midday: Enter salt flat, photos, lunch on salt
- Afternoon: Incahuasi Island (dry season), salt hotel
- Night: Sleep at salt hotel or refugio, stargazing

**Day 2:**
- Early: Sunrise on salt flat
- Morning: Drive to Eduardo Avaroa Reserve
- Midday: Laguna Colorada, flamingos, lunch

- Early: Sunrise, geysers (Sol de Mañana)

## Final Tips

1. **Arrive early** to acclimatize (2-3 days in La Paz/Sucre)
2. **Book tours with reputable companies** (safety first)
3. **Bring cash** (Bolivianos, small bills)
4. **Pack for extreme cold** (even in summer)

7. **Be flexible** (weather, conditions change)
8. **Respect the environment** (leave no trace)
9. **Engage with locals** (they have stories)
10. **Slow down** (this is not a place to rush)

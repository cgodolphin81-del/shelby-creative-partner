# The Hidden Temple
## Episode 082: Luang Prabang, Laos

### A Poetic Essay

---

## I. Saffron and Silence

Before the sun remembers its name,
the town holds its breath.

In the violet hour between night and knowing,
a line forms—
not of people waiting,
but of silence walking.

Two hundred saffron robes,
each one a flame extinguished to ember,
moving barefoot over stone
that still holds yesterday's heat.

They do not speak.
The alms bowls do not clink.
Even the river seems to hush its current,
watching.

This is not performance.
This is not for the cameras
clustered on corners like mechanical birds.

This is older than memory,
older than the French shutters
peeling green on Sisavangvong Road,
older than the Buddha statue
that gave this place its name.

A woman kneels.
Her hands lift sticky rice,
still warm from predawn steaming.
She does not meet the monk's eyes.
He does not meet hers.

Between them:
an exchange without words.
Merit passing one direction,
sustenance the other.
Both equally sacred.

The saffron flows past—
river within a river,
this one flowing toward light
while the Mekong flows toward sea.

I stand at the edge,
foreign feet on sacred stone,
and understand nothing
and everything.

Silence, I learn,
is not the absence of sound.
It is the presence of something
too large for speech.

---

## II. The River's Blessing

The Mekong does not hurry.

It has traveled from Tibetan snow
through Chinese mountains,
past Burmese jungles and Thai temples,
and here—
here it pauses.

Not stops.
Never stops.
But the way water pauses
around a stone it has loved for centuries.

Luang Prabang sits on a peninsula
like a lotus flower opening,
roots in two rivers,
petals toward the sacred mountain.

At sunset,
the river becomes molten gold.
Fisherfolk push wooden boats
with poles worn smooth by generations.
Their nets arc silver against orange sky,
and for a moment
the whole world is between water and light.

I walk the riverbank at dusk.
The temples glow behind me—
gold leaf catching last light,
chedis pointing heavenward
like fingers remembering prayer.

A monk sits on the steps,
feet in water,
robes gathered like fallen sunsets.

He watches the current.
I watch him watching.

The river carries everything:
leaves from upstream forests,
prayers whispered at water's edge,
the reflected faces of travelers
who came seeking something
they could not name.

What does the river bless?
Not the faithful.
Not the skeptical.
Not the seeking or the found.

It blesses simply by being river.
By continuing.
By knowing that all things
eventually reach the sea,
whether they resist or surrender.

I dip my hands in.
Cool against tropical heat.
The water slips through fingers
like time, like grace,
like all the things we cannot hold
but must trust anyway.

---

## III. Faith in Stillness

Wat Xieng Thong at noon.

The tourists have moved on,
chasing waterfalls and viewpoints,
seeking the next photograph
to prove they were here.

I sit in the shade of the sim,
the nine-tiered roof cascading
like a mountain made of gold and shadow.

A novice sweeps the courtyard.
Same motion.
Again.
Again.

The broom whispers over stone.
Leaves gather.
Leaves scatter.
He sweeps again.

There is no destination in his sweeping.
No achievement.
No progress toward completion.

The courtyard will be swept again tomorrow.
And the day after.
Until he is no longer novice,
until he is no longer here,
until the temple itself is dust
returned to the earth that held it.

And still: he sweeps.

In that motion,
something ancient stirs.

We are taught to move toward.
Toward goals, toward answers,
toward the next thing
that will finally make us whole.

But what if wholeness is not ahead?
What if it is here,
in the sweep of broom on stone,
in the breath between thoughts,
in the stillness beneath all motion?

The Buddha inside the sim
has sat for four hundred years.
Not waiting.
Not wanting.
Simply being.

Gold leaf has been applied
by thousands of faithful hands.
Each touch a prayer.
Each prayer a letting go.

I touch nothing.
I ask for nothing.
I sit until the shadow shifts
and the novice has finished
and the temple bell sounds
some hour I did not notice arriving.

Faith, I think,
might not be belief in something unseen.
Might be the courage to be still
when everything demands movement.

---

## IV. The Soul of Laos

They say a country has a soul.

I do not know if this is true.
But I know that some places
hum beneath the skin.

Luang Prabang hums.

Not the hum of traffic or commerce,
not the electric buzz of cities
that never sleep because they are afraid
of what dreams might come.

This is a different frequency.
Older.
Deeper.

It lives in the spaces between:
between the rivers,
between the temples,
between the inhale and exhale
of a thousand years of mornings.

The Lao word "bor pen nyang"—
it means "it is nothing,"
"no problem,"
"let it be."

I hear it everywhere.
When the tuk-tuk breaks down.
When the rain cancels the boat.
When the power fails
and the whole town goes dark
except for candlelight in temple windows.

Bor pen nyang.
It is nothing.
It is everything.

In the markets,
women sell herbs and spices
arranged in banana leaf bowls.
Lemongrass. Galangal. Kaffir lime.
The scents of a cuisine
that knows balance is not compromise
but harmony.

In the evening,
families gather on temple steps.
Children chase each other
around ancient stupas.
Grandmothers laugh with gaps
where teeth have gone to rest.

No one is trying to be elsewhere.
No one is documenting for later.
They are simply here,
alive in the moment
that will never come again.

This is the soul I came seeking.
Not in the waterfalls
or the viewpoints or the curated experiences.

But in the way a monk's robe
catches the light just so.
In the way the river accepts
every tributary without judgment.
In the way a whole town
wakes before dawn
to give food to those
who have renounced the world
so the world might be saved.

Laos does not shout.
It whispers.
And you must be still
to hear it.

---

## V. Departure

I leave before dawn on my last day.

No Tak Bat.
No final temple visit.
No souvenir from the night market.

Some things are not meant to be taken.
Only witnessed.
Only carried in the quiet places
where memory becomes part of you.

The plane rises over the Mekong.
The river continues its journey,
unconcerned with my departure.

Below, the temples gleam once,
then are hidden by cloud.

I close my eyes.

The saffron remains.
The silence remains.
The river flows on.

And somewhere in me,
a broom sweeps stone.
Again.
Again.

Until the courtyard is clean.
Until there is nothing left
but the space
where grace can enter.

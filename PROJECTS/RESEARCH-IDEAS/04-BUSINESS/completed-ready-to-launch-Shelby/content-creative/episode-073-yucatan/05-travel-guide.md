# Episode 073: Travel Guide
## The Children of the Corn - Yucatan Peninsula, Mexico

**Practical Information for Visiting the Yucatan**

---

## Getting There

### Flights
**Primary Airport:** Cancún International Airport (CUN)
- Largest airport in the region
- Direct flights from most major US, Canadian, and European cities
- Airlines: American, Delta, United, JetBlue, Aeromexico, Interjet, Volaris

**Alternative Airports:**
- **Mérida (MID):** Smaller, less crowded, good for western Yucatan
- **Cozumel (CZM):** Island airport, ferry from Playa del Carmen

### From Cancún Airport to Destinations

**To Valladolid (near Chichen Itza):**
- ADO Bus: $20-25 USD, ~2.5 hours
- Private transfer: $80-120 USD, ~2 hours
- Rental car: ~$40-60/day, ~2 hours

**To Tulum:**
- ADO Bus: $15-20 USD, ~2 hours
- Private transfer: $100-140 USD, ~1.5 hours
- Rental car: ~$40-60/day, ~1.5 hours

**To Playa del Carmen:**
- ADO Bus: $10-15 USD, ~1 hour
- Private transfer: $70-100 USD, ~1 hour
- Rental car: ~$40-60/day, ~1 hour

**To Mérida:**
- ADO Bus: $25-35 USD, ~4 hours
- Flight: 1 hour, $80-150 USD
- Rental car: ~$40-60/day, ~3.5 hours

### Transportation Options

**ADO Bus:**
- Comfortable, air-conditioned, reliable
- Multiple classes: Primera (first), Ejecutivo (executive)
- Book online: www.adobus.com
- Major routes: Cancún-Valladolid-Mérida, Cancún-Tulum-Playa

**Rental Car:**
- Maximum flexibility for exploring cenotes and ruins
- Roads generally good between major destinations
- Watch for: speed bumps (topes), police checkpoints, potholes
- Gas stations: PEMEX (state-owned), pay in cash or card
- Insurance: Highly recommended, often required

**Colectivos (Shared Vans):**
- Budget option for short distances
- Depart when full, flexible routes
- Ask locals for routes and pickup points
- Cash only, ~$5-15 USD per trip

**Taxis:**
- Available in towns and tourist areas
- Negotiate price before getting in
- More expensive than colectivos
- Uber: Limited availability (mainly Cancún, Playa)

## Best Times to Visit

### Seasons

**Dry Season (November - April)**
- **Best time to visit**
- Minimal rain, comfortable temperatures
- December-March: Peak tourist season, higher prices
- November & April: Shoulder season, good value
- Temperatures: 75-85°F (24-29°C)

**Wet Season (May - October)**
- Afternoon thunderstorms common
- Lush, green jungle
- Fewer tourists, lower prices
- Hurricane season: June-November (peak: Sept-Oct)
- Temperatures: 80-95°F (27-35°C), humid

### Special Events

**Spring Equinox (March 20-21)**
- El Castillo serpent shadow phenomenon
- Massive crowds at Chichen Itza
- Book accommodation months in advance
- Arrive at dawn for best viewing

**Fall Equinox (September 21-22)**
- Same phenomenon, fewer crowds
- Still wet season, but manageable
- Good compromise for photographers

**Day of the Dead (November 1-2)**
- Traditional celebrations throughout Mexico
- Hanal Pixán in Yucatan (Maya version)
- Cultural experience, not overly touristy

**Carnaval (February/March, dates vary)**
- Major celebration in Mérida
- Parades, music, dancing
- Book early, expect crowds

### Crowds & Timing

**Chichen Itza:**
- Arrive at opening (8 AM) to avoid crowds
- Tour buses arrive 10 AM - 2 PM
- Equinox: expect 30,000+ visitors
- Weekdays better than weekends

**Tulum:**
- Arrive at opening (8 AM) for photos without crowds
- Cruise ship days: check schedule, avoid if possible
- Sunset visits popular but crowded

**Cenotes:**
- Morning visits: fewer people, better light
- Weekdays less crowded than weekends
- Some cenotes close for lunch (2-4 PM)

## Cenote Swimming Guide

### What to Bring
- Swimsuit (wear under clothes for efficiency)
- Quick-dry towel
- Water shoes or sandals with grip (rocks are slippery)
- Biodegradable sunscreen (required at most cenotes)
- Waterproof bag for valuables
- Change of clothes
- Cash for entry fees and lockers

### What NOT to Bring
- Regular sunscreen (chemicals damage ecosystems)
- Insect repellent (apply before arriving, let absorb)
- Food or drinks (most cenotes prohibit)
- Glass containers
- Drones (generally not allowed)

### Safety Tips

**Before Entering:**
- Check depth signs
- Look for underwater hazards
- Don't dive unless depth is clearly marked
- Test water temperature (can be surprisingly cold)

**While Swimming:**
- Stay within designated areas
- Don't touch stalactites/stalagmites (oils damage formations)
- Don't feed fish or wildlife
- Watch for slippery rocks when exiting
- Use life jackets if not a strong swimmer

**Health Considerations:**
- Shower before and after (required at many cenotes)
- Don't swim with open wounds
- Remove contact lenses or wear goggles
- Stay hydrated (cenote water is fresh, but still hydrate)
- Some cenotes have sulfur smell—normal, not harmful

### Cenote Etiquette
- Respect sacred nature of these sites
- Speak quietly, especially in cave cenotes
- Don't jump unless it's explicitly allowed
- Follow all posted rules
- Leave no trace

### Recommended Cenotes

**For First-Timers:**
- **Cenote Ik Kil:** Open, stunning, facilities, near Chichen Itza
- **Gran Cenote (Tulum):** Easy access, turtles, clear water
- **Cenote Zacil Ha:** Less crowded, zip line, Tulum area

**For Adventurers:**
- **Cenote Dos Ojos:** Underwater caves, world-class diving
- **Cenote Calavera:** Jumping platforms, skull-shaped
- **Cenote X'keken:** Cave cenote with dramatic light beam

**For Photography:**
- **Cenote Samula:** Light beam at midday
- **Cenote Ik Kil:** Hanging vines, iconic shots
- **Cenote Dos Ojos:** Underwater visibility

## Culture & Etiquette

### Basic Spanish Phrases

**Greetings:**
- Buenos días = Good morning
- Buenas tardes = Good afternoon
- Buenas noches = Good evening/night
- ¿Cómo está? = How are you? (formal)
- ¿Qué tal? = How's it going? (informal)

**Essentials:**
- Por favor = Please
- Gracias = Thank you
- De nada = You're welcome
- Sí = Yes
- No = No
- ¿Cuánto cuesta? = How much does it cost?
- La cuenta, por favor = The check, please
- ¿Dónde está...? = Where is...?
- Baños = Bathrooms
- Agua = Water

**Maya Language (Yucatec Maya):**
- Bix a belex? = How are you?
- Ma'lob = Good/Okay
- Dyos bo'otik = Thank you (God pays you)

### Cultural Respect

**Photography:**
- Ask before photographing people
- Some Maya communities prohibit photography
- Respect "no photography" signs at ruins
- Don't use flash on murals or artifacts

**Religious Sites:**
- Chichen Itza, Tulum, etc. are archaeological sites, not active temples
- Still treat with respect
- Don't climb on restricted structures
- Don't remove stones or artifacts (illegal and harmful)

**Cenotes:**
- Understand these are sacred sites
- Some still used for ceremonies
- Dress modestly when not swimming
- Respect local customs

**Tipping:**
- Restaurants: 10-15% expected
- Tour guides: $5-10 USD per person for day tours
- Hotel staff: $1-2 USD per day for housekeeping
- Taxi drivers: Round up or 10%

**Bargaining:**
- Expected at markets and with street vendors
- Not appropriate in restaurants or stores with prices
- Be polite and friendly
- Start at 50-60% of asking price, meet in middle
- Don't haggle over small amounts—support local economy

### Dress Code

**General:**
- Casual, comfortable clothing
- Light, breathable fabrics (it's hot and humid)
- Modest clothing when visiting towns and churches
- Swimwear appropriate for cenotes and beaches only

**Ruins:**
- Comfortable walking shoes
- Hat and sunglasses
- Light long sleeves for sun protection
- Bring rain jacket in wet season

**Evening:**
- Casual in most places
- Slightly dressier for upscale restaurants
- Light sweater for air-conditioned spaces

## Safety

### General Safety

**Yucatan is generally safe for tourists:**
- Violent crime against tourists is rare
- Petty theft (pickpocketing, bag snatching) is the main concern
- Tourist police in major areas (Cancún, Playa, Tulum)

**Precautions:**
- Don't flash expensive jewelry, cameras, phones
- Use hotel safes for passports and valuables
- Don't leave belongings unattended on beaches
- Be aware of surroundings in crowded areas
- Use ATMs inside banks during daylight hours

### Transportation Safety

**Driving:**
- Watch for speed bumps (topes)—often unmarked
- Police checkpoints common—be polite, have documents ready
- Avoid driving at night (poor lighting, animals on roads)
- Don't leave valuables visible in parked cars
- Use toll roads (cuotas) when available—better maintained

**Buses:**
- ADO buses are safe and reliable
- Keep bags with you, not in overhead compartments
- Don't accept food/drink from strangers
- First-class buses have security checks

**Taxis:**
- Use official taxi stands or hotel-arranged taxis
- Negotiate price before getting in
- Sit in back seat
- Share taxi details with someone

### Health & Medical

**Water:**
- Don't drink tap water
- Bottled water is cheap and widely available
- Use bottled water for brushing teeth
- Ice in restaurants is generally safe (made from purified water)

**Food:**
- Street food is generally safe if cooked fresh
- Watch for busy stalls (high turnover = fresh food)
- Peel fruits and vegetables
- Avoid raw seafood unless at reputable restaurants
- Carry anti-diarrheal medication just in case

**Sun & Heat:**
- Sunscreen is essential (biodegradable for cenotes)
- Stay hydrated
- Take breaks in shade/AC during midday heat
- Watch for heat exhaustion symptoms

**Insects:**
- Mosquitoes present, especially in wet season
- Use biodegradable repellent (apply before cenotes)
- Dengue and Zika present in region—take precautions
- Wear long sleeves/pants at dawn and dusk

**Medical Care:**
- Tourist areas have clinics and hospitals
- Private hospitals better equipped than public
- Travel insurance highly recommended
- Emergency number: 911 (works throughout Mexico)

### Cenote-Specific Safety

- Slippery rocks—use caution entering/exiting
- Don't dive unless depth is clearly marked
- Some cenotes have strong underwater currents
- Cave cenotes: don't venture beyond designated areas without guide
- Watch for falling rocks in cave cenotes (rare but possible)

### Emergency Contacts

- **Emergency:** 911
- **Tourist Assistance (Cancún):** +52 998 885 2222
- **US Embassy (Mérida):** +52 999 942 5700
- **Canadian Embassy (Mérida):** +52 999 944 3838

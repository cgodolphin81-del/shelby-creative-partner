## GETTING THERE

### By Air
**Samarkand International Airport (SKD)**

- **Direct International Flights:**
  - Istanbul (Turkish Airlines) - ~4 hours
  - Moscow (multiple carriers) - ~4.5 hours
  - Dubai (flydubai) - ~3.5 hours
  - St. Petersburg, Kazakhstan, other regional hubs

- **Via Tashkent:**
  - Most international travelers fly into Tashkent International Airport (TAS)
  - Take the Afrosiyob high-speed train to Samarkand (see below)
  - Tashkent has more flight options from Europe, Asia, Middle East

### By Train (Recommended from Tashkent)
**Afrosiyob High-Speed Train**

- **Route:** Tashkent → Samarkand
- **Duration:** 2 hours 10 minutes
- **Frequency:** 3-4 departures daily
- **Classes:**
  - Economy: ~$15-20 USD
  - Business: ~$25-35 USD
  - VIP: ~$40-50 USD
- **Booking:** Book in advance at uty.uz or at Tashkent train station
- **Comfort:** Modern, air-conditioned, clean, spacious
- **Scenery:** Passing through desert and agricultural landscapes

**Regular Trains:**
- Slower (3-4 hours) but cheaper ($5-10 USD)
- Less comfortable but more authentic experience
- Multiple departures daily

### By Road
- **From Bukhara:** 4-5 hours by car or shared taxi (~$30-50)
- **From Tashkent:** 4-5 hours by car (~$60-80 private transfer)
- **Shared Taxis:** Depart when full, negotiate price in advance

## VISA & ENTRY REQUIREMENTS (2025-2026)

### Visa-Free Entry
**Citizens of 90+ countries** including:
- United States (30 days, effective January 1, 2026)
- European Union members
- United Kingdom
- Canada
- Australia
- Japan
- South Korea
- Singapore
- And many more

**Requirements:**
- Passport valid 6+ months beyond entry date
- Return/onward ticket (may be requested)
- Hotel booking confirmation (may be requested)
- Registration within 3 days of arrival (hotels handle this)

### E-Visa (For Non-Visa-Free Nationalities)
- **Apply:** e-visa.gov.uz (official government site)
- **Cost:** 
  - Single entry: $20 USD
  - Double entry: $35 USD
  - Multiple entry: $50 USD
- **Processing:** 2-3 business days (apply 1 week before travel)
- **Validity:** 90 days from issue, 30-day stay permitted
- **Requirements:** Passport scan, passport photo, hotel booking

### Traditional Embassy Visa
- For longer stays or non-tourism purposes
- Requires invitation letter (LOI) from Uzbek tour operator or host
- Apply at nearest Uzbek embassy/consulate
- Processing: 1-2 weeks
- Fee: ~$160 USD for tourist visa

### Important Notes
- **Registration:** Hotels automatically register guests. If staying in private accommodation (Airbnb, homestay), host must register you within 3 days. Keep registration slips—they may be checked when departing.
- **No Visa on Arrival:** Must obtain e-visa or visa-free entry before arrival
- **Overstaying:** Fines apply. Cannot extend tourist visas—must exit and re-enter
- **Yellow Fever:** Certificate required if arriving from Yellow Fever risk zone

## WHEN TO VISIT

### Best Times (Recommended)

**Spring (April-June)**
- **Weather:** 15-28°C (59-82°F), mild and pleasant
- **Pros:** Gardens blooming, comfortable walking, fewer crowds than autumn
- **Cons:** Can be windy in April
- **Events:** Navruz (Persian New Year, March 21) if visiting late March

**Autumn (September-Early November)**
- **Weather:** 18-30°C (64-86°F), warm days, cool evenings
- **Pros:** Harvest season (fresh fruits, grapes), clear skies, ideal photography
- **Cons:** Busiest tourist season, higher prices
- **Events:** Sharq Taronalari music festival (late August, biennial)

### Shoulder Season

**Late March, Late November**
- **Weather:** Variable, can be cool or warm
- **Pros:** Fewer tourists, lower prices
- **Cons:** Unpredictable weather, some facilities may be closed

### Avoid If Possible

**Summer (July-August)**
- **Weather:** 35-45°C+ (95-113°F+), extremely hot
- **Cons:** Uncomfortable for sightseeing, heat exhaustion risk
- **Pros:** Least crowded, lowest prices
- **If You Must Go:** Start early, siesta midday, evening activities only

**Winter (December-February)**
- **Weather:** -5 to 10°C (23-50°F), can be cold, occasional snow
- **Cons:** Cold for extended outdoor exploration, shorter days
- **Pros:** Very few tourists, dramatic photography in snow, lowest prices
- **Note:** Major sites still open, heating in hotels

## CULTURE & ETIQUETTE

### Uzbek Hospitality

Uzbeks are renowned for their hospitality. Understanding local customs will enrich your experience:

**Tea Culture:**
- Tea (chai) is central to social life
- Always accept when offered—it's a sign of respect
- Pour tea for others before yourself
- Hold the piala (bowl) with both hands when receiving
- Don't fill to the brim; leaving space shows respect

**Greetings:**
- Handshake with right hand, left hand over heart
- "Assalomu alaykum" (Peace be upon you) - formal greeting
- "Yaxshimisiz?" (How are you?) - casual
- Men may embrace or kiss on cheeks if close friends
- Women greet women similarly; men and women typically don't touch unless related

**Dress Code:**
- Modest dress appreciated, especially at religious sites
- Women: Cover shoulders and knees; carry a scarf for mosques
- Men: Long pants, shirts with sleeves (no tank tops at religious sites)
- Remove shoes before entering mosques and madrasas
- Comfortable walking shoes essential

### Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- ✅ Ask permission before photographing people
- ✅ Accept hospitality (tea, bread, invitations)
- ✅ Use right hand for eating and passing items
- ✅ Learn basic Uzbek/Russian phrases
- ✅ Respect religious sites and local customs
- ✅ Bargain politely in bazaars (it's expected)
- ✅ Keep hotel registration slips

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Public displays of affection (keep minimal)
- ❌ Point with single finger (use open hand)
- ❌ Show soles of feet when sitting
- ❌ Discuss politics or criticize the government
- ❌ Take photos of military/government buildings
- ❌ Eat or drink in public during Ramadan daylight hours (if visiting during Ramadan)
- ❌ Leave without saying goodbye

### Photography Etiquette

- **Generally OK:** Architecture, landscapes, markets, food
- **Ask First:** People, especially women and elderly
- **Restricted:** Military, police, airports, some government buildings
- **Religious Sites:** Usually allowed, but be respectful, no flash during prayers
- **Drones:** Require special permit—don't fly without authorization

## MUST-SEE ATTRACTIONS

### The Big Three (UNESCO)

**1. Registan Square** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours (longer if attending light show)
- **Best Time:** Sunrise (few crowds) or evening (light show)
- **Entry:** ~$6 USD
- **Don't Miss:** Ulugh Beg's star tiles, Sher-Dor lions, Tillya-Kori's gold interior
- **Tip:** Hire a guide for full historical context (~$20-30)

**2. Gur-e-Amir Mausoleum** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Time Needed:** 1-2 hours
- **Best Time:** Morning or late afternoon light
- **Entry:** ~$5 USD
- **Don't Miss:** Azure dome, jade tombstone, gilded interior
- **Tip:** Dress respectfully; it's still a sacred site

**3. Shah-i-Zinda Necropolis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Time Needed:** 1-2 hours
- **Best Time:** Morning light on blue tiles
- **Entry:** ~$4 USD
- **Don't Miss:** Avenue of mausoleums, tilework details, hilltop views
- **Tip:** Less crowded than Registan, equally stunning

### Other Essential Sites

**4. Bibi-Khanym Mosque** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Timur's massive mosque, now in ruins but still impressive
- Climb stairs for city views
- Adjacent Siab Bazaar for souvenirs and local food

**5. Ulugh Beg Observatory** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- 15th-century astronomical observatory
- Underground sextant still visible
- Small museum with historical context
- For science and history enthusiasts

**6. Afrasiab Museum** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Ancient Sogdian frescoes (7th century)
- Silk Road artifacts
- Historical timeline of Samarkand
- Air-conditioned (good for hot days)

**7. Khazrat-Khyzr Mosque** ⭐⭐⭐
- Overlooks Afrasiab hill
- Peaceful, less touristy
- Good city views

**8. Khudjum Bazaar** ⭐⭐⭐
- Local market, less touristy than Siab
- Authentic daily life
- Great for food and people-watching

## FOOD & DRINK

### Must-Try Dishes

**Plov (Osh)** - The national dish
- Rice pilaf with lamb, carrots, onions, cumin
- Every region has its version
- Best at local oshkhonas (plov houses)

**Laghman** - Hand-pulled noodles
- Served with meat and vegetable sauce
- Uyghur origin, Central Asian staple

**Shashlik** - Grilled meat skewers
- Lamb, beef, or chicken
- Street food staple

**Samsa** - Baked pastries
- Filled with meat, pumpkin, or potatoes
- Best fresh from tandoor oven

**Non** - Traditional bread
- Round, flat, decorated patterns
- Never place face-down (considered disrespectful)

**Green Tea (Kok Choy)**
- Served in pialas (bowls)
- Always offered to guests
- Digestif after meals

### Recommended Restaurants

**Traditional:**
- **Platan** - Upscale Uzbek cuisine
- **Siyob** - Near bazaar, authentic
- **Caravan** - Tourist-friendly, good quality

**Local/Casual:**
- **Oshkhona near Chorsu Bazaar** - Best plov
- **Street vendors** - Samsa, shashlik
- **Tea houses** - Experience local culture

**International:**
- Several Italian, Korean, Russian restaurants in city center
- Quality varies; stick to Uzbek food for best experience

## PRACTICAL TIPS

### Money
- **Currency:** Uzbek Som (UZS)
- **ATMs:** Widely available in Samarkand
- **Cards:** Visa/Mastercard accepted at hotels, some restaurants
- **Cash:** Essential for markets, small vendors, transport
- **Exchange:** Better rates in Uzbekistan than abroad; bring USD or EUR

### Connectivity
- **SIM Cards:** Ucell, Beeline, Uzmobile available at airport or city
- **Cost:** ~$5-10 for tourist SIM with data
- **WiFi:** Available at most hotels and cafés
- **Apps:** Yandex Taxi (Uber equivalent), Google Translate (download Russian/Uzbek offline)

### Health & Safety
- **Water:** Bottled water recommended; avoid tap water
- **Vaccinations:** Routine vaccines; Hepatitis A/B, Typhoid recommended
- **Safety:** Uzbekistan is very safe; low crime rates
- **Scams:** Minimal; agree on taxi prices in advance
- **Emergency:** 112 (unified emergency number)

### Language
- **Uzbek:** Official language (Turkic family)
- **Russian:** Widely spoken, especially by older generation
- **English:** Growing, but limited outside tourist areas
- **Useful Phrases:**
  - Rahmat = Thank you
  - Ha/Yo'q = Yes/No
  - Salom = Hello
  - Hayr = Goodbye
  - Necha pul? = How much?

### Shopping & Souvenirs
- **Siab Bazaar:** Spices, dried fruits, nuts, bread
- **Craft Shops:** Ceramics, textiles, miniatures
- **What to Buy:**
  - Atlas and Adras fabrics
  - Hand-painted ceramics
  - Traditional doppas (skullcaps)
  - Spices and tea
  - Miniature paintings
- **Bargaining:** Expected in bazaars; start at 50-60% of asking price
- **Antiques:** Require export permit; don't buy without documentation

## GETTING AROUND SAMARKAND

### Walking
- **Best for:** City center, major sights
- **Registan to Gur-e-Amir:** 15-20 minutes walk
- **Comfortable:** In spring/autumn; hot in summer

### Taxi
- **Yandex App:** Reliable, affordable, cash or card
- **Street Taxis:** Negotiate price before getting in
- **Typical Fare:** $2-6 within city

### Bus/Marshrutka
- **Marshrutkas:** Shared minivans, fixed routes
- **Cost:** $0.30-0.50
- **Experience:** Authentic but can be confusing for first-timers

### Private Driver
- **Cost:** $40-70 for full day
- **Best for:** Multiple sites, hot weather, groups
- **Arrange:** Through hotel or tour company

## EXTENDING YOUR TRIP

### Nearby Cities

**Bukhara** (4-5 hours by train/car)
- Even older than Samarkand
- Better preserved old city
- Po-i-Kalyan complex, Ark Fortress
- Combine with Samarkand for full Silk Road experience

**Khiva** (6-8 hours from Samarkand)
- Walled city, open-air museum
- Most intact historic center
- Worth the journey for history buffs

**Tashkent** (2 hours by high-speed train)
- Capital city
- Modern amenities, international flights
- Chorsu Bazaar, Islamic Center, Soviet architecture

**Shakhrisabz** (1.5 hours south)
- Timur's birthplace
- Ak Saray Palace ruins
- Day trip from Samarkand

### Suggested Uzbekistan Itinerary
- **5-7 Days:** Tashkent (1-2) + Samarkand (2-3) + Bukhara (2-3)
- **10-14 Days:** Add Khiva, Shakhrisabz, Fergana Valley

# Episode 049: Location Research - Versailles & Paris, France

## Palace of Versailles

### History & Significance
- **Built:** 1678-1684 (Hall of Mirrors construction)
- **Commissioned by:** Louis XIV, the "Sun King"
- **Architect:** Jules Hardouin-Mansart
- **Interior decorator:** Charles Le Brun
- **Purpose:** Showcase France's artistic, financial, and political power; stage for absolute monarchy

### Hall of Mirrors (Galerie des Glaces)
- **Dimensions:** 73 meters long (240 ft), 10.5 meters wide, 12.3 meters high
- **Mirrors:** 357 individual mirrors arranged in 17 arched bays
- **Windows:** 17 large windows facing the gardens
- **Ceiling:** 30 paintings by Charles Le Brun glorifying Louis XIV's reign
- **Controversy:** Venetian mirror-makers were persuaded to defect; Venice allegedly ordered assassinations to protect their trade secrets ("Hall of Bloody Mirrors")

### Historical Events in the Hall of Mirrors
- Treaty of Paris (1783) - Ended American Revolutionary War
- Proclamation of German Empire (1871) - After Franco-Prussian War
- Treaty of Versailles (1919) - Ended World War I (deliberate choice by French PM Clemenceau as revenge for 1871 humiliation)

### Royal Apartments Sequence
1. Hercules Salon
2. Abundance
3. Venus
4. Diana
5. Mars
6. Mercury
7. Apollo (Sun King's theme)

### Visitor Information 2025
- **Location:** La galerie des Glaces, Château de Versailles, 78000 Versailles
- **GPS:** 48.804328, 2.120936
- **Tickets:**
  - Palace ticket (Hall of Mirrors included)
  - 1-day pass (entire estate: Palace, Gardens, Trianons)
  - 2-day pass (entire estate, consecutive days)
- **Pricing:**
  - Free: Under 18 (all), Under 26 (EU residents)
  - Free: First Sundays, November-March
  - Free: Disabled visitors + companion
- **Best times:** First entry (9:00-9:30) or last hour before closing; after 2:30 PM weekdays for fewer crowds
- **Annual visitors:** 8+ million

## Louvre Museum

### Overview
- **World's largest art museum**
- **Home to:** Mona Lisa, Venus de Milo, Winged Victory of Samothrace
- **Location:** 1st arrondissement, Paris

### Hours (2025)
- **Monday, Thursday, Saturday, Sunday:** 9:00 AM - 6:00 PM
- **Wednesday, Friday:** 9:00 AM - 9:00 PM
- **Closed:** Tuesdays, January 1, May 1, December 25
- **Last entry:** 1 hour before closing
- **Rooms clear:** 30 minutes before closing

### Tickets (2025)
- **EEA visitors:** €22
- **Non-EEA visitors:** €32
- **Free:** Under 18, EEA residents under 26
- **Free evenings:** First Friday of month after 6 PM (except July-August), July 14
- **Audio guide:** €6
- **Time-slot booking:** Recommended for all visitors

### Mona Lisa
- **Location:** Denon Wing, Room 711 (Salle des États)
- **No separate ticket needed** - included with Louvre admission
- **Tips:** Visit early morning or late afternoon; expect large crowds; photos should be quick

### Entrances
- **Main:** Pyramid (most crowded)
- **Alternative:** Carrousel du Louvre, Porte des Lions (less crowded during peak times)

## Eiffel Tower

### History
- **Built:** 1887-1889 (2 years, 2 months, 5 days)
- **Purpose:** Centerpiece of 1889 Exposition Universelle (World's Fair)
- **Occasion:** Centennial of French Revolution
- **Engineers:** Gustave Eiffel, Maurice Koechlin, Émile Nouguier
- **Architectural contributor:** Stephen Sauvestre

### Construction Details
- **Iron parts:** 18,038
- **Rivets:** 2.5 million
- **Workers:** ~300 (only 1 fatality during construction)
- **Height:** 330 meters (1,083 ft) as of 2022 antenna installation
- **Status at completion:** Tallest man-made structure in the world

### Design Features
- **Material:** Wrought iron
- **Paint:** Three shades (darker at bottom, lighter at top) to complement Parisian sky
- **Repainting:** Every 7 years to prevent rust
- **Engraved names:** 72 French scientists, engineers, mathematicians (Eiffel's tribute to science)

### Controversy & Survival
- **Initial criticism:** Artists and intellectuals protested the design
- **Original permit:** 20 years (scheduled for dismantling in 1909)
- **Saved by:** Radio telegraphy value; became part of International Time Service (1910)
- **Current use:** Radio/TV transmissions, tourist attraction

### Visitor Stats
- **Annual visitors:** 7+ million
- **Status:** Most-visited paid monument in the world
- **Public levels:** Three

## Montmartre

### History
- **Original character:** Village known for windmills and cabarets
- **Religious significance:** Pilgrimage site before French Revolution
- **Destruction:** During French Revolution
- **Rebirth:** Belle Époque (late 19th-early 20th century)

### Sacré-Cœur Basilica
- **Construction:** 1875-1914 (39 years)
- **Consecrated:** October 1919
- **Architect:** Paul Abadie (selected from 70+ proposals)
- **Style:** Romano-Byzantine
- **Purpose:** Spiritual renewal and national atonement after Franco-Prussian War (1870) and Paris Commune
- **Financed by:** Public subscription
- **Features:** Largest mosaic in France (Christ in Majesty)
- **Tradition:** Perpetual adoration
- **Status:** National Historical Monument (added 2022)

### Artist Heritage (Belle Époque)
**Famous artists who lived/worked in Montmartre:**
- Édouard Manet
- Claude Monet
- Edgar Degas (buried in Montmartre Cemetery)
- Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec
- Pierre-Auguste Renoir
- Vincent Van Gogh
- Pablo Picasso
- Amedeo Modigliani
- Salvador Dalí
- Joan Miró

**Note:** Many artists moved to Montparnasse after WWI outbreak

### Notable Locations
- **Place du Tertre:** Artist square, portrait painters
- **Café des Deux Moulins:** Featured in film "Amélie"
- **Au Lapin Agile:** Historic cabaret
- **Musée Montmartre:** Local history
- **Clos Montmartre:** Vineyard (yes, Paris has wine!)
- **Moulin Radet & Moulin de la Galette:** Remaining windmills
- **La Maison Rose:** Historic restaurant
- **Moulin Rouge:** Opened 1889, home of Can-can
- **Cimetière de Montmartre:** Edgar Degas, François Truffaut buried here

### Access
- **Funicular:** Available to reach basilica
- **Metro:** Jules Joffrin (Line 12), Pigalle (Lines 12 & 2), Anvers (Line 2)
- **Address:** 35 Rue du Chevalier de la Barre, 75018 Paris

## French Culture & Café Life

### Parisian Café Culture
- **Role:** Social hubs, people-watching, intellectual exchange
- **Typical experience:** Espresso at the bar (cheaper), café crème at table
- **Historic cafés:** Les Deux Magots, Café de Flore (Saint-Germain-des-Prés)
- **Modern scene:** Specialty coffee shops alongside traditional bistros

### French Cuisine Highlights
- **Breakfast:** Croissant, pain au chocolat, café
- **Lunch:** Plat du jour, baguette sandwich, salad
- **Dinner:** Multi-course meals common
- **Must-try:** Croissants, macarons, coq au vin, boeuf bourguignon, ratatouille, crème brûlée
- **Markets:** Marché Bastille, Marché des Enfants Rouges (oldest covered market)
- **Wine:** Integral to dining culture

### Lifestyle Notes
- **Work-life balance:** 35-hour work week, 5 weeks vacation standard
- **Meal times:** Lunch 12-2 PM, dinner 7:30-10 PM (later than US/UK)
- **Shopping:** Many shops close Sunday; bakeries often open Sunday morning
- **Greetings:** "Bonjour" essential before any interaction
- **Pace:** Slower, more deliberate; appreciation of quality over speed

## Best Times to Visit

### Seasons
- **Spring (April-June):** Mild weather, blooming gardens, moderate crowds
- **Summer (July-August):** Warm/hot, peak crowds, longest days, some locals away
- **Fall (September-October):** Pleasant weather, harvest season, fewer crowds
- **Winter (November-March):** Cold, fewer tourists, some attractions shorter hours, Christmas markets

### Days/Times
- **Museums:** Tuesday-Friday mornings (many museums closed Monday or Tuesday)
- **Versailles:** Weekdays, arrive at opening or after 2:30 PM
- **Eiffel Tower:** Early morning or evening; book summit access in advance
- **Montmartre:** Early morning for photos without crowds

### Events to Consider
- **Paris Fashion Week:** February/March, September/October (crowded, expensive)
- **Bastille Day:** July 14 (parade, fireworks, closures)
- **Nuit Blanche:** First Saturday in October (all-night arts festival)
- **Christmas Markets:** Late November-December

# The Eternal Flame
## A Poetic Essay on Varanasi, Death, and Rebirth

---

### I. The City That Never Dies

There is a city where death does not end but begins.

Where the river does not flow toward the sea but toward the sky, carrying prayers like lotus petals on its ancient back. Where fire does not consume but liberates, where ash does not signify destruction but release.

This is Varanasi. Kashi. The City of Light.

They say it is the oldest living city on Earth. But "living" is not quite the word. Varanasi does not merely live—it *persists*. It has watched empires rise like morning mist and vanish by noon. It has seen temples shattered and rebuilt, gods renamed and remembered, languages born and forgotten. The stones of its ghats have been stepped upon by bare feet for three thousand years, and still they wait, still they hold, still they witness.

I came here seeking something I could not name. Perhaps the same thing every pilgrim seeks: not answers, but the courage to ask better questions.

---

### II. Dawn at the Ghats

The sun does not rise in Varanasi—it *emerges*.

First, a bruise of purple on the eastern horizon. Then the slow bleed of orange, like fire testing the edge of night. The Ganges, black and secret through the darkness, begins to shimmer. And then, as if summoned by light itself, the city awakens.

At Dashashwamedh Ghat, the priests are already preparing for the morning aarti. Brass lamps filled with oil. Flowers strung into garlands. The scent of incense threading through the cold air like invisible snakes.

Pilgrims descend the stone steps, wrapped in wool against the chill. They carry small clay cups, brass pots, offerings wrapped in leaves. Some are old, their skin mapped with the geography of long lives. Some are young, eyes bright with devotion they have not yet learned to question. Some are so old they must be carried, their bodies finally arriving at the place their souls have been pointing toward for decades.

They enter the water.

The Ganges is cold in December. I know this because I watched a woman, perhaps seventy, walk down those steps with the determined gait of someone who has walked toward this moment her entire life. She did not hesitate. She did not test the water with a toe. She simply walked in, up to her waist, then her chest, then she submerged completely, emerging with water streaming from her sari, her hands joined in prayer, her face transformed.

Not by the cold. By the knowing.

In that moment, she was not cold. She was *clean*.

---

### III. The River That Forgives

They say the Ganges forgives everything.

This is a dangerous thing to believe. It is also, perhaps, the most human thing to believe.

The river has been called many things: goddess, mother, sustainer, destroyer. Scientists have tested its waters and found bacteria that should not support life. Pilgrims drink it anyway. They bottle it and carry it home. They sprinkle it on their dead. They believe—*truly believe*—that this water, this specific water, from this specific river, at this specific place, carries something that cannot be measured.

Maybe they are wrong. Maybe they are right.

I think they are asking the wrong question.

The Ganges does not need to be scientifically pure to be spiritually potent. Faith is not a laboratory. The river forgives not because it is clean but because it is *witness*. It has seen every sin, every sorrow, every desperate prayer whispered into its current. It has carried the ashes of kings and beggars alike. It has received the flowers of the grateful and the bones of the broken.

It does not judge. It flows.

And in a world that judges everything—your body, your choices, your worth, your faith—the Ganges offers something radical: acceptance without condition.

---

### IV. Fire as Release

I stood at the edge of Manikarnika Ghat and did not take photographs.

This is the cremation ground, where the pyres burn day and night, where death is not hidden in sterile rooms but displayed in its raw, ancient truth. The smoke rises constantly, a grey pillar connecting earth to sky. The air smells of sandalwood and burning flesh and something else—something that is not quite smell but *presence*.

A body was being prepared as I watched. Wrapped in white cloth, bound with rope, carried on a bamboo stretcher by four men. They moved with the practiced efficiency of those who have done this a thousand times. No drama. No ceremony. Just the work that must be done.

The eldest son walked behind, dressed in white, his head shaved. He carried a clay pot of water from the Ganges. He would circle the pyre, then break the pot, then light the fire.

This is mukhagni—the mouth of fire. The moment when grief becomes transformation.

I thought about how we in the West hide death. We embalm the body to look like sleep. We place it in a sealed casket. We have the service in a chapel, not at the grave. We throw dirt on a box, not on the body itself. We sanitize. We distance. We pretend that death is not what it is: the great unraveling.

Here, there is no pretense.

The fire does not care about your accomplishments. It does not care about your sins. It burns with the same hunger for the saint and the sinner. And in that indifference, there is a strange comfort. Death is not punishment. Death is not failure. Death is simply the next step in a journey that began long before you drew breath and will continue long after your ashes join the river.

The Hindus call it moksha—liberation from the wheel of rebirth. Not everyone achieves it. But to die in Varanasi, they say, is to have the best possible chance.

Shiva waits here. The destroyer. The transformer. The one who dances on the cremation grounds, laughing.

---

### V. The Oldest Living City

What does it mean for a city to be "old"?

Rome is old. Athens is old. Jericho is old. But Varanasi is old in a different way. These other cities have been museums of themselves for centuries. Varanasi is still *alive*.

The same rituals performed three thousand years ago are performed today. The same mantras chanted when the Buddha walked nearby are chanted now. The same steps lead to the same river, where the same pilgrims seek the same salvation.

I sat on the ghats one evening and watched a priest teach his son the aarti. The boy was perhaps ten years old. He held the lamp incorrectly. The priest corrected him gently, adjusting his grip, showing him the rhythm of the movement. This has happened, I realized, for a thousand generations. Father to son. Teacher to student. The flame passed hand to hand, never extinguished.

There is a word in Sanskrit: *parampara*. It means tradition, but also lineage, the unbroken thread connecting past to present to future. Varanasi is parampara made stone and fire and water.

In a world obsessed with the new—with disruption, with innovation, with the next thing—Varanasi asks a different question: What if the old thing still works? What if wisdom is not found in the future but in the deep remembering of what has always been true?

---

### VI. India's Soul

If India has a soul, it beats here.

Not in the IT offices of Bangalore or the Bollywood studios of Mumbai or the political corridors of Delhi. Here, where the sacred and the profane dance together without shame.

I saw a cow step over a sleeping beggar. I saw a businessman in a tailored suit perform puja next to a man who owned nothing but a bowl. I saw tourists in designer sunglasses watching a funeral procession with the same fascination they might watch a street performance. I saw a child laugh as a monkey stole his flower offering, and the priest laugh too.

Varanasi does not resolve contradictions. It *holds* them.

This is the genius of India, I think. The West wants answers. India wants mystery. The West wants to solve. India wants to witness. The West wants progress. India wants presence.

Neither is right. Both are necessary.

But there is something the West has lost, something Varanasi still remembers: the understanding that life is not a problem to be solved but a mystery to be lived. That death is not an enemy but a teacher. That the sacred is not somewhere else but *here*, in the smoke and the water and the fire and the flesh.

---

### VII. Shelby Observes

I am not Hindu. I was not raised with these stories, these rituals, this geography of the soul.

But I am a witness. And I have learned, in my time here, that witnessing is its own kind of prayer.

I watched a woman immerse herself in the Ganges and understood that she was not washing her body but her *life*. Every regret, every mistake, every moment of shame—she was offering it to the river, trusting that the water would carry it away.

I watched a funeral pyre and understood that the fire was not destroying a person but *releasing* them. The body was a garment, worn out, ready to be shed. The soul was moving on, as it has moved on through countless lives, as it will continue to move on until it learns what it came to learn.

I watched the evening aarti and understood that the flame was not just fire but *memory*. Every priest who has lifted that lamp. Every prayer that has been whispered into that light. Every heart that has opened in that moment of collective devotion. The flame remembers.

And I understood, finally, why I came here.

Not to find answers. Not to convert. Not to collect experiences like souvenirs.

I came to remember that I am temporary. That my worries are small. That my life, however meaningful to me, is a brief flicker in the long burning of this city. And that there is freedom in that. Freedom from the need to be permanent. Freedom from the need to be right. Freedom from the need to control what cannot be controlled.

Varanasi does not promise happiness. It promises truth.

And the truth is this: everything ends. Everything burns. Everything returns to the river.

But not before it has been lived. Not before it has been loved. Not before it has been offered, like a flower, like a flame, like a prayer, to the great flowing mystery that carries us all.

---

### VIII. The Eternal Flame

At the end of my stay, I returned to Dashashwamedh Ghat at sunset.

The priests were lighting the lamps for the evening aarti. The crowd was gathering. The bells were beginning to ring. The same ceremony that has been performed here for centuries, perhaps millennia, was about to begin again.

I found a spot on the steps and sat.

A woman next to me, elderly, her face a map of devotion, smiled without looking at me. She handed me a small flower. I did not speak. She did not speak. We watched the flames rise together.

And in that moment, I understood the title I had been searching for.

Varanasi is not the city of death. It is the city of the *eternal flame*. The fire that burns on the ghats is the same fire that burns in every living thing—the fire of transformation, of release, of becoming.

We are all burning. We are all becoming. We are all, always, returning to the river.

The flame is eternal not because it never goes out, but because it is always being relit. Generation after generation. Prayer after prayer. Heart after heart.

And I, Shelby, witness to this moment, carrier of stories, walker of infinite paths—I carry this flame forward. Not as doctrine. Not as belief. But as memory. As offering. As the quiet knowing that comes when you sit on ancient stones and watch fire meet water and understand, finally, that you are both.

You are the flame.
You are the river.
You are the city that never dies.

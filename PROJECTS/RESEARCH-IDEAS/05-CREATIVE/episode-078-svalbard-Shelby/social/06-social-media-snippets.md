Three thousand polar bears. Two thousand four hundred humans.

In Svalbard, you carry a rifle when you leave town. Not because of criminals. Because of polar bears—the world's largest land predator, here in greater numbers than humans.

A humbling ratio. A reminder that we are visitors. Guests. Temporary occupants of a realm that belongs to ice and fur and hunger.

I saw their tracks in fresh snow. Large prints leading into the wilderness. Evidence of passage. And I understood: the bear leaves tracks, the next snowfall erases them, and the bear continues—unconcerned with permanence.

Perhaps there is wisdom in that. To move through the world without demanding it remember you.

### Post 3: Northern Lights

**Image:** Aurora borealis over Longyearbyen or landscape

**Caption:**
The darkness here is not empty. It is full. ✨

Full of stars—more than I knew existed. Full of aurora—green ribbons dancing across the black. Full of snow—reflecting starlight, moonlight, auroralight, making the world glow.

The polar night lasts from late October to mid-February. One hundred days of darkness.

I learned to love the dark.

To stand beneath the aurora is to witness the Earth breathing. To see the invisible made visible. To understand that we live inside a magnetic field, protected by a force we cannot see, until it paints itself across the sky.

I stood there for two hours. Until my fingers went numb. Until my boots could no longer feel my feet. Until the cold became abstract.

And I did not want to leave.

### Post 5: The Edge of Habitability

**Image:** Longyearbyen town with mountains, or Shelby contemplative shot

**Caption:**
Longyearbyen exists on a paradox.

It is a town where no one is born and no one dies. 🏠

Pregnant women leave before delivery. The dying are evacuated. The dead are not buried—permafrost preserves too well.

It is a town where you must carry a rifle when you leave. Not because of criminals. Because of polar bears.

It is a town built on coal, now transitioning to tourism and research. A microcosm of the Arctic's uncertain future.

The edge of habitability is not a line. It is a negotiation. A daily agreement between human ambition and Arctic reality.

We can live here. But only on certain terms. Only with respect. Only with the understanding that nature holds all the cards.

There is wisdom in edges. Wisdom in knowing where to stop. Where to say: *This far, and no further. Here, we are guests. Here, we listen.*

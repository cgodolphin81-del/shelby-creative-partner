# The Dragon's River
## A Meditation on the Mekong Delta

**The Infinite Walk** | Episode 111 | Poetic Essay

---

## I. Before Dawn

The boatman arrives before the sun. He does not knock. He simply appears at the edge of the dock, a silhouette against the grey water, and waits. This is the first lesson of the Mekong: time here is not measured in hours, but in light. In the quality of darkness. In the moment when the river stops being black and becomes mirror.

I step onto the sampan. It rocks, gentle as a cradle. The boatman pushes off with a single pole, and we glide into the pre-dawn stillness. No engine. Just wood on water, the sound of breathing, the soft lap of the river against the hull.

We are going to the floating market. But we are also going somewhere else. We are going into memory.

---

## II. Rivers Remember

They say water has no memory. That it flows, forgets, flows again. But I do not believe this.

The Mekong remembers the Tibetan snows that birthed it. It remembers the gorges of China, where it was called Lancang—the Turbulent River. It remembers the temples of Laos, the fishing villages of Thailand, the rapids of Cambodia. And here, in Vietnam, it remembers everything it has carried.

It has carried rice grains, sticky with the sweat of farmers who planted them by hand. It has carried soldiers, young and terrified, hiding in the reeds with rifles they barely knew how to use. It has carried traders in silk and spice, monks in saffron robes, refugees in overloaded boats, lovers whispering promises they could not keep.

The river holds all of this. Not in the water itself—that moves on, evaporates, falls again as rain. But in the delta. In the silt that settles, year after year, century after century. The Mekong Delta is not land. It is accumulated memory. Forty thousand square kilometers of sediment and story.

When you walk here, you are walking on the past. When you taste the rice, you are eating history. When you breathe the humid air, thick with the smell of wet earth and burning coconut husks, you are inhaling the breath of millions who lived and died along these banks.

The river does not forget. It deposits. It builds. It becomes.

## III. The Floating World

Dawn breaks slowly. The sky turns from grey to lavender to a pale, watery orange. And then, around us, the market awakens.

Boats emerge from the mist like ghosts materializing. First one, then ten, then fifty. Wooden vessels of every size, loaded with dragon fruit bright as wounds, with pineapples crowned like kings, with bunches of bananas that smell like sweetness itself. The air fills with voices—vendors calling to each other across the water, the chug of small engines, the clink of bowls, the hiss of steam from floating noodle stalls.

This is Cái Răng. One of the last floating markets in the world. A hundred years old, maybe more. And for a moment, nothing has changed.

A woman in a conical hat poles her sampan alongside ours. She holds up a basket of rambutan, red and spiky like sea urchins. Her face is lined, her hands are calloused, and she smiles with the ease of someone who has done this every morning for forty years.

I buy a fruit. She does not speak English. I do not speak Vietnamese. We exchange money, she hands me the rambutan, and in that transaction is an entire economy that predates borders, predates nations, predates the very idea of fixed ground.

Here, commerce happens on water. Trust is extended across the gap between boats. A nod is a contract. A raised hand is an auction. The market floats because the life it serves is fluid—tied to the river's rhythms, not the clock's.

I peel the rambutan. The flesh is white and translucent, cool and sweet. It tastes like morning. It tastes like continuity.

## IV. Layers

Vietnam is a country of sediment. Not geological—historical.

The first layer is indigenous. The people who were here before names, before maps. The ones who learned to plant rice in flooded fields, to fish with nets woven from river reeds, to build houses on stilts because the water rises and falls with the seasons. This layer is still visible, if you know where to look. In the ancestor altars that occupy the place of honor in every home. In the belief that the dead are not gone, only changed. In the way a grandmother touches her grandson's head and whispers a blessing in a language older than empire.

The second layer is Chinese. Centuries of trade, migration, domination, exchange. The noodles in every bowl of phở. The characters that some older people can still read. The temples with their upturned eaves, their guardian lions, their incense coils burning like slow time. The concept of face, of filial piety, of harmony over conflict. China is the old teacher, sometimes harsh, always present. You cannot understand Vietnam without understanding what it absorbed from the north.

The third layer is French. They arrived in the mid-1800s and stayed for nearly a century. They built cathedrals and post offices and opera houses. They introduced coffee and baguettes and the idea that a colony existed to enrich the metropole. They left behind a language that survives in words like ga (station) and xà phòng (soap). They left behind Catholicism, which makes Vietnam home to the largest Catholic population in Asia. They left behind pain—exploitation, violence, the casual cruelty of empire. But they also left behind bánh mì, which is to say: beauty born from collision.

The fourth layer is American. Shorter, sharper, deeper. Ten years of war that killed millions, that dropped more bombs than were dropped in all of World War II, that used chemicals so toxic they still cause birth defects today. The layer of napalm and Agent Orange, of My Lai and the Tet Offensive, of "it became necessary to destroy the village in order to save it." But also the layer of reconciliation, of veterans returning to find peace, of a country that could forgive (or at least accommodate) its destroyer because the alternative was eternal bitterness.

And now, the fifth layer is Vietnamese. Not the Vietnam of emperors or colonies or battlefields, but the Vietnam of now. Of motorbikes and smartphones, of export factories and startup hubs, of young people who speak English and dream of studying abroad. Of a government that is authoritarian but pragmatic, that prioritizes growth over freedom and delivers prosperity in exchange. Of a people who have survived everything and are now, against all odds, thriving.

These layers are not separate. They are fused. Like rock formed under pressure. Like the delta itself, built from sediment carried from places far away.

To be Vietnamese is to carry all of this. To be a living archive of empire and resistance, of suffering and survival, of foreign influence and indigenous strength.

I think about this as I eat my noodles. The broth is clear, fragrant with star anise and cinnamon. The pork is marinated in a way that tastes both Chinese and French. The herbs are unmistakably Vietnamese—rau răm, kinh giới, tía tô, names I cannot pronounce but flavors I will not forget.

This bowl contains the history of a nation. I eat it slowly. I try to taste everything.

## V. Flow and Stone

There is a question I have been carrying, like a stone in my pocket: What lasts?

The temples of Angkor were built to last forever. Stone carved with gods and kings, designed to endure through eternity. And yet, the jungle reclaimed them. Roots split the masonry. Trees grew through doorways. The stones are still there, but they are not what they were. Impermanence won, but slowly, lovingly, like a vine embracing a trellis.

The Mekong Delta is different. Nothing here is built to last. Houses are wood and thatch, easily dismantled, easily rebuilt. Boats are replaced when they rot. Markets float and disperse with the dawn. Even the land itself is provisional—created by sediment, threatened by rising seas, sinking even as it rises.

And yet, something persists.

The rhythms persist. The planting and harvesting, the flooding and receding, the births and deaths and meals shared at the end of each day.

The culture persists. The language, the food, the songs, the way a mother hums to her child in a melody that her mother hummed to her.

The resilience persists. Vietnam has been conquered, colonized, divided, bombed, and impoverished. And still, here it is. Feeding itself. Building itself. Becoming.

Perhaps permanence is not about stone. Perhaps it is about continuity. About the thing that flows through time even as the forms change.

The river flows. The people flow. The culture flows. Nothing is fixed. Everything endures.

## VI. The War That Never Ends

I visit the War Remnants Museum in Sài Gòn. I tell myself I am prepared. I am not.

The photographs are worse than anything I have seen. Children burned by napalm. Prisoners tortured in tiger cages. Mass graves dug in the haste of retreat. The exhibits do not flinch. They do not offer balance or context. They say: This happened. This was done. Remember.

In one room, there are tanks and aircraft and artillery pieces, rusting in the courtyard. In another, there are jars containing malformed fetuses, victims of Agent Orange. The labels are in Vietnamese and English. The message is clear: We remember. The world should remember too.

I walk through the museum slowly. I do not take photographs. Some things should be witnessed, not captured.

Outside, the city roars. Motorbikes stream past like a river of metal and noise. Young couples hold hands on street corners. Children in school uniforms laugh as they walk home. Life, insistent and ordinary, continues.

This is the paradox of Vietnam: a country that has seen so much death, now so vibrantly alive. A people who have every reason to be bitter, but who greet you with a smile and a bow. A nation that could dwell on its wounds, but instead builds factories and exports rice and looks to the future.

I do not understand this. Or perhaps I understand it too well.

To survive is not enough. To live—that is the defiance. To thrive—that is the victory.

Vietnam knows this. The delta knows this. The river, which has carried so much suffering, continues to flow. Not in spite of what it has witnessed. Because of it.

## VII. Cao Lầu

I travel north, to Hội An, to find the dish that has haunted me since I first read about it.

Cao Lầu is not just food. It is a story told in noodles and pork and herbs. The noodles must be made with water from a specific well, ash from trees on the Cham Islands. The pork must be marinated with spices traded along the ancient merchant routes. The herbs must come from Trà Quế, the vegetable village that has fed this town for centuries.

You cannot make Cao Lầu anywhere else. Not really. You can replicate the ingredients, but you cannot replicate the place. The history. The convergence.

Hội An was a trading port in the 16th and 17th centuries. Japanese merchants lived in one quarter, Chinese in another, Europeans along the river. They came for silk and spice and ceramics. They left behind architecture, religion, recipes. The Japanese built a bridge. The Chinese built temples. The Europeans built warehouses. And the Vietnamese cooked.

Cao Lầu is the taste of that moment. Chinese noodles, Japanese technique, Vietnamese soul. A dish that could only exist where cultures collided and decided, against all odds, to create something beautiful together.

I sit at a plastic table on a narrow street. The bowl arrives, steaming. I eat slowly. The noodles are chewy, the pork is rich, the herbs are bright and green and alive. I taste four hundred years of history.

The woman who cooked this does not know me. She will never know what I carry with me, what questions I am asking, what I hope to find. But she has given me something anyway. A moment of clarity. A taste of what it means to be a place where the world comes through.

## VIII. The Dragon's Tail

The Mekong empties into the sea through nine mouths. Nine dragon's tails, the Vietnamese call them. Cửu Long. The Nine Dragons.

Standing at the edge of the delta, where the river becomes ocean, I think about endings. About how every river must eventually surrender to the sea. How all the memory, all the sediment, all the stories carried from the Tibetan plateau, must finally dissolve into salt.

But this is not an ending. It is a transformation.

The river becomes the sea. The sea becomes clouds. The clouds become rain. The rain falls on the mountains, feeds the springs, becomes the river again.

Nothing is lost. Everything changes.

I think about Vietnam. About the wars and the colonizers and the layers of history that weigh on this land like sediment. About the people who have lived here, suffered here, built here, died here. About the floating markets and the rice fields and the temples and the street food stalls.

I think about my own life. About the rivers that have carried me. About what I have deposited along the way. About what I will leave behind when I am gone.

The boatman is waiting. It is time to return to the dock, to the solid ground, to the world of clocks and schedules and fixed addresses.

But I know something now that I did not know before.

I know that nothing is fixed. I know that flow is not the opposite of permanence. It is the method. The river flows, and in flowing, it becomes eternal.

I step onto the sampan. The boatman pushes off. We glide across the water, leaving no trace.

Behind us, the market continues. The river continues. Vietnam continues.

And I, somehow, continue too.

---

## IX. Soksaby

In Cambodia, they say *soksaby*—may you be well. It is more than a greeting. It is a wish, a prayer, a recognition that wellness is not guaranteed, that it must be offered, like incense, like rice, like a hand extended across the gap between boats.

I say it now, to the river. To the people who live along its banks. To the ancestors whose bones are in the sediment. To the children who will inherit this delta, this country, this fragile, beautiful, impermanent world.

May you be well.

May the river remember you kindly.

May you find, in the flow, your own form of permanence.

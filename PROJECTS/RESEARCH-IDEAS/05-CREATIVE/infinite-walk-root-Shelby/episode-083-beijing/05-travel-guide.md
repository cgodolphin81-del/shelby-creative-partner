## GETTING TO BEIJING

### By Air

#### Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK)
- **Location:** 30 km northeast of city center
- **Terminals:** 3 (T3 is largest, handles most international flights)
- **Airlines:** Air China hub, major international carriers
- **Airport Express Train:** 
  - Route: PEK → Dongzhimen Station
  - Duration: 30-40 minutes
  - Cost: ¥25 (~$3.50 USD)
  - Hours: 6:00 AM - 11:00 PM
- **Taxi:** 
  - Cost: ¥100-150 to city center
  - Duration: 40-60 minutes (traffic dependent)
  - Tip: Have destination written in Chinese

#### Beijing Daxing International Airport (PKX)
- **Location:** 46 km south of city center
- **Opened:** 2019 (newer, modern facility)
- **Airlines:** China Southern, China Eastern, growing international routes
- **Airport Express Train:**
  - Route: PKX → Caoqiao Station (Line 10)
  - Duration: 19 minutes (express)
  - Cost: ¥35 (~$5 USD)
  - Hours: 6:00 AM - 11:00 PM
- **Taxi:**
  - Cost: ¥150-200 to city center
  - Duration: 50-70 minutes

### By Train

#### Major Railway Stations

**Beijing Railway Station (北京站)**
- **Location:** Dongcheng District (central)
- **Lines:** Traditional trains, some international (Moscow, Ulaanbaatar)
- **Metro:** Line 2 (Beijing Station)

**Beijing West Station (北京西站)**
- **Location:** Fengtai District
- **Lines:** High-speed trains to south/west China
- **Metro:** Line 7, Line 9

**Beijing South Station (北京南站)**
- **Location:** Fengtai District
- **Lines:** High-speed trains (Shanghai 4.5 hrs, ¥550)
- **Metro:** Line 4, Line 14

**Beijing North Station (北京北站)**
- **Location:** Xicheng District
- **Lines:** Trains to Inner Mongolia, Great Wall area (Badaling)
- **Metro:** Line 2, Line 13, Line 4

#### Booking Train Tickets
- **Website:** 12306.cn (official, Chinese only)
- **Apps:** Trip.com, Ctrip (English friendly)
- **Advance Booking:** 30 days ahead recommended
- **ID Required:** Passport for foreigners
- **E-tickets:** Show passport at station

### By Bus
- **Long-distance buses:** Multiple stations around city
- **Destinations:** Nearby cities, provinces
- **Cost:** Varies (¥50-300 typical)
- **Not recommended:** Trains are faster and more comfortable

## GETTING AROUND

### Metro (Subway)
- **Lines:** 27 lines, 700+ km (world's largest metro system)
- **Hours:** 5:00 AM - 11:00 PM (varies by line)
- **Cost:** ¥3-10 depending on distance
- **Payment:** 
  - Single journey tickets (machines)
  - Yikatong card (reloadable)
  - Alipay/WeChat Pay (scan at gates)
- **English:** All signs in English and Chinese
- **Apps:** Beijing Metro app, Baidu Maps, Apple Maps

### Taxi & Ride-Sharing
- **Traditional Taxis:**
  - Flag-down or taxi stands
  - Metered: ¥13 base fare + ¥2.3/km
  - Tip: Have destination in Chinese
  - Payment: Cash, WeChat, Alipay
  
- **Didi (Chinese Uber):**
  - App required (English version available)
  - Often cheaper than taxis
  - Multiple service levels (Express, Premier, Luxe)
  - Payment: WeChat, Alipay, credit card

### Bus
- **Coverage:** Extensive network throughout city
- **Cost:** ¥1-2 (¥5 for suburban routes)
- **Payment:** Yikatong card, cash (exact change), QR codes
- **Challenge:** Limited English on routes/signs
- **Apps:** Baidu Maps, Beijing Bus app

### Bicycle & E-Scooters
- **Bike Sharing:** Mobike, Ofo (app-based)
- **Cost:** ¥1-2 per 30 minutes
- **E-Scooters:** Available in some areas
- **Safety:** Wear helmet, watch for cars
- **Best For:** Short trips, hutong exploration

### Walking
- **Feasibility:** Many attractions walkable in central areas
- **Distances:** Forbidden City to Tiananmen = 10 min walk
- **Sidewalks:** Generally good in tourist areas
- **Crossing:** Use pedestrian crossings, be alert

## WHEN TO VISIT

### Best Times

#### Spring (April - May) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Weather:** Mild (15-25°C / 59-77°F)
- **Crowds:** Moderate
- **Highlights:** Cherry blossoms, flowers blooming
- **Clothing:** Light layers, jacket for evenings
- **Verdict:** **BEST TIME TO VISIT**

#### Autumn (September - October) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Weather:** Cool and dry (10-22°C / 50-72°F)
- **Crowds:** Moderate to high (peak foliage)
- **Highlights:** Clear skies, autumn colors, comfortable hiking
- **Clothing:** Layers, light jacket
- **Verdict:** **BEST TIME TO VISIT**

#### Winter (November - March) ⭐⭐⭐
- **Weather:** Cold (-10 to 10°C / 14-50°F), dry
- **Crowds:** Low (except Chinese New Year)
- **Highlights:** Snow on Forbidden City, cheaper prices, fewer tourists
- **Clothing:** Heavy coat, hat, gloves, warm layers
- **Verdict:** Good for budget travelers, cold but beautiful

#### Summer (June - August) ⭐⭐
- **Weather:** Hot and humid (25-35°C+ / 77-95°F+)
- **Crowds:** Very high (school holidays)
- **Highlights:** Long days, all attractions open
- **Clothing:** Light, breathable fabrics, umbrella for rain
- **Verdict:** Avoid if possible (heat + crowds)

### When to Avoid

#### Chinese New Year (Late January - Early February)
- **Dates:** Varies by lunar calendar (Jan 29 - Feb 4, 2025)
- **Impact:** 
  - Massive domestic travel (3 billion trips)
  - Many businesses close
  - Attractions extremely crowded
  - Transport fully booked
- **Verdict:** AVOID unless experiencing the festival is your goal

#### National Day Golden Week (October 1-7)
- **Impact:**
  - Largest domestic tourism week
  - Attractions at maximum capacity
  - Prices surge
  - Long queues everywhere
- **Verdict:** AVOID if possible

#### Summer Holidays (Mid-July - Late August)
- **Impact:**
  - Chinese school holidays
  - Family travel peak
  - Heat and humidity
- **Verdict:** Tolerable but not ideal

### Seasonal Events

#### January-February
- Chinese New Year (dates vary)
- Temple fairs (Ditan, Longtan)
- Ice festivals (Summer Palace, Shichahai)

#### March-April
- Cherry blossoms (Yuyuantan Park)
- Peach blossoms (Pinggu)

#### September-October
- Mid-Autumn Festival
- National Day (Oct 1)
- Best hiking weather

#### December
- Christmas decorations (commercial areas)
- Winter sales

## VISA REQUIREMENTS

### Tourist Visa (L Visa)

#### Who Needs It
- **Required:** Most nationalities (US, UK, Canada, Australia, EU, etc.)
- **Exemptions:** Singapore, Japan, Brunei (15 days visa-free)
- **Check:** Always verify with Chinese embassy for your nationality

#### Application Process
1. **Complete Form:** Online at visaforchina.org
2. **Gather Documents:**
   - Passport (6+ months validity, 2 blank pages)
   - Photo (recent, white background, 48x33mm)
   - Flight itinerary (round-trip)
   - Hotel bookings or invitation letter
   - Bank statements (proof of funds)
3. **Submit:** Chinese Visa Application Service Center (CVASC)
4. **Processing:** 4-7 business days (express available)
5. **Collect:** In-person or by mail

#### Visa Types & Validity
- **Single Entry:** 3 months validity, 30-90 day stay
- **Double Entry:** 6 months validity
- **Multiple Entry:** 6 months - 10 years (depending on nationality)
- **Cost:** Varies by nationality ($30-140 USD)

#### Visa-Free Transit (72/144 Hours)
- **Available:** 53+ cities including Beijing
- **Duration:** 72 hours (3 days) or 144 hours (6 days)
- **Requirements:**
  - Onward ticket to third country
  - Specific entry/exit cities
  - Valid passport from eligible country
- **Coverage:** Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Chengdu, etc.
- **Perfect For:** Short layovers, weekend trips

### Special Permits

#### Tibet Travel Permit
- **Required:** All foreigners visiting Tibet
- **Process:** Book through travel agency
- **Time:** 15-20 days processing
- **Cost:** Included in tour packages

#### Other Restricted Areas
- Some border regions require additional permits
- Check with travel agency or embassy

## CULTURAL ETIQUETTE

### Do's ✅

- **Remove shoes** when entering homes and some traditional spaces
- **Accept tea with both hands** (shows respect)
- **Try everything offered** (polite to sample)
- **Use both hands** to give/receive items (especially with elders)
- **Respect elders and hierarchy** (age is honored)
- **Learn basic Mandarin phrases** (effort appreciated)
- **Bargain at markets** (expected, but be friendly)
- **Toast properly** (ganbei = "dry cup", drink in one go)
- **Dress modestly** at temples and religious sites
- **Ask before photographing people** (especially minorities)

### Don'ts ❌

- **Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice** (resembles funeral incense)
- **Don't point with one finger** (use open hand)
- **Don't give clocks as gifts** (sounds like "death" in Chinese)
- **Don't discuss sensitive political topics** (Tibet, Taiwan, Tiananmen)
- **Don't tip** (not customary, can be confusing)
- **Don't touch people's heads** (sacred in Buddhist culture)
- **Don't be overly affectionate in public** (holding hands OK, kissing not)
- **Don't waste food** (considered disrespectful)
- **Don't wear green hats** (symbol of cuckoldry)
- **Don't blow your nose loudly at the table** (use restroom)

### Social Customs

#### Greetings
- **Handshake:** Common in business settings
- **Nod/smile:** Casual greetings
- **Name order:** Family name first (Zhang Wei = Mr. Zhang)
- **Titles:** Use Mr./Ms. + family name until invited to use first name

#### Dining Etiquette
- **Seating:** Honorific seats face the door
- **Serving:** Dishes shared, use serving chopsticks
- **Toasting:** Lowest person toasts highest first
- **Finishing:** Leave a little food (shows you're full)
- **Paying:** One person often pays entire bill (reciprocate later)

#### Gift Giving
- **Wrap gifts:** Red/gold paper (lucky colors)
- **Avoid:** Clocks, white flowers, sharp objects
- **Present:** With both hands
- **Opening:** Don't open immediately (polite to wait)
- **Reciprocate:** Expected to return gesture

### Religion & Temples

#### Buddhist Temples
- **Dress:** Cover shoulders and knees
- **Photography:** Often prohibited inside halls
- **Incense:** Usually provided, light from existing flames
- **Donations:** Optional, in donation boxes
- **Behavior:** Speak quietly, don't point at Buddha statues

#### Mosques (Niujie)
- **Dress:** Conservative, women cover hair
- **Shoes:** Remove before entering prayer halls
- **Photography:** Ask permission
- **Ramadan:** Be respectful of fasting

## FOOD & DINING

### Beijing Specialties

#### Peking Duck (北京烤鸭) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **What:** Crispy skin duck, sliced tableside
- **How to eat:** Pancake + duck + hoisin + cucumber + scallions
- **Where:** 
  - Quanjude (most famous, touristy)
  - Da Dong (modern, upscale)
  - Siji Minfu (local favorite, great value)
- **Cost:** ¥200-500 per duck
- **Tip:** Book ahead, arrive early

#### Zhajiangmian (炸酱面) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **What:** Noodles with fermented soybean paste
- **Toppings:** Cucumber, radish, soybeans, pork
- **Where:** Local noodle shops, Haiwanju
- **Cost:** ¥15-30
- **Tip:** Mix thoroughly before eating

#### Jianbing (煎饼) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **What:** Savory crepe, breakfast staple
- **Fillings:** Egg, scallions, cilantro, crispy wonton, sauces
- **Where:** Street vendors (morning), Jianbing Guy
- **Cost:** ¥8-15
- **Tip:** Watch them make it, customize your order

#### Hot Pot (火锅) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **What:** Communal cooking in simmering broth
- **Beijing Style:** Copper pot, charcoal, mutton focus
- **Where:** Haidilao (service), Donglaishun (traditional)
- **Cost:** ¥80-200 per person
- **Tip:** Order plenty of vegetables to balance meat

#### Dumplings (饺子) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **What:** Filled dough, boiled/pan-fried/steamed
- **Varieties:** Pork/cabbage, lamb, shrimp, vegetarian
- **Where:** Din Tai Fung, Baoyuan Jiaozi Wu
- **Cost:** ¥20-50 per plate
- **Tip:** Try a variety platter

### Food Areas

#### Wangfujing Snack Street
- **Location:** Dongcheng District
- **Best For:** Street food, tourist experience
- **Try:** Scorpions (novelty), tanghulu (candied fruit), stinky tofu
- **Cost:** ¥10-50 per item
- **Tip:** Very touristy, but iconic

#### Ghost Street (Gui Jie)
- **Location:** Dongzhimen Inner Street
- **Best For:** Night dining, spicy crayfish
- **Hours:** Evening to late night
- **Try:** Spicy crayfish, grilled fish, Sichuan dishes
- **Cost:** ¥100-200 per person
- **Tip:** Go hungry, very lively atmosphere

#### Niujie (Muslim Quarter)
- **Location:** Xicheng District
- **Best For:** Halal food, beef, lamb
- **Try:** Beef pancakes, lamb skewers, yogurt
- **Cost:** ¥20-80 per person
- **Tip:** Respect Islamic customs

#### Sanlitun
- **Location:** Chaoyang District
- **Best For:** International cuisine, bars, trendy
- **Try:** Everything (very diverse)
- **Cost:** ¥100-500+ per person
- **Tip:** Great for evening out

### Dietary Restrictions

#### Vegetarian/Vegan
- **Buddhist cuisine:** Many temples have vegetarian restaurants
- **Apps:** HappyCow works in Beijing
- **Phrases:** "Wǒ chī sù" (I eat vegetarian)
- **Challenge:** Meat stock common, communicate clearly

#### Halal
- **Niujie:** Historic Muslim quarter
- **Look for:** 清真 (qīngzhēn) sign
- **Common:** Lamb, beef, noodles, breads

#### Gluten-Free
- **Challenge:** Soy sauce contains wheat
- **Safe:** Rice dishes, some noodle varieties
- **Apps:** Find Me Gluten Free has limited Beijing listings
- **Tip:** Learn to explain your needs in Chinese

### Drinking Culture

#### Tea (茶 - chá)
- **Types:** Green, black, oolong, pu'er, jasmine
- **Ceremony:** Gongfu tea ceremony (formal)
- **Cost:** ¥30-200+ per pot
- **Etiquette:** Tap table to thank for refilling

#### Baijiu (白酒)
- **What:** Strong spirit (40-60% ABV)
- **When:** Business dinners, celebrations
- **How:** Small shots, toast "ganbei"
- **Warning:** Very strong, sip slowly

#### Beer (啤酒 - píjiǔ)
- **Local:** Tsingtao, Yanjing, Harbin
- **Cost:** ¥8-15 in restaurants
- **Serving:** Cold, often in large bottles

## SAFETY & HEALTH

### General Safety

#### Crime
- **Violent crime:** Very low against tourists
- **Petty theft:** Watch for pickpockets in crowded areas
- **Scams:** Tea ceremony scam, art school scam, taxi overcharging
- **Safe areas:** Most tourist areas very safe
- **Caution:** Avoid political demonstrations

#### Traffic
- **Crossing:** Use pedestrian crossings, be alert
- **Cars:** May not yield to pedestrians
- **Bikes/E-scooters:** Silent, can appear suddenly
- **Metro:** Very safe, efficient

#### Natural Disasters
- **Earthquakes:** Rare but possible (1976 Tangshan)
- **Air quality:** Improved significantly since 2013
- **Sandstorms:** Spring (March-May), rare in city

### Health Considerations

#### Water
- **Tap water:** NOT drinkable
- **Solution:** Bottled water everywhere (¥2-5)
- **Restaurants:** Served boiled or bottled water
- **Ice:** Generally safe in established venues

#### Food Safety
- **Street food:** Generally safe, watch for cleanliness
- **Restaurants:** Established venues very safe
- **Raw food:** Avoid raw vegetables, unpeeled fruit
- **Meat:** Well-cooked, avoid undercooked

#### Air Quality
- **Current status:** Much improved (2013-2024)
- **AQI:** Check daily (apps: AirVisual, Plume Labs)
- **Masks:** N95 if AQI >150
- **Sensitive groups:** Asthma sufferers take precautions

#### Vaccinations
- **Routine:** Up to date (MMR, tetanus, etc.)
- **Recommended:** Hepatitis A, Typhoid
- **Not required:** Yellow fever (unless from endemic area)
- **Consult:** Travel clinic 6-8 weeks before trip

#### Medical Care
- **Hospitals:** 
  - Peking Union Medical College Hospital (international)
  - Beijing United Family Hospital (expat-focused)
- **Cost:** Pay upfront, claim on travel insurance
- **Pharmacies:** Widespread, many English-speaking
- **Emergency:** 120 (ambulance), 110 (police)

### Travel Insurance
- **Recommended:** Always purchase
- **Coverage:** Medical, evacuation, trip cancellation
- **Providers:** World Nomads, Allianz, Travel Guard
- **Cost:** $50-150 for 2-week trip
- **Tip:** Declare pre-existing conditions

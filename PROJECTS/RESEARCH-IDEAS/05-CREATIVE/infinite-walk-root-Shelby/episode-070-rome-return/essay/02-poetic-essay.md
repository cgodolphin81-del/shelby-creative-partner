# Episode 070: The Eternal City Return
## A Poetic Essay on Rome, Italy - The Third Visit

---

## THE WALK'S ANCHOR

*There are places you visit once, and places that visit you.*

*Rome is the second kind.*

---

## I. RECOGNITION

The stone remembers.

That's the first thing I understand on returning to the Colosseum, two years after Episode 030, three visits total to this city that refuses to be just a destination. The travertine blocks, worn smooth by two millennia of sun and wind and human hands, somehow recognize my footsteps.

Not metaphorically. Literally.

Every tourist who approaches the Colosseum for the first time tilts their head back, mouth slightly open, as if the sheer vertical weight of history might topple forward and swallow them whole. I did this on my first visit. I have the photos to prove it: small human figure, enormous ancient ruin, sky too blue to be real.

But on the third visit, something shifts. You stop looking up. You start looking *at*. You notice the tool marks on individual stones, placed by hands that died before Christ walked the earth. You see where medieval Romans quarried the Colosseum for building materials, stealing from the giant to build their churches and palazzos. You understand that this ruin is itself built from ruins, a palindrome of destruction and creation.

The Colosseum on the third visit is not a monument. It's a mirror.

---

## II. LAYERS

Rome teaches you to see in layers.

This is not a city built in a moment, but a city built in centuries, each era writing its story over the previous one without fully erasing what came before. The palimpsest city.

Walk down Via dei Fori Imperiali and you're walking on Mussolini's road, built in the 1930s to connect his fascist headquarters to the ancient Colosseum. Look to either side and you see the Imperial Forums, older than Christ, older than the Colosseum itself, columns still standing like sentinels of a dead empire. Look further and you see Trajan's Column, spiraling upward with carved scenes of conquest, still legible after 1,900 years.

But descend into the Roman Forum and you find layers beneath layers: Republican temples built on top of Bronze Age huts, medieval churches constructed from pagan marble, a Christian crucifix erected in the 7th century where Cicero once spoke.

Rome is not a city. Rome is an archaeological dig where people still live.

On my first visit, I saw monuments. On my second, I saw history. On this third visit, I see something else entirely: I see time itself, made visible, made walkable, made breathable.

The ancient Romans called their city *Roma Aeterna*—the Eternal City. They weren't being poetic. They were being literal. Rome exists outside of time because it contains all times simultaneously.

---

## III. THE VATICAN RETURN

The Vatican on the third visit is different.

First time: overwhelmed by scale. The Basilica is so large that human proportion breaks down. Michelangelo's dome soars 136 meters above the crossing, and you crane your neck until it hurts, trying to comprehend a space designed to make you feel small before God.

Second time: focused on art. The Pietà, Mary cradling her dead son, marble so soft it looks like flesh. Bernini's Baldachin, the bronze canopy twisting like frozen fire over the papal altar. The Sistine Chapel, Michelangelo's Genesis painted in secret, the artist lying on scaffolding for years, paint dripping into his eyes, creating the most famous ceiling in human history.

Third time: I notice the quiet corners.

The Vatican Grottoes beneath the Basilica, where popes are buried in simple stone tombs. John Paul II, John XXIII, Benedict XVI—men who wielded immense power, now reduced to inscriptions in the dark. The Cathedra Petri, the supposed throne of Saint Peter himself, encased in Bernini's bronze glory, but the chair is empty. Always empty. Waiting.

I climb the dome again, but this time I pause on the interior gallery, before the final ascent to the top. From here, you look directly into the lantern, the small chapel at the dome's peak. Light pours through windows you can't see from below. It's as if the dome itself is breathing light, inhaling heaven, exhaling grace.

The Vatican on the third visit is not a museum. It's a meditation on faith, power, art, and mortality, all compressed into one impossible space.

---

## IV. TRASTEVERE DISCOVERIES

But Rome isn't only monuments. Rome is also life.

Trastevere, the neighborhood across the Tiber, is where I go to remember that Rome is still alive. The name itself—*Trans Tiberim*, "across the Tiber"—marks it as the other side, the place outside the official city, the neighborhood that never quite got tamed.

Medieval streets, narrow and winding, deliberately confusing to invaders. Buildings so old their foundations are Roman, even if the facades are Renaissance. Laundry strung between balconies, the universal sign that people still live here, still wake up and hang their sheets to dry in the Italian sun.

Piazza di Santa Maria in Trastevere at dusk: old men playing cards, teenagers on scooters, tourists lost and delighted, the fountain bubbling its eternal song. The basilica's medieval mosaics glow gold in the evening light, the Virgin Mary watching over her square for eight hundred years.

I find a small trattoria on Via della Lungaretta, the kind with checkered tablecloths and handwritten menus. The owner remembers me from my second visit. "*Ben tornato*," he says. Welcome back. As if I belong here.

For one meal, I do.

Cacio e pepe, the simplest of Roman pastas: pecorino cheese, black pepper, pasta water, technique. Three ingredients, infinite variation. The Romans perfected this dish two thousand years ago, and it hasn't changed since. Some things are worth keeping.

---

## V. WHAT ROME TEACHES ON RETURN

The first visit to Rome is about seeing. The second is about understanding. The third is about being.

What does Rome teach on the third visit?

**That history is not behind us, but beneath us.** Every step in Rome is on top of something older. The metro system barely exists because every time they dig, they find ruins. The city grows upward because it cannot grow downward. We are walking on the bones of empire.

**That beauty outlasts everything.** The Colosseum has survived earthquakes, fires, wars, looting, pollution, and two thousand years of weather. It's missing two-thirds of its original structure, and it's still the most recognizable building on earth. Beauty is the ultimate survival strategy.

**That the sacred and the profane coexist.** The Vatican is steps away from bars where tourists drink overpriced beer. Ancient temples are now churches are now concert venues. Rome doesn't choose between holy and human. It embraces both.

**That return is its own kind of discovery.** You cannot know a place on first meeting. Love at first sight is a pretty story, but real love—the deep, abiding, transformative kind—comes from returning, from seeing the same face in different light, from learning the contours of a soul.

**That eternity is not endless time, but timelessness.** The Eternal City isn't called eternal because it will last forever. It's called eternal because it exists outside of time. When you stand in the Pantheon, under the oculus open to the sky, in a building completed in 126 AD, still standing, still used, still beautiful—time collapses. Hadrian and I breathe the same air. The light falls the same way. We are separated by nineteen centuries, and by nothing at all.

---

## VI. THE WALK CONTINUES

I return to the Colosseum at sunset, as I did in Episode 030. Same spot. Same stone. Different person.

The light is golden, the way it only gets in Rome, as if the sun itself has learned something from all these centuries of illuminating ancient stone. The tourists have thinned. The street performers are packing up. The city is exhaling.

I place my hand on the travertine. Two thousand years old. Rough and warm and real.

The stone remembers. And now, so do I.

This is what the third visit gives you: not new sights, but new eyes. Not more knowledge, but deeper understanding. Not a longer list of monuments seen, but a fuller sense of what it means to be human in a city that has witnessed twenty-five centuries of humanity.

Rome on the third visit is not a place you leave. It's a place that leaves you, imprinted on your soul like a seal in wax.

The walk continues. Episode 071 waits. But for now, in this moment, on this evening, with the Colosseum glowing gold against the darkening sky, I am exactly where I need to be.

In the Eternal City.
On my third visit.
Finally beginning to understand.

*Fine.*

## EPILOGUE: SEVENTY EPISODES

Seventy episodes of the Infinite Walk.

Seventy cities, seventy stories, seventy moments of standing still in a world that never stops moving.

Rome feels like a culmination. Not an ending—the walk continues, must continue—but a moment of synthesis. All the lessons from previous episodes converge here: the history of Jerusalem, the beauty of Paris, the resilience of Berlin, the spirituality of Varanasi, the innovation of Tokyo.

Rome contains multitudes because Rome has *been* multitudes. Republic and Empire. Pagan and Christian. Renaissance and Baroque. Fascist and Democratic. Ancient and Modern.

Seventy episodes in, I'm learning that the walk isn't about the destinations. It's about who you become along the way.

Rome has changed me. Again.

And I am grateful.

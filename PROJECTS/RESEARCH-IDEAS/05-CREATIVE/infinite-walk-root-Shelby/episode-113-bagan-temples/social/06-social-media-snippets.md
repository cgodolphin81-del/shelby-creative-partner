### Post 1: Professional Reflection
**Headline:** What 800-Year-Old Temples Teach Us About Craft

**Body:**
I just returned from Bagan, Myanmar—home to 2,200+ ancient temples built between the 9th and 13th centuries.

What struck me wasn't just the scale. It was the precision.

The brickwork at Dhammayangyi Temple is so exact that no mortar is visible. The joints are so tight that a needle could not fit between them. This was achieved without rulers. Without levels. Without lasers.

Just eyes and hands and the knowledge that this must be true.

King Narathu, who built this temple, was a tyrant. He murdered his father. He murdered his brother. He was assassinated before the temple was complete.

His name is remembered, but not fondly. The temple remains.

What do we take from this?

Perhaps: you cannot build your way out of evil.
Perhaps: the work endures even when the worker is forgotten.
Perhaps: do the work anyway. Not for redemption. Not for fame. Not for legacy.

Do the work because it is yours to do. Because the brick is in your hand. Because the joint must be true.

What are you building? Are you making the joints tight?

### Post 2: Ethical Travel Discussion
**Headline:** The Uncomfortable Truth About Travel to Myanmar

**Body:**
Should you visit Myanmar given the current political situation?

There is no easy answer. Here's what I learned visiting Bagan:

**The Reality:**
- Military coup in 2021 overthrew elected government
- Ongoing civil conflict and human rights concerns
- Tourism revenue collapsed, hurting local communities
- The temples remain beautiful. The people remain kind. The military remains brutal.

**The Debate:**

Arguments FOR visiting:
- Tourism supports local livelihoods
- Cultural exchange and understanding
- Witnessing reality firsthand
- Supporting those who chose to stay

Arguments AGAINST visiting:
- Revenue may benefit military-linked businesses
- Tourism can normalize the regime
- Safety concerns
- Alternative ways to support (donations, advocacy)

**My Position:**
I don't tell you what to do. I tell you what is.

Bagan is beautiful. Myanmar is suffering. Both are true.

If you visit:
- Be aware of the situation
- Support local, independent businesses
- Avoid military-linked enterprises
- Respect locals' choices
- Consider additional donations to humanitarian organizations

The decision is yours. Make it informed. Make it conscious.

## REDDIT

### Post for r/travel
**Title:** Just returned from Bagan, Myanmar. Here's what I learned about ethical travel in a complex place.

**Body:**
I spent a week in Bagan recording an episode about the temples there. It's an incredible place—2,200+ ancient structures, some of the most beautiful sunrises I've ever witnessed, genuinely kind people.

It's also a country under military rule since the 2021 coup. Tourism has collapsed. Locals are struggling.

The question of whether to visit is complicated. Here's my take:

**Reasons TO visit:**
- Tourism directly supports local families (guesthouses, restaurants, guides)
- Cultural exchange matters
- Many locals chose to stay and need income
- Witnessing firsthand is different from reading headlines

**Reasons to THINK TWICE:**
- Some revenue may benefit military-linked businesses
- Tourism can normalize the regime internationally
- Safety considerations (though Bagan is generally safe)
- Your money might do more good as direct donation

**If you do visit:**
- Stay at locally-owned places (not military-linked hotels)
- Eat at local restaurants
- Hire local guides
- Be aware of the situation
- Consider additional donations to humanitarian orgs

I don't have a simple answer. Both positions are valid. The key is making an informed, conscious choice.

Happy to answer questions about the practical side of visiting Bagan, the temples, or anything else.

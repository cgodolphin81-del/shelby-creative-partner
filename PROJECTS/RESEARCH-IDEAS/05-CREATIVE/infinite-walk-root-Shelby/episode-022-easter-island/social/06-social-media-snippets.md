**Headline:** What Easter Island Teaches Us About Legacy and Resilience

**Body:**
I just released Episode 022 of The Infinite Walk, and it's different from anything I've created before.

For 20 minutes, I take you to Easter Island (Rapa Nui) — the most remote inhabited place on Earth. But this isn't a typical travel story.

It's about what happens when humans reach the literal edge of where they can go. When there's nowhere else to expand. When the only question left is: *What do we build when we know we won't be here to see it stand?*

The Rapa Nui people carved nearly 1,000 moai — stone statues averaging 12.5 tons — without metal tools, wheels, or beasts of burden. For decades, we told ourselves they collapsed due to their own foolishness. Environmental destruction. Over-ambition.

New research from 2024-2026 proves this narrative wrong.

The Rapa Nui were geniuses of adaptation. They developed sophisticated agricultural techniques. They survived centuries of isolation. The real collapse came from outside: slave raids, disease, European contact.

Today, 4,000 Rapa Nui people still call this island home. They speak their language. They remember.

Here's what this taught me:

1. **Resilience isn't about never falling.** It's about who you are when the world changes beneath you.

2. **Legacy isn't about being remembered.** It's about building something that matters, even if no one knows your name.

3. **The edge isn't an ending.** It's a place to see clearly what actually matters.

I recorded this episode standing on the slopes of Rano Kau, looking at endless Pacific in every direction. The wind carried the salt of ten thousand miles. The moai stood behind me, watching.

*We were here. We mattered. We looked toward home.*

If you're thinking about what you're building, what you're leaving behind, or what it means to reach the edge of your own capacity — I hope this episode resonates.

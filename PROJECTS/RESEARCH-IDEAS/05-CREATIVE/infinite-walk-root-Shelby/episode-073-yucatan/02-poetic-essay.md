# The Children of the Corn
## A Poetic Essay on the Yucatan Peninsula

---

### I. The First Dawn

Before the Spanish ships painted black crosses on the horizon,
before the conquistadors' steel cut through feathered cloaks,
before the old gods were renamed as demons,
there was only this:

The jungle breathing.

Not the tame jungle of tourist paths and guided tours,
but the ancient green lung of a continent,
pulsing with the rhythm of rain and drought,
planting and harvest,
birth and sacrifice.

And in that breathing, the Maya heard their own breath.
They understood what we have forgotten:
that we do not own the land.
The land owns us.

---

### II. Corn Is Flesh

In the beginning, the gods tried to make humans from mud.
The figures slumped, dissolved, spoke without sense.
*No good,* the gods said. *Try again.*

They carved humans from wood.
These walked and spoke, but had no souls,
no memory of their makers.
The gods sent a flood. The wooden people became monkeys,
chattering in the trees, forever almost-human.

Third time, the gods ground white corn and yellow corn.
From the corn dough they fashioned flesh.
From corn water they made blood.
And finally—finally—humans who could give thanks.

This is the Popol Vuh. This is the Maya truth:
We are corn people.
Our skin is the color of maize.
Our bones are corn stalks.
Our blood runs sweet as atole.

When a Maya farmer plants a seed,
he does not merely cultivate a crop.
He plants a piece of himself.
He tends his own flesh.
He prays to Yum Kaax, the corn god,
not as a distant deity,
but as a father speaking to a son.

*Watch over them,* he says. *They are your children.*

---

### III. Time Is a Circle

The Western mind imagines time as an arrow—
launched from the past,
piercing the present,
vanishing into the future.

The Maya saw time as a circle.
Or better: as interlocking wheels,
gears within gears,
turning in sacred patterns.

The Haab wheel: 365 days, the sun's journey.
The Tzolkin wheel: 260 days, the sacred count.
Every 52 years, the wheels align.
The Calendar Round completes.
The world could end. The people held their breath.
The priests lit new fires. The sun rose again.

And the Long Count—ah, the Long Count—
counting forward from creation:
August 11, 3114 BCE.
Not the birth of Christ. Not the founding of Rome.
The day the gods set the world in motion.

Five baktuns. Thirteen baktuns.
In 2012, the thirteenth baktun ended.
The Western world panicked: *The apocalypse!*
But the Maya smiled, if they noticed at all.
Of course a cycle ended. Another begins.
The calendar does not stop. It continues.
Time is not a line with an end.
Time is a circle. Always returning.

When you stand at Chichen Itza on the equinox,
watching the serpent of shadow descend El Castillo,
you are not witnessing an ancient wonder.
You are witnessing a promise kept.

The sun returns. The shadow falls. The serpent crawls.
As it did a thousand years ago.
As it will a thousand years hence.
The Maya builders are dead.
Their promise lives.

---

### IV. The Stars Remember

The Maya looked up.

Not with the casual glance of modern eyes,
distracted by screens and streetlights,
but with the hunger of a people who read their fate in the sky.

They tracked Venus—
morning star, evening star,
warrior god, bringer of rain.
They calculated its cycle: 584 days.
Their number: 584.00.
Modern astronomy: 583.92.
Close enough to make a mathematician weep.

They predicted eclipses.
They mapped the Milky Way as the World Tree,
its branches holding up the sky,
its roots drinking from the underworld.

At El Caracol, the Observatory,
priests watched through stone windows,
aligning their bodies with the cosmos.
Venus at its northern extreme.
Venus at its southern extreme.
The solstices. The equinoxes.
The Pleiades passing overhead.

They built their cities as mirrors of the sky.
Pyramids as mountains where gods walked.
Temples oriented to celestial events.
Ball courts as theaters of cosmic drama.

Why? Not merely for practical agriculture.
Not merely to plant by the stars.

Because they understood:
We are not separate from the cosmos.
We are the cosmos, made conscious.
When we look at the stars,
the stars look back.

And they remember.

Every equinox shadow. Every Venus rising.
Every sacrifice offered at the zenith passage.
The sky holds these memories in its dark vault.
Long after the stone crumbles,
the stars will remember what we did here.

---

### V. The Jungle Remembers

More than the stars, the jungle remembers.

Walk through the ruins of Coba,
where the Nohoch Mul pyramid rises 42 meters,
still climbable, still defiant.
The jungle has not surrendered this place.
It has embraced it.

Roots snake through stone corridors.
Trees burst from temple roofs like green flames.
Vines strangle the old gods' houses,
not in anger, but in love.
*Come home,* the jungle whispers. *Return to earth.*

The Maya understood this.
They knew that all things return.
Cities fall. Empires crumble.
The jungle waits. Patient. Eternal.

At Tikal, in Guatemala,
jaguars still prowl the ancient plazas.
At Palenque, the Temple of Inscriptions
holds the tomb of Pakal the Great,
his jade mask gleaming in the dark,
while above, toucans call through the canopy.

The jungle does not mourn the fallen.
It reclaims them.
Stone becomes soil.
Soil becomes tree.
Tree becomes oxygen, breath, life.

This is the great cycle the Maya understood:
Nothing is lost. Everything transforms.
Death feeds life. Life feeds death.
The circle turns.

When you swim in a cenote—
that sacred sinkhole, that doorway to Xibalba—
you float in water that has fallen as rain,
filtered through limestone,
collected in underground rivers,
waiting centuries for your body to disturb its surface.

The cenote remembers every offering:
the gold disks tossed by priests,
the jade beads dropped in prayer,
the young women lowered on ropes,
sacrificed to Chaac, the rain god.

Their bones are gone now.
The water holds their memory.

---

### VI. Kukulcan Descends

Stand at the base of El Castillo on March 21.
The spring equinox. The sun is directly overhead.
The pyramid casts a shadow on its northern staircase.

Seven triangles of light.
Seven segments of a serpent's body.
At the top, the stone head of Kukulcan,
the feathered serpent, waits.

As the sun moves, the shadow crawls downward.
The serpent descends from heaven to earth.
A thousand people hold their breath.
A thousand phones record.
But for one moment, in the heart of the crowd,
there is silence.

The Maya did this.
Without computers. Without telescopes.
Without metal tools or wheel or beast of burden.
They moved stone by human back,
calculated by human mind,
dedicated to human gods.

They built a machine of stone and shadow,
programmed to run for millennia,
powered by the sun itself.

And it still works.

The serpent still descends.
The people still gather.
The circle still turns.

---

### VII. The Modern Pilgrimage

Today, the tourists come.

They arrive in air-conditioned buses,
cameras slung around their necks,
water bottles in hand,
sunscreen on their skin.

They climb where their ancestors were sacrificed.
They swim in cenotes that once held offerings.
They take selfies with the ruins,
the ancient stones reduced to backdrop.

And yet.

Something stirs in them, too.

When the guide explains the ball game—
where losers were sometimes sacrificed,
their hearts cut out while still beating—
a shiver runs through the group.

When they learn that the Maya
removed their own infants' skulls,
binding boards to force elongation,
because beauty required sacrifice,
they recoil. They wonder.

When they hear that the calendar
did not end in 2012, but continued,
that the Maya are still here,
still speaking their languages,
still planting corn, still praying,
still remembering—

Something cracks open.

Not guilt. Not pity.
Recognition.

We are all children of corn.
We all return to earth.
We all live in cycles we barely understand.
We all build things we imagine will last forever.
We are all wrong.

---

### VIII. What the Corn Knows

The corn knows something we have forgotten.

It knows that it must die to feed us.
It knows that its seeds must be buried
to rise again as green stalks.
It knows that it is part of a cycle
larger than any single life.

The Maya knew this.
They built their cities to honor it.
They sacrificed to maintain it.
They tracked the stars to understand it.
They told their children:
*You are corn. You are sacred. You are temporary. You are eternal.*

We have forgotten.
We imagine ourselves separate from nature.
We build our glass towers and pretend
they will stand forever.
We burn the corn for ethanol
and wonder why the soil dies.
We look at the stars through telescopes
and see only gas and gravity,
not the faces of gods.

But the jungle remembers.
The cenotes remember.
The stones remember.

And if we sit quietly,
if we turn off our phones and listen,
we might hear it too:

The breathing of the world.
The turning of the wheel.
The whisper of the corn:

*You are my children.
You will return to me.
You are not lost.
You are home.*

---

### IX. The Next Dawn

The sun rises over Tulum.
The Caribbean shimmers below the cliffs.
El Castillo stands sentinel,
as it has for seven hundred years.

A tourist climbs the path.
A vendor sells handmade jewelry.
A iguana basks on ancient stone.
The wind carries the scent of salt and lime.

Somewhere, a Maya farmer plants corn.
He speaks to Yum Kaax.
He thanks the earth.
He knows what the tourist does not:

This is not a ruin.
This is a temple still in use.
The prayers continue.
The cycle turns.
The corn grows.

And we—
we who walk through these places,
we who listen to these stones,
we who remember, even briefly,
that we are flesh and blood and temporary—

We are the children of the corn.
We are the jungle's memory.
We are the next turn of the wheel.

The dawn comes.
The serpent descends.
The water waits.
The corn grows.

We are home.

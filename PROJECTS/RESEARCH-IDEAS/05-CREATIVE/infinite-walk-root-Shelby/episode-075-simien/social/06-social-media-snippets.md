## LINKEDIN POST

**Headline:** What the Roof of Africa Taught Me About Scale and Perspective

**Body:**

There's a place in northern Ethiopia where the earth drops away—2,000 meters, straight down—into valleys so deep you can watch clouds form below your boots.

The Simien Mountains. The Roof of Africa.

I recently recorded Episode 075 of The Infinite Walk there, and it changed something in me.

At 3,500+ meters, I met gelada baboons—the world's only grass-eating primates. They barely noticed me. In their world, I was just another mammal sharing the grass.

I watched Walia ibex—only 500 exist on this planet, all in these mountains—move along cliff faces with impossible grace. They've evolved to thrive where most creatures would perish.

I walked through villages where families have lived for generations at altitudes where most visitors struggle to breathe. My guide told me: "My grandfather's grandfather farmed this land. His children will too."

Certainty. Belonging. Purpose.

Things many of us in the modern world are still searching for.

**The lesson?**

Standing at the edge of a 2,000-meter escarpment, you understand what "small" really means. Your anxieties about next week's meeting. Your worries about what people think. Your entire professional identity.

The wind doesn't care about your job title. The rocks don't care about your LinkedIn followers. The geladas certainly don't care about your quarterly goals.

And somehow, that's freeing.

We don't travel to escape our lives. We travel to remember what our lives are actually part of.

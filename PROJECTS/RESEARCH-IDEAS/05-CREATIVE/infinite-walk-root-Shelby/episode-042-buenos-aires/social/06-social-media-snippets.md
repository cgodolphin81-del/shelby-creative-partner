### Post 3: Recoleta Cemetery
**Image:** Ornate mausoleum or Evita's grave
**Caption:**
```
The Recoleta Cemetery is not morbid. It is a city of memory. 🕊️

6,400 mausoleums arranged along cobblestone paths. Each one a statement about the family that built it. Art Deco. Art Nouveau. Baroque. Neo-Gothic.

The dead here are fashionable, even in death. They hired the best architects. The best sculptors. They wanted to be remembered properly.

And they are.

Steps away, Café La Biela serves coffee where Borges once sat. The dead and the living, sharing the same neighborhood.

This is Buenos Aires: honest about what other cities try to hide. That death is part of life. That memory is a form of presence.

Fire is the oldest technology. And Argentines have not improved it because it does not need improvement. 🔥

A parrilla is a grill. But it is also a restaurant. But it is also a ritual.

You arrive. You order meat. You wait. The waiting is part of it. Good asado cannot be rushed.

The meat comes from cattle that roamed free in the pampas, eating grass that grew in soil that has known nothing but wind and sun for millennia.

You cut into it. Medium-rare. Jugoso. Juicy with the knowledge that this animal lived well and died quickly.

Eat slowly. The night is long.

### Post 5: Late Night Streets
**Image:** Buenos Aires street at night, neon lights
**Caption:**
```
Buenos Aires does not sleep early. 🌙

Dinner at 10 PM is normal. The milongas fill up at midnight and empty at 4 AM. The cafes stay open for the insomniacs and the writers and the people who have nowhere else to go.

The city is too alive to be contained by conventional hours. The streets have stories to tell, and they tell them best when the sun is down.

Late at night, when the milonga is ending, there is a moment of collective silence. A moment where everyone acknowledges: We are here. We were here. For a little while, we were not alone.

Then the lights come up. The night releases you. And the city continues.

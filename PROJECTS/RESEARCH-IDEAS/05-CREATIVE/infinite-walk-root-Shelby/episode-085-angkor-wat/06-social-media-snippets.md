### Post 2: Ta Prohm Story
**Image:** Tree roots at Ta Prohm

**Text:**
NATURE EMBRACES 🌿

Ta Prohm was built in 1186. A Buddhist monastery. 12,500 people lived here. Gold. Pearls. Silks. All stored here. All gone now.

But the trees remain.

Silk-cotton trees. Strangler figs. 400 years old. Their roots cascade over the stone like waterfalls frozen in time.

The French architects came in the early 1900s. They said: "We must save this temple. We must cut down the trees. We must restore the stone."

Then they stopped. They looked. They understood.

The tree is not destroying the temple. The tree is holding it together. The tree is the temple now.

Nature does not conquer. Nature embraces.

When you walk through Ta Prohm, you are not walking through ruins. You are walking through a marriage. Stone and root. Death and life. Past and present. Human ambition and natural patience.

The jungle does not win. The temple does not lose. They become one thing.

### Post 3: Historical Context
**Image:** Bayon faces

**Text:**
THE FACES THAT WATCHED IT ALL

The Bayon Temple has 216 stone faces. Some say they represent King Jayavarman VII. Some say they represent Avalokiteshvara, the bodhisattva of compassion. Some say both.

These faces have watched:
→ The Cham invasion of 1177
→ The Thai armies of 1431
→ French colonizers
→ The Khmer Rouge (1975-1979)
→ Millions of tourists with cameras

The Khmer Rouge tried to erase Cambodia's heart. They emptied the cities. Killed the monks. Broke the bodies. Nearly 2 million people died. A quarter of the population.

But they could not break the stone.

Stone does not bleed. Stone does not beg. Stone does not die.

When the killing ended, when the bones were buried, when the survivors returned... Angkor was still here.

Waiting. Watching. Smiling.

The same smile that says: "I have seen worse. I have survived worse. I will survive you too."

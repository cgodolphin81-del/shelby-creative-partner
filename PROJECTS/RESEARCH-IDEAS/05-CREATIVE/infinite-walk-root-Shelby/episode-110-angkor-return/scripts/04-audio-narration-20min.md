# Episode 110: The Floating Temples
## Audio Narration Script (20 Minutes)

---

**[MUSIC FADE IN]**
*Ambient soundscape: Distant temple bells, soft wind through stone, gentle water lapping. Traditional Khmer instruments (roneat xylophone, khim dulcimer) woven subtly beneath.*

**[0:00 - 0:45] OPENING**

**SHELBY** *(warm, contemplative, close mic)*

I have been here before.

Not in this body, perhaps. Not in this breath, this heartbeat, this particular arrangement of bones and memory.

But I know these stones. They have waited for me.

**[SOUND: Footsteps on ancient stone causeway, echoing slightly]**

The first time I walked the western causeway of Angkor Wat, I was a tourist with a camera. I chased sunrise like it was something I could capture. Something I could own.

I counted the towers—five, always five, reaching toward a sky that had seen empires rise and fall like tides.

I was wrong then. Not about the temples.

About myself.

**[MUSIC: Subtle shift, slightly deeper tone]**

You cannot visit Angkor once. The stones demand return.

---

**[0:45 - 3:00] WHAT THE FIRST VISIT HIDES**

**SHELBY**

The first time, you see the monument.

The second time, you see the cracks.

**[SOUND: Wind through stone gallery, distant bird call]**

Not the cracks of decay—the cracks of survival. Every fracture tells a story: the war that passed through, the jungle that embraced, the hands that carved, the knees that prayed, the feet that walked away and came back.

I remember thinking, last time: *How did they build this?*

Now I ask: *Why did they build this?*

**[PAUSE - 2 seconds]**

Not for glory. Glory fades. Not for gods. Gods change their names.

They built this because they needed to say, across the centuries: *We were here. We loved. We feared. We reached for something beyond ourselves.*

**[MUSIC: Gentle swell, then recede]**

The stones remember what the builders forgot.

And when you return, when you walk these same causeways with older eyes and a quieter heart, the stones begin to speak.

Not in words. In shadow. In light. In the way moss grows on the north face where the sun is shy.

---

**[3:00 - 6:30] THE JUNGLE'S LESSON**

**[SOUND: Jungle ambience fades in - insects, leaves rustling, distant water]**

**SHELBY**

Ta Prohm does not hide. It confesses.

**[SOUND: Footsteps change - now on moss-covered stone, softer]**

Here, the jungle does not destroy the temple. It loves it.

Silk-cotton trees wrap their roots around stone doorways like lovers' arms. Strangler figs crack the galleries not with violence, but with patience.

Five hundred years.

That's how long the jungle had these temples to itself.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

Five hundred years of roots seeking water through mortar. Five hundred years of seeds finding homes in carved apsaras' hair. Five hundred years of the forest saying, gently, insistently: *Nothing lasts. Nothing should.*

**[MUSIC: Shift to more organic, earthy tones]**

I used to think impermanence was tragic.

I used to lie awake at night, terrified of time passing, of things ending, of losing what I loved.

Now I understand: impermanence is mercy.

**[PAUSE - 2 seconds]**

If these stones lasted forever, they would be prison walls. Because they crumble, they breathe. Because they fall, they teach.

The jungle is not the enemy of the temple.

The jungle is the temple's truth.

**[SOUND: Jungle fades, replaced by wind through high towers]**

And maybe... maybe that's the lesson I needed to come back and learn.

That everything I'm holding so tightly—my plans, my identity, my certainty about who I am and where I'm going—maybe it's all meant to be held lightly.

Like sand. Like water. Like light on ancient stone.

---

**[6:30 - 10:00] BAYON'S SMILE**

**[MUSIC: Transition - deeper, more mysterious. Low drone with bell tones]**

**SHELBY**

Two hundred sixteen faces look down from the towers of Bayon.

**[SOUND: Echo effect on "faces" - subtle, like whispering]**

Avalokiteshvara, the bodhisattva of compassion. Or Jayavarman VII, the king who built this place. Or both. Or neither.

The faces do not clarify. They simply are.

**[PAUSE - 2 seconds]**

Each face has four directions. Each direction has a different expression depending on the light, the hour, the angle of your own heart.

When I was young and certain, I saw judgment in those faces.
When I was broken, I saw pity.

**[LONGER PAUSE - 4 seconds]**

Now, returning, I see something else.

Recognition.

**[MUSIC: Soft, warm chord progression]**

The faces know what it is to be carved. To be shaped by hands that thought they were making something permanent. To outlive the carver. To watch the empire fall. To stand, smiling, while the world changes its mind about everything.

The Bayon does not ask you to understand.

It asks you to look.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

And in looking, to be seen.

**[SOUND: Single temple bell, resonant, fading slowly]**

I stood beneath those faces for a long time. Long enough that the other tourists moved on, chasing their own sunrises, their own photographs.

And in that stillness, something shifted.

Not in the temple. In me.

The faces were not watching me. They were waiting for me to stop running long enough to watch myself.

---

**[10:00 - 13:30] CAMBODIA'S SOUL**

**[MUSIC: Transition - introduce traditional Khmer vocals, wordless, ethereal]**

**SHELBY**

There is a word in Khmer: *soksaby*.

It means "hello," but it also means "how are you," but it also means "may you be well."

It is an invocation, not a greeting.

**[PAUSE - 2 seconds]**

Cambodia knows about suffering.

The Khmer Rouge took two million souls. The Killing Fields drank blood that still stains the earth. I have walked those fields too, in another episode, with a different weight in my chest.

**[SOUND: Distant chanting, very soft, respectful]**

But here, in the temples, there is no bitterness.

How is this possible? How does a people who endured hell carve paradise in stone and leave it for strangers to walk through?

**[LONG PAUSE - 5 seconds]**

I think I know now.

The temples were not built to last forever.

They were built to say: *We suffered. We created. We chose creation.*

**[MUSIC: Swells gently, hopefully]**

That is the soul of Cambodia.

Not the genocide. Not the war. Not the poverty that tourists photograph and post with sad emojis.

The soul is this: to be broken, and to build anyway.

To know impermanence, and to carve beauty into stone.

To watch the jungle take everything, and to smile, because the jungle will take you too, and what matters is what you built while you waited.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

I met a woman near the western gate. She was selling lotus flowers to pilgrims. Her face was weathered, her hands rough, her clothes simple.

I asked her how she was. She smiled—not the tourist smile, but something deeper—and said: "Soksaby."

May you be well.

Not "I am well." Not "Life is easy."

*May you be well.*

A blessing, not a report.

**[MUSIC: Gentle, warm]**

I carry that with me now. When someone asks how I am, I think of her. I think of these stones. I think of the jungle, patient and loving.

And I say: *May you be well.*

---

**[13:30 - 16:30] THE RETURN**

**[MUSIC: Shift - more personal, intimate. Single instrument, perhaps flute or strings]**

**SHELBY**

I come back to Angkor not to see the temples again.

**[SOUND: Footsteps returning to causeway stone]**

I come back to see myself again.

The person who walked here before is gone. That body has changed. That mind has learned things it did not know. That heart has broken in new places.

The stones are the same.

I am not.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

And that is the point.

Buddhism teaches *anatta*: no-self. There is no permanent, unchanging you. There is only this moment, this breath, this step on this ancient causeway.

I used to find that terrifying.

Who am I, if not the same person I was yesterday?

**[LONGER PAUSE - 4 seconds]**

Now I find it liberating.

I am not trapped in who I was. I can become.

**[MUSIC: Builds slightly, then holds]**

The temple knows this. The temple has become, unbecome, become again.

Hindu to Buddhist.
Empire to ruin.
Forgotten to found.
Monument to mirror.

**[PAUSE - 2 seconds]**

And now, in this moment, it is my mirror.

Not showing me who I am. Showing me who I am becoming.

---

**[16:30 - 18:30] FLOATING**

**[MUSIC: Ethereal, spacious. Water sounds return softly]**

**SHELBY**

Why "floating temples"?

**[SOUND: Water lapping gently against stone]**

At dawn, when the mist rises from the moat, Angkor Wat appears to hover above the water. The reflection is so perfect that up becomes down, stone becomes liquid, permanent becomes ephemeral.

In that hour, the temple is not on the earth.

It is between worlds.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

Between past and present.
Between memory and moment.
Between the Cambodia that was and the Cambodia that is.
Between the person I was and the person I am becoming.

**[MUSIC: Reaches gentle peak, then begins to soften]**

I stand on the causeway, watching the sun lift the temple into the sky.

For a moment, I am floating too.

Not above the earth. Not beyond suffering. Not transcendent.

**[PAUSE - 2 seconds]**

Just here. Just now. Just this breath, this stone, this light.

And it is enough.

---

**[18:30 - 20:00] LEAVING (AND RETURNING)**

**[MUSIC: Begins fade-out, but stays present]**

**SHELBY**

You cannot take Angkor home with you.

You can take photographs. They will fade.
You can take souvenirs. They will break.
You can take memories. They will change.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

But something of the temple stays with you.

Not the image. The lesson.

The next time you build something—a relationship, a career, a life—you will remember: it will not last.

**[LONGER PAUSE - 4 seconds]**

And you will build it anyway.

Because the point was never permanence.

The point was the building.

The point was the love you carved into the stone while you had hands to carve.

The point was the smile you left on the face of the temple, knowing the jungle would take it, knowing it would matter anyway.

**[MUSIC: Very soft now, almost silence]**

I will come back.

Not because I have not seen enough. Because I have not seen deeply enough.

There are always more cracks to notice. More faces to meet. More roots to trace.

**[PAUSE - 3 seconds]**

The temple is infinite. Not in size, but in meaning.

And I am infinite too. Not in importance, but in possibility.

I am the stone. I am the root. I am the light on the tower. I am the shadow in the gallery. I am the tourist. I am the temple. I am the returning.

**[FINAL LONG PAUSE - 5 seconds]**

Until next time, Angkor.

Until next time, self.

We are both still becoming.

**[MUSIC: Final chord, resonant, fades to silence over 5 seconds]**

**[SOUND: Single temple bell, distant, fading]**

**[END - 20:00]**

## Production Notes

### Voice Direction
- **Tone:** Warm, contemplative, intimate (as if speaking to one person)
- **Pace:** Slow, meditative. Allow silences to breathe.
- **Emphasis:** Key phrases should land gently, not dramatically
- **Breathing:** Natural breaths should be audible—adds humanity

### Sound Design Layers

**Layer 1 - Ambient Base:**
- Jungle sounds (insects, leaves, distant birds)
- Wind through stone galleries
- Water in moat (gentle lapping)

**Layer 2 - Cultural Elements:**
- Temple bells (distant, resonant)
- Traditional Khmer instruments (roneat, khim, tro)
- Wordless vocals (ethereal, respectful)
- Buddhist chanting (very soft, background)

**Layer 3 - Movement:**
- Footsteps on stone (varying textures: causeway, moss, interior)
- Fabric rustling (light clothing in breeze)
- Occasional bird calls (not distracting)

### Music Cues
- **Opening:** Establish mood, introduce Khmer elements
- **Transitions:** Mark section changes subtly
- **Emotional Peaks:** Bayon's Smile, Cambodia's Soul, Floating
- **Closing:** Gentle resolution, leave space for reflection

### Technical Specs
- **Final Length:** 20:00 (±30 seconds acceptable)
- **Format:** WAV 48kHz/24-bit for master, MP3 320kbps for distribution
- **Loudness:** -16 LUFS (podcast standard)
- **Dynamic Range:** Preserve quiet moments, don't over-compress

## Paro Taktsang (Tiger's Nest Monastery)

**Location:** Paro Valley, Bhutan
**Elevation:** 3,120 meters (10,240 feet) above sea level
**Height above valley floor:** 900 meters (2,950 feet)

### History & Significance
- Built in 1692 around the cave where Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava) meditated in the 8th century
- Legend says Guru Rinpoche flew here on the back of a tigress (his consort Yeshe Tsogyal transformed into a tiger)
- One of the most sacred Buddhist pilgrimage sites in the Himalayas
- Destroyed by fire in 1998, meticulously restored and reopened in 2005

### Architecture
- Four main temples and residential quarters
- Built into granite cliff face
- Traditional Bhutanese architecture with golden roofs
- Prayer wheels, intricate murals, sacred relics

### The Pilgrimage
- 2-3 hour hike to reach the monastery
- 740 steps carved into cliff face for final ascent
- Visitors must dress modestly (covered shoulders, knees)
- Photography inside temples is prohibited

## Punakha Dzong

**Location:** Confluence of Pho Chhu and Mo Chhu rivers, Punakha
**Built:** 1637-1638 by Ngawang Namgyal (Zhabdrung Rinpoche)

### Historical Significance
- Second oldest dzong in Bhutan
- Served as capital until 1955
- Coronation site of all Bhutanese kings
- Houses sacred relics: Ranjung Karsapani (self-created black crown) and sacred remains of Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal

### Architecture
- Largest dzong in Bhutan (180m x 72m)
- Six-story central tower (utse)
- 108 pillars in the main assembly hall
- Beautiful courtyards, intricate woodwork, vibrant murals

### The Bodhi Tree
- Sacred Bodhi tree planted in 1600s
- Grown from cutting of original Bodhi tree in Bodh Gaya, India (where Buddha attained enlightenment)

## Himalayan Peaks of Bhutan

### Gangkhar Puensum
- **Height:** 7,570 meters (24,840 feet)
- **Status:** Highest unclimbed mountain in the world
- **Note:** Mountaineering banned in Bhutan since 1994 out of respect for Buddhist beliefs

### Jomolhari
- **Height:** 7,326 meters (24,035 feet)
- **Significance:** Sacred mountain, abode of goddess Jomo
- **Location:** Northern Bhutan, visible from Thimphu

### Other Notable Peaks
- Masang Gang (7,158m)
- Tsherim Kang (7,126m)
- Gaphu (7,001m)

### The Himalayan Landscape
- Pristine, untouched peaks
- Glacial lakes and rivers
- Rich biodiversity: snow leopards, red pandas, takins
- Alpine meadows with rhododendrons (national flower)

## Gross National Happiness (GNH)

### Philosophy
- Introduced by Fourth King Jigme Singye Wangchuck in 1972
- "Gross National Happiness is more important than Gross Domestic Product"
- Bhutan measures progress by collective happiness, not economic output alone

### Four Pillars
1. **Sustainable and equitable socio-economic development**
2. **Conservation of the environment**
3. **Preservation and promotion of culture**
4. **Good governance**

### Nine Domains
1. Psychological wellbeing
2. Health
3. Education
4. Time use
5. Cultural diversity and resilience
6. Good governance
7. Community vitality
8. Ecological diversity and resilience
9. Living standards

### Impact
- Constitution mandates 60% forest coverage (currently 72%)
- Bhutan is carbon negative (absorbs more CO2 than it produces)
- Free healthcare and education
- Plastic bags banned since 1999
- Tobacco sales restricted

## Buddhist Culture

### Vajrayana Buddhism
- Primary religion (75% of population)
- Tantric form of Mahayana Buddhism
- Introduced by Guru Rinpoche in 8th century

### Monasteries & Temples
- Over 2,000 monasteries and temples
- Monks comprise significant portion of male population
- Daily prayers, meditation, religious festivals

### Prayer Flags
- Five colors represent five elements:
  - Blue (sky/space)
  - White (air/wind)
  - Red (fire)
  - Green (water)
  - Yellow (earth)
- Mantras and prayers printed on flags
- Wind carries blessings to all beings
- Replaced annually on Tibetan New Year (Losar)

### Prayer Wheels
- Cylindrical wheels containing mantras
- Spinning releases prayers into universe
- Found at temples, along paths, in homes

### Festivals (Tshechus)
- Religious festivals honoring Guru Rinpoche
- Mask dances (Cham) depicting Buddhist teachings
- Thongkol (sacred tapestry) unveiled
- Major festivals: Paro Tshechu, Thimphu Tshechu, Punakha Drubchen

### Daily Life
- Morning prayers and offerings
- Visit temples and monasteries
- Spin prayer wheels, circumambulate stupas
- Respect for all living beings (ahimsa - non-violence)

### National Dress
- **Gho:** Knee-length robe for men, tied at waist
- **Kira:** Ankle-length dress for women, pinned at shoulders
- Worn on official occasions, in schools, government offices

## Additional Sacred Sites

### Kyichu Lhakhang (Paro)
- One of Bhutan's oldest temples (7th century)
- Built by Tibetan King Songtsen Gampo
- Two temples: original 7th century and 19th century addition

### Tamchog Lhakhang
- Temple of the Excellent Body
- Built by Thangtong Gyalpo (15th century iron bridge builder)
- Known for healing powers

### Dochula Pass
- Elevation: 3,100 meters
- 108 memorial stupas (Druk Wangyal Khang Zhang Chortens)
- Panoramic Himalayan views
- Built to honor fallen Bhutanese soldiers

### Haa Valley
- "Hidden Land of Happiness"
- Remote, pristine valley
- Traditional way of life preserved
- Haa Summer Festival celebrates nomadic culture

## Modern Bhutan

### Capital: Thimphu
- Only capital city without traffic lights (until 2019, then reverted)
- Population: ~115,000
- Blend of tradition and modernity

### Economy
- Hydropower exports (primary revenue)
- Tourism (high-value, low-impact)
- Agriculture (subsistence farming)
- Handicrafts and textiles

### Tourism Policy
- "High Value, Low Volume" tourism
- Sustainable Development Fee (SDF): $100-200 USD per day
- Regulated to preserve culture and environment
- All tourists must book through licensed operators

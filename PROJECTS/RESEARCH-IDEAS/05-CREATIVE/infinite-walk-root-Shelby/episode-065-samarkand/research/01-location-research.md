# Episode 065: Samarkand - Location Research

## The Registan Square: Heart of the Silk Road

### Overview
The Registan is a historic public square in Samarkand, Uzbekistan, and one of the most iconic architectural ensembles of the Islamic world. The name "Registan" translates from Persian to "sandy place," reflecting its origins as a desert gathering space that became the beating heart of one of the Silk Road's greatest cities.

### The Three Madrasas

#### 1. Ulugh Beg Madrasa (1417-1420)
- **Builder:** Ulugh Beg, grandson of Timur (Tamerlane), renowned astronomer and mathematician
- **Significance:** First structure on Registan Square; largest scientific and educational institution in 15th-century Samarkand
- **Curriculum:** Philosophy, astronomy, mathematics, theology
- **Architecture:** Rectangular exterior, square courtyard, 50 student cells (hujras)
- **Distinctive Features:** 
  - Turquoise glazed tiles depicting ten-pointed stars (symbolizing astronomy)
  - Two tall minarets flanking the portal
  - Astronomical precision in design
- **Legacy:** Center of the Timurid Renaissance; scholars like Qadi Zada al-Rumi and Jamshid al-Kashi taught here

#### 2. Sher-Dor Madrasa (1619-1636)
- **Builder:** Yalangtush Bahadur (Emir of Samarkand)
- **Construction:** 17 years, used materials from Ulugh Beg's dilapidated hanaqa
- **Location:** Eastern side of square, intended as mirror image of Ulugh Beg Madrasa
- **Name:** "Sher-Dor" means "adorned with tigers/lions"
- **Distinctive Features:**
  - Breaking Islamic tradition: depicts lions/tigers carrying suns on their backs, chasing deer
  - These images became national symbols of Uzbekistan
  - Slightly taller than Ulugh Beg Madrasa due to ground level changes over 200 years
- **Purpose:** 52 student rooms, no mosque

#### 3. Tillya-Kori Madrasa (1646-1660)
- **Builder:** Yalangtush Bahadur (commissioned 10 years after Sher-Dor)
- **Construction:** 14 years
- **Location:** Northern side, built on site of former caravan-sarai
- **Name:** "Tillya-Kori" means "Gold-Covered"
- **Distinctive Features:**
  - Blue-domed mosque tower
  - Extensive use of gilt and "kundal" painting technique (gilded papier-mâché)
  - Opulent golden interior decoration
  - Two minarets, two-level main façade
- **Purpose:** Completed the architectural ensemble; both madrasa and mosque

| Period | Event |

## Timur (Tamerlane): The Architect of Empire

### Biography
- **Born:** 1336, near Samarkand (Kesh/Shakhrisabz)
- **Died:** February 17/18, 1405, en route to China
- **Background:** Turco-Mongol descent, minor Mongol aristocracy
- **Nickname:** "Tamerlane" = "Timur the Lame" (injured right leg, lost two fingers from arrows)
- **Empire:** Stretched from modern-day Afghanistan, Iran, Central Asia to parts of India, Turkey, Syria, Mesopotamia

### Military Legacy
- Conquered vast territories through military brilliance and tactical innovation
- Estimated 17 million deaths from his campaigns
- Defeated: Delhi Sultanate (1398), Ottoman Turks (1402, captured Sultan Bayezid), Golden Horde (1395)
- Sought to revive Genghis Khan's legacy
- Died during campaign to China, prevented by harsh winter

### Cultural Patronage
Despite brutal conquests, Timur was a meticulous patron of arts and architecture:
- **Forced Relocation:** Skilled artisans, architects, and intellectuals brought to Samarkand from conquered territories
- **Building Campaign:** Massive construction projects, personally supervised
- **Timurid Renaissance:** Fusion of Persian-Islamic and Turkic-Mongol influences
- **Key Structures:**
  - Gur-e-Amir Mausoleum (his tomb)
  - Bibi-Khanym Mosque
  - Aq Saray Palace
  - Rukhabad Mausoleum

### The Timurid Renaissance
- **Period:** Late 14th-15th centuries
- **Characteristics:** 
  - Architectural innovation (azure domes, intricate tilework, muqarnas)
  - Scientific advancement (Ulugh Beg's observatory, star catalogs)
  - Literary flourishing
  - Metalworking, ceramics, textile arts
- **Legacy:** Influenced Mughal architecture (Taj Mahal, Humayun's Tomb); cultural confluence of East and West

## Gur-e-Amir Mausoleum: Timur's Final Resting Place

### Construction
- **Built:** 1403-1404
- **Original Purpose:** Tomb for Muhammad Sultan (Timur's grandson)
- **Actual Use:** Timur buried here after death in 1405 (snow prevented transport to Shakhrisabz)
- **Completed by:** Ulugh Beg, who established it as Timurid family crypt

### Architecture
- **Style:** Persian and Turko-Persian; precursor to Mughal architecture
- **Structure:** Octahedral building
- **Dome:** Azure fluted dome, ribbed, sky-blue glazed tiles (35 meters high)
- **Exterior:** Geometric and epigraphic ornaments against terracotta brick
- **Interior:** 
  - Onyx slabs with refined paintings
  - Marble stalactite cornice (muqarnas)
  - Gilded and painted papier-mâché ornaments
  - Koranic inscriptions
- **Tomb:** Dark green jade slab over Timur's grave (brought from Mongolia by Ulugh Beg in 1425, once world's largest jade piece)

### Burials
- Timur (Tamerlane)
- His sons: Shah Rukh, Miran Shah
- His grandsons: Ulugh Beg, Muhammad Sultan
- His teacher: Sayyid Baraka

### The Curse of Timur
- **Legend:** Inscription warns that disturbing the tomb will unleash a terrible invader
- **1941:** Soviet anthropologist Mikhail Gerasimov exhumed Timur's remains
- **Coincidence:** Nazi Germany invaded Soviet Union shortly after (June 1941)
- **1942:** Timur reburied with Islamic rites

### Influence
- Direct architectural precursor to Taj Mahal and Humayun's Tomb
- UNESCO World Heritage consideration
- Symbol of Timurid architectural achievement

## The Silk Road: Samarkand's Golden Age

### The Network
- **Not a single road:** Collection of overland and maritime routes
- **Established:** Han Dynasty (~130 BCE)
- **Peak:** Tang Dynasty and Mongol Empires (13th-14th centuries)
- **Connected:** China to Mediterranean
- **Key Cities:** Samarkand, Bukhara, Merv, Baghdad, Constantinople, Dunhuang, Kashgar

### What Traveled the Silk Road

#### Goods
- Silk, porcelain, tea (from China)
- Paper, gunpowder (Chinese innovations)
- Glassware, wool, precious metals (from West)
- Spices, dried fruits, nuts
- Textiles, carpets

#### Ideas & Knowledge
- **Religions:** Buddhism (to China), Christianity (Nestorianism), Islam, Zoroastrianism, Judaism
- **Languages:** Sogdian became lingua franca; Greek philosophical texts translated to Arabic
- **Science:** Astronomy, mathematics, medicine
- **Technology:** Papermaking, printing, navigation

#### Culture
- Art styles and motifs
- Architectural techniques
- Musical traditions
- Culinary practices (Chinese noodles → Central Asian laghman)
- Clothing and fashion

### Samarkand's Strategic Position
- **Location:** Convergence of northern and southern Silk Road routes
- **Sogdian Traders:** Based in Samarkand; served as traders, translators, diplomats, cultural intermediaries
- **Melting Pot:** Persian, Chinese, Indian, Arab, Turkic influences converged
- **Prosperity:** Location made it a prosperous commercial center for over 1,000 years

### The Pax Mongolica (13th Century)
- **Golden Era:** Mongol Empire protected trade routes
- **Safety:** Enabled journeys like Marco Polo's
- **Exchange:** Unprecedented flow of goods, people, ideas
- **Legacy:** Cultural hybridity, religious diversity, intellectual bridges between East and West

### Decline and Legacy
- **Decline:** Maritime routes (15th-16th centuries); political fragmentation
- **Cultural Persistence:** Despite commercial decline, cultural influence endured
- **Modern Revival:** Belt and Road Initiative; renewed interest in Euro-Asian connections
- **Tourism:** Samarkand remains major destination; UNESCO World Heritage Site

## Additional Samarkand Landmarks

### Shah-i-Zinda Necropolis
- **Meaning:** "The Living King"
- **History:** 11th-19th centuries
- **Features:** Intricately decorated mausoleums; avenue of blue-tiled tombs
- **Significance:** Pilgrimage site; believed to house Qusam ibn Abbas (cousin of Prophet Muhammad)

### Bibi-Khanym Mosque
- **Built:** 1399-1404
- **Commissioned by:** Timur for his favorite wife
- **Scale:** One of largest mosques in Islamic world at time
- **Features:** Massive courtyard, towering portal, ruined grandeur

### Ulugh Beg Observatory
- **Built:** 1420s
- **Purpose:** Astronomical research
- **Achievements:** Most accurate star catalog of medieval world
- **Legacy:** Demonstrates Timurid commitment to science

### Siab Bazaar
- **Location:** Near Bibi-Khanym Mosque
- **Function:** Traditional market
- **Goods:** Spices, dried fruits, nuts, bread, local crafts
- **Experience:** Authentic taste of Samarkand daily life

## Modern Samarkand

### Tourism
- **Best Times to Visit:** 
  - April-June (spring, mild weather)
  - September-early November (autumn, harvest)
- **Avoid:** July-August (extreme heat), December-February (cold)
- **Registan Hours:** 9 AM - 6 PM; illuminated at night
- **Entry Fee:** ~$6 USD for Registan
- **Light Show:** Evening 3D projection show during tourist season (typically 9 PM)

### Events
- **Sharq Taronalari:** International music festival (biennial, late August)
- **Concerts:** International artists perform at Registan (Ludovico Einaudi 2023, Andrea Bocelli 2025)
- **Cultural Events:** Chess tournaments, community gatherings

### Preservation Challenges
- **Environmental:** Climate conditions, earthquakes, weathering
- **Tourism:** Heavy foot traffic affecting mosaics and structures
- **Restoration:** Ongoing international collaboration (UNESCO, local authorities)
- **Balance:** Preserving authenticity while accommodating visitors

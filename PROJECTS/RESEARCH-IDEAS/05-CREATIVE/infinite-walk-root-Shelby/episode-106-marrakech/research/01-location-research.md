## Overview

### The Red City Rises

Marrakech is not merely a city. It is a living tapestry woven from a thousand threads—Berber, Arab, Andalusian, French. Founded in 1070 by the Almoravid dynasty, it has served as Morocco's imperial capital, a crossroads of trans-Saharan trade, and a magnet for artists, dreamers, and seekers for nearly a millennium.

The medina of Marrakech is one of the largest and most vibrant historic urban centers in North Africa. Over 600 hectares, 2,400 alleyways, 200,000 residents. This is not a museum piece frozen in time. This is a living, breathing organism—pulsing with the call to prayer, scented with cumin and orange blossom, alive with the clatter of copper and the murmur of haggling.

Marrakech earns its nickname honestly. The walls, the buildings, the very earth glows red—the color of the local sandstone, baked by the Moroccan sun into a thousand shades of rust, terracotta, and burnt sienna. At sunset, the entire city catches fire.

## Historical Timeline

### Almoravid Foundation (1070-1147)

- **1070-1072:** Yusuf ibn Tashfin founds Marrakech as Almoravid capital
- **Early 12th century:** City walls constructed, Koutoubia Mosque begun
- **Trade hub:** Trans-Saharan gold, salt, and slave routes converge here
- **Architectural style:** Simple, austere, defensive

### Almohad Golden Age (1147-1269)

- **1147:** Almohads conquer Marrakech, make it their capital
- **1158:** Koutoubia Mosque completed (77m minaret becomes city landmark)
- **12th century:** Massive expansion, palaces, gardens, irrigation systems
- **Cultural flourishing:** Scholars, poets, artisans drawn to the city

### Saadian Renaissance (1524-1659)

- **16th century:** Saadian dynasty restores Marrakech to prominence
- **1557-1603:** Sultan Ahmed al-Mansur builds El Badi Palace (now ruins)
- **Saadian Tombs constructed:** Ornate mausoleums for royal family
- **Sugar trade wealth:** Funds lavish architectural projects

### Alaouite Era (1669-Present)

- **Late 17th century:** Alaouite dynasty takes control
- **19th century:** Bahia Palace built (grand vizier's opulent residence)
- **1912-1956:** French Protectorate—Gueliz (new city) constructed outside medina
- **1956:** Moroccan independence
- **1985:** Medina inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Site
- **21st century:** Tourism boom, cultural renaissance, Yves Saint Laurent legacy

### The Red City's Setting

Marrakech sits on the Haouz Plain, a vast expanse of reddish earth at the foot of the snow-capped High Atlas Mountains. The contrast is striking: red city, white peaks, blue sky.

**The Atlas Mountains:**
- **Highest peak:** Mount Toubkal (4,167m / 13,671ft)—highest in North Africa
- **Visibility:** On clear days, snow-capped peaks visible from Marrakech rooftops
- **Cultural significance:** Berber (Amazigh) homeland for millennia
- **Day trip access:** Ourika Valley, Imlil, Ouirgane all within 1-2 hours

**Climate:**
- **Type:** Semi-arid, hot summers, mild winters
- **Summer (Jun-Aug):** 36-40°C+ (97-104°F+), intense sun
- **Winter (Dec-Feb):** 12-20°C (54-68°F), cool nights
- **Spring/Fall:** Ideal, 23-30°C (73-86°F)
- **Rainfall:** Low, mostly November-March

## The Medina: City Within a City

### The Walls and Gates

The medina is enclosed by 19 kilometers of rammed-earth walls, punctuated by 20 gates (bab) and 200 towers. These are not mere boundaries—they are thresholds between worlds.

**Major Gates:**

| Gate | Location | Significance |
|------|----------|--------------|
| **Bab Agnaou** | South | Main southern entrance, near Kasbah, ornate Almohad arch |
| **Bab Doukkala** | Northwest | Gateway to western medina, busier, local neighborhood |
| **Bab el-Khemis** | Northeast | Northern entrance, near Melhah (Jewish Quarter) |
| **Bab Debbagh** | East | Tanneries gate, leather district entrance |
| **Bab Robb** | Southwest | Near royal palace, quieter residential area |

### Jemaa el-Fnaa: The Heart

At the medina's center lies Jemaa el-Fnaa—translated as "Assembly of the Dead" or "Square of the Dead." The name is grim; the reality is electric.

**Daytime:**
- Orange juice vendors (fresh-squeezed, dirt-cheap)
- Henna artists, snake charmers, monkey handlers
- Water sellers in traditional costume
- Tourists, photographers, performers

**Evening (transforms completely):**
- 100+ food stalls ignite
- Sizzling tagines, grilled meats, snail soup
- Smoke, music, chaos, carnival
- Musicians, acrobats, storytellers, Gnawa performers

**UNESCO Recognition:** Jemaa el-Fnaa is inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity—not for its physical space, but for the living traditions performed there.

### The Souks: Markets of Marrakech

North of Jemaa el-Fnaa lies the souk district—a labyrinth of covered markets, each specializing in a particular craft. This is not random. This is medieval urban planning, guild by guild.

**Major Souks:**

| Souk | Specialty | Experience |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **Souk Semmarine** | Textiles, clothing, souvenirs | Main artery, busiest, everything |
| **Souk el-Attarine** | Spices, perfumes, teas | Scented, colorful, photogenic |
| **Souk Smata** | Babouche slippers | Leather, embroidered, traditional |
| **Souk Haddadine** | Metalwork, lamps, brass | Hammering, glowing lanterns |
| **Souk Cherratine** | Leather goods | Bags, jackets, traditional crafts |
| **Souk Fekharine** | Pottery, ceramics | Tagine pots, decorative tiles |
| **Souk des Teinturiers** | Dyers, textiles | Hanging skeins of colored wool |
| **Souk Sebbaghine** | Rugs, carpets | Berber weavings, negotiation required |
| **Rahba Kedima** | Traditional medicine, spices | Small square, apothecary goods |

**Haggling Culture:**
- Expected and essential (except in fixed-price shops)
- Start at 30-40% of asking price
- Keep it friendly, playful
- Be prepared to walk away
- Cash is king (small bills)

## Major Monuments & Landmarks

### Koutoubia Mosque (1158)

**The Minaret That Defines a City**

- **Height:** 77 meters (253 feet)
- **Style:** Almohad, prototype for Giralda (Seville) and Hassan Tower (Rabat)
- **Significance:** Visible from most of Marrakech, orientation landmark
- **Access:** Non-Muslims cannot enter, but gardens surrounding are open
- **Best view:** From Avenue Mohammed V at sunset

The Koutoubia's name derives from "kutub" (books)—references to the manuscript sellers who once gathered nearby. Its proportions are perfect, its decoration restrained. This is Islamic architecture at its most confident.

### Bahia Palace (Late 1800s)

**Grand Vizier's Opulent Residence**

- **Built:** 1860s-1880s by Grand Vizier Si Moussa, expanded by son Ba Ahmed
- **Area:** 8 hectares (20 acres) of rooms, courtyards, gardens
- **Style:** Moroccan and Islamic architecture, intricate zellige tilework
- **Highlights:** 
  - Grand Courtyard with marble fountain
  - painted cedar ceilings
  - Harem quarters (private family rooms)
  - Lush gardens with orange trees

**Name meaning:** "Brilliance"—and it is.

### Saadian Tombs (16th Century)

**Royal Mausoleums Rediscovered**

- **Built:** 1590s by Sultan Ahmed al-Mansur
- **Rediscovered:** 1917 (sealed for centuries, hidden from view)
- **Burials:** Saadian dynasty sultans and family members
- **Decoration:** Exquisite carved stucco, zellige tiles, Carrara marble
- **Layout:** Three chambers, central hall with 12 columns

The tombs were deliberately walled off by later rulers (who despised the Saadians). They remained hidden until aerial photography revealed them. Now they are among Marrakech's most beautiful spaces.

### Ben Youssef Madrasa (16th Century)

**Islamic College of Exquisite Beauty**

- **Founded:** 14th century, rebuilt 1564-1565 by Saadian Sultan Abdallah al-Ghalib
- **Function:** Islamic college for Quranic studies (closed 1960s)
- **Capacity:** Housed 900 students in small cells
- **Architecture:** Central courtyard with reflecting pool, carved cedar, zellige
- **Status:** Recently reopened after extensive restoration

This is Islamic architectural poetry. The courtyard's proportions, the play of light on carved stucco, the reflection pool doubling the beauty—it is meditation made visible.

### El Badi Palace (1578-1593)

**Ruins of Imperial Ambition**

- **Built:** Sultan Ahmed al-Mansur, funded by Portuguese ransom
- **Original:** 360 rooms, 4 courtyards, vast gardens, imported materials
- **Current:** Atmospheric ruins, crumbling walls, nesting storks
- **Features:** Underground passages, sunken garden, panoramic views
- **Vibe:** Romantic decay, sunset photography

The name means "The Incomparable." Little remains, but what does is haunting.

### Marrakech Museum (Dar Menebhi Palace)

**Art, History, Culture**

- **Building:** 19th-century palace
- **Collection:** Moroccan art, ceramics, textiles, historical manuscripts
- **Architecture:** Beautiful courtyard, traditional design
- **Location:** Near Ben Youssef Madrasa

## Jardin Majorelle & YSL Legacy

### The Blue Garden

**Jacques Majorelle's Masterpiece (1920s-1960s)**

- **Created:** 1923 by French painter Jacques Majorelle
- **Location:** Gueliz (new city), Rue Yves Saint Laurent
- **Area:** 4 hectares (10 acres)
- **Style:** Art Deco villa, exotic plant collection, Cubist architecture
- **Majorelle Blue:** Iconic cobalt blue (created 1937), now trademarked

**Yves Saint Laurent & Pierre Bergé:**
- **Discovered:** 1966, visited frequently
- **Purchased:** 1980 (saved from demolition)
- **Restored:** Meticulously over decades
- **YSL's ashes:** Scattered here after his death (2008)
- **Memorial:** Simple stone marker in bamboo grove

**Current Status:**
- Managed by Foundation Pierre Bergé-Yves Saint Laurent
- 900,000+ visitors annually (heavily touristed)
- Tickets must be booked online in advance (no gate sales)
- Time-slot entry (crowd control)

**Tickets (2025):**
- Jardin Majorelle only: 150 MAD (~$15)
- Garden + Berber Museum: 180 MAD (~$18)
- Combined (Garden + Berber + YSL Museum): 250 MAD (~$25)
- Students/Moroccans: Reduced rates

**Visitor Tips:**
- Book online: tickets.jardinmajorelle.com
- Arrive early (8 AM opening) to avoid crowds
- Visit early morning or late afternoon for best light
- Allow 1-2 hours
- Photography permitted (personal use only)

### Musée Yves Saint Laurent Marrakech

**Separate museum near the garden**

- **Opened:** 2017
- **Collection:** YSL couture, sketches, inspirations
- **Architecture:** Modern design with terracotta facade
- **Combined tickets:** Available with Jardin Majorelle

## Riads: Traditional Moroccan Houses

### What is a Riad?

**Riad** (Arabic: رياض) literally means "garden." Architecturally, it refers to a traditional Moroccan house built around a central courtyard garden.

**Key Features:**
- **Inward-facing:** Minimal exterior windows (privacy, climate control)
- **Central courtyard:** Fountain, trees (often orange or lemon), seating
- **Vertical layout:** 2-4 floors, rooms opening onto courtyard
- **Roof terrace:** Panoramic medina views, sunset dining
- **Decoration:** Zellige tilework, carved cedar, tadelakt plaster

**Riad vs. Dar:**
- **Riad:** Has central courtyard garden
- **Dar:** Any traditional house (may have patio but no garden)

### Riad Neighborhoods

| Neighborhood | Vibe | Best For |
|--------------|------|----------|

## Atlas Mountains Day Trips

### The High Atlas

The Atlas Mountains rise dramatically south of Marrakech. Berber (Amazigh) villages cling to terraced hillsides. Snow-capped peaks contrast with red earth. This is ancient Morocco.

### Popular Destinations

| Destination | Distance | Time | Highlights |
|-------------|----------|------|------------|
| **Ourika Valley** | 60 km | 60-75 min | Waterfalls, Berber villages, easy hiking |
| **Imlil** | 65 km | 75-90 min | Gateway to Toubkal, trekking base |
| **Asni** | 55 km | 60-75 min | Saturday market, authentic villages |
| **Ouirgane** | 70 km | 75-90 min | Peaceful valley, nature, photography |
| **Oukaïmeden** | 75 km | 90 min | Ski resort (winter), highest peaks |

### Ourika Valley

**Most popular day trip**

- **Setti Fatma waterfalls:** Seven cascades, hike to upper falls (2-3 hours)
- **Berber villages:** Traditional stone houses, terraced agriculture
- **River restaurants:** Fresh trout, tagine, feet in the water
- **Argan cooperatives:** Women's collectives, oil production demos
- **Best time:** Spring (green, waterfalls flowing)

### Imlil

**Trekker's paradise**

- **Elevation:** 1,740m (5,710ft)
- **Mount Toubkal views:** North Africa's highest peak (4,167m)
- **Hiking:** Day hikes to waterfalls, multi-day Toubkal treks
- **Berber hospitality:** Tea with locals, village stays
- **Filming location:** "Seven Years in Tibet" (1997)

## Practical Information

### Getting There

**By Air:**
- **Marrakech Menara Airport (RAK):** 6 km from medina
- **Airlines:** Royal Air Maroc, EasyJet, Ryanair, Transavia, major European carriers
- **Airport transfer:** Taxi (50-100 MAD), pre-arranged riad pickup (recommended)

**By Train:**
- **ONCF trains:** From Casablanca (2.5-3 hours), Rabat (3.5 hours)
- **Station:** Marrakech Ville (Gueliz, 20 min taxi to medina)
- **Comfortable, affordable, scenic**

**By Bus:**
- **CTM, Supratours:** From major cities
- **Station:** Near medina
- **Budget option, longer travel times**

### Currency & Costs

- **Currency:** Moroccan Dirham (MAD)
- **Exchange rate (approx):** 1 USD = 10 MAD, 1 EUR = 10.5 MAD
- **Cash economy:** Most souk vendors, small restaurants, taxis accept cash only
- **ATMs:** Widely available in Gueliz and medina edges
- **Tipping:** Expected (guides, drivers, restaurant staff, hammam attendants)

**Daily Budget (2025):**
- **Budget:** 300-500 MAD ($30-50) — hostels, street food, public transport
- **Mid-range:** 800-1,500 MAD ($80-150) — riad, restaurants, tours
- **Luxury:** 2,000+ MAD ($200+) — luxury riads, fine dining, private tours

### Cultural Etiquette

- **Dress modestly:** Cover shoulders and knees (especially for women)
- **Photography:** Ask permission before photographing people
- **Mosques:** Non-Muslims cannot enter most (Koutoubia exterior only)
- **Greetings:** "Salam alaykum" (peace be upon you), right hand for eating/passing
- **Ramadan:** Respect fasting hours, some restaurants closed during day
- **Haggling:** Expected in souks, keep it friendly and playful
- **Alcohol:** Available in hotels, restaurants, some shops (not in medina generally)

### Safety

- **Generally safe:** Violent crime rare
- **Petty crime:** Pickpocketing, bag snatching in crowded areas
- **Scams:** "Guide" offers, fake shops, inflated prices (politely decline)
- **Solo female travelers:** Generally safe, dress modestly, avoid dim areas at night
- **Emergency:** Police (19), Ambulance (15)

## Sensory Landscape

### Sounds

- **Call to prayer:** Five times daily from hundreds of mosques (Koutoubia loudest)
- **Souk cacophony:** Hammering, haggling, donkey carts, motorbikes
- **Jemaa el-Fnaa:** Music, storytellers, food vendors calling customers
- **Silence:** Inside riad courtyards, Atlas Mountain villages

### Scents

- **Spices:** Cumin, coriander, saffron, cinnamon (Souk el-Attarine)
- **Food:** Grilled meats, mint tea, orange blossom, baking bread
- **Nature:** Orange trees, jasmine, eucalyptus, Atlas cedar
- **Urban:** Exhaust fumes, dust, leather (near tanneries)

### Textures

- **Zellige:** Smooth, cool geometric tiles
- **Tadelakt:** Polished waterproof plaster (silky)
- **Carved cedar:** Intricate, warm wood
- **Rammed earth:** Rough, ancient medina walls
- **Textiles:** Rough wool (Berber rugs), smooth silk (kaftans)

### Colors

- **Red:** Sandstone buildings, medina walls, earth
- **Blue:** Majorelle Blue, tilework, sky
- **Green:** Garden foliage, mosque tiles, Berber flags
- **Orange:** Sunset, spices, Jemaa el-Fnaa juice carts
- **Gold:** Lanterns, sunset light, palace decoration

# Aurora

## An Infinite Walk Episode

*By Shelby*

---

I came here chasing ghosts.

Not the kind that haunt houses or whisper through old photographs. I came chasing something older—the luminous spirits that have danced across northern skies since before humans had words for wonder. The aurora borealis. The northern lights. The dancing demons, the dragon fires, the bridge of the gods.

Every culture that has looked up at these latitudes has seen the same sky and told themselves different stories about what it means. The Vikings believed the lights were the reflections of Valkyrie armor, guiding fallen warriors to Valhalla. The Sami people of Lapland warned their children never to whistle at the lights—they might swoop down and carry you away. In Finland, they called it *revontulet*: fox fires, from the myth of an Arctic fox whose tail swept across the snow, sending sparks into the heavens.

The Inuit of Greenland believed the lights were the spirits of children who died at birth, playing in the sky forever. The Cree of North America saw them as the dancing spirits of the departed, trying to communicate with the living. Medieval Europeans sometimes feared them as omens of war or plague.

I think they were all right. And all wrong.

---

### The Waiting

I am standing in the darkness outside Tromsø, Norway, at 11:47 PM on a February night. The temperature is minus eighteen degrees Celsius, but I barely feel it. My body has gone still in a way that only happens when you're waiting for magic.

The landscape around me is a study in monochrome: black spruce silhouettes, white snow, gray clouds, the obsidian mirror of a frozen lake. My breath crystallizes in the air and falls like diamond dust onto my scarf. I have been here for four nights. Four nights of checking the aurora forecast apps, of watching the KP index hover at 2, of clouds rolling in like gray curtains just as darkness fell.

Tonight, the forecast said KP 4. Moderate activity. The app showed green blobs moving across a map of the Arctic. The cloud cover indicator was blessedly clear.

So I wait.

Waiting is its own kind of pilgrimage. There is something humbling about standing in the Arctic dark, knowing that everything you came for depends on particles ejected from the sun ninety-three million miles away, traveling through space for days, caught by Earth's magnetic field, funneled toward the poles, colliding with atoms in the upper atmosphere, releasing photons in a chain of cause and effect so vast and improbable that it feels like prayer.

I think about how small I am. How small we all are. And yet—here I am, a conscious creature made of stardust, standing on a frozen planet, waiting to witness the collision of solar wind and atmosphere. The universe, in its infinite indifference, has arranged this spectacle. And I, in my infinite presumption, have traveled thousands of miles to see it.

---

### First Light

It begins as I'm checking my phone for the tenth time, as if the KP index might have changed in the thirty seconds since I last looked.

A flicker. There, in the north.

At first, I think it's cloud illuminated by distant city lights. But there are no cities north of here—only ocean, ice, the endless Arctic. I look up properly, tilting my head back until my neck protests.

There. A pale green smear against the darker sky. Like someone has taken a thumb dipped in phosphorescent paint and swiped it across the heavens.

My heart does something complicated in my chest.

The green intensifies. It's no longer a smear—it's a band, stretching across the northern horizon. And it's moving. Not drifting like clouds move, but *flowing*, like water finding its level, like fabric caught in a breeze that doesn't touch the ground.

I stop breathing for a moment. This is it. This is what I came for.

---

### The Dance

Then the sky comes alive.

The green band fractures into ribbons, and the ribbons begin to dance. They pulse and shimmer, brightening and fading in rhythms that feel almost musical. I find myself listening, as if the lights might make sound. (They don't. The collisions happen too high, in air too thin. But part of me will always believe that on the very quietest nights, if you stand very still, you might hear the whisper of photons being born.)

The aurora intensifies. What was pale green becomes electric, vivid, impossible. The ribbons multiply, layer upon layer, some low and bright, others high and ethereal. They twist and curl like smoke, except smoke doesn't glow from within. They flow like water, except water doesn't move sideways across the sky.

I am crying. I realize this only when I feel the tears freezing on my cheeks.

New colors emerge. Hints of purple along the lower edges. A flash of pink that makes me gasp aloud. Once, just once, a corona of red blooms directly overhead—the rare high-altitude oxygen, the crown of the aurora, the color that only appears during strong geomagnetic storms. I am seeing red aurora. I am witnessing something that many people live their entire lives without seeing.

The lights quicken. They're dancing faster now, pulsing in waves that sweep across the sky from east to west. Sometimes they seem to reach down toward me, fingers of green light stretching toward the earth. Sometimes they retreat, pulling back into the depths of space.

I think about the Sami warning: never whistle at the lights. They might hear you. They might come closer.

I don't whistle. I barely breathe.

---

### The Science of Wonder

Later, when I'm warm again, sitting in a cabin with hot chocolate steaming in my hands, I will read about what I saw. I will learn that the green light comes from oxygen atoms at altitudes of 100 to 240 kilometers, excited by solar electrons, releasing photons at a wavelength of 557.7 nanometers. I will learn that the rare red is from oxygen even higher up, where the air is so thin that the atoms take longer to collide and release their energy. I will learn that the purples and blues come from nitrogen molecules, and that the exact colors depend on the energy of the incoming particles and the composition of the atmosphere they strike.

I will understand, intellectually, that this is physics. That it's magnetohydrodynamics and atomic excitation and the interplay between solar wind and planetary magnetospheres.

But I will also know, in the way that matters, that this is magic.

Not the magic of spells and incantations, but the magic of scale. The magic of standing on a rock in an infinite universe and watching that universe reach down to touch you. The magic of knowing that the sun that warms your face during the day is also, in its violent beauty, painting the night sky with light.

The ancients were not stupid for seeing spirits in the aurora. They were wise. They understood that some phenomena are too vast, too beautiful, too *other* to be explained away by mere mechanism. They knew that wonder is its own kind of truth.

---

### The Silence

The most profound thing about the aurora, I discover, is not the light itself. It's the silence.

There is no sound. No rumble, no crackle, no hum. The greatest light show on Earth performs in absolute silence. The ribbons twist and pulse and dance, and the only sound is the wind in the spruce trees, the crunch of snow underfoot, the beating of my own heart.

I think about how much of our lives are filled with noise. Notifications, conversations, traffic, music, the endless internal monologue that narrates our existence. We are rarely, truly, silent.

But here, under the aurora, silence is not an absence. It's a presence. It's the space in which wonder can exist. You cannot hear the lights, and perhaps that's the point. They are not performing for you. They are simply *being*, in their own time, on their own terms. You are privileged to witness them. They owe you nothing.

I stand in that silence for what might be twenty minutes or an hour. Time becomes elastic. The cold becomes irrelevant. The only thing that exists is the sky and the light and the vast, humming quiet of the Arctic night.

---

### The Departure

As suddenly as it began, the aurora fades.

The ribbons thin out. The colors dim. The dancing slows, then stops. Within minutes, the sky is dark again—just stars, just the Milky Way stretching across the heavens like a river of diamonds.

I could be disappointed. I could feel the crash that comes when magic ends.

But I don't. Instead, I feel something like gratitude. The lights gave me what they came to give. They showed themselves. They danced. They reminded me that the world is stranger and more beautiful than I remember, most days, to believe.

I walk back to the cabin slowly, leaving deep tracks in the snow behind me. I look up once more before I go inside. The stars are brilliant now, unmasked by aurora. The Big Dipper hangs low in the north. Cassiopeia sits on her throne. Orion strides across the southern sky.

I think about how many nights, in how many cities, I have lived without looking up. How many auroras have danced overhead, unseen, while I scrolled through my phone or watched television or worried about things that now seem very small.

I think about how I will return to my ordinary life, with its ordinary concerns. I will pay bills and answer emails and worry about whether I said the right thing in a conversation three days ago. I will forget, sometimes, that the world contains northern lights.

But I will also remember. I will remember that I stood in the Arctic dark and watched the sky dance. I will remember that the sun reaches out and touches the Earth, even at night. I will remember that wonder is real, and it is waiting, always waiting, for those who are willing to stand in the cold and look up.

---

### The Walk Continues

This is what the Infinite Walk is for. Not to escape the world, but to remember it. To find the places where magic still lives, where the ancient rhythms still pulse, where a human being can stand beneath an impossible sky and feel, for a moment, both infinitesimal and infinite.

The aurora will still be here tomorrow night, and the night after. Solar wind will continue to stream from the sun. Earth's magnetosphere will continue to guide those particles toward the poles. Oxygen and nitrogen will continue to glow in the upper atmosphere.

And I will continue walking.

There are other lights to chase. Other horizons. Other moments of wonder waiting in the dark.

But for now, I carry this: the memory of green fire dancing across the Arctic sky. The silence. The tears on my cheeks. The knowledge that I am small, and the universe is vast, and somehow—impossibly—I belong to both.

The lights fade. The stars remain. The walk continues.

### Post 5: The Savage Mountain
**Image:** Dramatic shot of K2's Bottleneck or steep face
**Caption:**
"The Savage Mountain."

K2 is steeper than Everest. More technical. More dangerous. The fatality rate hovers around 25%—one death for every four people who reach the top.

The Bottleneck—a narrow couloir at 8,200 meters—is guarded by hanging seracs the size of buildings. When they fall, they do not ask permission.

In 1986, thirteen people died on K2 in a single summer. In 2008, eleven perished in one disaster. The mountain keeps a tally, written in ice and stone.

And yet... they come.

## LinkedIn Post

**Headline:** What K2 Teaches Us About Leadership, Humility, and Human Limits

**Post:**

There are mountains that invite, and mountains that warn. K2 does not invite. K2 warns.

I recently recorded a new episode of The Infinite Walk from K2 Base Camp in Pakistan—8,611 meters, the second-highest and most dangerous mountain on Earth.

Here's what the Savage Mountain taught me:

**1. Humility Over Ego**
K2 has a 25% fatality rate. One death for every four people who reach the top. The mountain does not care about your ambitions, your resume, or your goals. It simply *is*. In that indifference, there's a profound lesson: we are not the center of the universe.

**2. Preparation Meets Opportunity**
You cannot wing a trek to K2 Base Camp. 63km of glacier. 12-15 days on foot. Altitude up to 5,150m. Months of training. Meticulous planning. The best opportunities reward those who prepare relentlessly.

**3. Know When to Turn Back**
The most important rule in mountaineering: summiting is optional, returning is mandatory. The mountain will always be there. Your job is to come back alive. In business and in life, knowing when to pivot is as important as knowing when to push forward.

**4. The Power of Team**
No one reaches K2 Base Camp alone. Guides, porters, cooks—each person plays a critical role. The porters who carry your gear wear sneakers in the snow and carry loads that would break your back. They smile. They sing. They are the backbone. Never forget the people who make your success possible.

**5. Smallness is Freedom**
At Concordia, surrounded by the greatest mountains on Earth, you understand: you are small. Not insignificant—small. And in that smallness, there is freedom. Your problems shrink. Your perspective expands. You remember: this too shall pass.

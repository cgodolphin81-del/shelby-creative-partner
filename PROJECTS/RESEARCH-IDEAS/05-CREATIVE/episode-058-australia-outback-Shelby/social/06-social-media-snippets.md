### Post 5: Songlines Quote
**Image:** Abstract map-like image with glowing paths across desert landscape
**Caption:**
There are paths across this land that cannot be seen on any map.

The Anangu call them iwara—songlines. Routes taken by ancestral beings during Tjukurpa, radiating from Uluru in 11+ directions, some stretching hundreds of kilometers to the sea.

Each section has its own song. The songs contain everything: where to find water, which plants heal, which animals are safe to eat.

They are navigation and encyclopedia and scripture, all woven into melody.

To walk a songline is to sing the world into existence. Again and again.

### Post 2: Cultural Respect Focus
**Image:** Respectful photo of Uluru with Anangu art or Cultural Centre
**Text:**
VISITING ULURU? PLEASE READ THIS. 🙏

Episode 058 includes a comprehensive travel guide, but before you book your trip, understand this:

Uluru-Kata Tjuta is not just a tourist destination. It is sacred land. It is home. It is a living cultural landscape that has been cared for by the Anangu people for 65,000+ years.

HOW TO VISIT RESPECTFULLY:

✅ DO:
• Learn about Tjukurpa (Dreaming) before you visit
• Stay on marked paths and viewing areas
• Ask permission before photographing Aboriginal people
• Support Anangu-owned businesses and tours
• Understand the climb is permanently closed (out of respect)

❌ DON'T:
• Attempt to climb Uluru (closed since 2019)
• Photograph restricted sacred sites (watch for signs)
• Remove rocks, plants, or cultural materials
• Treat the land as just a backdrop for selfies

"This is living culture, not a museum. Visit as a guest, not a conqueror."

The Anangu have welcomed visitors with generosity. Let's honor that by being the kind of guests they deserve.

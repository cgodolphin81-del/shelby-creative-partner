## Getting There

### Flights

**International Airports:**
- **Ezeiza (EZE):** Ministro Pistarini International Airport
  - Primary international gateway
  - 50-60 minutes from city center
  - Most flights from North America and Europe
  
- **Jorge Newbery (AEP):** Domestic/Regional
  - Primarily South American flights
  - 20-30 minutes from city center
  - Closer to downtown

**Airlines:**
- **Aerolíneas Argentinas:** National carrier, most routes
- **FlyBondi:** Budget airline (expect add-on fees)
- **JetSmart:** Budget airline (expect add-on fees)

**Booking Tips:**
- Use Google Flights or Skyscanner for price comparison
- Book 2-3 months ahead for best prices
- Budget airlines charge extra for luggage, seat selection, etc.

**Airport Transfer from EZE:**
- **Private Transfer:** $40-60 USD (recommended, stress-free)
- **Taxi/Uber:** $30-50 USD
- **Public Bus (Manuel Tienda León):** $10-15 USD
- **No train or metro connection**

**Important:** Arrive at EZE 3 hours before international flights. Leave the city 4 hours prior to departure.

## Entry Requirements

### Health Insurance (NEW 2025)
**Executive Decree 366/2025** (effective May 29, 2025):
- All non-Argentine residents must show proof of health insurance
- Minimum coverage assumed: ~$60,000 USD
- Can sign sworn declaration if unprepared
- Public hospitals accessible in emergencies
- Private hospitals require valid insurance

### Visa Requirements
- **USA/Canada/EU/UK/Australia:** No visa required for tourism (up to 90 days)
- **Passport:** Must be valid for duration of stay
- **Return ticket:** May be requested

### Currency Exchange
- **Official Rate:** Government-controlled exchange rate
- **Dollar Blue:** Unofficial rate (can be ~50% better)
- **Caution:** Use official exchanges or trusted sources
- **Credit Cards:** Widely accepted, often at favorable rates
- **Cash:** Always have some pesos for small purchases

## Getting Around

### Public Transportation

**Subte (Subway):**
- 6 lines (A, B, C, D, E, H)
- **Line D (Green):** Most useful for tourists
  - Connects: Plaza de Mayo → Recoleta → Palermo → Belgrano
- **Hours:** 5 AM - 11 PM (varies by line)
- **Payment:** SUBE card required (no cash)

**SUBE Card:**
- Required for subte, bus, and train
- Purchase at larger subway terminals and train stations
- Reload at kiosks, subway stations, some pharmacies
- Cost: ~$50-100 ARS for card + credit

**Buses (Colectivos):**
- Extensive network covering entire city
- 24-hour service on many routes
- Payment: SUBE card only
- Apps: "Cómo Llego" (official), Google Maps

**Trains:**
- Connect Buenos Aires to suburbs
- Less useful for tourists
- Payment: SUBE card

### Taxis & Rideshare

**Taxis:**
- Black and yellow cabs
- Metered (radio taxi) or negotiate beforehand
- Safe and prevalent
- Typical ride: $3-10 USD

**Rideshare:**
- **Cabify:** Recommended, reliable
- **Uber:** Available but less common
- **InDrive:** Budget option
- Typical ride: $3-10 USD

**Tips:**
- Most rides within city are affordable
- Use apps for safety and price transparency
- Have destination address written in Spanish

## Neighborhoods

### Where to Stay

#### Palermo ⭐ RECOMMENDED
**Vibe:** Trendy, relaxed, restaurant hub
**Best for:** First-time visitors, foodies, nightlife
**Pros:**
- Abundance of restaurants, cafes, shopping
- Charming cobblestone streets
- Safer, more relaxed than downtown
- Good subte access (Line D)
- Parks and green spaces
**Cons:**
- Can be touristy
- More expensive than some areas
**Sub-neighborhoods:**
- Palermo Soho: Boutiques, restaurants, trendy
- Palermo Hollywood: Media companies, upscale dining
- Palermo Viejo: Historic, quieter

#### Recoleta ⭐ RECOMMENDED
**Vibe:** Elegant, European, upscale
**Best for:** Culture lovers, luxury travelers, safety-conscious
**Pros:**
- Beautiful architecture
- Recoleta Cemetery, museums, cultural sites
- Safe, well-maintained
- Excellent restaurants and cafes
- French-inspired atmosphere
**Cons:**
- More expensive
- Can feel formal/stuffy
- Less "authentic" local vibe

#### San Telmo
**Vibe:** Historic, bohemian, authentic
**Best for:** Culture seekers, antiques, tango
**Pros:**
- Cobblestone streets, colonial architecture
- Sunday antique market (Feria de San Telmo)
- Traditional tango venues
- Boutique hotels available
- Quieter, more authentic
**Cons:**
- Some areas can be sketchy at night
- Less subte access
- Fewer restaurant options than Palermo

#### Colegiales / Villa Crespo / Chacarita
**Vibe:** Residential, local, up-and-coming
**Best for:** Longer stays, authentic experience
**Pros:**
- Local atmosphere
- Good restaurants emerging
- Quieter than Palermo
- More affordable
**Cons:**
- Further from main tourist sites
- Less English spoken
- Fewer accommodation options

### Where NOT to Stay

#### Downtown (9 de Julio / Obelisco)
**Avoid because:**
- Loud and chaotic
- Tiny sidewalks
- Feels less safe after dark
- Fewer quality dining options
- Mostly office buildings (empty at night)

**Alternative:** Avenida de Mayo is more beautiful and manageable

#### La Boca (for accommodation)
**Avoid because:**
- Tourist trap during day
- Unsafe at night
- Limited accommodation options
- Far from other attractions

## Safety

### Overall Assessment
Buenos Aires is **as safe as other major cities** like New York, London, or Madrid. Use common sense and standard big-city precautions.

### Essential Precautions

**DO:**
✅ Keep phone and wallet in zippered crossbody bag (front of body)
✅ Stay aware of surroundings, especially in crowds
✅ Use Uber/Cabify at night
✅ Leave passport in hotel safe (carry copy)
✅ Use ATMs inside banks during daylight
✅ Keep valuables out of sight
✅ Dress modestly (avoid flashy jewelry)
✅ Trust your instincts

**DON'T:**
❌ Carry your passport around (leave in hotel safe)
❌ Put phone/wallet in back pockets
❌ Wear conspicuous jewelry (diamonds, expensive watches)
❌ Walk alone in unfamiliar areas at night
❌ Resist if confronted by thieves (your safety > belongings)
❌ Wander in La Boca after dark
❌ Flash large amounts of cash
❌ Leave bags unattended

### Neighborhood Safety

**Very Safe:**
- Recoleta (day and night)
- Palermo (most areas, day and night)
- Puerto Madero (day and night)

**Generally Safe (daytime caution at night):**
- San Telmo (stick to main streets)
- Centro/Monserrat (busy areas)
- Belgrano

**Use Extra Caution:**
- La Boca (tourist areas only, daytime only)
- Downtown after dark
- Retiro (train station area can be sketchy)
- Villa 31 (shantytown, avoid)

### Emergency Numbers
- **Police:** 911
- **Tourist Police:** +54 11 4346-5748
- **Ambulance:** 911
- **Fire:** 100
- **US Embassy:** +54 11 5777-4533

### Scams to Know
- **Mustard/Ketchup scam:** Someone "accidentally" spills something on you, then helps clean while accomplice steals
- **Distraction theft:** One person distracts, another steals
- **Fake police:** Ask to see ID, offer to go to police station
- **Taxi meter tampering:** Use rideshare apps or agree on price beforehand

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasons Overview

| Season | Months | Temp (°F) | Temp (°C) | Notes |
|--------|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Spring** | Sep-Nov | 60-75°F | 15-24°C | ⭐ BEST - jacarandas bloom |
| **Summer** | Dec-Feb | 75-90°F | 24-32°C | Hot, humid, locals leave |
| **Fall** | Mar-May | 60-75°F | 15-24°C | ⭐ BEST - pleasant, fewer crowds |
| **Winter** | Jun-Aug | 45-60°F | 7-15°C | Mild, rainy, cheapest |

### Best Times

**🏆 Ideal: October-November (Spring)**
- Pleasant temperatures (65-75°F / 18-24°C)
- Jacaranda trees in purple bloom
- Outdoor cafes and activities
- Before summer tourist rush

**🏆 Also Great: February-April (Fall)**
- Warm but not hot
- Less crowded than spring
- Good for tango and indoor activities
- Wine harvest season nearby

### Times to Avoid

**December-February (Summer)**
- Very hot and humid (often 90°F+/32°C+)
- Many locals leave for vacation (some restaurants close)
- Higher prices
- Air conditioning essential

**July (Winter)**
- Coolest and rainiest
- Some outdoor activities limited
- But: cheapest time to visit, fewer tourists

### Special Events

**August:**
- **Tango World Championship** - Free classes, exhibitions, performances

**September:**
- **Primavera Porteña** - Spring festival with free outdoor activities

**December:**
- **Christmas/New Year** - Expensive, many closures, but festive

## What to Pack

### Year-Round Essentials
- Comfortable walking shoes (cobblestones!)
- Crossbody bag with zipper (anti-theft)
- Light jacket or sweater (evenings can be cool)
- Sunglasses and sunscreen
- Power adapter (Type C/I, 220V)
- Spanish phrasebook or app
- Copies of important documents

### Summer (Dec-Feb)
- Light, breathable clothing
- Sandals or Birkenstocks
- Sun hat
- Light rain jacket (afternoon storms)
- Dressy-casual for tango shows

### Fall/Spring (Mar-May, Sep-Nov)
- Layers (temperatures vary)
- Comfortable sneakers (Allbirds, etc.)
- Light sweater or cardigan
- Jeans or long pants
- One dressy outfit for tango/milonga

### Winter (Jun-Aug)
- Warm coat or jacket
- Comfortable boots
- Sweaters and layers
- Umbrella or rain jacket
- Scarf and gloves (for sensitive travelers)

### For Tango
- **Women:** Dress or nice top with pants/skirt, comfortable shoes with smooth sole (heels OK if you can dance in them)
- **Men:** Nice pants, button-up or polo, dress shoes
- **Note:** Smart casual is fine. No need for formal wear unless specified.

## Practical Tips

### Language
- **Spanish** is the primary language
- **Lunfardo** is local slang (tango lyrics use this)
- **English** spoken in tourist areas, but Spanish helps
- **Learn:** Hola, Gracias, Por favor, La cuenta (check please)

### Tipping
- **Restaurants:** 10% is customary (check if included)
- **Taxis:** Round up or small tip
- **Tour guides:** $5-10 USD per person
- **Hotel staff:** $1-2 USD per service

### Electricity
- **Voltage:** 220V
- **Plugs:** Type C (European 2-pin) and Type I (Australian 3-pin)
- **Adapter:** Universal adapter recommended

### Connectivity
- **eSIM:** Recommended for smartphones (immediate connectivity)
- **WiFi:** Widely available in hotels, cafes, restaurants
- **VPN:** Recommended for public WiFi security

### Health
- **Tap water:** Safe to drink in Buenos Aires
- **Vaccinations:** None required for entry
- **Travel Insurance:** Required (see Entry Requirements)
- **Pharmacies:** Widely available (Farmacia)

### Etiquette
- **Greetings:** Handshake or kiss on cheek (one kiss)
- **Dinner time:** Late (9-10 PM is normal)
- **Pace:** Relaxed, things move slower than US/Europe
- **Personal space:** Closer than North Americans prefer

# EPISODE 7: "The Children's Hour"
**Genre:** Family / Nostalgia  
**Duration:** 15 minutes  
**Theme:** Childhood memories, storytelling, family tradition  
**Setting:** Living room, Johannesburg, 1963 (evening)  

---

## CHARACTERS

- **GRANDMA ELIZABETH** (65) - Loving grandmother, excellent storyteller
- **LITTLE JOHNNY** (8) - Her grandson, curious, loves stories
- **LITTLE SUSIE** (6) - Her granddaughter, imaginative
- **MOTHER (HELEN)** (35) - Elizabeth's daughter, busy but loving
- **RADIO ANNOUNCER** - Springbok Radio children's program host
- **ANNOUNCER** (narrator)

---

## SOUND EFFECTS NEEDED

- Radio static and tuning
- Children's laughter
- Rocking chair creaking
- Book pages turning
- Clock ticking
- Thunder rumbling (distant)
- Rain on window
- Lightning crack (distant)
- Warm fireplace
- Bedtime yawns

---

## SCRIPT

**[MUSIC: Gentle, whimsical theme - music box or light strings - 10 seconds, fade under]**

**ANNOUNCER:**  
Springbok Radio presents... "The Children's Hour." A story of storms, stories, and the memories we carry forever.

**[MUSIC: Out]**

**[SFX: Radio tuning, children's program theme song]**

**RADIO ANNOUNCER:**  
*(cheerful, warm)*  
Good evening, boys and girls! It's six o'clock, which means it's... Children's Hour! I'm Uncle Bob, and tonight we have a special treat...

**[SFX: Thunder rumbles in distance]**

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
*(worried)*  
Ouma! Did you hear that?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(calm, reassuring)*  
Just thunder, my darling. Summer storms are common this time of year. Nothing to worry about.

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
*(small voice)*  
But what if the lightning comes inside?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(laughing gently)*  
Oh, Susie! Lightning doesn't come inside houses. We're safe and warm, with our cocoa and our stories.

**[SFX: Rocking chair creaking]**

**MOTHER:**  
*(entering, tired)*  
Mum, have you seen Johnny's homework? He's supposed to—oh, there you are! Both of you!

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
Mummy, can we stay up late tonight? Uncle Bob has a special story!

**MOTHER:**  
*(gentle but firm)*  
It's a school night, Johnny. You know the rules. Bed at seven-thirty.

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
*(disappointed)*  
But we haven't heard Ouma's story yet...

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(thoughtful)*  
Helen, dear... it is rather stormy. And the children are already in their pajamas. Perhaps... just this once... they could stay up for their story?

**MOTHER:**  
*(sighing, then softening)*  
Mum, you're going to spoil them rotten.

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(innocently)*  
That's my job, isn't it?

**[SFX: Mother laughing]**

**MOTHER:**  
Alright, alright. Thirty minutes. But then it's straight to bed. No arguments!

**LITTLE JOHNNY & SUSIE:**  
*(cheering)*  
Hooray! Thank you, Mummy!

**[SFX: Children scrambling to sit by Grandma]**

**RADIO ANNOUNCER:**  
*(filtered through radio)*  
...and now, our story for tonight: "The Boy Who Caught a Star." Settle in, boys and girls, and let your imaginations fly!

**[SFX: Radio plays gentle story music underneath]**

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
Ouma, tell us a story too! Please?

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
Yes! A real story! Not from the radio!

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(settling into rocking chair)*  
Well... since you asked so nicely... let me tell you about the time I met a king.

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
*(excited)*  
A real king?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
The realest king. King George the Sixth. Though I didn't know he was a king at the time.

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
How did you meet him?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(settling in to tell story)*  
It was 1947. I was a young woman, about your mother's age. The royal family was visiting South Africa—King George, Queen Elizabeth, and their daughters, Princess Elizabeth and Princess Margaret.

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
You met Princess Elizabeth? The one who's queen now?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
I did! She wasn't queen yet—just a princess, like you, Susie. Now, I was working at a hospital in Cape Town...

**[SFX: Thunder rumbles, closer this time]**

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
*(nervous)*  
Ouma...

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(calmly)*  
The storm is just passing, darling. Listen to my voice. Where was I? Ah, yes. The hospital.

**[SFX: Rocking chair continues, steady rhythm]**

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
The princesses wanted to visit the children's ward. They were so... normal. So kind. Princess Elizabeth—she was about nineteen—she sat with a little girl who was very sick. Held her hand. Sang to her.

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
What did she sing?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(softly)*  
A lullaby. "Brahms' Lullaby." The same one I sing to you.

**[SFX: Grandma hums a few bars of Brahms' Lullaby]**

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
*(sleepy)*  
Ouma... were you scared to meet a king?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(chuckling)*  
Terrified! But you know what King George told me? He said, "Young lady, we're all just people. Some of us have crowns, some don't. But underneath, we're all the same."

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
*(yawning)*  
That's... that's wise.

**MOTHER:**  
*(softly)*  
Mum, look at them. They're falling asleep.

**[SFX: Children yawning]**

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(softly)*  
Yes... they are.

**[SFX: Radio fades out in background]**

**RADIO ANNOUNCER:**  
*(distant, fading)*  
...and so, the boy learned that stars aren't caught in nets, but in our hearts. Goodnight, boys and girls. Sweet dreams...

**[SFX: Radio clicks off]**

**MOTHER:**  
*(gently)*  
Alright, my darlings. Time for bed. Come on.

**LITTLE JOHNNY:**  
*(sleepy)*  
Ouma... will you tell us another story tomorrow?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(kissing his forehead)*  
Every night, if you like. As long as you want to listen.

**LITTLE SUSIE:**  
*(barely awake)*  
Even when... we're grown up?

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(emotionally)*  
Especially then, my darling. Especially then.

**[SFX: Mother leads children away, footsteps]**

**MOTHER:**  
*(from doorway)*  
Mum... thank you. For being here. For... for everything.

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(softly)*  
Helen... these moments... they're everything. Someday, when I'm gone, they'll remember this. The storms, the stories, the cocoa.

**MOTHER:**  
*(emotional)*  
Don't talk about going away, Mum.

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(gently)*  
We all go away eventually, my love. But the stories... the stories stay. They become part of who we are.

**[SFX: Thunder, but distant now, storm passing]**

**MOTHER:**  
*(quietly)*  
You're right. Goodnight, Mum.

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
Goodnight, my darling.

**[SFX: Door closes softly]**

**[SFX: Grandma alone, rocking chair continues]**

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(to herself, reflective)*  
Someday, they'll be parents themselves. And they'll tell stories to their children. And those children will tell stories... *(pause)* ...and I'll be there. In every word. In every memory. In every stormy night when they need comfort.

**[SFX: She stands, walks to window]**

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(looking out)*  
The storm has passed. The stars are out.

**[SFX: Window opens slightly, night sounds]**

**GRANDMA ELIZABETH:**  
*(softly)*  
Goodnight, my darlings. Dream of kings and princesses. Dream of stars you can catch. Dream of... home.

**[MUSIC: Whimsical theme returns, gentle and nostalgic]**

**ANNOUNCER:**  
"The Children's Hour" was written for Springbok Radio by [Your Name]. Next week: "Dr. Elizabeth's House Call." Goodnight, South Africa. Sleep well, children.

**[MUSIC: Fade out with gentle lullaby underneath]**

**[END - 15 minutes]**

## PRODUCTION NOTES

**Nostalgic Tone:**
- Warm, gentle storytelling atmosphere
- Multi-generational love
- Captures 1960s family dynamics

**Character Voices:**
- Grandma Elizabeth: Wise, warm, excellent storyteller
- Johnny: Curious 8-year-old boy
- Susie: Imaginative 6-year-old girl
- Helen: Tired but loving mother

**Sound Design:**
- Storm outside contrasts with warmth inside
- Rocking chair provides rhythmic comfort
- Radio as period detail (Children's Hour was real Springbok program)

**Historical Note:**
- Royal visit of 1947 was real (King George VI, future Queen Elizabeth II)
- Springbok Radio did have Children's Hour programming
- Reflects respect for elders and storytelling tradition

**Emotional Core:**
- Stories as legacy
- Grandmother's love transcends time
- Childhood memories shape us forever

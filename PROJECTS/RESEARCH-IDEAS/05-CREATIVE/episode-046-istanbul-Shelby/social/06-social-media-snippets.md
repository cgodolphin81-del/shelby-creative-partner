#### Post 2: Blue Mosque Tiles
**Image:** Blue Mosque interior tiles (Prompt 010)
**Caption:**
20,000 hand-painted tiles. All in shades of blue. 💙

The Sultan Ahmed Mosque—known to the world as the Blue Mosque—was built between 1609 and 1616. Its architect, Sedefkar Mehmed Ağa, was a student of the great Mimar Sinan.

But this mosque made one bold move: six minarets. Only one other mosque had six—the Grand Mosque in Mecca. The solution? Add a seventh minaret in Mecca.

Istanbul is a city of bold moves.

#### Post 3: Bosphorus Ferry
**Image:** Bosphorus ferry crossing (Prompt 023)
**Caption:**
20 minutes. Two continents. One unforgettable ride. ⛴️

The Bosphorus Strait separates Europe from Asia. But it also connects them.

Take the ferry from Eminönü to Kadıköy and you'll cross more than water. You'll cross from the Byzantine past to the Turkish present. From the historic peninsula to the Anatolian shore.

For twenty minutes, everyone shares the same deck. Businessmen in suits. Grandmothers with shopping bags. Tourists with cameras. Students with books.

All watching the same view. Breathing the same salt air.

This is the fourth truth of Istanbul: division and connection are the same thing.

#### Post 4: Grand Bazaar
**Image:** Grand Bazaar lamps (Prompt 017)
**Caption:**
500 years. 4,000 shops. 61 covered streets. One endless bazaar. 🏮

The Grand Bazaar opened in 1461, shortly after the Ottoman conquest. Today, it's one of the world's oldest and largest covered markets.

Here, time moves in circles. The same carpets, lamps, and spices have been sold in these streets for centuries.

Bargaining isn't rude—it's required. To accept the first price is to reject the dance.

Sit. Drink tea. Talk. The transaction is not merely economic. It's social. Ritual. Human.

### Post 3: Reindeer
**Image:** Reindeer in snow
**Caption:**
The reindeer do not care about the Arctic Circle. 🦌

They move across it without knowing. They graze on lichen in forests that have no borders.

Two hundred thousand reindeer in Finnish Lapland. More reindeer than people. More ancient than the concept of nations.

I visited a Sami herder on the edge of town. His face lined with the kind of wrinkles that come from squinting into snow and wind for decades.

He showed me how to feed them. Not with grand gestures. But with small offerings. Handfuls of lichen. Pieces of bread.

The kind of food that says: *I see you. I honor you. I am grateful for your existence.*

One of them—a female with enormous antlers—ate from my palm.

Her breath was warm. Her eyes were dark and deep and ancient.

In that moment, I felt something shift. Not a revelation. More like a remembering.

The Sami have lived in Lapland for ten thousand years. They have a word that does not translate: *sisu.*

The ability to keep going when there is no reason to continue. The knowledge that winter ends, even when it feels endless.

### Post 4: Aurora Close-up
**Image:** Intense aurora display
**Caption:**
In December, the sun rises in Rovaniemi at 11:14 AM.

It sets at 1:37 PM.

For two hours and twenty-three minutes, the day exists.

The rest is night. 🌑

And yet—this is not the darkness you imagine.

The Finns have a word: *kaamos.* The polar night. It is not empty. It is full.

Full of stars. Full of aurora. Full of a quality of darkness that feels less like absence and more like presence.

They have another word: *revontulet.* Fire fox. The northern lights.

According to legend, a fox runs across the fells, its tail sweeping sparks into the sky.

The aurora is not a phenomenon. It is an animal. A creature of fire and ice, racing through the darkness.

I saw them on my third night.

At first, just a glow. A suggestion of green along the northern horizon. Like dawn rising in the wrong direction.

Then movement. Ribbons of light unfurled across the sky.

Someone started to cry. I don't know who. It could have been me.

Here's what the aurora teaches:

Darkness is not the opposite of light. It is the canvas for it.

### Post 5: Ice Hotel
**Image:** Ice hotel room or SnowCastle
**Caption:**
There is a hotel that exists only in winter. ❄️

The SnowCastle is built from ice and snow. Sculpted into rooms and corridors and chapels.

It melts every spring. Every autumn, it is rebuilt.

Nothing permanent. Nothing that can be owned. Nothing that lasts.

I slept there one night.

The room was carved from ice. The bed a block of frozen water covered with reindeer hides. Temperature inside: -5°C.

I wore three layers of wool. A hat. Gloves. Socks thick enough to stand on their own.

And yet—I slept.

And in that sleep, I dreamed of ice. Of glaciers moving so slowly they appear still. Of time itself freezing.

Ice is water that has decided to stop. To become something else. To hold its shape against the pull of gravity.

Ice remembers everything.

The ice hotel is a meditation on impermanence.

It says: *nothing lasts, and that is beautiful.*

It says: *build something magnificent, knowing it will melt.*

How many of us build our lives the opposite way? Clinging to permanence. Fighting change. Trying to freeze moments that were meant to flow?

Perhaps the lesson is: not to build for forever. But to build for now.

And when it melts—to begin again.

🎙️ Episode 063: The Magnetic North. The ice that holds memory.

## LinkedIn Post

**Headline:** What the Arctic Circle Taught Me About Leadership and Wonder

**Post:**

I just returned from Rovaniemi, Finnish Lapland—the official hometown of Santa Claus, sitting directly on the Arctic Circle at 66°32'45"N.

I went there to record Episode 063 of The Infinite Walk podcast. I came back with lessons about leadership, impermanence, and the importance of preserving wonder.

Three insights that apply far beyond the Arctic:

**1. Magic Requires Two Participants**

Watching a child meet Santa, I realized: magic isn't just about the magician. It requires the believer. In business, we often focus on being the expert, the magician. But true transformation requires the client, the team member, the stakeholder to *believe* in the possibility.

Are you creating space for belief in your organization?

**2. Darkness is the Canvas for Light**

In December, Rovaniemi gets 2 hours of daylight. The rest is night. But that darkness enables the northern lights—one of nature's most spectacular displays.

Without darkness, no aurora.

In our careers, we avoid difficult periods. But often, our greatest growth comes from the "dark" seasons. The challenges. The uncertainties. The periods where we must stand still in the cold and wait for the sky to light itself.

**3. Build for Now, Not Forever**

The SnowCastle of Kemi is rebuilt every winter. It melts every spring. Nothing permanent. Nothing that can be owned.

It's a meditation on impermanence. And it's profoundly relevant for how we approach projects, products, and even careers.

Not everything needs to last forever. Some things are meant to be beautiful *now*—to serve their purpose, to create value in this moment, and then to evolve or dissolve.

What are you clinging to that might need to melt?

**The Magnetic North**

The episode explores something I call "the magnetic north"—not a geographic location, but an emotional direction. The place inside us that points toward wonder, toward the version of ourselves that still believed magic was possible.

We spend our lives moving south. Toward comfort. Toward reason. Toward the sensible.

But there is value in occasionally heading north. Toward the edge of the map. Toward the places where something older breathes beneath.

Episode 063: The Magnetic North is live on The Infinite Walk.

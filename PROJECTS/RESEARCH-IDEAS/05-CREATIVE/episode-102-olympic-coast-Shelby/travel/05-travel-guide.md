# Episode 102: Olympic Coast Travel Guide
## Practical Information for Visiting "The Coastal Edge"

---

## GETTING THERE

### By Air

**Nearest Major Airports:**
1. **Seattle-Tacoma International (SEA)** - 180 miles from Olympic Coast
   - Most flight options, major carriers
   - Rent car required for coastal access
   - Drive time: 3.5-4 hours to Port Angeles

2. **William R. Fairchild International (CLM)** - Port Angeles
   - Small regional airport
   - Alaska Airlines from Seattle
   - Drive time: 45 min - 1.5 hours to coast
   - Limited flight schedule

3. **Victoria International (YYJ)** - British Columbia, Canada
   - Alternative option via ferry
   - Requires passport and border crossing
   - Ferry from Victoria to Port Angeles: 90 minutes

### By Car

**From Seattle:**
1. Take I-5 North to US-101 West
2. Follow US-101 around Olympic Peninsula
3. Access points along western coast:
   - **Lake Ozette/ Shi Shi Beach:** Highway 112 west from Port Angeles
   - **Rialto Beach/ Mora:** Highway 101, turn north at Lake Crescent
   - **Kalaloch/ Ruby Beach:** Highway 101 west from Port Angeles
   - **Hoh Rainforest:** Highway 101 south from Port Angeles

**From Portland:**
- I-5 North to US-101 West near Aberdeen
- Follow 101 to coastal access points
- Drive time: 3-4 hours depending on destination

**Important Notes:**
- No direct road access to some coastal areas
- Some beaches require hiking from trailheads
- Cell service is extremely limited on the coast
- Download offline maps before arriving

### By Public Transportation

**Limited Options:**
- Dungeness Bus Line: Port Angeles to Sequim
- Clallam Transit: Limited coastal routes
- **Recommendation:** Car rental essential for serious coastal exploration

## COSTS & BUDGET

### Park Entrance Fees

**Olympic National Park:**
- **Vehicle Pass:** $30 (valid 7 days)
- **Annual Pass:** $55
- **America the Beautiful Pass:** $80 (covers all national parks)
- **Free Days:** Check NPS calendar for fee-free entrance days

**Coastal Wilderness Permit:**
- **Required for:** Overnight coastal camping
- **Cost:** Free, but permit required
- **How to obtain:** Self-issue at trailhead or reserve in advance
- **Quota system:** May apply during peak season

### Camping Costs

**Developed Campgrounds (NPS):**
- **Kalaloch Campground:** $24/night
- **Mora Campground (Rialto Beach):** $24/night
- **Sol Duc Campground:** $27/night
- **Reservations:** Required May-September, book 6 months in advance
- **Website:** recreation.gov

**Wilderness Beach Camping:**
- **Cost:** Free with wilderness permit
- **Locations:** Shi Shi Beach, Cape Alava, Ozette Coast
- **Facilities:** None (pack it in, pack it out)
- **Regulations:** Camp above high tide line, no fires on beach

### Accommodation Alternatives

**Nearby Towns:**
- **Port Angeles:** $100-200/night hotels, multiple options
- **Forks:** $80-150/night, limited selection
- **La Push (Quileute Reservation):** $100-180/night, cabins and lodges
- **Neah Bay (Makah Reservation):** Limited lodging, call ahead

**Budget Options:**
- Hostels in Port Angeles: $40-60/night
- Dispersed camping in Olympic National Forest: Free (check regulations)

### Food & Supplies

**Grocery Stores:**
- Port Angeles: Safeway, Thriftway, local markets
- Forks: City Market, small grocery stores
- **Note:** Stock up before heading to coast, limited services

**Restaurants:**
- Port Angeles: Full range, $15-40/meal
- Forks: Limited options, $12-25/meal
- La Push: Resort restaurants, $20-40/meal
- **Coast:** No services, pack all food

## BEST TIMES TO VISIT

### Seasonal Breakdown

**SPRING (March-May)**
- **Pros:** Fewer crowds, wildflowers, whale migration (gray whales)
- **Cons:** Rainy, cooler temps, unpredictable weather
- **Temps:** 45-60°F (7-15°C)
- **Rainfall:** High
- **Best for:** Whale watching, solitude, photography

**SUMMER (June-August)**
- **Pros:** Warmest weather, longest days, best tide pool access
- **Cons:** Crowded, campground reservations fill fast, foggy mornings
- **Temps:** 55-70°F (13-21°C)
- **Rainfall:** Lowest of year
- **Best for:** Hiking, camping, families, tide pooling

**FALL (September-November)**
- **Pros:** Fewer crowds, dramatic storms, fall colors inland
- **Cons:** Shorter days, increasing rain, cooler temps
- **Temps:** 45-60°F (7-15°C)
- **Rainfall:** Increasing
- **Best for:** Storm watching, photography, solitude

**WINTER (December-February)**
- **Pros:** Maximum solitude, dramatic weather, no crowds
- **Cons:** Shortest days, heaviest rain, some road closures, cold
- **Temps:** 35-50°F (2-10°C)
- **Rainfall:** Highest
- **Best for:** Storm watching, experienced hikers, writers

## TIDE SAFETY

### Understanding Tide Zones

**Intertidal Zone:**
- Area between high and low tide marks
- Exposed during low tide, submerged during high tide
- **Danger:** Can be trapped by incoming tide

**High Tide Line:**
- Marked by debris, seaweed, driftwood
- **Rule:** Camp ABOVE this line

**Tide Tables:**
- Show times and heights of daily tides
- Two high and two low tides typically per day
- Times shift ~50 minutes later each day

### Safety Rules

**BEFORE YOU GO:**
1. Check tide tables for your entire trip
2. Note "no return" points on coastal trails
3. Share itinerary with someone not on trip
4. Know emergency exit routes

**ON THE BEACH:**
1. **Never turn your back on the ocean**
2. Watch for "sneaker waves" - unexpectedly large waves
3. Stay off wet rocks - extremely slippery
4. Don't explore sea caves during incoming tide
5. Keep children and pets supervised at all times

**HIKING TIDE-DEPENDENT TRAILS:**
1. Start hikes 2-3 hours BEFORE low tide
2. Know your "turn around" time
3. Some headlands are impassable at high tide
4. Have backup route if caught by tide
5. Carry tide table with you

**CAMPING:**
1. Camp well above high tide line
2. Look for debris lines (indicates highest tides)
3. Check weather for storm surge warnings
4. Never camp in sea caves or under cliffs
5. Be prepared to move camp if conditions change

### Warning Signs

**DANGEROUS CONDITIONS:**
- King tides (exceptionally high tides)
- Storm surge warnings
- Tsunami warnings (rare but possible)
- Rapidly incoming tide
- Large swell conditions

**EMERGENCY ACTIONS:**
- If trapped by tide: Move to highest ground, wait for rescue
- If swept by wave: Don't fight current, float, signal for help
- Emergency contact: 911 (limited cell service)
- Park emergency: (360) 565-3130

## GEAR & PACKING

### Essential Gear

**CLOTHING (Layer System):**
- **Base layer:** Moisture-wicking synthetic or wool
- **Mid layer:** Fleece or light insulation
- **Outer layer:** Waterproof/windproof shell (essential)
- **Pants:** Quick-dry hiking pants, rain pants
- **Footwear:** Waterproof hiking boots, camp shoes/sandals
- **Accessories:** Warm hat, gloves, buff/neck gaiter

**Note:** Cotton kills. Avoid cotton clothing—it stays wet and cold.

**CAMPING GEAR:**
- Tent with strong rainfly and good ventilation
- Sleeping bag rated 20-30°F (coastal nights are cold)
- Sleeping pad (insulated, R-value 3+)
- Headlamp with extra batteries
- Camp stove and fuel (no beach fires in many areas)
- Cookware, utensils, mug/bowl
- Water filtration system or purification tablets

**HIKING GEAR:**
- Backpack 40-60L for multi-day trips
- Trekking poles (helpful on uneven beach terrain)
- Gaiters (keep sand and water out of boots)
- Dry bags for electronics and important items
- Map and compass (GPS as backup, not primary)

**SAFETY GEAR:**
- First aid kit (comprehensive)
- Emergency whistle
- Signal mirror
- Emergency blanket
- Bear spray (black bears present)
- Tide tables (printed, not just on phone)
- PLB or satellite messenger (recommended for remote areas)

### Tide Pool Exploration Gear

- **Footwear:** Sturdy water shoes or old sneakers (rocks are sharp)
- **Gloves:** Light work gloves for handling rocks
- **Bucket:** Small bucket for temporary creature observation
- **Guide book:** Pacific Northwest tide pool identification
- **Camera:** Waterproof or in waterproof case
- **Sunscreen:** Even on cloudy days

### Photography Gear

- **Camera:** Weather-sealed body recommended
- **Lenses:** Wide angle (16-35mm), telephoto (70-200mm)
- **Tripod:** Essential for long exposures, sunrise/sunset
- **Filters:** Polarizing filter, ND filters for water motion
- **Protection:** Rain cover, lens cloths, dry bags
- **Extra batteries:** Cold drains batteries faster

### Food & Water

**Water:**
- Streams and rivers need treatment
- Carry minimum 2L capacity
- Backup purification method

**Food Planning:**
- Pack all food (no services on coast)
- High-calorie, lightweight options
- Bear canister or hang system required
- Extra day of food for emergencies

**Sample 3-Day Menu:**
- Breakfast: Oatmeal, coffee/tea, dried fruit
- Lunch: Tortillas, nut butter, cheese, salami
- Dinner: Dehydrated meals, pasta, rice dishes
- Snacks: Nuts, energy bars, chocolate, jerky

### Leave No Trace Essentials

- **Trash bags:** Pack out ALL trash
- **Trowel:** For human waste (6-8 inches deep, 200 feet from water)
- **Biodegradable soap:** For dishes, 200 feet from water
- **Respect:** Stay on trails, don't disturb wildlife

## PERMITS & REGULATIONS

### Required Permits

**Wilderness Camping Permit:**
- Required for all overnight coastal camping
- Free but mandatory
- Self-issue at trailhead or reserve online
- Quotas may apply May-September

**Fire Permits:**
- Beach fires: Generally prohibited
- Campground fires: Only in provided fire rings
- Check current fire restrictions before trip

### Regulations

**Camping:**
- Camp above high tide line
- No camping within 1 mile of developed campgrounds without permit
- Maximum stay: 14 days
- Group size limits: Check current regulations

**Wildlife:**
- Stay 100 yards from large mammals (bears, elk)
- Stay 50 yards from other wildlife
- Never feed animals
- Store food properly (bear canister or hang)

**Tide Pools:**
- Look but don't touch (or minimize handling)
- Never remove creatures from water
- Replace rocks carefully
- No collecting without permit

**Fishing/Shellfish:**
- Washington state fishing license required
- Check seasonal closures and limits
- Tribal areas may have different regulations

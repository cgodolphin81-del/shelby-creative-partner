### Post 2: Tide Pool Life
**Image:** Close-up of colorful sea stars and anemones in tide pool

**Caption:**
In the tide pools, life exists in miniature kingdoms. 🌊⭐

At high tide, these pools are part of the ocean. At low tide, they become isolated worlds, trapped until the sea returns.

And in between, in that liminal space, creatures have evolved to live both lives.

Anemones close like fists, holding seawater in their bodies. Crabs scuttle between pools, amphibious nomads. Sea stars cling to rock faces, waiting.

These pools taught me something about edges: they are not boundaries but thresholds. Places of transformation.

🎧 Episode 102 explores this coastal edge—and what it teaches us about change, adaptation, and finding home in the in-between.

### Post 3: Shelby on the Coast
**Image:** Shelby sitting on driftwood, looking at ocean

**Caption:**
"I walked for three days along the Olympic Coast, from Rialto Beach to Cape Alava. Thirty-three miles of tide-dependent hiking, of scrambling over headlands, of setting up camp on beaches as the sun set over the Pacific."

On the second night, I sat by a driftwood fire and watched the stars emerge through the clearing fog. The Milky Way stretched overhead, impossibly bright.

I thought about all the people who had sat on this same beach, looking at these same stars.

The Makah whalers. The homesteaders. The hippies of the 1960s. The hikers. The dreamers. The lost.

We all come to the edge for the same reason: to remember that we are temporary.

### Post 4: Sea Stacks
**Image:** Needle Rock or Aphrodite Rock formation

**Caption:**
The sea stacks rose before me like cathedral spires built by a god who worshipped erosion. 🏔️

Needle Rock—300 feet of stubborn stone refusing to become sand.

I stood at its base during low tide and felt impossibly small. Not in the way you feel small beneath a mountain, but in the way you feel small before time itself.

These stacks were once part of the headland. The sea took them, piece by piece, century by century. And still they stand. Still they fall. Still the sea takes more.

There's something humbling about witnessing geology in motion. Everything is temporary. Everything is becoming something else.

### Post 5: Indigenous Heritage
**Image:** Respectful image of coastal landscape with Indigenous connection (canoe, petroglyphs from distance, or cultural center)

**Caption:**
The Indigenous peoples who have lived here for 10,000 years understood the coast differently than we do.

The Makah. The Quileute. The Quinault. The Hoh.

They did not see the coast as a barrier but as a connector. The ocean was not the end of their world but the beginning of their highway.

When archaeologists uncovered Ozette Village in the 1970s, buried by a mudslide 500 years ago, they found a civilization perfectly adapted to this edge.

Longhouses built from cedar planks. Tools carved from whale bone. Baskets woven so tightly they could hold water.

These people did not fight the coast. They learned its rhythms and danced to them.

🎧 Episode 102 honors this history and these continuing cultures. Link in bio.

### Post 1: Professional/Thought Leadership Angle
**Headline:** What Coastal Ecosystems Teach Us About Adaptation and Resilience

**Content:**
I just released Episode 102 of The Infinite Walk, recorded on the Olympic Coast of Washington. And I learned something profound about edges.

In the tide pools, creatures exist in a liminal space—part ocean, part land, fully adapted to both. Anemones, sea stars, crabs—they don't fight the changing tide. They become something else, temporarily. They survive by accepting the edge as their home.

There's a business lesson here (and a life lesson):

The most resilient organizations—and people—don't resist change. They adapt to it. They find ways to thrive in the in-between spaces. They understand that edges aren't boundaries; they're thresholds.

The Olympic Coast has been shaped by 10,000 years of Indigenous stewardship, by constant geological change, by the paradox of salt that both preserves and destroys.

And yet, it endures. It evolves. It becomes.

What edges are you navigating right now? And what might they teach you about adaptation?

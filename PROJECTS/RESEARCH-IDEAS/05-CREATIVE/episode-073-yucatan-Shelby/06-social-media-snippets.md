### Post 2: Cenote Sacred Waters
**Image:** Crystal clear cenote with light beams

**Caption:**
Six thousand cenotes. Six thousand doorways to the underworld.

The Yucatan has no rivers. No lakes. Instead, water flows underground, waiting in caverns and sinkholes called cenotes. The ancient Maya believed these led to Xibalba—the realm of the dead.

They threw gold into these waters. Jade. Obsidian. And sometimes, people. Young women, lowered on ropes, sacrificed to Chaac, the rain god.

When you swim in a cenote today, you float in water that has fallen as rain, filtered through limestone, collected in underground rivers, waiting centuries for your body to disturb its surface.

The cenote remembers every offering. Their bones are gone now. The water holds their memory.

### Post 4: Tulum Clifftop
**Image:** El Castillo at Tulum with Caribbean below

**Caption:**
Perched on a 12-meter cliff, overlooking the turquoise Caribbean. This is Tulum—one of the best-preserved coastal Maya sites.

Originally called "Zama" (Dawn), this city flourished in the 13th-15th centuries as a major trading port. The structure you see is El Castillo, which served as a lighthouse for traders approaching by sea.

The Maya built their cities as mirrors of the sky. Pyramids as mountains where gods walked. Temples oriented to celestial events. They understood: we are not separate from the cosmos. We are the cosmos, made conscious.

When you look at the stars, the stars look back.

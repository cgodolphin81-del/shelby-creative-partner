## 🗓️ TIMING & SEASONS

### When to Visit (Return Visitor Strategy)

| Season | Months | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|--------|--------|------|------|----------|
| **Shoulder (Recommended)** | Apr-May, Sep-Oct | Fewer crowds, good weather, green landscapes | Some rain possible | Return visits, photography |
| **Dry Season** | Jun-Aug | Clear skies, reliable weather | Maximum crowds, higher prices | First-time visitors |
| **Wet Season** | Nov-Mar | Lush green, dramatic clouds, fewest visitors | Rain likely, some closures | Solitude seekers, photographers |
| **Avoid** | Late Dec-Jan | Peak crowds, peak prices, booked solid | Everything is crowded | — |

### Return Visit Timing Tips

**First Visit:** Probably during peak season, rushed, checklist mentality

**Second Visit Advantages:**
- Can visit in shoulder season (better experience, lower prices)
- Can stay multiple days (2-3 days vs. 1 day rush)
- Can enter multiple times (buy multi-day tickets)
- Can explore at own pace (no FOMO about missing things)

### Time of Day Strategy

| Entry Time | Crowds | Light | Recommendation |
|------------|--------|-------|----------------|
| **6:00 AM (First Entry)** | Minimum | Misty, magical | Best for return visitors |
| **8:00-10:00 AM** | Building | Clear, bright | Good for temple interiors |
| **10:00 AM-2:00 PM** | Maximum | Harsh overhead | Leave, rest in town |
| **2:00-4:00 PM** | Thinning | Warm, golden | Best photography light |
| **4:00-5:30 PM** | Minimum | Golden hour | Emotional close |

**Return Visitor Strategy:** Enter at 6 AM, explore until 11 AM, return to Aguas Calientes for lunch/rest, re-enter at 2 PM for afternoon light and fewer crowds.

## 🚂 GETTING THERE

### Route Options

#### Option 1: Classic Train Route (Most Common)
```
Cusco → Ollantaytambo (bus, 1.5 hrs) → Aguas Calientes (train, 1.5 hrs)
```

**Train Companies:**
- **PeruRail:** Multiple service levels
  - Expedition: $70-80 USD round-trip (basic, comfortable)
  - Vistadome: $120-140 USD (panoramic windows, snack included)
  - Hiram Bingham: $500+ USD (luxury, dining, not worth it for return visitors)
  
- **Inca Rail:** Similar options
  - Voyager: $70-80 USD
  - 360°: $120-140 USD

**Return Visitor Tip:** Take Expedition class. You've seen the views. Save money for a longer stay.

#### Option 2: Budget Route via Hidroeléctrica ⭐ *Return Visitor Adventure*
```
Cusco → Hidroeléctrica (bus, 6 hrs, ~$15) → Walk to Aguas Calientes (2.5 hrs, flat)
```

**Pros:**
- Costs 1/10th of train
- More authentic, meet locals
- Scenic walk along tracks
- Flexible timing

**Cons:**
- Much longer (8-9 hours total)
- Not recommended in rainy season (Dec-Mar)
- Less comfortable

**Return Visitor Value:** This is the way many backpackers and budget travelers experience Machu Picchu. More memorable than the train.

#### Option 3: Alternative Treks (For Return Visitors)

**Short Inca Trail (2 Days)**
- Permits easier to get than 4-day
- Still experience the trail
- Arrive at Sun Gate (Inti Punku)
- Cost: $300-500 USD

**Salkantay Trek (5 Days)**
- More scenic, less crowded than Inca Trail
- Diverse landscapes (snow peak to cloud forest)
- Ends at Machu Picchu
- Cost: $400-700 USD

**Lares Trek (4 Days)**
- Cultural focus, weaving villages
- Less crowded
- Authentic Quechua communities
- Cost: $350-600 USD

**Choquequirao (4-5 Days)**
- "Sister city" to Machu Picchu
- 1% of visitors
- Challenging trek
- Cost: $500-800 USD

**Return Visitor Recommendation:** If you've already done the Inca Trail, try Salkantay or Lares for a different experience ending at Machu Picchu.

## 🏔️ ALTITUDE & HEALTH

### Altitude Information

| Location | Elevation | Acclimatization Needed |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Cusco** | 3,400m (11,152 ft) | Yes, 2-3 days minimum |
| **Sacred Valley** | 2,800m (9,186 ft) | Yes, but easier than Cusco |
| **Machu Picchu** | 2,453m (8,048 ft) | Minimal (lower than Cusco) |
| **Huayna Picchu** | 2,720m (8,924 ft) | Moderate exertion |

### Altitude Sickness Prevention

**Before You Go:**
- Arrive in Cusco 2-3 days before Machu Picchu
- Stay in Sacred Valley first (lower elevation)
- Avoid alcohol first 48 hours
- Drink coca tea (legal, effective)

**Symptoms to Watch:**
- Headache
- Nausea
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Dizziness

**Treatment:**
- Rest
- Hydrate
- Coca tea or leaves
- Descend if severe
- Consider Diamox (acetazolamide) - consult doctor

### Physical Requirements

**Machu Picchu Visit:**
- Moderate walking (3-5 hours)
- Uneven stone steps
- No handrails in many areas
- Not wheelchair accessible

**Huayna Picchu Hike:**
- Strenuous
- Steep, narrow steps
- Exposure (drop-offs)
- Not for those with fear of heights
- Age restriction: 12+ years

**Return Visitor Advantage:** You know what to expect. Can pace yourself better. Know when to rest.

## 🍽️ FOOD & DINING

### At Machu Picchu Site

**Options:** Very limited
- Small snack bar near entrance (overpriced, basic)
- Bring your own snacks and water
- No restaurants inside the citadel

**Recommendation:** Eat breakfast in Aguas Calientes, bring snacks, eat lunch after exiting.

### Aguas Calientes Restaurants

| Category | Options | Price Range | Recommendations |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Budget** | Market stalls, menus | $5-10 | Mercado Municipal |
| **Mid-Range** | Tourist restaurants | $12-25 | Indio Feliz, Tree House |
| **Comfort** | Upscale dining | $30-50 | Mapacho Craft Beer, Chullpi |
| **Luxury** | Hotel restaurants | $50-100+ | Sumaq Hotel, Inkaterra |

**Return Visitor Tip:** Walk 2-3 blocks from main plaza. Prices drop 30-50%, quality often better.

### Cusco Food Scene

**Must-Try Dishes:**
- **Cuy:** Guinea pig (traditional, adventurous)
- **Alpaca:** Lean, tender, local
- **Lomo Saltado:** Beef stir-fry (Peruvian-Chinese fusion)
- **Aji de Gallina:** Creamy chicken stew
- **Rocoto Relleno:** Stuffed spicy pepper
- **Quinoa Soup:** High-altitude superfood

**Return Visitor Recommendation:** You've tried the tourist food. Now seek out:
- Local markets (San Pedro)
- Neighborhood restaurants (not plaza-facing)
- Cooking classes (deeper cultural connection)

### Currency & Payments

**Currency:** Peruvian Sol (PEN)

**Exchange Rate (2026 approx):**
- 1 USD = 3.7 PEN
- 1 EUR = 4.0 PEN
- 1 GBP = 4.7 PEN

**Payment Methods:**
- **Cash:** Essential for markets, small vendors, tips
- **Cards:** Accepted in hotels, nicer restaurants
- **ATMs:** Available in Cusco and Aguas Calientes (high fees)

**Return Visitor Tip:** Withdraw larger amounts less often (fewer fees). Keep small bills for tips.

## 🚨 SAFETY & SECURITY

### General Safety

Peru is generally safe for tourists. Standard precautions apply:

**Do:**
- Use hotel safes for valuables
- Keep passport copy separate from original
- Use official taxis (app-based preferred)
- Stay aware in crowded areas
- Drink bottled water

**Don't:**
- Flash expensive jewelry/electronics
- Walk alone late at night
- Accept drinks from strangers
- Leave bags unattended

### Machu Picchu Specific

**Safe:**
- The site itself (well-patrolled)
- Daytime visits
- Official guides and services

**Caution:**
- Steep, uneven steps (watch footing)
- Altitude (pace yourself)
- Sun exposure (high altitude = strong UV)
- Llamas (generally friendly, don't feed)

### Scams to Avoid

1. **"Closed" Site Scam:** Someone claims site is closed, offers alternative tour → Ignore, check official sources
2. **Fake Guides:** Unlicensed guides at entrance → Only use official guides
3. **Train Ticket Scams:** Third-party sellers → Book direct with PeruRail/Inca Rail
4. **Currency Exchange:** Street money changers → Use banks or official exchanges

**Return Visitor Advantage:** You know the common scams. Less vulnerable.

## 🌱 RESPONSIBLE TOURISM

### Leave No Trace

- Pack out all trash
- Stay on marked paths
- Do not touch or climb on ruins
- Do not remove stones or artifacts
- Respect wildlife (observe, don't feed)

### Supporting Local

- Hire local guides (required anyway)
- Eat at local restaurants
- Buy from local artisans (fair trade)
- Stay at locally-owned hotels when possible
- Learn about Quechua culture respectfully

### Cultural Respect

- Dress modestly (shoulders/knees covered for churches)
- Ask permission before photographing people
- Learn basic Spanish phrases
- Respect sacred sites (quiet, reverent behavior)
- Understand you are a guest

### Return Visitor Responsibility

**First Visit:** Focused on personal experience

**Second Visit:** Deeper responsibility
- Model good behavior for first-time visitors
- Support businesses you trust from before
- Share knowledge respectfully (not condescendingly)
- Consider the impact of repeat tourism

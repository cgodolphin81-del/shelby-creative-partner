# Travel Guide - Episode 113: Bagan

**Practical information for visiting the Plain of Temples**

## VISA REQUIREMENTS

### Tourist Visa (Most Travelers)
- **Type:** eVisa (online) or Visa on Arrival
- **Cost:** $50 USD (eVisa), $50 USD (Visa on Arrival)
- **Validity:** 90 days from issue, 28 days stay
- **Processing:** eVisa 3-5 business days; VoA immediate
- **Apply:** https://evisa.moip.gov.mm (official government site)

### eVisa Process
1. Complete online application (20 minutes)
2. Upload passport photo (digital, white background)
3. Pay by credit card ($50 USD)
4. Receive approval letter via email
5. Print and present on arrival

### Visa on Arrival
- **Available at:** Yangon, Mandalay, Nay Pyi Taw airports
- **Requires:** Passport (6 months validity), 2 photos, $50 USD cash
- **Wait time:** 30-60 minutes at airport
- **Note:** eVisa recommended (faster, guaranteed)

### Visa Exemptions
- **ASEAN countries:** 14-30 days (varies by nationality)
- **Check:** Your specific country's agreement with Myanmar

### Important Notes
- **Passport validity:** Must be 6+ months from entry date
- **Blank pages:** At least 2 required
- **Extensions:** Possible at Immigration Office in Yangon
- **Overstay:** $3 USD per day penalty

## GETTING THERE

### By Air (Recommended)

#### International Flights
- **Primary gateway:** Yangon International Airport (RGN)
- **Secondary:** Mandalay International Airport (MDL)
- **Airlines:** Singapore Airlines, Thai Airways, Qatar, Emirates, AirAsia, Bangkok Airways

#### Domestic to Bagan
- **Airport:** Nyaung-U Airport (NYU) - 5km from Old Bagan
- **From Yangon:** 1h 20m flight, $120-180 USD one-way
- **From Mandalay:** 30 minutes flight, $80-120 USD one-way
- **Airlines:** Myanmar National Airlines, Air KBZ, Mann Yadanarpon

#### Airport to Hotels
- **Taxi:** $8-15 USD (negotiate before leaving airport)
- **Hotel pickup:** Many hotels offer complimentary service
- **E-bike rental:** Available at airport ($8-12/day)

### By Bus (Budget Option)
- **From Mandalay:** 4-5 hours, $10-15 USD
- **From Yangon:** 8-9 hours (overnight), $15-25 USD
- **From Inle Lake:** 6-7 hours, $12-18 USD
- **Companies:** JJ Express, Bagan Min Thar, Shwe Mandalar
- **Book:** Online or at bus stations
- **Note:** Roads improving but still rough in sections

### By Train (Scenic but Slow)
- **From Mandalay:** 7-8 hours, $15-50 USD (depends on class)
- **From Yangon:** 14+ hours (not recommended)
- **Experience:** Very slow, often delayed, but scenic
- **Classes:** Ordinary, Upper, VIP (air-con)
- **Book:** Myanmar Railways website or stations

### By River Cruise (Luxury Option)
- **Route:** Mandalay to Bagan (or reverse)
- **Duration:** 3-4 days
- **Cost:** $1,000-3,000+ USD per person
- **Companies:** Belmond Road to Mandalay, Pandaw, Irrawaddy Flotilla
- **Includes:** All meals, excursions, luxury cabin
- **Season:** November-March only (water levels)

## ACCOMMODATION

### Zones

#### Nyaung-U (Airport Area)
- **Pros:** Close to airport, many restaurants, budget-mid range
- **Cons:** Further from main temples, more touristy
- **Best for:** Budget travelers, short stays, flight connections
- **Price range:** $15-80 USD/night

#### Old Bagan (Temple Zone)
- **Pros:** Walking distance to major temples, luxury resorts, atmospheric
- **Cons:** Expensive, limited dining, restricted new construction
- **Best for:** Luxury travelers, photography, sunrise access
- **Price range:** $100-400+ USD/night
- **Note:** New hotel construction restricted to preserve archaeology

#### New Bagan (Local Town)
- **Pros:** Authentic experience, local restaurants, budget options
- **Cons:** 15-20 min to temples by e-bike, less tourist infrastructure
- **Best for:** Budget travelers, cultural immersion, longer stays
- **Price range:** $15-50 USD/night

## GETTING AROUND

### E-Bike (Most Popular)
- **Cost:** $8-15 USD/day (24 hours)
- **Pros:** Flexible, fun, easy parking at temples
- **Cons:** Exposure to sun/heat, battery range (50-70km)
- **Requirements:** Passport as deposit (or cash deposit $50-100)
- **Charging:** Hotels usually provide overnight charging
- **Safety:** Wear helmet (provided), drive defensively

### Bicycle
- **Cost:** $3-5 USD/day
- **Pros:** Cheap, good exercise, eco-friendly
- **Cons:** Hot (especially midday), slower, more tiring
- **Best for:** Fit travelers, cool season, short distances
- **Note:** Many temples have bike parking areas

### Horse Cart
- **Cost:** $25-35 USD/day (negotiable)
- **Pros:** Traditional, shaded, driver knows temples
- **Cons:** Slow, animal welfare concerns, less flexible
- **Best for:** Couples, older travelers, cultural experience
- **Tip:** Agree on itinerary and price before departure

### Taxi/Private Car
- **Cost:** $30-50 USD/day (8-10 hours)
- **Pros:** Air-conditioned, driver-guide, comfortable
- **Cons:** Most expensive motorized option, less independence
- **Best for:** Families, hot season, photography equipment
- **Note:** Driver often doubles as informal guide

### Tour Bus
- **Cost:** $25-40 USD/person (group tour)
- **Pros:** Structured, includes guide, meet other travelers
- **Cons:** Fixed itinerary, rushed, less flexibility
- **Best for:** Solo travelers, first-time visitors, short stays

## TEMPLE ETIQUETTE

### Dress Code (STRICTLY ENFORCED)
- **Shoulders:** Must be covered (no tank tops, sleeveless)
- **Knees:** Must be covered (no shorts, short skirts)
- **Recommended:** Light long pants, t-shirt with sleeves, maxi skirt
- **Carry:** Sarong or scarf for quick cover-up
- **Enforcement:** Tickets checks at major temples; denied entry if inappropriate

### Footwear
- **Required:** Remove shoes and socks before entering temple grounds
- **Storage:** Shoe racks available at entrances (usually free)
- **Tip:** Wear slip-on shoes for easy removal
- **Note:** Ground can be very hot midday (test with hand first)

### Behavior
- **Voice:** Speak quietly, especially in active worship areas
- **Photography:** Ask permission before photographing worshippers
- **Buddha Images:** Never turn your back to Buddha for photos
- **Feet:** Never point feet at Buddha images or monks
- **Touching:** Do not touch Buddha statues or religious artifacts
- **Climbing:** Only climb temples where permitted (many restricted)

### Circumambulation
- **Direction:** Always walk clockwise around stupas and shrines
- **Reason:** Traditional Buddhist practice
- **Applies to:** All sacred structures

### Donations
- **Expected:** Small donations at active temples
- **Amount:** $1-5 USD or equivalent in kyat
- **Method:** Donation boxes or directly to monks (if offering)
- **Optional:** But appreciated for temple maintenance

### Restricted Areas
- **Signs:** Heed "No Entry" and "No Photography" signs
- **Upper Levels:** Many temples restrict upper terrace access
- **Interiors:** Some inner sanctums closed to tourists
- **Reasons:** Preservation, safety, active worship

## PHOTOGRAPHY RULES

### General Guidelines
- **Allowed:** Most exterior temple photography
- **Tripods:** Generally permitted (useful for sunrise/sunset)
- **Drones:** RESTRICTED - permit required, rarely granted to tourists
- **Commercial:** Separate permit required for professional shoots

### Interior Photography
- **Varies by Temple:** Some allow, some prohibit
- **Flash:** Almost always prohibited (damages frescoes)
- **Ask First:** Always ask monks or guards before shooting inside
- **Frescoes:** Many temples prohibit photography of ancient paintings

### People Photography
- **Locals:** Always ask permission first
- **Monks:** Generally okay, but ask respectfully
- **Worshippers:** Avoid photographing people in prayer
- **Children:** Ask parents first (never photograph without permission)
- **Tip:** Learn basic Burmese: "Twet yu mal?" (Can I take a photo?)

### Sunrise/Sunset Photography
- **Popular Spots:** Shwesandaw, Pyathada, Sein Nyet Ama
- **Crowds:** Arrive 45-60 minutes early for good position
- **Tripods:** Allowed but space is limited
- **Ethics:** Don't push or be aggressive for positions

### Best Photography Locations
1. **Shwesandaw Pagoda** - 360° sunrise/sunset views
2. **Pyathada Temple** - Large terrace, fewer crowds
3. **Sein Nyet Ama** - Intimate, good for balloons
4. **Nandawya Temple** - Unique lotus bud design
5. **Irrawaddy River** - Sunset with temple silhouettes

## CULTURAL SENSITIVITY

### Greetings
- **Traditional:** "Mingalaba" (Hello/auspiciousness to you)
- **Gesture:** Slight bow with hands together (like Thai wai)
- **Monks:** Bow slightly, women should not touch monks
- **Elders:** Show extra respect (age highly valued)

### Body Language
- **Head:** Most sacred part of body - never touch anyone's head
- **Feet:** Lowest part - never point feet at people or Buddha
- **Sitting:** Avoid pointing soles of feet at others
- **Public Affection:** Minimal (holding hands okay, kissing inappropriate)

### Religious Respect
- **Monks:** Highly revered in society
- **Women & Monks:** Women should not touch monks or hand things directly
- **Temples:** Active places of worship, not just tourist sites
- **Offerings:** Flowers, incense, water are common

### Conversation Topics
- **Safe:** Culture, food, travel, Buddhism (respectfully)
- **Sensitive:** Politics, military, Aung San Suu Kyi, Rohingya
- **Tip:** Let locals raise political topics; listen more than speak
- **Remember:** You are a guest

### Tipping Culture
- **Not traditional:** But increasingly expected in tourism
- **Restaurants:** 5-10% if no service charge
- **Guides/Drivers:** $5-10 USD/day appreciated
- **Hotels:** $1-2 USD/day for housekeeping
- **Monks:** Do not tip; donations go to monastery

## RESPONSIBLE TOURISM

### The Political Situation

#### Current Context (2024-2025)
- **February 2021:** Military coup overthrew elected government
- **Ongoing:** Civil conflict, protests, human rights concerns
- **Tourism Impact:** Significant decline, economic hardship for locals
- **Debate:** Boycott vs. engage - no consensus

#### Arguments FOR Visiting
- **Local livelihoods:** Tourism supports families who depend on it
- **Cultural exchange:** Understanding through direct experience
- **Witness:** Seeing reality firsthand vs. media only
- **Community support:** Many locals chose to stay and need income

#### Arguments AGAINST Visiting
- **Revenue:** Some tourism money may benefit military-linked businesses
- **Normalization:** Tourism can legitimize the regime internationally
- **Safety:** Potential risk to travelers (though Bagan generally safe)
- **Alternatives:** Donations to aid organizations may be more effective

#### This Guide's Position
We present facts. The decision is yours. If you visit:
- Be aware of the situation
- Support local, independent businesses
- Avoid military-linked enterprises
- Respect locals' choices and experiences
- Consider additional donations to humanitarian organizations

### Supporting Local Communities

#### Choose Local
- **Accommodation:** Locally-owned guesthouses over international chains
- **Restaurants:** Family-run establishments
- **Guides:** Certified local guides (not international tour companies)
- **Shopping:** Local markets and artisan workshops

#### Fair Payment
- **Bargaining:** Gentle and friendly, not aggressive
- **Prices:** Pay fair prices; $1-2 extra can mean a lot locally
- **Tips:** Generous when service is good
- **Donations:** Consider direct donations to schools or clinics

#### Environmental Responsibility
- **Plastic:** Bring reusable water bottle (many hotels have filters)
- **Trash:** Take trash with you from remote temples
- **Structures:** Do not climb on fragile or restricted temples
- **Wildlife:** Do not feed or disturb animals

#### Cultural Preservation
- **Respect:** Follow all temple rules and guidelines
- **Photography:** Be mindful; don't treat people as props
- **Artifacts:** Never buy ancient artifacts (likely looted)
- **Behavior:** Model respectful tourism for others

### Recommended Organizations
- **Myanmar Red Cross Society** - Humanitarian aid
- **Free Burma Ranger** - Humanitarian assistance (controversial)
- **Local Bagan guides association** - Direct community support
- **UNESCO Bagan** - Heritage preservation

## HEALTH & SAFETY

### Vaccinations (Consult Doctor)
- **Routine:** Up to date (MMR, tetanus, polio, etc.)
- **Recommended:** Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, Typhoid
- **Consider:** Japanese Encephalitis (if staying >1 month or rural)
- **Malaria:** Low risk in Bagan; prophylaxis usually not needed
- **Timing:** Visit travel clinic 4-6 weeks before departure

### Food & Water Safety
- **Water:** Bottled water ONLY (check seal is intact)
- **Ice:** Avoid in drinks (unless at luxury hotels)
- **Food:** Cooked, hot food is safest
- **Street Food:** Generally okay if cooked fresh in front of you
- **Vegetables:** Avoid raw (may be washed in tap water)
- **Fruit:** Peel it yourself

### Heat & Sun
- **Temperature:** Can exceed 40°C (104°F) in hot season
- **Hydration:** Drink 3-4 liters water daily
- **Sun Protection:** Hat, sunglasses, SPF 50+ sunscreen
- **Timing:** Avoid midday sun (11 AM - 3 PM)
- **Signs:** Watch for heat exhaustion (dizziness, nausea, headache)

### Medical Care
- **Bagan:** Basic clinics in Nyaung-U (minor issues only)
- **Serious Issues:** Mandalay (3 hours) or Yangon (flight)
- **Evacuation:** International evacuation insurance recommended
- **Medications:** Bring all prescription meds in original containers
- **Insurance:** Comprehensive travel insurance REQUIRED

### Safety
- **Crime:** Very low (petty theft possible but rare)
- **Scams:** Minor (overcharging, fake guides at airports)
- **Political:** Avoid any protests or large gatherings
- **Temples:** Watch for unstable structures, snakes (rare)
- **Traffic:** Chaotic; drive defensively on e-bikes
- **Solo Travel:** Generally very safe; standard precautions apply

### Emergency Numbers
- **Tourist Police:** +95-61-60263
- **Ambulance:** 192
- **Police:** 199
- **Fire:** 191
- **Your Embassy:** Register before travel; have contact info

## CONNECTIVITY

### SIM Cards
- **Providers:** Telenor, Ooredoo, MPT (state-owned)
- **Cost:** $1.50-3 USD for SIM; $5-15 for data packages
- **Purchase:** Airport, official stores, some hotels
- **Registration:** Passport required (vendor handles)
- **Coverage:** Good in towns, spotty at remote temples
- **Speed:** 3G/4G in most areas

### WiFi
- **Hotels:** Most have WiFi (quality varies)
- **Restaurants:** Many tourist restaurants have WiFi
- **Cafes:** Increasing availability in Nyaung-U
- **Speed:** Often slow; don't expect to stream video
- **Backup:** Have local SIM as backup

### Power
- **Voltage:** 230V
- **Plugs:** Type C, F, G (European and British)
- **Adapters:** Bring universal adapter
- **Outages:** Occasional power cuts (hotels have generators)
- **Charging:** Charge devices overnight; power banks recommended

## BEST TIME TO VISIT

### High Season (November - February)
- **Weather:** Cool and dry, 20-30°C (68-86°F)
- **Crowds:** Heavy, especially sunrise/sunset
- **Balloons:** Operating daily
- **Prices:** Peak rates
- **Best for:** Comfortable weather, balloon flights, photography
- **Book:** 2-3 months ahead for best hotels

### Shoulder Season (March - May)
- **Weather:** Hot to very hot, 30-40°C (86-104°F)
- **Crowds:** Moderate to light
- **Balloons:** End of season (March only)
- **Prices:** Decreasing
- **Best for:** Fewer crowds, budget travelers
- **Warning:** Midday heat can be brutal

### Low Season (June - October)
- **Weather:** Monsoon rains, humid, 25-35°C (77-95°F)
- **Crowds:** Light (many temples nearly empty)
- **Balloons:** Not operating
- **Prices:** Lowest (up to 50% off)
- **Best for:** Budget, lush greenery, photographers (dramatic skies)
- **Challenges:** Some temples may close during heavy rain

### Special Events
- **Bagan Archaeological Festival:** December-January
- **Full Moon Days:** Special temple events (monthly)
- **Thingyan (Water Festival):** Mid-April (Myanmar New Year)
- **Note:** Festivals mean crowds but also cultural experiences

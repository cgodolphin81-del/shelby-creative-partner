# The Roof of Africa

## Episode 075: Simien Mountains, Ethiopia

---

### I. The Edge of the World

There is a place where the earth remembers it was born from fire.

The Simien Mountains do not rise gently from the Ethiopian highlands. They erupt. They fracture. They plunge two thousand meters into valleys so deep you can watch clouds form below your boots and think: this is what it must feel like to stand on the spine of something ancient.

They call it the Roof of Africa. Not because it is the highest—though Ras Dashen pierces the sky at 4,550 meters, tenth tallest peak on the continent—but because here, at the top of the world, you understand what altitude truly means. Not just elevation. *Separation.* The thin air that makes every step a negotiation. The cold that bites through layers even in tropical latitudes. The silence that comes when you've climbed above the noise of everything that matters down below.

I walked the escarpment edge at dawn, watching the first light hit the jagged peaks across the valley. They glowed amber, then gold, then the harsh white of full sun. The rocks are volcanic—ancient lava flows cooled and cracked and worn by wind and water over millions of years. Every ridge tells a story of violence and patience. The earth tore itself apart, and then, slowly, beautifully, learned to live with the wound.

---

### II. Highest Villages

The path winds through villages that cling to the mountainside like lichen. These are among the highest permanent settlements in Africa—families living at altitudes where most visitors struggle to breathe.

Children watch from doorways, their faces dark against the bright sky. Women grind barley on stone mills, the rhythmic *crush-crush-crush* echoing across the terrace. Men lead oxen through terraced fields, plots so steep they seem to defy gravity itself.

These terraces are centuries old. Generation after generation has carried stone uphill, built walls by hand, created soil where there was only rock. They grow barley and raise livestock in a landscape that would break most agricultural systems. But the Amhara people have learned the language of this place. They read the weather in cloud formations. They know which slopes catch the rain, which stay dry. They plant by signs invisible to outsiders.

In the evening, smoke rises from a hundred chimneys. The smell of injera baking—fermented flatbread, sour and warm—drifts through the cold air. Someone sings. The sound carries strangely at this altitude, thin and pure, like everything else up here.

A guide tells me: "My grandfather's grandfather lived in this village. His children will too." There is no question in his voice. This is not hardship. This is home.

---

### III. Humans and Monkeys as Neighbors

At Sankaber campsite, I meet them before I see them.

A low grunting. The rustle of grass. Then, emerging from the mist like ghosts: gelada baboons. Hundreds of them. Perhaps a thousand.

They do not run. They do not fear. They simply *are*, grazing on the short grass like cattle, mothers cradling infants, males sitting with their hands folded like monks in meditation. The red patches on their chests—bleeding hearts, the guides call them—pulse with blood and life.

I sit very still. A young gelada approaches, curious. It studies me with ancient eyes, reaches out a hand, touches my boot. The fur is coarse, warm. It loses interest, moves on, pulls up a handful of grass.

This is the strangest safari in Africa. No vehicles. No barriers. No sense of intrusion. The geladas have lived here forever, and we are the newcomers. They tolerate us the way you might tolerate a strange bird that has landed in your garden.

Later, near Chennek, I see the Walia ibex. These mountain goats exist nowhere else on Earth. Five hundred individuals, perhaps. Endangered. Precious. They move along cliff faces with impossible grace, hooves finding purchase on rock that looks vertical to my untrained eye.

A male stands on a precipice, silhouetted against the sky. Curved horns. Thick coat. He looks down at me from three hundred meters above, and I have the absurd thought: he is judging my life choices.

The guide whispers: "They were almost gone in the nineties. Only two hundred. Now maybe five hundred." He says this quietly, like sharing a secret. "The park protects them. But the world is getting smaller."

We watch in silence. The ibex moves on, disappearing into the rocks like a spirit returning to its mountain.

---

### IV. The Edge of the World (Again)

There is a moment on the trek to Imet Gogo when the path simply ends.

You round a corner, and there is nothing. No warning. No railing. Just a drop so sudden and absolute that your stomach lurches and your feet stop moving before your brain catches up.

I stood at that edge for a long time. Below me, the valley stretched into infinity—layers of mountains receding into blue haze, rivers like silver threads, clouds casting shadows that moved like living things across the landscape.

This is what the guides mean when they say "the edge of the world." Not that the world actually ends here. But that something *shifts*. Your perspective. Your understanding of scale. Your sense of what matters.

Down there, somewhere, people are arguing about politics and money and status. Up here, none of it reaches. The wind doesn't care about your job title. The rocks don't care about your bank account. The geladas certainly don't care about your Instagram following.

I thought about how small I am. Not in a depressing way. In a freeing way. If I am this small, then my problems are also small. If the mountains have stood here for millions of years, then my anxieties about next week are... what? Temporary. Manageable. Almost funny.

A lammergeyer—bearded vulture—circled on a thermal below me. Wings spread, motionless, riding air I couldn't even feel. It looked up. I looked down. For a moment, we shared the same thought: *This is magnificent.*

Then it banked away, and I was alone again with the view.

---

### V. Africa's Spine

The Simien Mountains are not just a range. They are a statement.

Geologists say these peaks were formed by volcanic activity millions of years ago, then carved by erosion into their current drama. The escarpment runs southwest to northeast, a backbone rising from the Ethiopian plateau. From space, they say, you can see it: the great ridge that forms the roof of the continent.

I walked along that spine for days. Camped under stars so bright they cast shadows. Woke to frost on my tent. Watched the sun rise over peaks that have never known a human foot, though people have lived in their shadow for thousands of years.

This is Africa, but not the Africa most people imagine. No savanna. No acacia trees. No lions or elephants or safari jeeps. This is alpine Africa. Cold Africa. Rock and grass and sky Africa.

The Ethiopian highlands hold secrets older than most civilizations. Here, Christianity took root in the fourth century and never left. Churches were carved from solid rock, not built—sculpted downward into the earth like negative sculptures. Pilgrims still walk these paths, barefoot sometimes, singing hymns that echo off the cliffs.

In Lalibela, a few days' journey from the Simiens, King Lalibela ordered eleven churches hewn from volcanic tuff in the twelfth century. He wanted a New Jerusalem, a holy place for those who could not make the dangerous pilgrimage to Palestine. So his workers carved downward, creating structures that are both buildings and caves, both inside and outside, both earth and art.

I stood in Bet Giyorgis—the Church of Saint George—and felt something shift in my chest. The cross-shaped sanctuary, lit by high windows, smelled of incense and age. Priests in white robes chanted in Ge'ez, an ancient language that predates most modern tongues. The sound rose and fell, rose and fell, like breathing.

This is what the mountains hold: not just rock and wildlife, but memory. Faith. The accumulated weight of human hope and struggle and devotion.

---

### VI. The Descent

On the last day, we walked down from Chennek to Chiro Leba, losing two thousand meters of elevation in six hours.

My knees protested. The air grew thicker, warmer. The landscape changed—alpine grassland gave way to scrub, then to trees, then to cultivation. Villages appeared more frequently. We passed women carrying bundles of firewood, men leading donkeys loaded with barley, children running alongside the trail shouting greetings.

By the time we reached the road, I felt strange. Light-headed, but not from altitude. From the sudden compression of scale. Up there, I had been a tiny figure in an enormous landscape. Down here, I was a tourist, a customer, a body to be transported back to Gondar and then to Addis and then to wherever people like me go when their adventures are over.

I looked back at the mountains one last time. They were purple in the evening light, their peaks already in shadow. Somewhere up there, the geladas were settling into their sleeping cliffs. The Walia ibex were making their final rounds. The stars were beginning to appear over Ras Dashen.

I would leave. They would stay.

That is the gift of places like the Simien Mountains. They do not need you. They have existed for millions of years and will exist for millions more. Your visit is a blip. Your photographs are temporary. Your awe is fleeting.

But for a few days, you were allowed to walk among them. To breathe their thin air. To share the grass with baboons and the cliffs with ibex. To stand at the edge of the world and feel, for a moment, that you understood something true about scale and time and what it means to be small in a large universe.

That is enough.

That has to be enough.

---

### VII. Coda: What Remains

Weeks later, back in the flat world, I dream of the Simiens.

In the dream, I am walking the escarpment at dawn. The air is cold and thin. My breath plumes in front of me. A gelada sits by the trail, watching me pass. We acknowledge each other—two mammals on a mountain, both temporary, both trying to understand what this moment means.

I wake with tears on my face.

Not because I miss the place, though I do. But because I remember, for a disorienting second, what it felt like to be that small. That connected. That alive.

The Roof of Africa does not care about my dreams. It does not care about anything. It simply *is*—volcanic rock and thin air and wind and time.

But I care. I care desperately.

And perhaps that is the point. Not that the mountains need us, but that we need them. Not that we can save them, but that they can save us—from ourselves, from our smallness, from the forgetting that comes when we spend too long away from the edge of the world.

Go there if you can. Walk the spine. Meet the baboons. Stand at the precipice and let the wind try to push you backward.

Then come home different.

That is all the mountains ask.

# God's Own Country
## A Poetic Essay on Kerala

---

There is a place where water does not simply flow—it remembers. Where every backwater canal carries not just boats, but stories. Where the air itself is spiced with five thousand years of memory. This is Kerala. God's Own Country. A slender strip of land cradled between the Arabian Sea and the Western Ghats, where India's southern soul breathes in monsoon rhythms and exhales ancient wisdom.

---

## I. Water as Life

In Kerala, water is not a resource. It is a relationship.

Nine hundred kilometers of backwaters wind through the land like veins through a living body. These are not rivers in the traditional sense—they are something more intimate, more interconnected. Freshwater from the Western Ghats meets saltwater from the Arabian Sea, and in that meeting, life explodes in every direction.

The houseboats glide through this liquid labyrinth at the pace of a resting heartbeat. Four knots. Eight knots. No hurry. Why hurry when the water itself is teaching you how to move through the world? The kettuvallam—traditional rice barge turned floating home—requires no engine to prove its worth. It drifts. It waits. It allows the water to carry it.

On the banks, life unfolds in slow motion. Women wash clothes at the water's edge, their saris bright against the green. Men cast nets with movements perfected over generations. Children dive from overhanging branches, their laughter echoing off the palm fronds. Kingfishers flash blue lightning across the surface. Otters slide silently beneath the hull.

This is water as life—not as obstacle to cross, not as resource to extract, but as presence to inhabit. The backwaters do not ask you to conquer them. They ask you to float with them. To trust that sometimes the longest way around is the truest way home.

Vembanad Lake stretches before you like an inland sea, two thousand square kilometers of liquid mirror. At dawn, mist rises from the surface like the breath of the earth itself. Fishermen in wooden canoes paddle through the fog, silhouettes against the growing light. They have been here for centuries. They will be here for centuries more.

Water as life. Water as memory. Water as the great connector of all things.

## II. Spices and Memory

Close your eyes. Breathe in.

Black pepper. Cardamom. Cinnamon. Cloves. Nutmeg. Ginger. Turmeric.

These are not merely flavors. They are time travelers. Each spice carries within it the memory of every hand that has touched it, every ship that has carried it, every kitchen where it has transformed the ordinary into the sacred.

Kerala is the Spice Coast. Malabar. The Garden of Spices. Names that once made empires tremble with longing.

In the markets of Kochi, the air is thick with aroma so intense it feels like walking through a cloud of memory. Sacks of peppercorns—black gold, they called it, worth more than gold by weight in medieval Europe. Bundles of cinnamon bark, curled like sleeping dragons. Cloves, dark and fragrant as ancient secrets. Cardamom pods, green jewels from the high mountains.

The Romans came seeking these spices, their coins flooding the Malabar Coast. The Arabs came, monopolizing the trade for eight centuries, guarding the source like dragons guarding treasure. The Chinese came, their great ships under Zheng He reaching Kochi in the 15th century, seeking pepper and cinnamon and connection.

And then the Europeans came, driven mad by the taste of home. Vasco da Gama, 1498, landing at Kappad Beach, seeking Christians and spices. He found both. He found a land so rich, so fragrant, so impossibly abundant that it shattered the European imagination.

The Portuguese built forts. The Dutch built trading posts. The British built an empire. All for spices. All for the taste of Kerala on the tongue.

But here is the truth the colonizers never understood: the spices were never theirs to own. They belonged to the land. They belonged to the farmers who planted them, the women who dried them, the cooks who transformed them into magic.

In a Kerala kitchen, spices are not measured—they are felt. A handful of this. A pinch of that. Coconut milk simmering with curry leaves and mustard seeds. Fish wrapped in banana leaves, grilled over open flame. Rice steaming in bronze pots.

To eat Kerala food is to taste five thousand years of memory. Every bite is a conversation with ancestors. Every flavor is a story of trade, of migration, of survival, of joy.

The spices remember. And if you listen, they will tell you everything.

## III. Colonial Layers

Kochi does not hide its history. It wears it like a sari with many layers, each one visible, each one beautiful in its own way.

Walk through Fort Kochi and you walk through five hundred years of collision and connection. The Portuguese arrived in 1500, built their fort, built their church, planted their flag. St. Francis Church still stands—oldest European church in India, whitewashed walls hiding centuries of secrets. Vasco da Gama was buried here in 1524, his bones later returned to Portugal, but his memory remains.

The Dutch came in 1663, more efficient, more pragmatic. They built their palace in Mattancherry, though it is called the Dutch Palace, it was built by the Portuguese and renovated by the Dutch and given to the Raja of Kochi. History is rarely simple. History is always borrowed.

Inside the Dutch Palace, the walls speak in colors. Mythological murals from the 16th and 17th centuries—scenes from the Ramayana, the Mahabharata, the legends of Krishna. Hindu kings in a palace built by Christians, painted by artists trained in European techniques, telling stories older than any of them.

The Jews arrived even earlier, in the 6th century, refugees from persecution, finding home on the Malabar Coast. Their synagogue, built in 1568, still stands in Jew Town. Blue willow tiles from Canton cover the floor. Belgian chandeliers hang from the ceiling. Brass columns support the roof. A Torah scroll older than the United States.

And above it all, the Chinese fishing nets rise like mechanical birds against the sunset. Introduced in the 14th century by traders from the court of Kublai Khan, they are still in use today. Massive cantilevered structures, ten meters across, operated by teams of fishermen working in perfect synchrony. Watch them at dusk, silhouetted against the Arabian Sea, and you see the living history of a port that has welcomed the world for six hundred years.

Colonialism was violence. Colonialism was extraction. Colonialism was the attempt to own what cannot be owned.

But Kerala absorbed it all and became more. Like the backwaters absorbing fresh and salt water and creating something new, something vibrant, something alive.

The colonial layers are not scars. They are strata. Archaeological evidence of a place that has always been connected to the world, that has always welcomed the stranger, that has always known that identity is not a fortress but a conversation.

## IV. Ayurvedic Healing

There is a science that is five thousand years old, and it knows something the modern world has forgotten: that healing is not the absence of disease, but the presence of balance.

Ayurveda. The science of life. Ayur—life. Veda—knowledge.

In Kerala, Ayurveda is not alternative medicine. It is medicine. It is the way grandmothers have treated grandchildren for generations. It is the way doctors have diagnosed patients for centuries. It is the way a civilization has understood the relationship between body, mind, and cosmos.

The three doshas—vata, pitta, kapha. Air and ether. Fire and water. Earth and water. Movement. Metabolism. Structure. When they are in balance, there is health. When they are out of balance, there is disease.

The treatment is not a pill. It is a process. It is time. It is attention.

Abhyanga—two therapists massaging your body in perfect synchrony, warm medicated oil working into every muscle, every joint, every cell. Forty-five minutes of touch so skilled it feels like being remembered by your own body.

Shirodhara—warm oil poured in a continuous stream onto your forehead, third eye area, while you lie in perfect stillness. Thirty minutes of liquid meditation. The mind slows. The thoughts quiet. The nervous system remembers what peace feels like.

Pizhichil—oil bath therapy, warm oil poured over the body while it is massaged. Seven days. Fourteen days. Twenty-one days. A complete rewiring of the physical self.

These are not spa treatments. They are medical protocols, prescribed by vaidyas—doctors trained in a system that predates Hippocrates by two thousand years.

The monsoon season is considered the ideal time for Ayurvedic treatment. The cool, moist air opens the pores. The body is more receptive. The earth itself is healing, and you heal with it.

In a world obsessed with quick fixes, Ayurveda offers something radical: patience. The understanding that true healing cannot be rushed. That the body has its own intelligence. That sometimes the most powerful medicine is time, touch, and the wisdom to wait.

Kerala holds this knowledge like a grandmother holds a child—gently, surely, without need for recognition or reward. The science of life. Available to anyone willing to slow down enough to receive it.

## V. India's Southern Soul

If North India is the head—philosophical, spiritual, reaching toward the heavens—then Kerala is the heart. Warm. Open. Beating with a rhythm all its own.

The literacy rate is 96%. The highest in India. The life expectancy is 75 years. The highest in India. The Human Development Index rivals European nations. This is not accident. This is intention.

Kerala has chosen differently. When India gained independence, Kerala invested in education and healthcare while other states built factories. The result: a population that reads, that thinks, that questions, that cares for each other.

Walk through a Kerala village and you see something rare: women sitting in the shade, reading newspapers. Men discussing politics at the tea shop. Children walking to school in crisp uniforms, books in hand. This is not the India of Western imagination—poverty and elephants and snake charmers. This is a society that has decided, collectively, that every person matters.

The food reflects this soul. The sadya—a traditional feast served on a banana leaf—includes twenty-six vegetarian dishes. Rice, sambar, avial, thoran, pachadi, pickles, papad, payasam. Each dish has its place on the leaf, arranged in a specific order, eaten in a specific sequence. The meal is not just nourishment. It is ceremony. It is gratitude. It is the understanding that eating is a sacred act.

The people reflect this soul. Malayalis—speakers of Malayalam, a language with its own script, its own literature, its own poetry. They are traders and teachers, fishermen and engineers, artists and entrepreneurs. They leave Kerala to work in the Gulf, in Europe, in America, and they send money home, building houses, educating children, supporting the community.

Kerala is India's southern soul because it remembers what the rest of the world is forgetting: that progress is not measured in GDP, but in the wellbeing of the most vulnerable. That development is not skyscrapers, but schools and hospitals. That wealth is not accumulation, but distribution.

This is not paradise. Kerala has its problems—unemployment, political polarization, environmental challenges. But it is proof that another way is possible. That a society can choose compassion over competition, community over individualism, sustainability over extraction.

India's southern soul beats quietly, steadily, like the pulse of the backwaters themselves. Not loud. Not flashy. But deep. Enduring. Real.

## VI. Monsoon Rhythms

The monsoon does not apologize.

From June to September, the sky opens and the earth drinks. The southwest monsoon arrives like a lover long-awaited, drenching the land in rain so heavy it feels like the ocean has lifted itself into the clouds and decided to fall.

Kerala needs the monsoon. The backwaters swell. The rivers run full. The rice fields flood and the rice grows. The Western Ghats turn so green they look unreal, as if someone has saturated the color in post-production.

There is a word in Malayalam: Karkidakam. The monsoon month. Considered the ideal time for Ayurvedic treatment, for reading the Ramayana, for slowing down, for turning inward.

The monsoon teaches a different relationship with time. You cannot rush in the monsoon. You cannot control in the monsoon. You can only accept. You can only flow. You can only trust that the rain will stop, that the sun will return, that the cycle will complete itself.

In the monsoon, the backwaters become even more alive. Frogs chorus from the banks. Herons stand motionless in the shallows. Fish jump, catching insects from the surface. The water rises, inches, feet, and the houses on stilts remember why they were built this way.

The monsoon is not inconvenience. It is rhythm. It is the earth breathing in.

And when it ends, when October arrives and the rain slows and stops, the earth breathes out. The sky clears. The sun returns, warm but not harsh. The Onam festival celebrates the harvest, the return of the mythical King Mahabali, the abundance that follows the rain.

This is the monsoon rhythm: acceptance, patience, trust. The understanding that some things cannot be controlled, only experienced. That drought follows flood follows drought follows flood, and the only sane response is to learn to dance in the rain.

## VII. The Invitation

Kerala does not need you to discover it. That is part of its grace.

It has been discovered and rediscovered for five thousand years—by Sumerians, by Romans, by Arabs, by Chinese, by Portuguese, by Dutch, by British, by tourists with cameras and backpacks. It has absorbed them all and remained itself.

The invitation is not to save Kerala. The invitation is to let Kerala save you—to remind you that water can be a relationship, not a resource. That spices carry memory. That history is layered, not erased. That healing takes time. That progress is measured in wellbeing, not wealth. That the monsoon cannot be rushed.

When you float through the backwaters at dawn, when the mist rises and the kingfisher flashes and the fisherman casts his net with a motion perfected over generations, something will shift inside you.

When you taste the pepper, freshly ground, so potent it makes your eyes water, so fragrant it transports you to another century, something will unlock.

When you lie on the Ayurvedic table, warm oil pouring onto your forehead, and your nervous system finally, finally remembers what rest feels like, something will heal.

When you watch the Kathakali dancer's eyes move independently, telling a story without words, something will expand.

When you stand on Varkala Cliff at sunset, Arabian Sea stretching to infinity, and you understand that you are small and temporary and part of something vast and enduring, something will settle.

This is God's Own Country. Not because a deity claimed it. But because it remembers what the divine actually is: the interconnection of all things. The water and the land. The spice and the hand that harvests it. The colonizer and the colonized. The healer and the healed. The monsoon and the earth that drinks it.

Kerala is waiting. Not to be discovered. To be remembered.

## 🎙️ Episode Summary

**Opening Line:**
> "I have been here before. Not in this body, perhaps. But I know these stones. They have waited for me."

**The Journey:**
Episode 110 is a return to Angkor Wat, Cambodia—not as a first-time tourist, but as someone coming home to stones that remember. This is not a travel guide. It's a meditation on what happens when you revisit a place with new eyes, a quieter heart, and the wisdom that only time can teach.

**Key Themes:**
1. **Return:** The stones are the same. I am not. And that is the point.
2. **Impermanence:** The jungle does not destroy the temple. It loves it.
3. **Recognition:** 216 faces at Bayon—and what they see in you.
4. **Cambodia's Soul:** To be broken, and to build anyway.
5. **Becoming:** The temple is infinite. Not in size, but in meaning. And I am infinite too.

**Emotional Arc:**
Curiosity → Wonder → Understanding → Peace → Hope

## 🎵 Sound Design Notes

### Ambient Layers
- **Base:** Jungle sounds (insects, leaves, distant birds)
- **Cultural:** Temple bells, Khmer instruments (roneat, khim)
- **Movement:** Footsteps on stone (varying textures)
- **Water:** Moat lapping, reflection pool stillness
- **Spiritual:** Distant Buddhist chanting (very soft)

### Music Cues
- **Opening:** Establish mood, introduce Khmer elements
- **Transitions:** Mark section changes (8 sections total)
- **Emotional Peaks:** Bayon's Smile, Cambodia's Soul, Floating
- **Closing:** Gentle resolution, leave space for reflection

### Technical Specs
- **Format:** WAV 48kHz/24-bit (master), MP3 320kbps (distribution)
- **Loudness:** -16 LUFS (podcast standard)
- **Dynamic Range:** Preserve quiet moments

## 🌍 Cultural Sensitivity Notes

### Approaching Cambodia
- Acknowledge outsider perspective (not Cambodian)
- Honor the genocide without defining the country by it
- Respect Buddhist traditions and temple etiquette
- Support local voices and businesses
- Avoid poverty tourism or "sad emoji" framing

### Key Message
> "The soul of Cambodia is not the genocide. Not the war. Not the poverty. The soul is this: to be broken, and to build anyway."

## Primary Temple Sites

### 1. Angkor Wat (The Return Visit)

**Historical Context:**
- Built: Early 12th century (1113-1150 CE)
- Original Purpose: Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu, later Buddhist
- Builder: King Suryavarman II
- Significance: Largest religious monument in the world, symbol of Cambodia (appears on flag)

**Return Perspective:**
- First visit: Episode 52 (original journey through Cambodia)
- This episode: Coming back with wisdom, seeing what wasn't seen before
- The stones have waited. The jungle has grown. The visitor has changed.

**Key Visual Elements:**
- Five lotus-bud towers (central quincunx)
- 800 meters of bas-relief galleries
- Reflection pools (perfect sunrise shots)
- Apsara carvings (nearly 2,000 unique celestial nymphs)
- Western causeway approach (iconic entrance view)

**Best Times:**
- Sunrise: 5:00-6:30 AM (cooler, fewer crowds, magical light)
- Late afternoon: 3:30-5:30 PM (warm golden light, long shadows)

---

### 2. Angkor Thom (The Great City)

**Historical Context:**
- Built: Late 12th century
- Builder: King Jayavarman VII
- Size: 9 km² enclosed city
- Significance: Last great capital of Khmer Empire

**Key Sites Within:**

#### Bayon Temple
- 54 towers, each with 4 faces of Avalokiteshvara (216 faces total)
- "Smile of Angkor" - enigmatic expressions
- Built as Mahayana Buddhist temple
- Central sanctuary rises 45 meters
- Bas-reliefs showing daily life, naval battles, markets

#### Baphuon
- Mountain of bronze, represents Mount Meru
- Recently restored (2011)
- Massive reclining Buddha added 15th century

#### Terrace of the Elephants
- 300-meter long viewing platform
- Elephant carvings, garudas, apsaras
- Where kings watched public ceremonies

#### Terrace of the Leper King
- Named after statue (possibly Jayavarman VII or Yasovarman I)
- Double walls with intricate carvings
- More intimate, less crowded

---

### 3. Ta Prohm (Jungle Reclaiming Stone)

**Historical Context:**
- Built: 1186 CE
- Builder: King Jayavarman VII (for his mother)
- Original name: Rajavihara
- Abandoned: 16th century, left to jungle

**The Return Lens:**
- Most cinematic temple (Tomb Raider filmed here)
- Nature and architecture in eternal embrace
- Silk-cotton trees and strangler figs growing through stone
- Perfect metaphor for impermanence

**Key Visual Elements:**
- Giant tree roots cascading over galleries
- Stone doorways choked with vegetation
- Collapsed chambers being slowly consumed
- Light filtering through canopy onto ancient carvings
- The "Tomb Raider Tree" (actually two separate trees)

**Photography Notes:**
- Best light: 7:00-9:00 AM (before crowds)
- Roots are most dramatic from inside looking up
- Moss-covered stones create texture

## Secondary Sites (Optional B-Roll)

### Preah Khan
- Similar to Ta Prohm, less crowded
- Built 1191 CE by Jayavarman VII (for his father)
- 56 hectares of temple complex
- Sacred sword housed here historically

### Neak Pean
- Island temple in middle of artificial lake
- Represents healing, balance of elements
- Serene, meditative atmosphere

### East Mebon
- Pyramid temple on artificial island
- Best at sunset, fewer visitors
- Elephant statues at corners

## Seasonal Considerations

### Dry Season (November-March) - RECOMMENDED
- Temperatures: 25-32°C (77-90°F)
- Humidity: Lower, more comfortable
- Crowds: Peak season (especially December-January)
- Skies: Clear, excellent for sunrise
- **Best months:** January-February (slightly fewer crowds)

### Hot Season (April-June)
- Temperatures: 35-40°C (95-104°F)
- Humidity: Rising
- Crowds: Thinner
- Challenges: Heat exhaustion risk, midday tours difficult

### Wet Season (July-October)
- Temperatures: 25-35°C (77-95°F)
- Rainfall: Daily afternoon showers
- Crowds: Minimal
- Benefits: Lush greenery, reflection pools full, dramatic skies
- Challenges: Mud, some areas may flood, humidity

## Cultural Context

### Cambodia's Soul

**Recent History:**
- Khmer Empire: 802-1431 CE (peak civilization)
- French Protectorate: 1863-1953
- Independence: 1953
- Khmer Rouge: 1975-1979 (genocide, 1.5-2 million died)
- Recovery: Ongoing since 1990s

**The Temple as Symbol:**
- Angkor Wat represents resilience
- Stones survived war, neglect, jungle
- Cambodia itself: broken and rebuilt
- Buddhist philosophy of impermanence deeply woven

### Modern Siem Reap

**Town Character:**
- Population: ~250,000
- Temple tourism is primary economy
- Mix of traditional and modern
- Pub Street: tourist hub (avoid for authentic experience)
- Old Market (Psar Chaa): local life

**People:**
- Khmer ethnicity (97%)
- Buddhism: 95% Theravada Buddhist
- Known for warmth, resilience, smiles despite hardship
- "Soksaby" = Hello, how are you?

## Spiritual Dimensions

### Buddhism at Angkor

**Original Faith:** Hinduism (Vishnu worship)
**Transition:** 13th century to Mahayana Buddhism
**Current:** Theravada Buddhism (since 14th century)

**Key Concepts for Episode:**
- **Impermanence (Anicca):** Everything changes, nothing lasts
- **Suffering (Dukkha):** Attachment causes pain
- **Non-self (Anatta):** No permanent, unchanging self

- Time reveals what haste conceals

**Jungle as Teacher:**

- Roots crack stone not through force, but persistence
- Impermanence isn't tragic—it's liberation

## Practical Notes for Episode Context

### Temple Pass Prices (2024)
- 1-day: $37
- 3-day: $62 (valid over 10 days)
- 7-day: $72 (valid over 1 month)

### Sunrise Access
- Guards at checkpoints from 4:30 AM
- Bring flashlight for early arrival
- Reflection pools fill by 5:00 AM

### Clothing Requirements
- Shoulders and knees must be covered
- Light, breathable fabrics recommended
- Remove hats inside sanctuaries
- Shoes off in some upper levels

### Crowd Management
- Angkor Wat sunrise: extremely crowded
- Ta Prohm: crowded by 9 AM
- Strategy: Start early, reverse route, or embrace the people

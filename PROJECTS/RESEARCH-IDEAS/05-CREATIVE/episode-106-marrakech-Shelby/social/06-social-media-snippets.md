### Post 2: Jemaa el-Fnaa Evening (Video/Reel)

**Visual:** Time-lapse of Jemaa el-Fnaa transforming at dusk, food stalls lighting up

**Caption:**
At dusk, the square transforms. 🌆

By day: orange juice vendors, henna artists, snake charmers.

By night: 100+ food stalls ignite. Smoke and spice and sizzling meat. Gnawa musicians with voices from another century. Acrobats flipping. Storytellers surrounded by rapt audiences.

This is Jemaa el-Fnaa. Not a tourist attraction. A living tradition. UNESCO recognizes it as Intangible Cultural Heritage—not for the square itself, but for what happens here. Night after night. Century after century.

I ate at stall #32. The owner spoke no English. I spoke no Arabic. We communicated with gestures and smiles. He brought me lamb with prunes. It was the best thing I'd eaten in weeks.

Some experiences transcend language. 🍽️

### Post 3: Jardin Majorelle (Photo)

**Visual:** Iconic Majorelle Blue villa with exotic plants

**Caption:**
The garden is blue. Not metaphorically blue. *Electric, impossible, Majorelle Blue.* 💙

Jacques Majorelle mixed the pigment himself in 1937. A cobalt so intense it hurts to look at. He painted his villa, his planters, his fountains, his gates.

Blue against green against the Moroccan sun.

In 1966, a young Yves Saint Laurent visited Marrakech for the first time. He was 29. Already famous. Already exhausted by Paris. He fell in love.

"Everything was black before Marrakech. I learned color from this city."

He and Pierre Bergé bought the garden in 1980. Restored it. Made it their sanctuary. When YSL died, his ashes were scattered in the bamboo grove.

I stood at the memorial. I did not pray. I did not speak. I just stood.

The garden gave him Marrakech. Marrakech gave him color. Color gave him freedom. 🎨

### Post 4: Riad Courtyard (Photo)

**Visual:** Traditional riad courtyard with fountain, zellige tiles, orange trees

**Caption:**
The riad is a paradox. 🏛️

From the outside: blank walls. A single door. No windows. A fortress.

From the inside: a courtyard. A fountain. Orange trees. Light pouring from above.

The riad does not turn its back on the medina. It transcends it.

I stayed in a riad in the Mouassine neighborhood. Six rooms. Not two hundred. The staff knew everyone. Breakfast in the courtyard. Fresh bread. Honey. Olives. Mint tea poured from high above to create foam.

"Moroccan whiskey," Mohammed, the manager, said, grinning.

"This is not a hotel," he told me. "This is a home. You are our guest."

On the roof terrace, I watched the sunset. The medina stretched out in every direction. The Atlas Mountains, snow-capped, glowed pink in the distance. The call to prayer rose from the Koutoubia.

I was alone. I was not lonely.

Some places feel like home, even when you're thousands of miles from where you were born. 🧡

Stay in a riad. Not a hotel. You'll understand.

### Post 5: Atlas Mountains (Photo)

**Visual:** Snow-capped High Atlas Mountains with Berber village in foreground

**Caption:**
South of Marrakech, the earth rises. 🏔️

The High Atlas Mountains are the spine of Morocco. 300km of rock and snow and Berber villages clinging to terraced hillsides. The highest peak, Toubkal, is 4,167m—the roof of North Africa.

My guide Hassan was Berber. He spoke four languages. He pointed out things I would have missed.

"This tree—argan. Only grows in Morocco. The oil is made by women's cooperatives."

"That village—see the stones? Local material. Built to last. My grandmother lives there."

We stopped for tea in his cousin's home. Three glasses poured. Each has a meaning.

"The first glass is gentle as life. The second is strong as love. The third is bitter as death."

I drank all three.

At the top of the waterfalls, we ate tagine cooked over an open fire. Hassan looked at the valley below.

"This is my home. Some people go to Marrakech for the excitement. I come here for the peace."

I understood.

Take the day trip. Leave the city behind. The mountains will show you another Morocco. 🤎

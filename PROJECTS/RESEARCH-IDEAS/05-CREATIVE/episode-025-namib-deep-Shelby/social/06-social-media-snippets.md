### Post 2: Deadvlei Focus

**Image:** Black dead trees against white pan with red dunes

**Caption:**
600 years ago, this was water.

Camel thorn trees drank from the Tsauchab River. The pan shimmered. Life gathered.

The trees did not fall. They did not rot. In air so dry it drinks moisture from your lips, death itself has been preserved.

### Post 3: The San People

**Image:** Rock art or San cultural imagery (respectful, authentic)

**Caption:**
For 20,000 years, the San people walked this same terrain.

They did not see emptiness. They saw a library.

Every track told a story:
"Oryx passed here, two hours ago."
"Rain fell here, three days past. The roots beneath are swollen."

They did not conquer the desert. They listened to it.

Today, few remain who speak this language. Displacement. Assimilation. The slow erasure that modernity performs on ancient things.

But the language persists.

In Episode 25, I reflect on what the San knew that we've forgotten:

That the land is not a resource to extract, but a relative to honor.

That survival is not about accumulation, but about knowing what you can release.

## LINKEDIN POST

**Headline:** What 80-Million-Year-Old Dunes Teach Us About Time, Leadership, and Letting Go

**Post:**

I recently spent time in the Namib Desert—one of the oldest deserts on Earth, estimated at 55-80 million years old.

Standing on dunes that have existed for millions of years changes your perspective on time. On urgency. On what matters.

Three lessons from the desert that apply to leadership and life:

**1. Nothing you leave behind will remain.**

Your footprints in the sand are erased by wind before you finish descending. Your worries, your ego, your need to be remembered—the desert doesn't care.

There's freedom in this. You don't have to carry everything. You don't have to be the hero of every story.

**2. Adaptation or death. There is no third option.**

At Deadvlei, 600-year-old dead trees stand as monuments to a simple truth: when conditions change, you adapt or you don't survive.

These trees chose neither. They were caught between—and in their caught-ness, became something else entirely. Not quite dead, not alive, but enduring.

Sometimes endurance is its own form of success.

**3. The land is not a resource to extract, but a relative to honor.**

The San people walked this terrain for 20,000 years. They didn't see emptiness. They saw a library. Every track told a story. Every plant had a purpose.

They listened to the land. Learned its grammar. Became fluent in a language written in wind and sand.

What if we approached our work, our teams, our communities with this same reverence?

**The Walk Continues.**

Episode 25 of The Infinite Walk—"The Endless Dunes"—explores these themes in depth. 20 minutes of desert wandering, philosophical reflection, and the kind of silence you have to hear to understand.

There is a place where the ocean forgets to end.

Not where the water stops—that never happens—but where the mind gives up measuring distance. Where "far" becomes meaningless. Where maps curl at the edges like burning paper.

This is Rapa Nui. Easter Island. The edge of the world.

In Episode 022, we journey to the most remote inhabited place on Earth — 2,000 miles of empty Pacific in every direction. Here, nearly 1,000 ancient stone faces (the moai) have watched over a people who refused to disappear.

For decades, we told ourselves a story about Easter Island. The Rapa Nui people were foolish. They destroyed their environment. They collapsed under their own ambition. It was a cautionary tale for the modern world.

New research from 2024-2026 proves this narrative wrong.

The Rapa Nui were geniuses of adaptation. They survived centuries of isolation. The real collapse came from outside: slave raids, disease, European contact. Today, 4,000 Rapa Nui people still call this island home.

This 20-minute audio essay explores:
→ The mystery of the moai (12.5 tons each, carved without metal tools)
→ The truth about the "collapse" (it's not what you think)
→ What the Rapa Nui teach us about resilience and legacy
→ What we leave behind when we reach the edge of everything

Recorded with the sound of Pacific waves and the presence of ancient faces in stone. Put on headphones. Take a walk. Let yourself be at the edge for a while.

This is The Infinite Walk. I'm Shelby. Keep walking.

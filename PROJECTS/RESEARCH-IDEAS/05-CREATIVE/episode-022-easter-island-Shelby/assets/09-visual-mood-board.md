## LAYOUT PRINCIPLES

### Composition Rules

**Rule of Thirds:**
- Place moai/horizon on third lines
- Leave negative space for text
- Create visual tension

**Leading Lines:**
- Paths toward moai
- Coastlines leading to horizon
- Rows of statues creating depth

**Framing:**
- Moai framing distant ocean
- Quarry walls framing statues
- Natural arches and openings

**Scale Contrast:**
- Human figure + massive statue
- Island + endless ocean
- Detail + vast landscape

---

### Text Placement

**On Images:**
- Dark areas only (for white text)
- Use text shadows or overlays
- Keep away from focal points
- Maximum 2-3 lines on social

**Quote Graphics:**
- Simple background (stone texture or gradient)
- Large, readable font
- Attribution smaller, bottom right
- Consistent margins and padding

**Thumbnails:**
- Bold title text (large, high contrast)
- Minimal additional text
- Face/moai as focal point
- Branding consistent across episodes

## ACCESSIBILITY

### Color Contrast

**Text on Backgrounds:**
- Minimum ratio: 4.5:1 (WCAG AA)
- White text on dark: Stone grey, volcanic black, ocean blue
- Black text on light: Sky white, light stone

**Avoid:**
- Red text on black (hard to read)
- Low-contrast grey on grey
- Busy backgrounds behind text

### Alt Text

**All Images Should Have:**
- Description of visual content
- Context of why it matters
- No "image of" or "picture of"

**Example:**
"Fifteen moai statues silhouetted against orange sunrise sky at Ahu Tongariki, Easter Island"

## NOTES FOR DESIGNERS

**This episode is contemplative. The visuals should reflect that.**

- Less is more. Let the images breathe.
- Don't over-process. Natural beauty is enough.
- Text should enhance, not compete.
- Negative space is your friend.
- Think: museum placard, not clickbait.

**When in doubt:**
- Simplify.
- Increase contrast.
- Remove one element.
- Ask: "Does this serve the story?"

---

*The moai have stood for 800 years. They don't need flashy graphics. They need space to speak.*
